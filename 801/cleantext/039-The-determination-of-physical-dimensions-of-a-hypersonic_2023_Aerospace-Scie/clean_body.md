## 1. Introduction

The performance of an air-breathing hypersonic vehicle is closely related with the performance of its intake system [1,2]. The intake system contains a crucial subsystem, i.e., compression subsystem, which is used to compress the free flow and create appropriate air inlet conditions for the combustor. A compression system, take the multi-stage planar one for instance, is formed by several compression ramps, which commonly adopt thin-panel structures in consideration of the low-weight requirement. However, the thin-panel structure is liable to be excitated by various vibration sources such as hypersonic flow (fluid-structural interaction), engine (thrust variation) and noise, and the induced panel vibration has significant effect on the performance of air intake system. Thus, for the design of a compression system, it is essential to obtain vibration characteristics of compression ramps and clarify their effects on the intake performance.

According to the geometric configurations, the intake system can be classified into axisymmetric, two-dimensional planar and three-dimensional ones [3]. The axisymmetric one adopts axisymmetric compression surfaces and is well adaptive to axisymmetric scramjets and vehicles, like HRE engine of NASA [4], GTX engine of NASA [5], SABRE engine of Reaction Engines Ltd. [6], and so on. The two-dimensional one always adopts planar compression surfaces consist of several ramps and can be found in various well-known lifting body vehicles like X-43A [7] and X-51A [8,9] of NASA. One of the advantages of two-dimensional intake system is its well adaptability to the integration design of intake and fuselage. The three-dimensional compression intake system is the one with additional lateral compression surfaces like the Strutjet engine of GenCorp Aerojet [10], and it can also be found as inward turning type like Rectangular-to-Elliptical-Shape-Transition intake of NASA [11]. The additional compression and turning processes in such three-dimensional intake system contribute to the improvement of compression efficiency.

In this work, an air-breathing hypersonic vehicle with nonaxisymmetric lifting body configuration is involved, and a rocket based combined cycle with three-stage planar compression intake system is adopted. The performance of intake system is always evaluated by the total pressure recovery and mass flow coefficient at the outlet. Such type of compression system contains external and internal compression sections, and the external one is always composed by several ramps and has more configurational parameters to be determined. Configurational parameters like the shock angles, compression angles and the length projections of compression ramps are always designed based on the so-called “shockon-lip” [12,13] and Oswatitsch [14] criterions. The “shock-on-lip” criterion means all the shock waves are converged to the cowl lip with no spillage and indicates the maximum mass flow coefficient (should be 100%), while the Oswatitsch criterion indicates the maximum total pressure recovery [15]. However, in real conditions, the viscous effect will lead to the reduction of intake performance, thus viscosity corrections by corresponding CFD simulations are essential subsequent steps. A great number of researches about complex fluid-structural phenomena, e.g., turbulent boundary layers [16,17], aeroelastic effects [18,19], dynamic aeroelastic effects [20,21], aerothermoelastic effects [22,23], shock-wave-boundarylayer interactions [24–27] and involved thermochemical [28] and high temperature non-equilibrium effects [29,30] also contribute to the cognition of viscous effects and the effective design of intake system.

For the intake system of air-breathing hypersonic vehicles, the widely adopted thin panel structure is liable to be excitated by various vibration sources such as hypersonic flow, engine and noise. Take the hypersonic flow for instance, the structural vibration that induced by hypersonic flows is always considered as the fluidstructural or aeroelastic interaction between hypersonic flow and intake panels. As discussed above, the static aeroelastic [18,19], dynamic aeroelastic [20,21] and aerothermoelastic [22,23] phenomena of hypersonic intake system are widely studied by the timeconsuming and sometimes unaffordable CFD analysis. The shape deformation and vibration of intake panels under such extreme conditions has high uncertainty and non-negligible influence on the engine performance, and the unpredicted deformation might cause serious consequences like the unstart of intake, chocking of combustor or failure of structures [18,31,32]. Thus, the structural design of intake system should consider objectives and constraints in two perspectives, i.e., the structural and intake performance. For the structural performance, the static and vibration mechanic analyses under certain hypersonic conditions need to be carried out. For the intake performance, the flow fields and the intake performance parameters, i.e., the total pressure recovery and mass flow coefficients, with panel deformations and vibrations should be simulated and obtained. A clear structural design path of external compression and intake system considering the various vibration sources, high uncertainty and complex design objectives and constraints is essential while still absent at present.

The ideal panels should have enough stiffness to reduce the effects of vibrations and the stiffness reinforcing design can be realized by increasing panel thickness, strengthening ribs and so on. The structural design path, take thickness for instance, should have three branch paths, i.e., avoiding too high stress of structure, averting resonance of structure and maintaining intake performance. As discussed above, there are several vibration sources like the fluid-structural interaction, engine thrust variation and so on for a hypersonic vehicle, from the structural point of view the available thickness of compression ramp panels should be determined by considerations of all the vibration sources, which is actually difficult to realize. Thus, in this work, the mode frequency under real constraint conditions and deformations under aerodynamic force of panels with different thicknesses are obtained first by mode and static mechanic analysis, respectively; then, the effects of forced panel vibrations, i.e., vibration frequency and amplitude on the intake performance are clarified; finally, the available panel thickness is determined by fluid-structural analysis from the structural and flow point of views and a design path of compression system is developed by considering both the structural and intake performance.

## 2. The hypersonic vehicle and multi-stage compression system

Fig. 1 shows the hypersonic vehicle and its intake system. Fig. 1(a) shows the configuration and the trajectory of the vehicle. The vehicle has length of about 30 m, width of about 5 m, and wingspan of about 15 m. The flight altitude is between 0 to 60 km and the Mach number is between 0 to 8. In this work, the air-breathing combined engine is adopted and the engine performance is closely dependent on the air intake system. The intake system is composed of compression section and isolator section, and as indicated by the red dashed line in Fig. 1(b), the compression section can be divided into external and internal sections. For the vehicle studied in this paper, the external compression section is designed to create three shocks and thus contains three stages of compression ramps, i.e., CI, CII and CIII. For a flight vehicle with such large scale and high speed, the noteworthy vibration of compression ramps can be caused by variation of aerodynamic force, propulsion force and so on. As shown in Fig. 1(c), such type of vibration will influence the near-wall flow field to a great extent and its effect on the vehicle’s intake performance should not be neglected.

The configuration of compression ramps CI, CII and CIII are obtained based on the so-called shock-on-lip criterion and Oswatitsch criterion and subsequent modification with consideration of viscous effects by a series of CFD simulations. The configurational parameters are shown in Fig. 2 and the obtained values are listed in Table 1: the total length of the intake system is 14.5 m; the first, second and third shock angles are 13.6◦, 15.2◦ and 16.9◦, respectively; the first, second and third compression angles are 4.5◦, 5.0◦ and 5.5◦, respectively; and the length projections in x-direction of CI, CII and CIII are 3.4, 2.1 and 2.9 m, respectively.

The vibration characteristic of compression ramps is related to several complex excitations which are difficult to simulate. In this work, the mode characteristics of compression ramps are analyzed and the highest available frequencies for different panel thicknesses are obtained. Then, the influences of ramps’ vibration on the intake performance are clarified by simulations under forced vibration conditions with typical frequencies and amplitudes. Finally, the vibration of compression ramps that induced by hypersonic flow and its related fluid-structural interaction effects are analyzed by coupling simulations, and the available panel thickness is thus determined from the structural and flow point of views.

## 3.2. Flow field analysis

## 3.2.1. Governing equations

In this work, flow field is numerically simulated based on the commercial software FLUENT. The unified governing equation can be described as:

$$\frac {\partial ( \rho \phi )} {\partial t} + \mathrm{div} ( \rho U \phi ) = \mathrm{div} ( \Gamma \mathbf {g r a d} \phi ) + Q _ {s}\tag{2}$$

where $\rho$ is the density, t is the time, and U is the velocity vector with three components in x, y and z directions; φ,  and $Q _ {s}$ are the universal variable, the generalized diffusion coefficient and source term, respectively. In this work, the mass, momentum and energy conservation equations are involved and the specific physical parameters for $\phi ,$  and $Q _ {s}$ are listed in Table 3. In Table $^ {3 ,}$ T and P represent temperature and pressure, respectively; η, λ and $c _ {p}$ represent kinetic viscosity, thermal conductivity and specific heat capacity, respectively. Besides, in this work, the SST k-ω model with good applicability is adopted to simulate the turbulence.

The unsteady flow field is very sensitive to the computational time step which should take both the accuracy and efficiency into account. In this work, the vibration frequency of compression ramp panels and the transition time of flow variation from inlet to outlet will influence the time step. During one time step, at least 20 data about the panel vibration and flow variation should be obtained. Then, from the point view of panel vibration, the time step should be:

$$\Delta t _ {1} \leq \frac {1} {20 f}\tag{3}$$

where $f$ is the vibration frequency which has maximum value of 250 Hz, thus the time step should be not greater than 0.0002 s.

From the point view of flow variation, the time step should be:

$$\Delta t _ {2} \leq \frac {L} {20 V _ {\infty}}\tag{4}$$

where L is the length of intake system which equals to 14.5 m, $V _ {\infty}$ is the velocity of free stream which equals to 1758.7 m/s, thus, the time step should be not greater than 0.000412 s.

Finally, 0.0002 s is adopted as the computational time step in this work.

The two-way fluid-structural coupling is shown in Fig. 4(b): for the present time step, the fluid field is calculated first and the obtained pressure at the interface element of fluid region is used as the boundary condition for the deformation analysis of structural region, and the calculated displacement on the interface node of structural region is then used as the boundary condition of the fluid field simulation for the next time step. The specific data transfer at the fluid-structural interface contains two primary processes, i.e., the mapping and subsequent interpolation. The fluid mesh node should be mapped to a structural mesh node to receive displacements and vice versa for pressures, and the mapping weight can be obtained for each node to receive data. The interpolation process is used to obtain displacement of fluid node based on the mapping weight and structural node displacement, and vice versa for pressure. In this work, the Bucket Surface and General Grid mapping methods are adopted in transfers of displacement and pressure which are non-conservative and conservative quantities, respectively, and detailed descriptions can be found in Ref. [35].

The performance of an intake system is always evaluated by total pressure recovery coefficient (σ ) and mass flow coefficient $( \varphi )$ , which represent the loss of total pressure and mass flow, respectively. σ can be calculated by the total pressure at the outlet divided by that of free stream, and $\varphi$ can be calculated by the mass flow at the outlet divided by that captured by the intake system.

## 4. Results and discussions

## 4.1. Mode analysis

As discussed above, the mode of compression ramp panel is analyzed under two types of constraint conditions, CoA and CoB.

Figs. 7 and 8 show the mode shape (normalized displacement pattern) of the six lowest order mode under CoA and CoB for CI with 5 mm panel thickness, respectively. The values in Figs. 7 and 8 are the normalized displacements and no real physical meaning is involved. From the figures one can find that CoA and CoB have very similar mode shape, the vibration area for CoA is larger while the vibration amplitude is lower than CoB. The natural frequency of CI under CoA and CoB conditions is listed in Table 6. One can find that for the $1 ^ {\mathsf{st}}$ to $6 ^ {\mathrm {{t h}}}$ natural frequencies, CoB results into higher values with absolute increment of 14.95 to 17.09 Hz and relative deviation of 54.86% to 28.48%. It shows that the CoB condition indicates noteworthy stiffness improvement. In this work, the compression ramp mode will be analyzed under CoB which is closer to the real engineering condition.

The stiffness of the panel is dependent on the thickness, and in this work, the influence of panel thickness is analyzed from 2 to 30 mm. Fig. 9 shows the natural frequency under different panel thicknesses. From Fig. 9, one can find that: the first order natural frequency increases from 16.8 to 249 Hz, 17.76 to 259 Hz, and 17.12 to 251 Hz, while the sixth order natural frequency increases from 29.9 to 435 Hz, 50.3 to 699 Hz, and 38.1 to 533 Hz with the panel thickness increases from 2 to 30 mm for CI, CII and CIII, respectively; under the same panel thickness, the natural frequency of CI has the lowest value, CII has the highest value and CIII has values in between. In real conditions, the operational vibration frequency of a panel with certain thickness should be smaller than the first order natural frequency under the same thickness. Thus, it can be concluded that when the panel thickness increases from 2 to 30 mm, the available upper bound of operational vibration frequencies in real conditions increases from 16.8 to 249 Hz, 17.76 to 259 Hz, and 17.12 to 251 Hz for CI, CII and CIII, respectively.

Panel stiffness is the main factor that influence the vibration characteristics, thus in the present work, the relation between panel stiffness and panel thickness which is the design parameter should be obtained. The elastic deformation characteristic of CI, CII and CIII panels with different thicknesses is numerical simulated, and the panel stiffness is calculated under two types of loads, i.e., the uniformly distributed and the concentrated loads and two types of constraint conditions, i.e., CoA and CoB. The numerical results are displayed in Fig. 10, the curves show that the panel stiffness increases with the increase of panel thickness, and the panel with CoB which has additional fixed nodes on rivets results into higher stiffness. To some extent, Fig. 10 provides necessary and quantitative description of relations among the panel thickness, stiffness and vibration characteristics.

In this work, the forced vibration with frequency of 10 to 250 Hz which correspond to panel thickness range of about 2 to 30 mm for CI, CII and CIII are assumed, and the influences of compression ramps vibration on the flow field are then studied. The amplitude range of the vibration is obtained based on the elastic deformation analysis under aerodynamic force. The pressures that created by the aerodynamic force under flight altitude of 24 km and Mach number of 6 are 0.0061, 0.0103 and 0.0196 MPa for CI, CII and CIII, respectively. Fig. 11(a), (b) and (c) show the displacement contours obtained from the elastic deformation analysis of CI, CII and CIII, respectively, one can find that the panel deformation has similar distribution of the first order mode and the maximum displacement is about 110 mm. Thus, the amplitude range of the forced vibration in subsequent subsections is assumed to be 0 to 110 mm.

## 4.2. Effects of the forced vibration of compression ramps

## 4.2.1. Effects of vibration amplitude

Fig. 12 shows the pressure (left) and velocity (right) fields under Mach 6 and 24 km altitude condition with vibrating CI (panel thickness of 5 mm) and rigid CII and CIII. Fig. 12(a), (b), (c), (d), (e) and (f) show the results under vibration amplitudes of 0.01, 0.03, 0.05, 0.08 and 0.11 m, respectively, and the frequency is assumed to be 100 Hz. From Fig. 12, one can find that the flow field of external compression section (left side of the yellow line) is more sensitive than the internal section (right side of the yellow line) to the CI vibration, and the upstream is more sensitive than the downstream. Such trend is more clear when it is presented specifically in sequence of the first, second, third shocks and following flow fields: the fluctuation of the first shock (indicated by white lines in pressure fields) and its deviation from the lip increase with the increase of CI vibration amplitude; the fluctuation of the second shock (indicated by white lines in pressure fields) appears when the amplitude is larger than 50 mm; the influence of CI vibration on the third shock and following internal flow field is limited.

The vibration of CI will finally lead to the fluctuation of intake performance over time. Fig. 13 shows the variation of total pressure recovery coefficient and mass flow coefficient along with time, and the frequency and amplitude are 100 Hz and 50 mm, respectively. The top red line, middle blue line and bottom black line indicate the total pressure recovery coefficient, mass flow coefficient and CI panel vibration displacement, respectively. From Fig. 13 one can find that: for the total pressure recovery coefficient, the average value and the fluctuation range are about 0.446 and 1.33%, respectively; for the mass flow coefficient, the average value and the fluctuation range are about 0.958 and 0.76%, respectively; the total pressure recovery coefficient and mass flow coefficient between 0 to 0.07 s have obviously higher values than those after 0.07 s, which means the flow fluctuation that induced by the CI panel vibration reaches to the outlet after 0.07 s; also, it can be found that the stable fluctuation section after 0.07 s of total pressure recovery coefficient and mass flow coefficient lines have close frequency with CI vibration.

The cases of vibrating CII and CIII have similar qualitative results with vibrating CI, i.e., similar pressure fields, velocity fields and intake performance variations along with time, thus no more detailed descriptions are supplied for the limited paper length. The vibration of compression ramps will finally influence the intake performance and the quantitative results should be obtained. Fig. 14 shows the average values and fluctuation ranges of total pressure recovery and mass flow coefficients under different vibration amplitudes for CI, CII and CIII. Fig. 14(a) and (b) are the average values of recovery and flow coefficients, respectively, while Fig. 14(c) and (d) are the fluctuation ranges of recovery and flow coefficients, respectively. The lines with rectangles, circles and triangles indicate results of CI, CII and CIII vibration, respectively. From Fig. 14(a) and (c), one can find that: with the increase of vibration amplitude from 0 to 0.11 m, the total pressure recovery coefficient decreases from about 0.452 to 0.442, 0.452 to 0.437 and 0.452 to 0.421, while the fluctuation range increases from 0.7% to 3.56%, 0.83% to 7.98% and 1.73% to 19.4% for CI, CII and CIII vibrations, respectively. It can be concluded in general that the panel vibrations of CI, CII and CIII have sequentially enhanced effect on the intake performance.

## 4.3. Fluid-structural interaction effects

The vibration of compression ramp panels can be induced by a variety of excitations like the fluid-structural interaction, engine thrust variation, various noises, etc. In this work, the primary vibration excitation, i.e., the hypersonic flow and the related fluidstructural interaction effects is simulated, the vibration of ramp structure, flow field and its effects on the intake performance are analyzed and available panel thicknesses of compression ramps are obtained.

## 4.3.1. Structural vibration of compression ramps

Fig. 17(a), (b) and (c) show the displacement of CI in spatial, time and frequency domain with panel thicknesses of 0.002, 0.005 and 0.010 m, respectively. From the table one can find that: the consideration of panels’ fluid-structural interaction will lead to the reduction and fluctuation of total pressure recovery and mass flow coefficients; the total pressure recovery and mass flow coefficients are smaller than that obtained in Subsection 4.2, and it could be due to the three-dimensional effects; the fluctuation of total pressure recovery coefficient is larger than that of mass flow coefficient, which reveals that the later one is less sensitive to the panels’ stiffness, and similar phenomena can be found in Figs. 14 and 16; the intake performance fluctuation range decreases with the increase of panel thickness which brings about greater stiffness, while it increases when the fluid-structural interaction source transfers from CI, CII to CIII, i.e., from the fuselage leading edge to the intake lip.

## 4.4. Design path of compression system

The available panel thickness should be determined by the intake flow performance and the panel structural performance. For the point of view of intake performance, one can find in Subsection 4.3.2 and Table 7 that the fluctuation of total pressure recovery and mass flow coefficients is very limited when the thickness is larger than 2.0 mm, which indicates the lower bound of available panel thickness for CI, CII and CIII according to the intake performance requirement. For the point of view of structural performance, the resonance limit is mainly considered in this work, i.e., the panel operational vibration frequency should be smaller than its first order natural frequency. Fig. 21 shows the natural and operational frequencies of CI, CII and CIII under different panel thicknesses. In Fig. 21, the black, red and blue solid and dashed lines show the natural and operational frequencies of CI, CII and CIII, respectively. One can find that the natural and operational frequencies have opposite trend along with thickness, i.e., the former increases while the latter decreases with the increase of panel thickness. An available panel thickness should enable the operational frequency smaller than the natural one, which means that the intersections of the two lines (indicated by orange points) in Fig. 21 should be the lower bound of available panel thickness for compression ramps. As shown in Fig. 21, the three intersections for CI, CII and CIII are between 5.0 to 5.5 mm, 5.5 to 6.0 mm, 6.0 to 6.5 mm, respectively, thus the lower bound of panel thickness for CI, CII and CIII can be assumed to be 5.5, 6.0 and 6.5 mm, respectively. In addition, such values are larger than 2.0 mm and reveal that the consideration of resonance limit is essential for the design of compression system.

According to above discussions of this work, the design process of compression system for an air-breathing hypersonic vehicle is summarized. The design path is shown in Fig. 22 and has three key steps: the first step is the preliminary design which consists of the acquirement of configurational parameters like shock angles, compression angles, ramp length, etc., and the input of physical dimensional parameters like panel thickness, etc.; the second step is the performance analysis which consists of static mechanical, mode vibration, fluid-structural interaction, etc., and outputs structural and flow performance parameters like stress, deformation, natural frequency, operational frequency, recovery coefficient, flow coefficient, etc.; the third step is the performance evaluation from point of views of material strength limit, structure resonance limit, intake design requirement, etc., in this step, if all the performance parameters meet the design requirements, then output the compression system, if not, the physical dimensions should be updated and the whole process should be iterated until the convergence is obtained.

The future engineering applications and limitations of this work should be further noted. In this work, the lower bounds of panel thicknesses for compression surfaces are obtained and an appropriate design path of compression system is developed, and these parameters and methods provide key references for the intake system design in similar flight conditions. However, from the point view of this work’s limitation, the design methods and path that developed in this work should be available for the design of twodimensional hypersonic intake system, the specific results and data that obtained in this work should be only available for the twodimensional intake system with three-stage compression ramps under Mach number of 6 and altitude of 24 km, and they are not available for other types of intake systems like axisymmetric and three-dimensional ones.

## 5. Conclusions

For an air-breathing hypersonic vehicle, the thin-panel structure is always adopted by the compression ramp of air intake system, the panel vibration that excitated by hypersonic flow, engine and noise has decisive effect on its performance. In this paper, a threestage compression intake system is designed under Mach number of 6 and flight altitude of 24 km. The mode vibration characteristics of compression ramps’ panels are obtained and the influence of panel vibration frequency and amplitude on the intake performance is clarified. The effect of fluid-structural interactions on the panel structure and intake performance is analyzed, and the available thicknesses are determined for the compression ramps. The design path of compression system is finally summarized. Some conclusions are obtained:

(1) In the mode analysis, higher and more appropriate natural frequency can be obtained by the clamped four side boundaries with additional strengthened internal boundary using certain number of rivets.
