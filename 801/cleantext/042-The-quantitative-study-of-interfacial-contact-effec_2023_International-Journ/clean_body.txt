## 1. Introduction

The high-speed and slender configuration of hypersonic vehicles bring about serious aerodynamic heat accumulation and concentration issues [1]. For the traditional thermal protection system (TPS), the heat is mainly dissipated by the thermal radiation on the surface, and the increasing amount of aerodynamic heat will lead to unaffordable size and weight of TPS [2,3]. The TPS integrated with thermoelectric (TE) conversion function [4,5] is a more efficient and promising technique for hypersonic vehicles. The TE conversion function can be realized by TE generators (TEGs), which consist of TE materials like N- and P-type ones and supplementary materials like electrodes. A TEG always includes at least four key contact interfaces, i.e., hot side N-type TE leg and electrode, cold side of N-type TE leg and electrode, hot side of P-type TE leg and electrode, cold side of P-type TE leg and electrode. TE legs and electrodes have micro-scale rough surfaces, and such type of roughness will lead to the very limited real contact region and a large proportion of contact gap. When the heat flow and electric current transfer between TE legs and electrodes, the contact gap leads to thermal contact resistance (TCR) and electrical contact resistance (ECR). Such contact effects have significant influences on the heat transfer, electric current flow and TE conversion process of TEGs.

The prediction of TCR mainly contains three steps, i.e., the rough surface characterization, contact deformation and heat transfer analysis. Proper characterization of rough surfaces is essential for the accurate estimation of TCR. The rough surface can be reconstructed based on the statistical parameters of Euclidean geometric ones [6], facial ones [7], and semi-deterministic-based approach with certain real topology information [8]. Aalilija et al. [9] proposed a numerical model based on the diffuse interface method, and the contact region is simulated by an intermediate thin material. The essential assumptions involved in above characterizations weakens their accuracy. The authors tried to establish TCR prediction models based on the measured real topology and the models have relatively good agreement with experiments. The authors [10] have reconstructed the contact model of Ti-6Al-4V and C/C-SiC based on the measured practical rough surfaces. Dai et al. [11] and Ren et al. [12] developed the method from rectangle-like cross section model to the circular ones. The materials that involved in above TCR researches are common materials like Ti alloy, C/SiC composite, etc. The study of TE materials is very limited, and the publicly reported studies mainly focus on the TCR between TEG and heat/cold sources [13–15].

In the experimental measurement of TCR, the steady-state method is kind of acknowledged technique for bulk materials [14,16–21], while the transient method (such as laser-flash measurements [22], photoacoustic techniques [23]) is mainly used in low dimensional materials [24]. In terms of steady-state measurement, Ren et al. [16] and Zhang et al. [18] measured the TCR under air gap. Sun et al. [17] and Chen et al. [20] measured the TCR under vacuum gap, and Chen et al. [20] found that thermal radiation contributes greatly to heat transfer in high temperatures (higher than $600~^{\circ}\mathrm{C})$ In terms of transient measurement, the non-contact laser photothermal method [22,23] is widely used. In the research on the TCR measurement for TEGs, Wang et al. [14] built a platform for measuring the TCR between TEG and heat/cold source, and they studied the effects of interface material and loading pressure on the performance of the TEG. Beltrán-Pitarch [15] determined the TCR between TEG and heat exchanger by using three current-voltage curves without measuring heat flux. Nevertheless, few published reports are on measuring the TCR between TE legs and electrodes. Since the TE material is brittle (difficult to punch signal holes), it is difficult to measure the TCR by direct methods. Thus, the indirect method is a feasible approach that uses the Seebeck effect of TE materials to support the measurement of the TCR.

ECRs play an important role in ensuring the energy efficiency, thermal behavior and stable operation of electrical connections. In terms of numerical prediction, Riba et al. [25] reconstructed the rough surfaces for contact electric conduction research by generating a random array of three-dimensional orthogonal parallelepipeds. Qiu et al. [26] predicted the ECR between the solid surface and porous material by the random line network model based on the multi-layer construction. In terms of experimental measurement, Fiedler et al. [27] studied the ECR between metal foam and graphite sheet by a power source and digital multimeters. Dutta et al. [28] designed a desktop indenter device to measure the ECR of nano-porous alumina filled with metal. Chen et al. [29] studied the ECR of mechanical lap joints made of high-temperature superconducting tape by X-ray computed tomography. However, few numerical models for predicting the ECR are based on the reconstructed real rough surface topography. In addition, although the test accuracy of ECR in the above study is high, it is complex for simple objects.

Scholars found that the contact effects have a remarkable negative influence on the performance of TEG [30,31]. For the theoretical and numerical study of the influences of TCR and ECR on TEG, Kim [32] established a computer simulation program, and Shittu et al. [33] established a numerical model. In terms of the proportion of the negative impact of TCR and ECR, Xu et al. [34] and Ouyang et al. [35] found that ECR has a greater negative impact than TCR. While Sun et al. [36] and Massaguer et al. [37] reached the opposite conclusion. This is mainly caused by the different characteristics of the research object and application conditions. For the experimental study of TCR, Wang et al. [14] studied the effects of loading pressure between the TEG and cold sources on the performance of the TEG. Karthick et al. [38] found that interface materials can significantly reduce the negative effect of TCR on the performance of TEGs. Nevertheless, previous studies lack of complete and reliable data to clarify the quantitative influence of TCR and ECR on TEGs, e.g., most of the numerical models are not verified by experimental measurement. Hence, the precise quantitative description of thermal and electrical contact effects and the accurate evaluation approach of their influences on TEGs need further study.

In this work, the contact effects between TE legs and electrodes are studied by real-topology-based simulations and novel indirect tests. The thermal and electrical contact resistances between TE legs and electrodes under different temperatures, loading pressures, gap mediums and thermal radiation conditions are predicted based on the reconstructed real topology of the micrometerscale rough surfaces. A novel indirect TCR test approach and a corresponding platform is developed, the TCR under different temperatures and loading pressures are measured and the numerical models are then validated. The platform is also altered to measure the ECR and power supply of the TEG. Furthermore, the heat transfer and conversion process of the TEG is simulated considering the interfacial contact effects, and the numerical model is validated by measuring the electric power supply of the TEG under different temperatures. Finally, two simplified formulas to predict the maximum output power of the TEG are established, which are in good agreement with the numerical results.

## 3.1.1. Equilibrium equation

$$\begin{array} {r} {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial \sigma _ {x}} {\partial x}} + \displaystyle {\frac {\partial \tau _ {y x}} {\partial y}} + \displaystyle {\frac {\partial \tau _ {z x}} {\partial z}} + f _ {x} = 0} \\ {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial \tau _ {x y}} {\partial x}} + \displaystyle {\frac {\partial \sigma _ {y}} {\partial y}} + \displaystyle {\frac {\partial \tau _ {z y}} {\partial z}} + f _ {y} = 0} \\ {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial \tau _ {x z}} {\partial x}} + \displaystyle {\frac {\partial \tau _ {y z}} {\partial y}} + \displaystyle {\frac {\partial \sigma _ {z}} {\partial z}} + f _ {z} = 0} \end{array}\tag{6}$$

where $f _ {x} , \ f _ {y}$ and $f _ {z}$ are components of unit body force in $x , y$ and z directions; $\tau _ {i j}$ is the shear stress; $\sigma _ {i} ( i = x , y , z )$ is the normal stress. The strain and displacement satisfy the geometric equations as Eq. (7).

## 3.1.2. Geometric equation

$$\begin{array} {l} {\varepsilon _ {x} = \displaystyle \frac {\partial u} {\partial x} ; \varepsilon _ {y} = \displaystyle \frac {\partial \nu} {\partial y} ; \varepsilon _ {z} = \displaystyle \frac {\partial w} {\partial z}} \\ {\gamma _ {y z} = \displaystyle \frac {\partial \nu} {\partial z} + \frac {\partial w} {\partial y} ; \gamma _ {x z} = \displaystyle \frac {\partial u} {\partial z} + \frac {\partial w} {\partial x}} \\ {\gamma _ {x y} = \displaystyle \frac {\partial u} {\partial y} + \frac {\partial \nu} {\partial x}} \end{array}\tag{7}$$

where $\varepsilon _ {i} ( i = x , y , z )$ is the strain; u, v and ware displacements in $x , y$ and zdirections; $\gamma _ {i j}$ is the shear strain. The elastic stress-strain relation follows the Hooke’s law as shown in Eq. (8).

## 3.1.3. Constitutive equations for elastic stage

$$d \sigma _ {i j} = D _ {i j k l} ^ {e} d \varepsilon _ {k l} ^ {e}\tag{8}$$

where $\varepsilon _ {k l} ^ {e}$ is the elastic strain; $D _ {i j k l} ^ {e}$ is the elasticity matrix.

## 3.1.4. Constitutive equations for plastic stage

$$d \sigma _ {i j} = D _ {i j k l} ^ {e p} \cdot d \varepsilon _ {k l} = ( D _ {i j k l} ^ {e} - D _ {i j k l} ^ {p} ) d \varepsilon _ {k l}\tag{9}$$

where $D _ {i j k l} ^ {e}$ is the elasticity matrix; $D _ {i j k l} ^ {p}$ is the plasticity matrix.

The source term for the energy equation (heat conduction) of TE materials is the Joule heating and Peltie effect (see Eq. (10)), while for the electric potential equation of TE materials it is the Seebeck effect (see Eq. (11)), and the thermoelectric coupling analysis is realized by such terms.

$$\frac {\partial ( \rho T )} {\partial t} = d i \nu ( \frac {\lambda} {c _ {p}} \mathbf {g r a d} T ) + ( j \psi + j ^ {2} / k )\tag{10}$$

$$\frac {\partial ( \rho U )} {\partial t} = d i \nu ( \frac {k} {\varepsilon} \mathbf {g r a d} U ) + k S \Delta T\tag{11}$$

where $\rho$ is the desity; λ is the thermal conductivity; $c _ {p}$ is the specific heat capacity; j is the electrical current density; ψ is the Peltier coefficient and equals to Seebeck coefficient multiplying absolute temperature; U is the electrical potential; k is the electrical conductivity; S is the Seebeck coefficient; -T is the temperature difference.

## 3.2. Calculation methods of TCR and ECR

The thermal contact resistance can be calculated by Eq. (12):

$$\mathrm{TCR} = \frac {\Delta T} {q _ {c} + q _ {r}}\tag{12}$$

where $\Delta T$ is the average temperature difference of two contact interfaces, while $q _ {c}$ and $q _ {r}$ are the heat flux of thermal conduction and radiation, respectively. The detailed calculation method of $q _ {r}$ is described in Appendix A.

The electrical contact resistance can be calculated by Eq. (13):

$$\mathsf{ECR} = \frac {\Delta U} {j}\tag{13}$$

where -U is the average potential difference of two contact interfaces, and j is the current density.

## 3.3. Boundary conditions

Fig. 3(a) and (b) show the boundary conditions of the contacting model and the TEG model, respectively. As shown in Fig. 3(a),

RP is a reference point above surface P1. The “MPC beam” constraint is established between RP and surface P1, and the loading pressure on surface P1 is thus simulated by the displacement wc of RP along Oz direction. In the contact mechanics simulation, the displacements of P1 in x and y directions, the displacements of P2 in x, y and z directions, and the normal displacements of P3, P4, P5 and P6 are constrained to be 0. In heat transfer simulation, the temperature $T _ {1}$ and $T _ {2}$ are applied on P1 and P2, respectively, and the four sides (P3, P4, P5 and P6) are set to be adiabatic. The gap mediums are considered to be vacuum, air and silver-epoxy. When the gap medium is vacuum, the heat conduction in the real contact region and the thermal radiation in the non-contact region are considered; when the gap medium is air, the heat conduction in the real contact region, the heat conduction in gap air and the thermal radiation in the non-contact region are considered; when the gap medium is the silver-epoxy adhesive, the heat conduction in the real contact region and gap silver-epoxy adhesive are considered. Besides, the convective effect is ignored for the microscale gap. In electric conduction simulation, the potential $U _ {1}$ and $U _ {2}$ are applied on P1 and P2, respectively, and the four sides (P3, P4, P5 and P6) are set to be insulated. Since air is regarded as a nonconductive medium (same as the vacuum), air and silver-epoxy adhesive are considered as gap mediums. Table 3 shows the calculation status of the TCR. The detailed parameter settings of boundary conditions of the contacting models are described in Appendix B. As shown in Fig. 3(b), the influence of contact effects on TEG is simulated by setting “equivalent interface layers”. The thickness of the equivalent interfacial layer is calculated by the average distance of the corresponding rough surfaces. For the layer, the electrical conductivity and thermal conductivity are assumed as the layer thickness divided by the ECR and TCR, respectively. For the boundary conditions of TEG, the temperature of the cold side (surfaces $P _ {0} 2$ and $P _ {0} 3 )$ is set to be 298 K, the temperature of the hot side (surface $P _ {0} 1 )$ is assumed to be 323, 373, 400, 423, 473 and 523 K, and other surfaces are set to be adiabatic. The convection and radiation effects need not to be considered in the TEG simulations. External resistance is connected between $P _ {0} 2$ and $P _ {0} 3$ to form a conductive circuit.

## 4. Experimental measurement

## 4.1. Experimental method for TCR

Fig. 5 shows the test method for TCR between TE leg and electrodes. The TCR is affected by both loading pressure and temperature. As shown in Fig. 5, the test sample is composed of one TE leg and two electrodes, and the TCRs of two different contacting pairs should be obtained in one measurement. Under the onedimensional steady-state heat transfer condition, the TCRs of the test sample can be obtained based on the calculation formulas of TCR, Seebeck effect of TE material and proportional relationship of TCRs obtained by numerical simulation. The detailed measurement principle of the TCR can be found in Appendix C.

The TCR between TE leg and electrodes in the test sample can be modelled by the one-dimensional steady heat conduction equation:

$$R _ {h} ^ {T} = \frac {T _ {h} - T _ {T E h}} {q} , R _ {c} ^ {T} = \frac {T _ {T E c} - T _ {c}} {q - P / A}\tag{14}$$

where $R _ {h} ^ {T}$ is the TCR between the hot side of TE leg and electrode, $R _ {c} ^ {T}$ is the TCR between the cold side of TE leg and electrode; $T _ {h}$ and $T _ {c}$ are the temperature for electrodes in contact with the hot- and cold side of TE leg, respectively; $T _ {T E h}$ and $T _ {T E c}$ are the temperature for the hot- and cold side of TE leg, respectively; P is the maximum output power of TE leg; A is the cross-sectional area of TE leg; q is the heat flux, which can be calculated by the one-dimensional steady heat conduction equation for the stainless-steel block:

$$q = \lambda {\frac {\Delta T} {\delta}}\tag{15}$$

where $\lambda$ and $\scriptstyle {\frac {\Delta T} {\delta}}$ is the thermal conductivity and temperature gradient of the stainless-steel block, respectively.

The maximum output power of the TE leg can be calculated by the following formula:

$$P = \frac {U ^ {2}} {4 R _ {i}} = \frac {S ^ {2} ( T _ {T E h} - T _ {T E c} ) ^ {2}} {4 ( R _ {T E} + R _ {h} ^ {C} + R _ {c} ^ {C} )}\tag{16}$$

where U is the circuit voltage produced by Seebeck effect of TE material; S is the Seebeck coefficient of TE materials at $( T _ {h} + T _ {c} ) / 2 ;$ $R _ {i}$ is the internal resistance of the test sample; $R _ {T E}$ is the resistance of TE legs at $( T _ {h} + T _ {c} ) / 2 ; R _ {h} ^ {C}$ is the ECR between the hot side of TE leg and electrode, $R _ {c} ^ {C}$ is the ECR between the cold side of TE leg and electrode. It should be noted that the values of $R _ {h} ^ {C}$ and $R _ {c} ^ {C}$ are assumed to be independent of temperature, and the ECR at 298K is adopted.

$$\frac {R _ {h} ^ {T}} {R _ {c} ^ {T}} \approx \frac {R _ {h , N u m} ^ {T}} {R _ {c , N u m} ^ {T}}\tag{17}$$

where $R _ {h , N u m} ^ {T}$ is the TCR between the hot side of TE leg and electrode that obtained by numerical simulation $; R _ {c , N u m} ^ {T}$ is the TCR between the cold side of TE leg and electrode obtained by numerical calculation. $T _ {1}$ and $T _ {2}$ are assumed to be the calculated temperature of $R _ {h , N u m} ^ {T}$ and $R _ {c , N u m} ^ {T} ,$ respectively.

By experimental measurement of $T _ {h} ,$ , Tc, Uand the temperature distribution in the stainless-steel block, the heat flux $q ,$ hot side temperature of TE leg $T _ {T E h}$ and cold side temperature of TE leg $T _ {T E c}$ can be determined by Eqs. (14) to (17). Furthermore, the TCR between the hot side of TE leg and electrode $R _ {h} ^ {T}$ and the TCR between the cold side of TE leg and electrode $R _ {c} ^ {T}$ can be determined.

## 5.2.1. The influence of thermal radiation

Fig. 11 shows the numerical results of the influences of thermal radiation on the TCR for the "E-N\_l" (at the compression displacement of -0.003 mm). The temperature nephogram of the TEG under the silver-epoxy adhesive gap is similar to that of the perfect contact condition. As shown in Fig. 16(b), the potential distribution of the TEG under air gap is similar to that of the vacuum condition. Compared with the perfect contact condition, the maximum potential of the TEG under air gap is reduces by about 55%, and that of the vacuum gap condition is reduced by about 57.8%. The potential distribution of the TEG under the silver-epoxy adhesive gap is similar to that of the perfect contact condition, and the maximum potential decreases by less than 1.0%. Fig. 17 shows the TE performance nephogram of the TEG considering the TCR in different loading pressures. Fig. 17(a) and (b) show the temperature and potential distribution under the same temperature boundaries $( T _ {1} = 400 \mathrm{K} , T _ {2} = 298 \mathrm{K} )$ , respectively. As shown in Fig. 17(a), when the loading pressure increases from 0.104 to 0.187 MPa, the temperature of the TEG under air and vacuum gap both changes little; the maximum potential of the TEG increases by about 3.3% under air condition and about 4% under vacuum condition (see Fig. 17(b)). The relatively small deviations indicate mesh-independence of the mesh size.

Fig. 18 shows the maximum output power of the TEG under different gap mediums and loading pressures. As shown in Fig. 18, under the same temperature boundaries, when the gap medium between the TE legs and electrodes is air, the maximum output power of the TEG is about one-quarter of that under perfect contact condition, and slightly higher than that of the vacuum condition. With the increases of the loading pressure, the maximum output power of the TEG under vacuum and air gap both increase gradually, while the increase rates are slow. When the gap medium is silver-epoxy adhesive, the maximum output power of the TEG is slightly lower than that of the perfect contact condition. the maximum output powers of the TEG under vacuum, air and the silverepoxy adhesive gap are about 16.5% to 19.0%, 19.2% to 22.5% and 98.1% to 98.16% of that under perfect contact condition, respectively. Observing the upper part of Fig. 18, the maximum output powers of the TEG under air and silver-epoxy adhesive gap show similar variation laws to that under the TCR considered condition. Compared with the perfect contact condition, the maximum output power of the TEG under air gap decreases by more than 83%; and that of the silver-epoxy adhesive gap decreases by about 0.7% to 1.9%. In summary, both TCR and ECR have negative influences on the performance of the TEG, especially under vacuum and air gap. Compared with ECR, TCR has a smaller negative impact under vacuum and air gaps, while has a slightly larger negative impact under the silver-epoxy adhesive gap.

TCR mainly affects the performance of the TEG by changing the temperature distribution of the TE legs. Fig. 19 shows the temperature differences of the TE legs under different gap mediums and loading pressures. As shown in Fig. 19, when the gap medium is vacuum or air, the temperature difference between the cold- and hot side of the P-type TE leg is higher than that of the N-type TE leg under the same temperature boundaries. The temperature difference between the P- and N-type TE leg is not obvious under the silver-epoxy adhesive gap, and it’s similar to that of the perfect contact condition. This means that the total TCR between the P-type TE leg and electrode is less than that of the N-type TE leg and electrodes. The temperature differences of the P- and Ntype TE legs under air gap are both higher than that of the vacuum gap. Compared with the perfect contact condition, the sum of the temperature differences of the P- and N-type TE legs under air gap is reduced by about 50%. The temperature differences of the P- and N-type TE legs both gradually increase with the rise of the loading pressure. Compared with the perfect contact condition, the temperature differences of the P- and N-type TE legs under the silver-epoxy adhesive gap decrease very little. Fig. 20 shows the average temperatures of the TE legs under different gap mediums and loading pressures. It can be found that the average temperatures of the P- and N-type TE legs are both not sensitive to the gap medium and loading pressure, while only affected by temperature boundaries. Thus, the TCRs between the TE legs and electrodes of the TEG may affect the TE conversion process by changing the temperature differences between the cold- and hot side of the TE legs.

## 6. Conclusion

In this work, the thermal and electrical contact effects between the TE legs and electrodes of the TEG applied to hypersonic vehicles are studied by real-topology-based predictions and novel indirect tests, their influences on the TEG are clarified by corresponding simulations and measurements, and two prediction formulas are finally developed. To begin with, the thermal and electrical contact resistances are predicted based on the reconstructed rough surfaces, and the influences of temperature, loading pressure, gap medium and thermal radiation are analyzed. For another thing, a novel indirect TCR test approach is developed and a corresponding platform is established, and the TCR under different temperatures and loading pressures are measured and the numerical models are then validated. The platform is also altered to measure the ECR and power supply of the TEG. And thirdly, the heat transfer and conversion process of the TEG is simulated with considering the interfacial contact effects by equivalent layers, the electric power supply of the TEG under different temperatures are measured and the numerical models are then validated. Finally, two simplified formulas for the prediction of the maximum output power with considerations of thermal and thermal-electrical contact resistance of the TEG are established. The results show some conclusions:

1 In the numerical prediction of the TCR, heat conduction and thermal radiation are considered, and the model is established based on the reconstructed real rough surface topographies of the TE legs and electrodes. The proposed approach can be used to predict the thermal contact resistance under different temperatures, loading pressures and gap mediums.

2 The influence of thermal radiation on the TCR increases gradually with the rise of the average interface temperature under vacuum or air gap. When the gap medium is vacuum, the TCR is mainly affected by the thermal conductivity of the TE materials; while when the gap medium is air, the TCR decreases with the rise of the average interface temperature.

3 The TCR decreases while the tendency slows down with the increase of the loading pressure. The TCR under air gap is about half of that under vacuum gap, and the TCR under the silverepoxy adhesive gap is about one-thousandth of that under vacuum gap.

4 The developed TCR test approach is essential to obtain reasonable experimental results for the test sample containing one TE leg and two electrodes. The corresponding experimental platform can measure the TCR under different temperatures and loading pressures, and the experimental and numerical results have a relatively good agreement with the largest difference of -27.51%. The platform can also be altered to measure the ECR and power supply of the TEG.

5 The negative influence of the ECR on the TEG is higher than that of the TCR under vacuum or air gap; while the TCR is slightly higher than that of the ECR under the silver-epoxy adhesive gap. The TE performance degradation of the TEG caused by contact effects under the silver-epoxy adhesive gap is no more than 4%; while that for air gap is over 96%.

6 The proposed numerical model for evaluating the influence of contact effects on the TEG is validated by measuring the electric power supply of the TEG, and the largest difference is 8.73%.

7 The prediction formulas for the maximum output power of the TEG with contact effects can be applicable for loading pressure from 0.104 to 0.187 MPa, temperature from 323 to 523 K, roughness from 3.55 to 32.53 μm and gap medium of vacuum, air and the silver-epoxy adhesive with the maximum deviation of 7.7%.
