## 1. Introduction

Spinning projectiles, which are stabilized through high-speed spinning, often exhibit significant errors when hitting targets [1]. To maintain gyro stabilization while improving attack precision, the dual-spin projectile with guidance, navigation, and control (GNC) devices is developed [1,2]. Accurate predictions of aerodynamic characteristics and flight trajectory of the projectile are essential to obtain flight states, impact point distribution, and flight stability [3], and we can assess the flight performance of the projectile based on the above parameters.

Although the aerodynamic characteristics of non-spinning vehicles exhibit unsteady and nonlinear properties during maneuverable flight at high angle of attack and large-amplitude-pitching, leading to phenomena like lift hysteresis and flow separation [4,5], the rapid spinning of the aft body in dual-spin projectiles induces asymmetric aerodynamic forces and moments perpendicular to the angle of attack plane, known as the Magnus effect [6]. This effect causes the projectile to deviate from the angle of attack plane, leading to cross-coupling between longitudinal and lateral motions, and strong coupling among pitch, yaw, and roll motions [7]. Furthermore, there is a highly complex aerodynamic interference effect between the canard and the aft body [8]. Additionally, flight parameters of the projectile, such as velocity, altitude, and aerodynamic angles, will vary within a large flight envelope [9]. Flight testing can obtain accurate performance parameters of a dual-spin projectile [10], but it is too expensive and time-consuming. The trajectory simulation using the traditional aerodynamic interpolation table is not sufficient to meet high-fidelity simulation requirements, as it cannot accurately reflect unsteady aerodynamic characteristics. The coupled CFD/RBD method can provide both the unsteady aerodynamics characteristics and the flight dynamics characteristics in an integrated manner without using a database [11,12], thus it can achieve high-fidelity. However, the high computational cost of the CFD simulations makes the method unsuitable for engineering applications. To address the above issues, it is necessary to replace the CFD module in the coupled simulation with an efficient and high-accuracy aerodynamic

model.

As a common concern in the fields of aerodynamics and flight dynamics, predicting aerodynamic parameters with high accuracy is the basis for flight dynamics analysis and flight trajectory simulation [13]. Thus, the accuracy of the aerodynamic model is crucial. For the dual-spin projectile, building aerodynamic modeling is more challenging due to the time-delayed effects [14]. In general, unsteady aerodynamic modeling methods are categorized into two types: the white-box method and the black-box method. The white-box method usually establishes a mathematical model with a known structure and parameters, thus it has good interpretability. The black-box method is a data-driven modeling method, which can build an input-output mapping model directly by a surrogate model [15] or machine learning approach [14], providing more powerful nonlinear description capabilities.

Commonly used white-box models include aerodynamic derivative models [16,17], differential and integral equation models [18,19], and state-space models [20]. Recently, another type of white-box method building aerodynamic model by system identification with explicit expressions, such as Kriging [21] and autoregressive moving average (ARMA) [22] methods, have been developed and applied to unsteady aerodynamic modeling. The ARMA method was used to build the aerodynamic model of a spinning projectile, which was integrated with flight dynamics equations for trajectory simulation [23]. Brouwer et al. [24] used the Kriging model to predict unsteady aeroelastic loads during shock-induced separation, which can accelerate the simulation greatly. However, there still exist two shortcomings in the current study: it is hard to maintain high accuracy in predicting strongly nonlinear and unsteady aerodynamic forces; the generalization ability of the aerodynamic model is limited.

With the increase in computational capability and storage resources, the black-box methods have been extensively developed and applied. Black-box methods, such as neural networks [25–27], can ignore physical features when constructing unsteady aerodynamic models. Commonly used black-box methods include radial basis function (RBF) neural networks [28,29], convolutional neural networks [30], and long short-term memory (LSTM) neural networks [31,32], etc. Wang et al. [33] proposed a fuzzy RBF neural network suitable for different input scales and applied it to aerodynamic prediction of forced oscillations of airfoils. Bagherzadeh et al. [34] used a neural network to establish an aerodynamic model based on flight testing data for aircraft maneuvering at a high angle of attack, which can greatly enhance model accuracy in comparison with the traditional dynamic derivative model. Lyu et al. [35] used an extreme learning machine method to model the unsteady aerodynamics of a fighter jet with large amplitude oscillation in a wind tunnel test, further they coupled the obtained aerodynamic model with the flight dynamics equations for designing its control law during maneuvering. Shi et al. [36] constructed the linear part and nonlinear correction part of the aerodynamic model respectively using ARMA and RBF neural networks, then they carried out flight dynamics simulations of an elastic aircraft based on the aerodynamic model.

From the above review, unsteady aerodynamic modeling using black-box methods for pitch maneuver of the longitudinal plane has been delved in numerous studies and achieved high accuracy. However, there remains a need for unsteady aerodynamic modeling of spinning projectiles, particularly in the model applicability for various flight states and model accuracy. Moreover, to accelerate the flight trajectory simulation, the simulation driven by the aerodynamic model is of our concern.

In this work, we propose an aerodynamic modeling method based on deep neural network and transfer learning (DNN-TL) to accurately predict unsteady aerodynamics of dual-spin projectile under different initial conditions. The DNN is first used to build a pre-trained aerodynamic model in the source domain, which can then be fine-tuned for other tasks. Moreover, we fine-tune the pre-trained model with a small amount of data when the initial flight states change, to fully use the knowledge learned in the source domain. Brside aerodynamic prediction, the established model is further used for fast and accurate flight trajectory simulations of dual-spin projectile.

## 2.1. Deep neural network for aerodynamic modeling

The general architecture of a DNN [37] is shown in Fig. 1. It can be divided into an input layer, hidden layers, and an output layer.

Based on the general DNN, the architecture of the DNN model used for aerodynamic modeling in this work is presented in Fig. 2.

The input of the DNN model is denoted as $\pmb {S} ( t )$ in Eq. (1). The timedelay effects both of flight states and aerodynamic forces and moments are considered in the inputs. The terms related to x in Eq. (1) represent

the influence of flight state on the unsteady aerodynamics, while the other terms containing y consider the impact of historical aerodynamic data on the current aerodynamic forces and moments.

$$\pmb {S} ( t ) = \left[ \pmb {x} ( t - m ) , . . . , \pmb {x} ( t - 1 ) , \pmb {x} ( t ) , \pmb {y} ( t - n ) , . . . , \pmb {y} ( t - 1 ) \right] ^ {T}\tag{1}$$

where, ${\pmb x} ( t )$ denotes the flight states at time $t ,$ and the detailed expression is

$${\pmb x} ( t ) = \Big [ {\pmb u} ( t ) ~ {\nu} ( t ) ~ {\pmb w} ( t ) ~ {\varphi} _ {f} ( t ) ~ {\varphi} _ {a} ( t ) ~ {\pmb \theta} ( t ) ~ {\psi} ( t ) ~ p _ {f} ( t ) ~ p _ {a} ( t ) ~ q ( t ) ~ r ( t ) ~ {\alpha} ( t ) \Big ] ^ {T}\tag{2}$$

in which, $u , \nu ,$ and w are components of the velocity respectively in the $x ,$ $y ,$ and z directions in the body reference frame; $p , q ,$ and r are components of the angular velocity vector in the body reference frame; $\varphi , \theta ,$ and ψ represent the rolling, pitching and yawing angle, respectively. Subscripts a and f respectively represent the aft body and forebody of the projectile. ${\pmb y} ( t )$ represents the aerodynamic forces and moments at time t,

$${\pmb y} ( t ) = \left[ F _ {x} ( t ) F _ {y} ( t ) F _ {z} ( t ) M _ {f x} ( t ) M _ {a x} ( t ) M _ {y} ( t ) M _ {z} ( t ) \right] ^ {T}\tag{3}$$

where, $F _ {x} , F _ {y} ,$ and $F _ {z}$ represent the axial force, normal force, and lateral force, respectively; $M _ {f x} , \ M _ {a x} , \ M _ {y}$ , and $M _ {z}$ respectively represent the rolling moment of the forebody, rolling moment of aft body, pitching moment, and yawing moment.

All the layers of the neural network are fully connected, thus the DNN [38] is expressed by

$$\begin{array} {l} {{\displaystyle z _ {i} ^ {( l + 1 )} = \sum _ {j = 1} ^ {k} {\pmb W} _ {i j} ^ {( l )} {\pmb a} _ {j} ^ {( l )} + {\pmb b} _ {i} ^ {( l )}}} \\ {{\displaystyle a _ {i} ^ {( l + 1 )} = \sigma \big ( z _ {i} ^ {( l + 1 )} \big )}} \end{array}\tag{4}$$

where $\sigma ( \cdot )$ is the activation function; ${\pmb w} _ {i j} ^ {( l )}$ is a weighting matrix that connects the neuron j in layer l and the neuron i in layer $l + 1 ; {\boldsymbol {b}} _ {i} ^ {( l )}$ is the bias matrix in layer l; $a _ {j} ^ {( l )}$ is an activation value of the neuron j in layer l.

Thus, $a _ {j} ^ {( 1 )} = S _ {j}$ is the activation value of the neuron j in the input layer. 
$z _ {i} ^ {( l + 1 )}$ is the output of neuron i in the layer l + 1.

The outputs of the model are the aerodynamic forces and moments at the current time, denoted as y(t). Therefore, the DNN model with l layers and k neurons in each layer can be expressed as

$${\pmb y} = \sum _ {j = 1} ^ {k} \left( \prod _ {i = 1} ^ {l} \sigma \left( \sum _ {r = 1} ^ {k} {\pmb W} _ {j i} ^ {( i )} \cdot \left( \sum _ {q = 1} ^ {k} {\pmb W} _ {i q} ^ {( i - 1 )} \cdot \left( \ldots \left( \sum _ {p = 1} ^ {m} {\pmb W} _ {r p} ^ {( 1 )} \cdot {\pmb a} _ {p} ^ {( 1 )} \right) \ldots \right) \right) + {\pmb b} _ {j} ^ {( i )} \right) \right)\tag{5}$$

Hence, the expression of Eq. (6) can be expressed as

$${\pmb y} ( t ) = \Psi _ {D N N} ( {\pmb S} ( t ) , {\pmb \xi} )\tag{6}$$

where, ξ includes all the parameters of the network.

## 2.2. Transfer learning based on the pre-training

Given a source domain $\mathcal {D} _ {S} = \left\{{\pmb x} _ {i} , {\pmb y} _ {i} \right\} _ {i = 1} ^ {n}$ and a target domain $\mathcal {D} _ {\mathrm{T}} =$ $\left\{x _ {i} , {\pmb y} _ {i} \right\} _ {i = 1} ^ {m}$ , where $\mathcal {D} \mathbf {\boldsymbol {s}} \neq \mathcal {D} _ {\mathrm {{T}}}$ . The source domain is the domain that has rich knowledge and is what we transfer from. The aim of the transfer learning based on pre-training and fine-tuning is to learn a predictive function f ∗ parameterized by ξ using the previous parameter $\pmb {\xi} _ {0}$ from the model trained on the source domain, such that $f ^ {*}$ can reach minimum prediction error on the target domain [39]:

$$f ^ {*} = \arg m i n _ {f} \mathbb {E} _ {( \pmb {x} , \pmb {y} ) \in \mathcal {D} _ {\mathrm{T}}} [ \ell ( \pmb {f} ( \pmb {\xi} | \pmb {\xi} _ {0} , \mathcal {D} _ {S} ) ) ]\tag{7}$$

where, ℓ is the loss function, E is the mathematic expectation. Thus, the transfer learning method can make full use of the information of the source domain, so as to effectively complete the training of the task in $\mathcal {D} _ {\mathrm{T}}$

The procedure of the transfer learning based on pre-training is outlined in Algorithm 1 and Fig. 3. This approach retains the first l − 1 hidden layers of the pre-training model as a part of the new model. Then those layers are restructured with a new randomly initialized hidden layer l, which is then retrained in the new DNN model. As the new DNN model is fine-tuned from the pre-trained model, the learning rate during the fine-tuning process is smaller than that of the pre-trained model.

## 2.3. Aerodynamic modeling method based on DNN-TL

The flowchart of the proposed modeling method is shown in Fig. 4. There are mainly four steps involved in the modeling procedure.

Step 1: Sample generation.

Given the initial flight states including velocity, angular velocity and attitude angle for a dual-spin projectile, the source domain $\mathcal {D} _ {S} =$ $\{\mathbf {{x}} _ {i} , \mathbf {{y}} _ {i} \} , i = 1 , 2 , \cdots , n$ can be obtained through coupled CFD/RBD simulation. Similarly, the target domain is obtained under the new initial states. Here, n and m represent the number of samples in the source domain and target domain, respectively.

Step 2: Pre-trained model establishment.

The main purpose of this step is to enable the network to learn information from the source domain. The pre-trained model $\Psi _ {D N N} ^ {S} ( pmb {S} , \pmb {\xi} _ {S} )$ is established on the source domain, where the input is $s ( t ) =$ $[ {\pmb x} ( t - m ) , . . . , {\pmb x} ( t - 1 ) , {\pmb x} ( t ) , {\pmb y} ( t - n ) , . . . , {\pmb y} ( t - 1 ) ] ^ {T}$ and the output is y(t). The weights and thresholds of the DNN are trained through the Adam algorithm.

Step 3: DNN-TL model establishment.

For this step, the main purpose is to apply the knowledge learned in the source domain to the target domain. We freeze the first several hidden layers of the $\Psi _ {D N N} ^ {S} ( {\pmb S} , {\pmb \xi} _ {S} )$ ) and then randomly initialize the thresholds and weights of the last hidden layer. ## 3.2.2. Flight dynamic equations

The forebody and aft body of the dual-spin projectile rotate at different rates, creating relative motion between them, which adds an extra degree of freedom for rolling. Thus, the dual-spin projectile can move in seven degrees of freedom (DOF) in an inertial reference frame. The coordinate systems of the projectile are defined in Fig. 6. The x-axis points to an arbitrary direction in the ground plane. The z-axis is vertical downward and the y-axis is vertical to the xOz plane. The body frame is fixed to the vehicle with its origin at the center of mass.

The 7-DoF RBD equations are introduced as follows. The centroid translational equation in the body frame is written as

$$\left\{\begin{array} {c} {m ( \dot {u} + w q - \nu r ) = F _ {x} + F _ {g x}} \\ {m \Big ( \dot {\nu} + u r - w p _ {f} \Big ) = F _ {y} + F _ {g y}} \\ {m \Big ( \dot {w} + \nu p _ {f} - w q \Big ) = F _ {z} + F _ {g z}} \end{array} \right.\tag{11}$$

where, m stands for mass; $F _ {g x} , F _ {g y}$ and $F _ {g z}$ are the components of the gravity force on the projectile along the x-axis, y-axis and z-axis in the body reference frame.

The equations of rotational around the mass center under the body shafting are expressed as

$$\left\{\begin{array} {l l} {I _ {f x} \dot {p} _ {f} = M _ {f x}} \\ {I _ {a x} \dot {p} _ {a} = M _ {a x}} \\ {I _ {y} \dot {q} + I _ {x} \overline {{p}} r - I _ {z} p _ {f} r = M _ {y}} \\ {I _ {z} \dot {r} + I _ {y} p _ {f} q - I _ {x} \overline {{p}} q = M _ {z}} \\ {\overline {{p}} = \left( I _ {f x} p _ {f} + I _ {a x} p _ {a} \right) \bigg / I _ {x}} \end{array} \right.\tag{12}$$

where, I is the moment of inertia.

The spatial position of the center of mass is calculated as follows

$$\begin{array}{c} \left\{\begin{array} {c c c} {\dot {x}} \\ {\dot {y}} \\ {\dot {z}} \end{array} \right\} = \left[ s _ {\varphi} s _ {\theta} c _ {\psi} - c _ {\varphi} s _ {\psi} & {c _ {\theta} s _ {\psi} + c _ {\varphi} c _ {\psi}} & {s _ {\varphi} c _ {\theta}} \\ {c _ {\varphi} s _ {\theta} c _ {\psi} + s _ {\varphi} s _ {\psi}} & {c _ {\varphi} s _ {\theta} s _ {\psi} - s _ {\varphi} c _ {\psi}} & {c _ {\varphi} c _ {\theta}} \end{array} \right] \left\{\begin{array} {c c c} {u} \\ {\nu} \\ {w} \end{array} \right\}\tag{13}$$

where, c and s respectively represent cosine and sine operation.

The orientation angles are computed by

$$\left\{\begin{array} {l} {\displaystyle {\dot {\varphi}} _ {f} = p _ {f} + \tan \theta \big ( q \mathrm{sin} \varphi _ {f} + r \mathrm{cos} \varphi _ {f} \big )} \\ {\displaystyle {\dot {\varphi}} _ {a} = {\dot {\varphi}} _ {f} + p _ {a} - p _ {f}} \\ {\displaystyle {\dot {\theta}} = q \mathrm{cos} \varphi _ {f} - r \mathrm{sin} \varphi _ {f}} \\ {\displaystyle {\dot {\psi}} = \frac {1} {\mathrm{cos} \theta} \big ( q \mathrm{sin} \varphi _ {f} + r \mathrm{cos} \varphi _ {f} \big )} \end{array} \right.\tag{14}$$

The RBD equations are solved with the improved Adams fourth-order predictor-corrector method in this work [42].

## 3.2.3. Coupled CFD/RBD method

The flowchart of the coupled CFD/RBD method is shown in Fig. 7. It mainly consists of two modes: the uncoupled mode and the coupled mode. In the uncoupled mode, the steady initial state is calculated only with the translational velocities. After achieving the steady-state solution, the initial angular velocity is considered for several unsteady dualtime steps until the computation fully converges. In the coupled CFD/ RBD mode, the aerodynamic forces and moments predicted by CFD are passed on to 7-DOF RBD equations, which compute the projectile’s responses, including translational and rotational accelerations. These accelerations are then integrated to obtain the velocity and angular velocity and further integrated to determine the position and angular orientation. After getting the positions and orientations of the next step, the solid wall and computational grid are moved to the corresponding place. Then the HUNS3D is called to predict the new aerodynamic loads. The above process is repeated until the end of the simulation.

## 4.1. Parameter analysis of the DNN-TL

To analyze the predictive accuracy of the constructed model, we define the error as follows. $\delta _ {R}$ represents the root mean square error (RMSE) and it can be computed by $\mathrm{Eq.} ( 15 ) . \delta _ {e}$ is the mean relative error (MRE). Since there are values close to 0 in the aerodynamic force and moment data, δ can be defined as the form in Eq. (16).

$$\delta _ {R} = \sqrt {\frac {1} {N} \sum _ {i = 1} ^ {N} {( y _ {i} - \widehat {y} _ {i} ) ^ {2}}}\tag{15}$$

$$\delta _ {e} = \sum _ {i = 1} ^ {N} | y _ {i} - \widehat {y} _ {i} | \left/ \sum _ {i = 1} ^ {N} | \widehat {y} _ {i} | \right.\tag{16}$$

where N is the number of samples; yi is the high-fidelity value calculated by the coupled CFD/RBD method; $\widehat {y} _ {i}$ is the value predicted by the aerodynamic model.

Aerodynamic models are constructed with different values of m and n in Eq. (1) to assess the impact of different input-memory and outputmemory orders on the model accuracy. The accuracy of different models is compared in the validation case. According to previous experience, a three-layer neural network can approximate any nonlinear function. Thus, we select a neural network with 3 hidden layers, each consisting of 120 neurons. The Adam is used as the training algorithm with an initial learning rate of 0.001, and the maximum number of training epochs is set to 2000. RMSE is used as the metric to measure network performance.

The variations in the prediction error of the pitching moment with respect to m and n are illustrated in Fig. 8. Along with the increase of m and n, the prediction error tends to be larger. It indicates that incorporating more historical data into modeling will reduce model accuracy.

## 4.2.1. Result analysis of the validation case

In this case, the aerodynamic model $\Psi _ {D N N} ^ {S}$ built by the DNN-TL method is used for the dual-spin projectile. ## 5. Conclusions

In this work, an unsteady aerodynamic modeling method for dualspin projectile based on DNN and transfer learning is proposed, and three test cases are carried out to validate and assess the proposed method. Further, the aerodynamic models constructed by the proposed method are employed in flight trajectory simulation to enhance simulation efficiency. Conclusions are drawn as follows:

(1) The proposed DNN-TL method can accurately model the aerodynamic characteristics of the dual-spin projectile under different initial conditions, with prediction errors of all the forces and moments smaller than 6%, but the accuracy of the proposed method decreases when applied the method to extrapolation prediction.

(2) The flight trajectory simulation of the dual-spin projectile based on the established aerodynamic model can significantly enhance simulation efficiency compared to the coupled CFD/RBD simulation, with $\delta _ {e} < 8 \%$

(3) In comparison with the commonly used ARMA method and LSTM networks, the proposed DNN-TL method exhibits a better generalization capability and higher accuracy in predicting aerodynamic forces and moments.
