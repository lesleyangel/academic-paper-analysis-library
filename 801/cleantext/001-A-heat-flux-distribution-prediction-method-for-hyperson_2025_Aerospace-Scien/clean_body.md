## 1. Introduction

Hypersonic flight vehicles exhibit significant spatial and temporal variations in heat flux distribution due to their dynamic flight states. Accurately determining heat flux distribution is essential for the design and operation of the vehicle. Spatially, precise heat flux information is critical for optimizing geometry, structure, materials, and thermal protection system. Temporally, it is vital for effective thermal management strategies and flight control strategies. However, accurately obtaining the heat flux distribution along the flight trajectory is challenging due to the influence of transient conjugate heat transfer (CHT). Typically, heat flux distribution can be precisely determined through three approaches: (1) direct numerical simulations using computational fluid dynamics (CFD) and computational structural thermodynamics (CST); (2) inverse methods based on temperature or heat flux measurements; and (3) datadriven predictive models. Although CFD/CST offers the highest accuracy, CFD is highly time-consuming, making time-stepping CHT analysis along the trajectory prohibitively expensive and impractical. Moreover, the inverse heat conduction methods are only applicable for ground tests or flights where sensor measurements are available. Data-driven models can rapidly provide accurate results after training, significantly reducing the computational workload of CFD simulations.

Existing data-driven heat flux prediction models can be divided into three primary categories [1]: modeling based on dimensionality reduction, point-by-point modeling, and modeling based on embedding aerodynamic physics information. Dimensionality reduction-based modeling efficiently lowers the complexity of high-dimensional heat flux distributions by retaining only the dominant modes. Yang et al. [2] applied proper orthogonal decomposition (POD) and an improved Gaussian process regression for steady-state heat flux distribution prediction (HFDP). While their framework omitted wall temperature effects, they introduced the Sobol method to analyze the sensitivities between inflow conditions and flow field variables. Nie et al. [3] and Zhang et al. [4] employed radial basis functions, while Chen et al. [5] used multilayer perceptrons, all integrating dimensionality reduction for HFDP at trajectory points. Their studies indicate that dimensionality reduction-based modeling is promising for efficient HFDP. However, they overlooked the effects of transient CHT on heat flux, which arise from wall temperature variations caused by aerodynamic heating along the trajectory. Point-by-point modeling can improve geometric variations adaptability and localized feature utilization. Dreyer et al. [6] and Zhang et al. [7] developed point-by-point heat flux prediction frameworks using Kriging algorithm and radial basis functions, respectively. However, such methodologies are inefficient in global heat flux modeling and suffer from limited generalization capabilities. Physics-embedded modeling enhances generalization by integrating supplementary flow-related variables as inputs features. Li et al. [8] developed a multilayer perceptrons model to predict isothermal wall heat flux using adiabatic wall data under identical inflow conditions. However, their work simplified thermal dynamics by assuming steady-state conditions and uniform wall temperature distributions. Wang et al. [9] and Yang et al. [10] employed boundary layer features extracted from Euler equations for heat flux prediction, with feature selection guided by random forest-based importance rankings. Dai et al. trained extremely randomized trees [11] and convolutional neural network (CNN) [12] models for HFDP using coarse-grid flow fields exhibiting macroscopic quantity convergence. These studies relied on steady-state aerothermal data, enabling HFDP across diverse flight states, wall temperatures, and geometries. Nevertheless, HFDP of vehicles flying along a trajectory is influenced not only by current flight condition and wall temperature but also by recent flight states. As the vehicle follows its trajectory, rapid changes in flight conditions frequently occur before the flow field can fully stabilize, resulting in the current flow field being only partially adjusted and still influenced by prior states. Traditional methods, which fail to account for temporal dynamics and therefore overlook transient effects, cannot accurately predict the heat flux distribution along the trajectory and struggle to quantify the duration of recent influential states.

Various existing data-driven transient aerodynamic modeling methods can serve as references, mainly including the eigensystem realization algorithm [13], AutoRegressive Moving Average (ARMA) with exogenous input [14], Volterra series [15], Kriging [16], and neural network [17]. Among these, neural network methods have received the most attention in transient aerodynamic modeling due to their powerful fitting capabilities. Examples include CNN [18,19], recurrent neural network (RNN) [20], gated recurrent unit (GRU) [21], long short-term memory (LSTM) network [22], combination neural network such as CNN-LSTM [23], and convolutional LSTM (ConvLSTM) [24,25]. Wang et al. [26] utilized ConvLSTM to establish an inverse heat conduction model from temperature sensor measurements to surface heat flux. They compared it with traditional inversion methods and concluded that ConvLSTM could achieve higher accuracy while reducing time consumption by an order of magnitude. To date, no existing research has successfully predicted heat flux distribution along the trajectory with time-varying flight states. We believe there are three main challenges: (1) The transient heat flux distribution is influenced by both historical flight states and wall temperature, yet existing methods struggle to manage the temporal dimensions of the input; (2) The high-dimensional distribution of heat flux results in models that are prone to overfitting and exhibit poor accuracy; (3) When applying a aerodynamic reduced-order model (ROM) in conjunction with other physical models in analysis such as CHT, it is necessary for ROM to possess accuracy, generalization, and stability [27]. Consequently, these challenges prevent traditional HFDP methods from developing effective ROM to replace CFD simulations in CHT analysis, highlighting the need for new approaches.

In this study, we propose a novel ROM for predicting transient heat flux distributions, which integrates a temporal convolutional network (TCN) with a CNN and is termed a temporal-spatial convolutional network (TSCN). This model accounts for the influence of both near-wall and wall temperatures on the heat flux distribution, reflecting realistic thermal processes. Initially, the high-dimensional heat flux distribution is reduced to low-dimensional mode coefficients using POD. The TCN, known for its superior temporal modeling capabilities over RNNs, is employed to extract temporal features influencing the near-wall temperature to predict heat flux. On the spatial front, the classical CNN recognized for its robust spatial feature extraction, is utilized to analyze wall temperature inputs. The fusion of temporal and spatial features enables modeling the evolution of POD mode coefficients, achieving precise predictions of heat flux distribution. The contributions of this work are outlined as follows:

i. The proposed method effectively replaces time-consuming CFD calculations to perform CHT analysis, enabling heat flux distributions along a new trajectory within an hour, with an efficiency increase of about 200 times in comparison with traditional CHT analysis.

ii. The developed TSCN model, leveraging recent flight states and non-uniform wall temperatures affecting CFD, can achieve better prediction accuracy than traditional HFDP methods.

iii. The predicted heat flux distribution of the re-entry capsule along its return trajectory exhibits high accuracy. Additionally, using a dropout-based Bayesian approximation method, it can be extended into a predictive interval that ensures high reliability by covering the calculated heat flux distribution.

The manuscript is organized as follows. The HFDP problem is described in Section 2. Section 3 introduces the HFDP method procedure. Furthermore, Section 4 displays and analyzes the outcome of the proposed framework, while its superiority is demonstrated through the comparison with other approaches. Finally, a brief conclusion is provided in the last section.

## 2. Problem description

Unlike a steady flow field that is uniquely determined by a fixed flight state, the flow field around the vehicle along a trajectory is transient. Influenced by past flight states, the flow field has insufficient time to stabilize before being altered by subsequent changes in flight conditions. This transient characteristic is also reflected in the structural field, where past flight-induced structural temperature changes interact with the flow field through complex conjugate heat transfer between structure and aerodynamic heating. To accurately quantify the evolution of heat flux distribution q(t) and wall temperature distribution $T ( t ) ,$ continuous time t within $0 \leq t \leq t _ {K}$ is discretized into K uniform intervals. Then, q(t) and $T ( t )$ are discretized in time as $q _ {k} ( k = 1 , 2 . . . K )$ and $T _ {k} ( k = 1 , 2 . . . K )$ , where $q _ {k}$ and $T _ {k}$ respectively represent the heat flux distribution and temperature distribution at time $t _ {k}$ This enables calculating these values at each interval using a tightly coupled CHT analysis with CFD and CST, as shown in Fig. 1.

Furthermore, the heat flux and temperature on the vehicle surface demonstrate spatial continuity. qk and $T _ {k}$ can be discretized at P aerodynamic grid nodes as $q _ {k p} ( p = 1 , 2 . . . P )$ and $T _ {k p} ( p = 1 , 2 . . . P )$ , where $q _ {k p}$ and $T _ {k p}$ respectively stands for the heat flux and temperature value at the $p ^ {\mathrm{th}}$ surface node at time $t _ {k} . \mathrm{~A~}$ schematic representation of them after spatial discretization is shown in Fig. 2. Therefore, $q ( t )$ and $T ( t )$ can be denoted as

$$\begin{array} {r l r} {{q ( t ) = [ \begin{array} {l} {q _ {1}} \\ {q _ {2}} \\ {\cdots} \\ {q _ {K}} \end{array} ] = [ \begin{array} {l} {q _ {11} , q _ {12} , \dots , q _ {1 P}} \\ {q _ {21} , q _ {22} , \dots , q _ {2 P}} \\ {\dots} \\ {q _ {K 1} , q _ {K 2} , \dots , q _ {K P}} \end{array} ] \mathrm{and} T ( t ) = [ \begin{array} {l} {T _ {1}} \\ {T _ {2}} \\ {\cdots} \\ {T _ {K}} \end{array} ]}} \\ & {} & {= [ \begin{array} {l} {T _ {11} , T _ {12} , \dots , T _ {1 P}} \\ {T _ {21} , q _ {22} , \dots , T _ {2 P}} \\ {\dots} \\ {T _ {K 1} , T _ {K 2} , \dots , T _ {K P}} \end{array} ]} \end{array}\tag{1}$$

The heat flux distribution qk on the vehicle’s surface at time $t _ {k}$ follows Fourier’s law, expressed as Eq. (1):

$$q _ {k} = - \lambda _ {f} \frac {\partial T} {\partial y} = - \lambda _ {f} \frac {T _ {k} ^ {e} - T _ {k - 1} ^ {w}} {y ^ {e}}\tag{2}$$

where $\lambda _ {f}$ is thermal conductivity of fluid; $T _ {k} ^ {e}$ represents the near-wall gas temperature distribution at time tk, specifically defined as the temperature at the outer edge of the innermost boundary layer; $T _ {k - 1} ^ {w}$ represents the wall temperature distribution obtained at the time $t _ {k - 1}$ , serving as the boundary condition for the flow field; $y ^ {e}$ refers the thickness of extremely thin innermost boundary layer. Eq. (2) is applicable in the near-wall region, known as the viscous sub-layer, which is assumed to have a nearly linear distribution [28]. This extremely thin wall-adjacent fluid layer experiences no slip relative to the wall due to viscous forces, and heat can only be transferred through conduction.

As the flight state continuously changes along the trajectory, the transient flow field is influenced by multiple historical states. Therefore, the near-wall gas temperature $T _ {k} ^ {e}$ in the transient flow field is also influenced by these historical states, as illustrated in Eq. (3):

$$T _ {k} ^ {e} = E ( X _ {k} , X _ {k - 1} , X _ {k - 2} . . . X _ {k - i} )\tag{3}$$

where $X _ {k}$ encapsulates the flight state at time $t _ {k} ,$ including altitude, velocity, and angle of attack; i represents the duration of recent influential flight states that impacts the transient flow field and $T _ {k} ^ {e} ;$ ; E denotes the mapping relationship between recent states and near-wall temperatures.

By combining Eqs. (2) and (3), the heat flux prediction, intended to replace computationally intensive CFD simulations, requires consideration of both recent influential states and $T _ {k - 1} ^ {w}$ as shown in Eq. (4):

$$q _ {k} = F \bigl ( X _ {k} , X _ {k - 1} , X _ {k - 2} . . . X _ {k - i} , T _ {k - 1} ^ {w} \bigr )\tag{4}$$

where F denotes the heat flux prediction model. $T _ {k - 1} ^ {w}$ is derived from CST solving for the structural field, with boundary conditions as shown in Eq. (5):

$$\rho c \frac {\partial T ^ {w} ( t )} {\partial t} = \nabla \cdot ( \lambda _ {s} \nabla T ^ {w} ( t ) ) + q\tag{5}$$

where $\rho , c$ and $\lambda _ {s}$ represent density, specific heat and thermal conductivity of solid, respectively. Integrating the boundary condition outlined in $\mathrm{Eq.~} ( 5 )$ over time yields Eq. (6):

$$T _ {k - 1} ^ {w} = T _ {k - 2} ^ {w} + \int _ {t _ {k - 2}} ^ {t _ {k - 1}} \frac {\nabla \cdot \left( \lambda _ {s} \nabla T ^ {w} ( t ) \right) + q _ {k - 1}} {\rho c} d t\tag{6}$$

This indicates that obtaining $T _ {k - 1} ^ {w}$ requires $T _ {k - 2} ^ {w}$ and $q _ {k - 1} ,$ which means that the prediction of $q _ {k}$ requires the value of qk− 1. Consequently, predicting $q _ {k}$ must adhere to the temporal sequence of the trajectory and cannot proceed without first predicting the heat flux at previous moments. This is known as recursive prediction, where the output of the previous time’s model serves as the input for the next time, advancing layer by layer, forming a prediction chain. Although this method is straightforward and effective, errors from earlier predictions introduce input noise that impacts subsequent forecasts, thus necessitating consistently high precision throughout the model’s predictions. Finally, combining the spatial discretization of heat flux and temperature, the heat flux prediction model is expressed by Eq. $^ {( 7 ) ,}$ derived from Eq. (4).

$$\bigl [ q _ {k 1} , q _ {k 2} , . . . , q _ {k P} \bigr ] = F \bigl ( X _ {k} , X _ {k - 1} , X _ {k - 2} , . . . , X _ {k - i} , [ T _ {( k - 1 ) 1} , T _ {( k - 1 ) 2} , . . . , T _ {( k - 1 ) P} ] \bigr )\tag{7}$$

## 3. Method

## 3.1. Heat flux distribution prediction method

Based on the above problem description, we introduced a datadriven method for predicting heat flux distribution, thereby replacing the time-consuming transient CFD simulations. This method facilitates the determination of heat flux and wall temperature at each time interval by employing the TSCN model in conjunction with CST, as illustrated in Fig. 3. The model was constructed based on simulated data derived from several potential flight trajectories and was subsequently utilized to predict heat flux along new trajectories. The methodological procedure is illustrated in Fig. $^ {4 ,}$ with detailed steps provided as follows.

(1). Data acquisition: The flight mission objectives, including start and end position, along with trajectory constraints such as maximum dynamic pressure, overload, and stagnation point heat flux, were defined. By sampling a comprehensive range of initial and terminal flight states to capture a wide variety of potential scenarios, a series of potential trajectories $X _ {1} , X _ {2} , . . . , X _ {n} , . . . , X _ {N}$ were generated using the Gauss pseudospectral optimization software (GPOPS) [29]. Subsequently, the heat flux and temperature distributions along these trajectories were computed iteratively through CFD and CST analyses. The surface heat flux calculated from CFD was applied as a boundary condition in the CST simulations, and the wall temperatures derived from CST were utilized as boundary conditions for CFD. Computations along all trajectories generated $\textstyle \sum _ {n = 1} ^ {N} K _ {n}$ data samples, with the duration of the $n ^ {t h}$ trajectory represented as $K _ {n}$

(2). Data Preprocessing: The data were partitioned with different trajectories randomly assigned to the test, validation, and training sets. The high-dimensional heat flux distribution $q ,$ with a dimensionality equal to the number of aerodynamic grid nodes $P ,$ was reduced to an m-dimensional $( m < P )$ representation using POD for the validation and training sets, while the test set distributions were retained for comparison with reconstructed predictions. POD has several equivalent forms [30], including principal component analysis, Karhunen-Lo\`eve decomposition, and singular value decomposition. Using compact singular value decomposition [31], POD efficiently decomposed $q ,$ represented as

$$q = U _ {R} \Sigma _ {R} {V _ {R}} ^ {T}\tag{8}$$

where R is the rank of $q ,$ U is the left singular matrix, and V is the right singular matrix. $\Sigma = \mathrm{diag} ( S _ {1} , S _ {2} , . . . , S _ {R} )$ is a diagonal matrix whose diagonal elements are the singular values arranged in descending order. Following the acquisition of eigenvalues $e = S ^ {2}$ from $\Sigma ,$ the energy contribution $W _ {m}$ of the first m eigenvalues was defined as:

$$W _ {m} = \sum {\sp m _ {i = 1} e _ {i} \sp i} \Big / \sum _ {i = 1} \sp R e _ {i}\tag{9}$$

By specifying $W _ {m} ,$ the value of m was determined, truncating $\textstyle \sum _ {R} , U _ {R}$ and $V _ {R} ^ {T}$ to the first m singular values and corresponding vectors. Multiplying the truncated matrices $\textstyle \sum _ {m} , U _ {m}$ and $V _ {m} ^ {T}$ reconstructed an approximate heat flux distribution, retaining only the most significant m features of the original data. The product of $U _ {m}$ and $\sum m$ is referred to as the POD mode coefficients a, while the columns of $V _ {m} ^ {T}$ are referred to as the POD modes ξ. Specifically, [ak1, $\boldsymbol {a} _ {k 2} . . . \boldsymbol {a} _ {k m} ]$ is employed as a lowdimensional surrogate for [qk1, qk2...qkP] while $\left[ \xi _ {1} , \xi _ {2} . . . \xi _ {m} \right] ^ {\mathrm{T}}$ is utilised to multiply with the predicted mode coefficients $[ \widehat {a} _ {k 1} , \widehat {\boldsymbol {a}} _ {k 2} . . . \widehat {\boldsymbol {a}} _ {k m} ]$ to obtain the predicted heat flux distribution ̂q.

Furthermore, the duration of influential states needs to be reduced from k to i. A duration that is too long can lead to dimensional disasters, while a duration that is too short might overlook the temporal influence of historical states. Similarly, energy contribution follows a comparable pattern: too small results in larger reconstruction errors, while too large keeps the output dimensionality high, impeding predictive performance.

To achieve optimal model performance, the appropriate duration of influential states and energy contribution for output dimensionality reduction need to be experimentally determined based on application scenarios and data characteristics.

(3). Modeling and training: The flight states $\left[ X _ {k} , X _ {k - 1} . . . X _ {k - i} \right] ^ {\mathrm{T}}$ and wall temperature $T _ {k}$ were used as input, while the mode coefficients $[ a _ {k 1} , a _ {k 2} . . . a _ {k m} ] ;$ , representing the heat flux distribution $q _ {k}$ at time $t _ {k} ,$ served as target outputs. $[ X _ {k} , X _ {k - 1} . . . X _ {k - i} ] ^ {\mathrm{T}}$ were treated as a temporal distribution to analyze temporal features using TCN. Simultaneously, a CNN was utilized to extract spatial features in $T _ {k}$ These temporal and spatial features were then integrated and processed through a fully connected layer (FCL) to derive the output. The output $[ \widehat {a} _ {k 1} , \widehat {a} _ {k 2}$ $. . \widehat {a} _ {k m} ]$ was compared against $[ a _ {k 1} , a _ {k 2} . . . a _ {k m} ]$ using root mean square error (RMSE) as the loss function. The model was iteratively trained to minimize this RMSE, thereby refining the parameters to improve prediction accuracy. After training, the validation set was employed to assess model accuracy. This cycle of training and validation was repeated to fine-tune the hyperparameters until optimal performance was achieved.

(4). Prediction: The model was tested to predict the heat flux distribution at each time step along the trajectories from the test set, as illustrated in Fig. 3 and Fig. 4. Starting from the initial time step $t _ {1} {\mathrm{:}}$ the flight states of the moments before $t _ {1}$ (initialized as zeros) were stacked with the flight state $X _ {1}$ at $t _ {1}$ to form the vector $[ X _ {1} , 0 . . . 0 ] ^ {\mathrm{T}}$ This vector, along with the initial wall temperature $T _ {0} ,$ was fed into the trained TSCN model to obtain the output $[ \widehat {a} _ {11} , \widehat {a} _ {12} . . . \widehat {a} _ {1 m} ] \mathrm{at} t _ {1}$ . By multiplying the output $[ \widehat {a} _ {11} , \widehat {a} _ {12} . . . \widehat {a} _ {1 m} ]$ with the POD modes $[ \xi _ {1} , \xi _ {2} . . . \xi _ {m} ] ^ {\mathrm{T}}$ , the heat flux distribution can be reconstructed to $\widehat {\boldsymbol {q}} _ {1}$ Next, the predicted $\widehat {q} _ {1}$ was used as the boundary condition for the CST model to compute the wall temperature $T _ {1}$ . Then the inputs of the TSCN model were updated to $\left[ X _ {2} , X _ {1} . . . 0 \right] ^ {\mathrm{T}}$ and $T _ {1}$ , thus the TSCN model can be used to predict $\widehat {\pmb q} _ {2}$ at time $t _ {2}$ . This process was repeated iteratively until all the heat flux distributions $\widehat {\pmb q} = [ \widehat {\pmb q} _ {1} , \widehat {\pmb q} _ {2} . . . \widehat {\pmb q} _ {k} . . . \widehat {\pmb q} _ {K} ] ^ {\mathrm{T}}$ from the time step $t _ {1}$ to $t _ {K}$ were obtained.

The following metrics were utilized to evaluate the model’s performance effectively, as indicated in Eqs. (10) to (12).

$$\varepsilon = \sqrt {\frac {1} {K P} \sum _ {k = 1} ^ {K} \sum _ {p = 1} ^ {P} \left( \widehat {q} _ {k p} - q _ {k p} \right) ^ {2}}\tag{10}$$

$$R ^ {2} = 1 - \sum _ {k = 1} ^ {K} \sum _ {p = 1} ^ {P} \bigg ( \widehat {q} _ {k p} - q _ {k p} \bigg ) ^ {2} \bigg / \sum _ {k = 1} ^ {K} \sum _ {p = 1} ^ {P} \bigg ( q _ {k p} - \overline {{q}} \bigg ) ^ {2}\tag{11}$$

$$\delta = | \sum _ {k = 1} ^ {K} \sum _ {p = 1} ^ {P} \widehat {q} _ {k p} - \sum _ {k = 1} ^ {K} \sum _ {p = 1} ^ {P} q _ {k p} | / \sum _ {k = 1} ^ {K} \sum _ {p = 1} ^ {P} q _ {k p} \times 100 \%\tag{12}$$

where $\varepsilon , R ^ {2}$ and δ represent RMSE, R-squared and relative error ${\widehat {q}} ,$ q and q stands for the predicted heat flux, calculated heat flux, and the mean of the calculated heat flux.

## 3.2. Temporal-spatial convolution

In this work, we leveraged a TCN to capture the impact of historical states on heat flux distribution and a CNN to analyze the spatial effects of wall temperatures.

The core of TCNs is temporal convolution, which is an improved onedimensional convolution structure designed to handle time series data. TCN has demonstrated higher accuracy and stability than recurrent neural networks such as LSTM and GRU in most tasks [32], making it well-suited for analyzing the influence of historical states on heat flux distribution. In our work, the historical flight states were represented as one-dimensional signals along the time, which allowed the use of a TCN to extract temporal features automatically. TCN combines causal convolution, dilated convolution, and residual connection to create an innovative variant of a CNN. The causal convolution ensures that the output at a given time depends only on the current and past inputs, which is crucial for the time-causal nature of time series prediction. Dilated convolution allows for accessing data at wider intervals, expanding the receptive field to capture more historical information, while residual connections help to avoid gradient vanishing. The dilated causal convolution used in TCN can be expressed by Eq. (13) and illustrated in Fig. 5.

$$H ( k ) = \sum _ {i = 0} ^ {I - 1} w ( i ) x _ {k - \tau i}\tag{13}$$

where $H ( k )$ represents the output of dilated causal convolution at time $t _ {k} ,$ x is the input, I is the size of the convolution kernel, $w ( i )$ is the $i ^ {\mathrm {{t h}}}$ weight in the convolution kernel, and τ is the dilation rate. $x _ {k - \tau i}$ represents the input at time $t _ {k - \tau i}$ When τ = 1, the dilated causal convolution reduces to a standard causal convolution. After combining multiple dilated causal convolutions with residual connection, TCN incorporates dropout, ReLU activation function, and weight normalization, forming a TCN block as shown in Fig. 6(a).

The CNN component of our model was used to capture the spatial features of wall temperature effects on heat flux distribution. The core of CNNs is convolution operation, which uses local receptive fields (kernels or filters) to extract spatial features, inspired by the way that the animal visual cortex processes images [33]. CNN is highly effective while suppressing noise and reducing the number of parameters through the combination of convolution and pooling operations. These advantages make CNN particularly suitable for analyzing the effect of wall temperature distribution on heat flux distribution. In the two-dimensional re-entry capsule example used in this work, the wall temperature distribution was represented as a one-dimensional signal along the Y-direction. By employing multi-layer one-dimensional convolutions and pooling, the CNN can automatically extract spatial features from the wall temperature. The one-dimensional convolution layer can be mathematically expressed by Eq. (14)

$$C _ {j} = \sigma \Bigg ( \sum _ {i = 1} ^ {I} w _ {i j} x _ {i} + b _ {j} \Bigg )\tag{14}$$

where $C _ {j}$ is the $j ^ {\mathrm{th}}$ feature of the convolution output, σ is the activation function, $x _ {i}$ is the input within the receptive field corresponding to the $i ^ {\mathrm {{t h}}}$ weight, and b is the bias. By continuously moving the convolution kernel across the input image with a predefined stride, the convolution layer extracts features from the original image distribution. The structure of a CNN not only includes convolution layers but also consists of input layer, pooling layers, fully connected layer, and output layer, as illustrated in Fig. 6(b).

## 4. Heat flux distribution prediction for the re-entry capsule

## 4.1. Physical model and dataset sampling

Without loss of generality, the re-entry capsule was chosen as a representative hypersonic vehicle to investigate the HFDP problem and demonstrate the proposed method. The 2D physical model and material configuration for the re-entry capsule were adopted from the piggyback atmospheric re-entry technology testbed, a microsatellite mission conducted by the Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency [34]. The re-entry trajectories of the capsule were defined between altitudes of 80 km and 20 km, encompassing the phases of rapid deceleration and intense aerodynamic heating. During these phases, re-entry trajectories were constrained by dynamic pressure, overload and stagnation point heat flux whose maximum allowable limits are 120 kPa, 10 g and 4000 kW/m2, respectively.

To cover a broad range of potential trajectories, the initial velocity was varied within the range of 7 km/s to 8 km/s, and the final velocity was varied between 950 m/s and 1050 m/s, employing uniform sampling intervals of 200 m/s and 50 m/s, respectively. Subsequently, potential trajectories were generated using GPOPS. The testing set comprised three trajectories, as depicted by the curves in Fig. 7. The remaining fifteen trajectories constituted the training set, as illustrated by the violin plot in Fig. 7, with three of these trajectories allocated for validation. In total, 13,862 time points were sampled from eighteen trajectories.

The re-entry capsule and its trajectory diagram, along with the CFD mesh and CST grid, are shown in Fig. 8. It only accounts for the CHT at the windward surface of the heat shield base, which plays a critical role in withstanding severe aerodynamic heating during re-entry. The boundary conditions, and mesh/grid, along with their independence verification, are similar to our previous work [35]. While the earlier study focused on predicting heat flux using internal discrete sensor measurements, this manuscript aims to extend the prediction capability to any trajectory. Heat flux and temperature distributions were computed along trajectories using CFD and CST, with a time step of one second. The surface heat flux derived from CFD was applied as a boundary condition for $\mathrm{CST,}$ and the wall temperature from CST served as a boundary condition for CFD. To manage differences in mesh sizes between CFD and CST, second-order interpolation was applied to interpolate data across the surface of the re-entry capsule. The CFD and CST simulations were performed using the FLUENT density-based solver and ANSYS Mechanical APDL with Plane 55 elements.

The verification of independence for the CFD mesh, which features a structured boundary layer and an unstructured outer region, alongside the structured CST grid, is presented in Fig. 9. Steady-state CFD simulations were performed at 78 km altitude, 20 Mach number, and 0◦ angle of attack using meshes with four different element counts. Mesh 3 was chosen for its average heat flux relative error of $< 0.3$ % compared to Mesh $^ {4 ,}$ offering comparable results with fewer elements and reduced computational cost. Similarly, a steady heat flux of $\mathsf{l} \times 10 ^ {6}$ was applied to the surface of the heat shield base in CST. Grid $^ {3 ,}$ which achieved an average temperature relative error of <0.04 % compared to Grid $^ {4 ,}$ was selected for the simulations due to its comparable accuracy and reduced grid size. The temperature and heat flux values are from 246 CFD surface nodes.

## 4.2. Modelling

In this work, the computational environment utilized consists of the following hardware and software specifications: the desktop computer was equipped with an Intel Xeon Silver 4112 CPU at 2.60 GHz, 64GB of RAM, and an NVIDIA GeForce RTX 4090 GPU. The software environment was based on Python 3.8, with the TensorFlow framework. The model used the Adam optimizer with an initial learning rate of 0.005. The baseline configuration for the TCN block included two dilated causal convolution layers and one residual connection, with each convolution layer comprising 64 filters of size 3. The baseline CNN block was configured with four 1D convolutional layers, each containing 64 filters with decreasing kernel sizes as the layers deepen, and four average pooling layers with a pool size of 2.

The data were normalized using Z-score standardization. To prevent overfitting and preserve the model’s generalizability, an early stopping criterion was applied. Specifically, if no improvement in accuracy was observed on the validation set after 100 iterations, the training process was halted, and the parameters from the best-performing iteration were retained. Additionally, a closed-loop mode was employed as the evaluation strategy for predicting transient heat flux distribution. This choice was motivated by the requirements of HFDP applications in hypersonic vehicles, where the closed-loop mode allows for rapid predictions of heat flux distribution along any given trajectory without relying on time-consuming CFD calculations as accurate inputs. Time series models can be evaluated using two strategies: open-loop and closed-loop modes. The distinction lies in whether a self-feedback mechanism is used, i.e., whether the model’s outputs are fed back as inputs for subsequent time steps. In open-loop mode, the model uses precise external outputs from the previous time step as the current input, without allowing past predictions to influence future decisions. In contrast, the closed-loop mode relies entirely on the model’s historical predictions to generate forecasts, making training more challenging and increasing the risk of error accumulation.

The ideal combination of input sequence length and output dimensionality was determined through multiple experiments using the baseline TSCN model, as shown in Fig. 10. The first 6, 19, 51, and 96 POD modes correspond to energy contributions of 99 %, 99.9 %, 99.99 %, and 99.999 %, respectively. When using deconvolution layers as the output layer, four deconvolution layers were employed. The durations of the most recent flight states input were 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 12, 32, 64, 128, and 256. The results indicate that for high-dimensional heat flux distributions, there is an optimal number of POD modes to retain, with an energy contribution of 99.9 % achieved by selecting the first 19 modes. When modes are too few, there is a noticeable increase in the proportion of reconstruction error; conversely, an excess of dimensions may lead to greater modeling errors. The prevalent method in existing studies, which sets the reduced dimensionality based on a specified energy contribution, poses risks as different numbers of reduced dimensions yield varying modeling outcomes. For the duration of influential recent flight states, using the most recent 4 states typically yields the best results. Sequences that are too short may overlook historical influences and fail to capture temporal dependence, while excessively long sequences input can lead to sparser data, making the model more prone to overfitting. Furthermore, the results reveal that the impact of varying the length of historical states on different output dimensions is nearly identical. Additionally, using POD dimensionality reduction improves model performance compared to models without POD or with deconvolution output layers. Overall, the subsequent model used 4 recent states and wall temperature to determine the mode coefficients of the first 19 modes of heat flux distribution.

Then, several baseline TSCN models were trained with varying numbers of time points from the training trajectory set to assess the impact of data volume on its performance. The results, detailed in Table 1, reveal that model accuracy improves with more data. Surprisingly, even with as few as 3 trajectories, the model achieved commendable results, with a relative error of <7 %. This error further decreased to <3 % when the training set included 15 trajectories, indicating highly accurate predictions of heat flux. This study employed data from 15 trajectories at each time point in the training set, aiming to explore the potential for improving accuracy.

We conducted hyperparameter tuning on the baseline TSCN model. The hyperparameter choices and corresponding optimal values for the TCN block are presented in Table 2, while those for the CNN block are provided in Table 3. Comparing Table 2 and Table 3, it is evident that CNN tends to use fewer filters. An excess of filters in CNNs leads to poorer predictive performance along the trajectory, as it results in increased influence from previously predicted results at $T _ {k - 1} ^ {w}$ and accumulates more errors.

Subsequently, ablation studies were performed to validate the necessity and superiority of the employed components and algorithms. The results are summarized in Table 4. The highest accuracy was achieved by simultaneously inputting both the recent states and wall temperature, with the TCN and CNN independently analyzing their respective inputs. Solely using CNN to analyze wall temperature resulted in the lowest accuracy, due to wall temperatures and predicted heat flux reaching equilibrium values over time, achieving a steady state in CHT, which means changes in flight states cannot be disregarded. The results in Table 4 also indicate that CNN exhibits superior spatial analysis capabilities of wall temperature distribution than FCL. Additionally, the ability to analyze recent states decreases in the order of TCN, LSTM, GRU, standard RNN, and FCL.

## 4.3. Discussion

Using the optimal parameters derived in previous section, the HFDP at each time point was achieved with an average prediction time of about 0.035 s. Integrating the proposed HFDP method with CST simulations eliminates the need for CFD calculations, which typically require at least 15 min per time step in traditional CHT analysis. This efficiency allows for completing CHT analysis along a new trajectory to obtain heat flux distributions within just one hour, marking an efficiency increase of about 200 times compared to traditional CHT analysis.

The spatio-temporal heat fluxes (both calculated and predicted) along the three test trajectories are shown in Fig. 11. Temporal slices show that the CFD-calculated heat flux (depicted in black) peaks at $Y =$ 0.27 m, which aligns with the spatial heat flux distribution trends on the re-entry capsule surface, as proposed by Duda and Nakamura [36]. Temporally, the maximum heat flux occurs around 100 s, corresponding to periods of rapid descent during re-entry that generate high thermal loads. Secondary temporal peaks between 300 and 500 s arise as the vehicle briefly exits and then re-enters the atmosphere, reducing exposure to high temperatures. The predicted heat flux (shown in red) closely aligns with the calculated heat flux (in black) across different times and spatial locations. The comprehensive results for the three test trajectories, including $R ^ {2} {\mathrm{,}}$ , RMSE, and relative error, are 0.9993, 31.330 kW/m², and 1.397 %, respectively. Both temporally and spatially, regions with high heat flux typically correspond to relatively larger prediction errors.

For an intuitive representation of the error distribution, Fig. 11 plots the temporally averaged absolute error on Y = 0.35 m plane and the spatially averaged absolute error on the final-time-step plane. Specific statistical data for the three test trajectories, with durations increasing sequentially, are listed in Table 5. From the perspective of RMSE, the error for Test Trajectory 2 is smaller than that for Test Trajectories 1 and $^ {3 ,}$ which are closer to the sampling boundary. Additionally, the error for the longer-duration Test Trajectory 3 is larger than that for Test Trajectory 1. The maximum spatially averaged absolute error for the three test trajectories occurred at 82 s, 466 s, and 657 s, respectively. The fact that the maximum error occurs later for the trajectories with longer durations is attributed to the increased error accumulation resulting from multiple iterations of the prediction model coupled with CST for conjugate heat transfer. The maximum temporally averaged absolute error for the three test trajectories occurred at $Y = 0.281$ m, $Y = 0.267$ m, and $Y = 0.278 ~ \mathrm{m} ,$ respectively, around $Y = 0.27 ~ \mathrm{m}$ , which corresponds to regions of high heat flux, and generally exhibits larger prediction errors.

Furthermore, we selected several critical time points in Test Trajectory 2 to assess the agreement between predicted and actual heat flux distributions, as shown in Fig. 12. These time points are the peak heat flux time $t _ {\mathrm{ph}} = 94 \ : s ,$ a local maximum heat flux time $t _ {\mathrm{lh}} = 372 s ,$ a local maximum spatially averaged absolute error time $t _ {\mathrm{le}} = 105 \ s ,$ and the peak spatially averaged absolute error time $t _ {\mathrm{pe}} = 466 ~ s$ . The spatially averaged absolute error shows a delayed extremum after the extremum heat flux, indicating that HFDP errors propagate and accumulate in the CHT process. In particular, the error peaks 11 s after $t _ {\mathrm{ph}} ,$ , and 94 s after $t _ {\mathrm{lh}} ,$ reflecting more persistent accumulation during later times. According to Fig. 12, higher heat flux typically corresponds to larger maximum relative errors, and this gap narrows near the extremum spatially averaged absolute error due to the accumulated CHT errors. Moreover, the average relative errors at the four critical time points $\left( t _ {\mathrm{ph}} , t _ {\mathrm{lh}} , t _ {\mathrm{le}} , a n d t _ {\mathrm{pe}} \right)$ are 1.398 %, 0.905 %, 2.246 %, and 2.842 %, respectively. While $t _ {\mathrm{ph}}$ (with higher heat flux) shows a higher error than $t _ {\mathrm{lh}} , \ t _ {\mathrm{le}}$ exhibits a lower average relative error than $t _ {\mathrm{pe}}$ This discrepancy arises from the accumulation of CHT errors later in the trajectory—not only because the actual heat flux (the denominator in relative error) decreases near the end, but also due to varying trajectory durations that slightly reduce prediction accuracy at later times. These factors underscore the effect of transient CHT on heat flux prediction and highlight the need for high prediction accuracy during long-duration flights.

To validate the effectiveness of our model, we compared its performance with traditional steady-state heat flux distribution prediction methods which use only the current flight state and wall temperature, including a dimensionality reduction-based method and a point-bypoint prediction method, as detailed in Table 6. The dimensionality reduction-based method utilized POD and a BPNN. POD was applied to reduce the dimensionality of the heat flux distribution, retaining the first 19 modes that accounted for at least 99.9 % of the energy contribution. For the point-by-point prediction method, a multi-layer perceptron model was constructed. The fully connected layers for both the dimensionality reduction-based and point-by-point methods were configured with 246 neurons. Our proposed TSCN method outperformed these traditional methods in prediction accuracy.

In practical applications, reliable heat flux predictions are crucial for vehicle thermal safety. To enhance the reliability of predictions, we conducted an uncertainty analysis of the TSCN model using the dropoutbased Bayesian approximation (DBA) method proposed by Gal [37]. This method maintains dropout during both the training and testing phases, allowing for random deactivation of network neurons with a specified probability. We utilized DBA to assign a probability distribution to the model parameters, thereby producing different models for each test. Consequently, this method generates multiple models corresponding to the number of tests, transforming the predicted values into a range of predictions. This approach allows the model to efficiently produce a prediction range for multiple tests, offering a faster alternative to repeatedly training and evaluating models.

The DBA-based TSCN model, which employed a dropout layer with a dropout rate of 0.1 and positioned before the final output layer, was chosen to balance the need for uncertainty without further compromising accuracy. Testing for 50, 200, and 800 times yielded prediction coverage for calculated heat flux durations of 91.551 %, 96.516 %, and 98.215 %, respectively, as shown in Fig. 13. The results indicate that the predicted intervals cover most of the calculated heat flux durations, and the number of tests performed is positively correlated with the coverage of calculated heat flux distribution by the predicted intervals. Further increasing the number of tests has the potential to ensure that the prediction intervals fully encompass the entire duration of the calculated heat flux. Specifically, with 800 tests, the prediction intervals for three trajectories with progressively longer durations show that the calculated heat flux was not fully included for 0.607 %, 0 %, and 4.071 % of the respective durations. Among these, the predicted intervals were too low for 0.287 %, 0 %, and 1.628 % of the durations, and too high for 0.320 %, 0 %, and 2.443 % of the durations. The trajectory with the highest deviation in the predicted interval duration was Trajectory 3, followed by Trajectory 1, while Trajectory 2 fully included the calculated values. This is likely due to the fact that Trajectory 3 has the longest duration and is closer to the sampling boundary, whereas Trajectory 1, although also near the boundary, has a shorter duration. In contrast, Trajectory 2 is situated near the center of a more densely sampled region. This suggests that the data-driven heat flux prediction method proposed in this study should consider a wide and thorough sampling of possible trajectory ranges. Overall, the model based on DBA successfully predicted intervals that covered most of the calculated heat flux durations, particularly providing complete coverage of the peak heat flux distribution for durations around 100 s. This work, which provides spatiotemporal heat flux predictions along several potential new trajectories, offers a significant computational advantage over traditional CFD/CST conjugate heat transfer simulations, which are typically computationally intensive. The TSCN-based HFDP method allows for quick heat flux distribution prediction within a few seconds, even after several hundred tests, thus forming reliable prediction intervals. This is highly beneficial for applications in thermal protection systems, thermal management, and attitude control, among others.

## 5. Conclusion

In this study, we proposed a heat flux distribution prediction method for hypersonic flight vehicles based on POD and TSCN. The method was validated by predicting the heat flux distribution along the return trajectory of the re-entry capsule. The conclusions of this study are summarized as follows:

• The proposed method predicted the heat flux distribution at each time step in about 0.035 s, effectively replacing time-consuming CFD calculations to perform CHT analysis. This allows for obtaining heat flux distributions along a new trajectory within an hour, with an efficiency increase of about 200 times in comparison with traditional CHT analysis.

• The predicted heat flux distributions of the re-entry capsule along the return trajectory achieved an average relative prediction error below 2 %, outperforming traditional heat flux prediction methods without accounting for recent states.

• The proposed method can achieve better accuracy when using the first 19 modes of the heat flux distribution and the four most recent flight states, which are the most important parameters for POD and TSCN, respectively.

• The TSCN model, employing dropout-based Bayesian approximation, produced prediction intervals that encompassed over 90 % of the calculated heat flux distribution, demonstrating reliability by preventing error accumulation, which is crucial for coupling analysis along the trajectory.
