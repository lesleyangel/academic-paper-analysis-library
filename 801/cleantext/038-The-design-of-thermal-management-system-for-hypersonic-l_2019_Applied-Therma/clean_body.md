## 1. Introduction

The high flight speed of hypersonic aerospace vehicles leads to severe aerodynamic heating (e.g., higher than 1800 K [1–3]) and brings great challenges for thermal management system (TMS). The well designed TMS should be able to manage the transfer process, and control the dissipation and even the reuse of aerodynamic heat in an efficient way. In this work, the TMS consists of a thermal protection system (TPS) which is responsible for preventing the heat entering the vehicle, and an active cooling network (ACN) which “takes the heat away” from the high-temperature region.

The traditional TPS can be classified as passive and active TPS. A passive TPS is composed of a variety of concepts. In this work, the TPS concepts are mainly those developed in Space Shuttle engineering by NASA [4], which consists of three types, i.e., flexible ceramic blankets (e.g., tailorable advanced blanket insulation, TABI), rigid ceramic tiles (e.g., alumina enhanced thermal barrier, AETB) and metallic panels (e.g., superalloy honeycomb sandwich panels, SA-HC). In general, such a type of concept is composed of an outer high-temperature layer, an insulation layer and an inner layer, which can be attached to the vehicle structure. The design key for such a TPS is to obtain optimum distribution of concepts (allowable temperature should be higher than the aerodynamic heated wall temperature) and scales (size and weight) of concept (larger heat load needs larger thickness of insulation layer).

For passive TPS, the aerodynamic heat is dissipated by surface radiation and internal energy rise of relevant materials. It is obviously a relative inefficient technology. Sometimes, especially for hypersonic vehicles, the size and the weight of such a purely passive TPS could be unacceptable. The active cooling system of TMS in this work adopts coolant convection cooling technology. The coolant convection cooling is wellknown as regenerative cooling technology when the fuel is used as coolant: the fuel flows through high-temperature region and is heated by kinetic energy (aerodynamic heat) before entering the propulsion system, thus a waste-heat utilization is accomplished. There are several typical active TPS technologies, such as ablative TPS [5,6], heat-pipe-cooled TPS [7], film cooling TPS [8–10], coolant convection TPS [11,12], etc. For TPS based on ablative technology, part of the aerodynamic heat will be dissipated by the ablation of relevant materials; such a TPS is widely used in external thermal protection of reentry vehicles and also rocket combustion chambers [13–16]; however, it is not appropriate to reusable vehicles due to its changing configuration during the flight mission. The heat pipe can transfer aerodynamic heat from high-temperature region to low-temperature region in a short time; it is popular in thermal protection of critical components like vehicle leading edges [17,18]; however, the heat pipe only transfers heat from dangerous region to safety region, and the final heat dissipation has to resort to surface radiation or other additional measures, thus such heat-pipe-cooled thermal protection technology is not suitable for surfaces of large area. The design of TMS involves several key variables including the aerodynamic heat load and wall temperature, the distribution and weight of passive TPS concept, the coolant mass flow rate and temperature rise of ACN. In real heat transfer process, these physical variables are in interactional relations, thus the TMS design process should be carried on in a coupled way. For this sake, in this work, an equivalent thermal equilibrium model is proposed to simulate the heat transfer process, an equivalent heat transfer coefficient (HTC) is applied to indicate the capacity of active cooling. A hypersonic launch vehicle (fuselage length of 30 m, width of 5 m and height of 4 m, and wingspan of 15 m) with a typical trajectory is studied, the TMS consists of passive TPS and ACN is established, and the influence of equivalent HTC is analyzed.

## 2. The vehicle and its trajectory

The launch vehicle and its typical trajectory are shown in Fig. 1 (the white ellipse in the tail wing surface shows “Northwestern Polytechnical University” in English abbreviation and Chinese). As shown in the left part, the vehicle has a length of 30 m, a wingspan of 15 m, a fuselage width of 5 m, and a fuselage height of 4 m. The vehicle structure is mainly composed of Ti- and Al-alloy and resin-based composites, and more details for similar vehicles can be found in Refs. [28,29]. The vehicle size will influence the TMS in three ways. First, it will influence the local Reynolds number and thus influence the calculation of aerodynamic heating. Second, it will influence the passive TPS, e.g., the size and weight of each type of concept, and also the weight of the whole TPS. Third, it will influence the ACN since the capacity of ACN will be dependent on the area of surface region. In this work, only one size of the vehicle is considered on account of the limited paper length.

The trajectory of a flight vehicle includes three main parameters, the flight altitude, velocity and angle of attack. The altitude determines the physical conditions (pressure and temperature) and properties (density, viscosity, thermal conductivity and heat capacity) of free stream. The energy of free stream is determined by the physical conditions and properties of free stream, and the velocity and attack angle of the vehicle. Thus, the aerodynamic heating and following design of TMS will be influenced by the trajectory. In this work, a typical trajectory is provided as shown in the right part of Fig. 1. The left vertical axis is flight altitude, the right vertical axis is Mach number, and the abscissa axis is flight time. The black solid and red dashed lines are flight altitude and Mach number, respectively. It can be noticed that the vehicle speed increases from 0 to 8 Ma within about 450 s, reaches its highest altitude of 60 km after about 500–600 s, and then the vehicle will accomplish its mission and return to the launch field. As shown by the blue curve in Fig. 1, the trajectory can be divided into two periods of “Ascent” and “Return”, and the flight range is 0–6600 km. In addition, as shown in the right top diagram, the angle of attack varies between 0.8° and 20°.

In this work, the passive TPS and ACN will be designed based on the vehicle size and trajectory shown in Fig. 1.

## 3. Methodology of TMS design

## 3.1. Equivalent thermal equilibrium model and HTC

The heat transfer processes involved in TMS includes aerodynamic heating and thermal radiation at TPS wall and heat conduction in TPS. The thermal equilibrium equation at surface of TPS is shown in Eq. (1):

$$q _ {a e r o} = \alpha ( T _ {r} - T _ {w} ) = \varepsilon \sigma T _ {W} ^ {4} + q _ {i n}\tag{1}$$

where $q _ {a e r o}$ is the aerodynamic heat flux, is the coefficient of heat convection, $T _ {r}$ is the recovery wall temperature, $T _ {w}$ is the wall temperature, $\sigma = 5.67 \times 10 ^ {- 8} \mathrm{W} / ( \mathrm{m} ^ {2} \mathrm{K} ^ {4} )$ is the Stefan-Boltzmann constant, is the surface emissivity, and $q _ {i n}$ is the heat flux that is conducted into TPS. The aerodynamic heat can be calculated based on Eq. (1), while in most previous studies, $q _ {i n}$ is assumed to be 0 to simplify computation.

In this work, to conduct a coupled design, the active cooling should be considered firstly in the calculation of aerodynamic heating. Therefore, an equivalent thermal equilibrium model is established as shown in Fig. 2. Fig. 2(a) and (b) show the TPS and ACN for real conditions and the assumed conditions, respectively. As shown in Fig. 2(a), for real engineering conditions, the active cooling channel is at the bottom of passive TPS, thus $q _ {i n}$ is:

$$q _ {i n} = h _ {l} ( T _ {b} - T _ {c} )\tag{2}$$

where $h _ {l}$ is the local HTC of active cooling which should be different at different regions, $T _ {b}$ is the temperature of TPS bottom (cold side) which is always a constrained parameter in design (e.g., 330, 367 and 440 K in this work), $T _ {c}$ is the temperature of coolant (assumed to be 280 K in this work).

For passive TPS, $T _ {w}$ will be used to determine the distribution of TPS concept, while $q _ {a e r o} , \ T _ {b}$ and q $q _ {i n}$ will be used to obtain the TPS scale (thickness of insulation layer, weight, etc.). For ACN, $q _ {i n}$ and $h _ {l}$ can be used to obtain the capacity of active cooling (coolant mass flow rate, temperature rise, etc.). However, for a flight vehicle, $q _ {i n}$ and hl should vary with aeroheating level $( \mathrm{i} . \mathrm{e} . , T _ {w} )$ at different regions, thus they are inappropriate to be selected as design parameters directly. Therefore, as shown in Fig. 2(b), we proposed an equivalent HTC, $h _ {e q}$ and a new formulation of $q _ {i n} \mathrm{:}$ the cooling channel is assumed to be under the TPS surface (Fig. 2(b)), and then $q _ {i n}$ is:

$$q _ {i n} = h _ {e q} ( T _ {w} - T _ {c} )\tag{3}$$

where $h _ {e q}$ is the so-called equivalent HTC, which indicates the overall capacity of ACN. As shown in Eq. (3), if $h _ {e q}$ is given a constant value for the whole vehicle, for regions with higher $T _ {w}$ (higher aeroheating level), $q _ {i n}$ is higher and enhanced active cooling is required, and vice versa.

Let the real conducting heat flux and the assumed one have the same value, then the local HTC can be calculated by:

$$h _ {l} = q _ {i n} / ( T _ {b} - T _ {c} ) = h _ {e q} ( T _ {w} - T _ {c} ) / ( T _ {b} - T _ {c} )\tag{4}$$

Therefore, the assumed equivalent model in Fig. 2(b) is reasonable and the equivalent HTC is an effective design parameter. For ACN, the mass flow rate and temperature rise of coolant should be the basic information for a specific design. Such two parameters can be evaluated by:

$$q _ {i n} {\cdot} A = m _ {f} {\cdot} c {\cdot} \Delta T\tag{5}$$

where A is the heat transfer area, $m _ {f} ,$ c and $\triangle T$ are the mass flow rate, heat capacity and temperature rise of coolant, respectively. The product of $m _ {f}$ and $\triangle T$ will be determined. In this work, the coolant is Chinese No. 3 kerosene (RP-3, similarly to Jet-A), a typical commercial Chinese jet fuel, whose physical properties can be found in Refs. [30–32], and in this work, its heat capacity is assumed to be a constant value 2100 J/ (kg K).

In real cooling channels, the temperature of coolant increases from the inlet initial temperature (the original fuel in the tank) to the outlet final temperature (the heated fuel), and in above equations $T _ {c}$ should be the integral mean value for each surface element. However, such values are unavailable at the beginning of the design. Thus, for a preliminary design, $T _ {c}$ is assumed to be the temperature of outlet heated coolant, from the conservative point of view. As shown in Fig. 3, the cooling channels can be connected in series or in parallel ways, if the parallel way is adopted in the preliminary design, the same value for $T _ {c}$ should be dedicated for the whole vehicle (280 K in this work). In addition, the initial temperature is not involved in the calculation of aerodynamic heating and the design of passive TPS, thus it is not specified in this work. However, it will be used to determine the mass flow rate of the ACN (see Eq. (5)) and has two limitations: 1st, it should be lower than $T _ {c} ;$ 2nd, in consideration of the improvement of cooling ability, it should approach the freezing point (224 K [33]) to the greatest extent.

It should be noted that in this work, the cooling with kerosene is just considered by the enthalpy increase via its heat capacity and the chemical heat sink is not considered. This is because the temperature of the fuel in ACN is lower than its chemical limit. For such an ACN that located under the bottom surface of passive TPS, the temperature of coolant should be lower than $T _ {b}$ which is constrained as 330, 367 and 440 K in this work, respectively, while the coking of autoxidation reaction occurs between 450 and 640 K and that of pyrolysis reaction occurs above 700 K [34]. However, if the ACN is located under the top surface of passive TPS, the temperature of coolant can exceed its chemical limit, and the influences of chemical heat sink, soot formation and coking have to be considered.

In short, based on Eqs. (1), (2) and $( 3 ) , h _ {e q}$ will be considered in the calculation of $q _ {a e r o} ,$ Tw, $q _ {i n}$ and $h _ {l}$ The passive TPS including the distribution and scale of TPS concepts, and the ACN including the mass flow rate and temperature rise will be finally determined in a coupled way.

## 3.2. Calculation of aerodynamic heat

For the design of TMS, the aerodynamic heat (heat load and wall temperature) is the boundary condition that should be obtained first. The prediction method includes numerical and engineering-based ones. At present, the numerical method is accurate, while limited by high requirement of mesh and large computational costs [35,36], on the other hand, the engineering-based programs, e.g., MINIVER [37] and LATCH [38], are much more efficient although they have lower accuracy. In this work, the engineering-based method is adopted. In addition, in most previous studies, the aerodynamic heat is calculated conservatively based on radiative equilibrium condition $( q _ {i n} = 0 )$ . In present paper, as shown in Eq. (1), the active cooling is considered and its influence on heat load and wall temperature will be discussed.

The engineering-based algorithm adopted in this paper is based on the theory of Prandtl’s boundary layer. The flow field that external and internal to the boundary layer are considered as inviscid and viscid flows, respectively. Three basic assumptions should be stated as:

(a) For the aerodynamic force calculation of inviscid flow field, the head, fuselage, and wings are considered as flat, and relevant methods are listed in Table 1 [29].

(b) For the viscid compressible flow, the reference temperature method, which is in fact using a compressibility correction, is adopted.

(c) When $\mathrm{Ma} < 2 ,$ i.e., 0–80 s of the trajectory in Fig. 1, the present method has low accuracy, thus data for this period is obtained by linear interpolation.

For the inviscid flow field (external to the boundary layer), the ideal gas is assumed and the aerodynamic parameters are calculated based on isentropic conditions. According to the configuration and flight speed, the calculation methods used for head, fuselage and wings are listed in Table 1 [29], respectively. The detailed information can be found in Ref. [39]. The inviscid field will provide information of the outer edge of boundary layer, which will be input in the calculation of viscid flow field.

For the viscid flow field (internal to the boundary layer), the reference temperature method is used, in which the reference temperature $T ^ {*}$ is defined by:

$$\begin{array} {l} {T ^ {*} = T _ {e} + 0.58 ( T _ {w} - T _ {e} ) + 0.19 ( T _ {r} - T _ {e} ) ( M a _ {e} < 5 ) \phantom {}} \\ {T ^ {*} = 0.7 T _ {e} + 0.58 ( T _ {w} - T _ {e} ) + 0.23 ( T _ {r} - T _ {e} ) ( 5 < M a _ {e} < 10 )} \end{array}\tag{6}$$

where the subscript $^ {\omega} e ^ {\gamma}$ means the outer edge of boundary layer, $T _ {r}$ is the recovery temperature and can be calculated by parameters of the outer edge of boundary layer. In addition, the boundary layer is assumed to be fully turbulent in this work. The heat convective coefficient in Eq. (1) can be obtained based on the thermodynamic parameters under the reference temperature. Finally, the aerodynamic heat flux can be obtained based on iterative solving.

It should be noted that the aerodynamic heat flux at stagnation point is calculated by the well-known Kemp-Riddell equation. Detailed description can be found in Ref. [40]. For specific regions like the leading edge, such an engineering-based method may have lower accuracy. This work mainly concerns the TMS design of the whole vehicle, thus no further CFD confirmation is made for those specific regions.

## 3.3. Process of TMS design

As shown in Fig. $^ {4 ,}$ the design of vehicle TMS contains four steps, calculate the aerodynamic heat, determine the distribution of TPS concept, compute the scale of passive TPS and capacity of ACN, and accomplish the design of TMS after several iterations.

(a) Cooling channels in series

(b) Cooling channels in parallel

Step 1, the aerodynamic heat should be calculated based on vehicle configuration, trajectory, and parameters of active cooling, which according to Eqs. (1) and (3) are $h _ {e q}$ (equivalent HTC) and $T _ {c}$ (temperature of coolant). After the calculation (see Section 3.2), according to Eqs. (1), (2) and (3), three variables, the aerodynamic heat load, Φ, which is the integral of heat flux and time, the equilibrium wall temperature $T _ {w} ,$ and the heat flux conducted into TPS (taken away by active cooling) $q _ {i n}$ can be an output.

Step 2, the distribution of TPS concept should be obtained. For specific regions of the vehicle surface, available TPS concept should meet the requirement of:

$$T _ {w} < T _ {T P S} < T _ {w} + T _ {t o l}\tag{7}$$

where $T _ {w}$ is the highest wall temperature of the region, $T _ {T P S}$ is the largest operational temperature of the concept and $T _ {t o l}$ is the temperature tolerance. Based on $T _ {w}$ of the whole vehicle surface and the concept database (see later Section $3.4 )$ , the concept distribution can be determined.

Step 3, the scale of passive TPS and capacity of ACN should be obtained. For passive TPS, after the concept distribution of whole vehicle is determined. The scale, i.e., thickness and weight of concepts can be obtained by a one-dimensional transient heat transfer analysis. As shown in ${\mathrm{Fig.~}} 5 ,$ the boundary condition of outside boundary is the aerodynamic heating and radiation dissipation, while the inside boundary is the heat flux that is taken by active cooling, $q _ {i n}$ The thermal protection performance is evaluated by the temperature of each layer. The temperature of each layer should be lower than the materials’ allowable temperature, and the inside boundary temperature should be lower than the specified $T _ {b}$ In this work, the thickness of insulation layer is a variable that will be used to determine the scale of a TPS concept. The system is optimized based on the sequential quadratic programming (SQP) method implemented in MATLAB. The optimization objective is the minimum TPS weight, and the mathematical description of the optimization problem should be:

$$\begin{array} {c} {{\mathrm{Min} \quad \mathrm{Weight} ( x ) = \sum _ {i = 0} ^ {n} \rho _ {i} x _ {i}}} \\ {{s . t . \quad T _ {i} ( x ) < T _ {i \mathrm{max}} , ~ i = 1 , 2 , . . . , n}} \\ {{T _ {n} ( x ) < T _ {b}}} \end{array}\tag{8}$$

where n is the number of material layers of TPS concept, x is the thickness of each layer, Ti(x) and $T _ {n} ( x )$ are the calculated temperature of each layer, and $T _ {i \operatorname* {m a x}}$ is the highest allowable temperature of the material of i-layer. Finally, for the whole vehicle, the passive TPS is established with the determined distribution and scale of concepts.

On the other hand, as shown in Fig. 4 and Eq. (5), the capacity of ACN, i.e., the mass flow rate and temperature rise of coolant can be obtained based on $q _ {i n}$ and coolant physical properties. As discussed above, $q _ {i n}$ has different values and thus the capacity parameters vary at different regions.

Step $^ {4 ,}$ the design of the TMS can be accomplished. According to the above design process, passive TPS and ACN are two interactional systems, and they are designed in a coupled way based on the equivalent HTC h . If the scales of passive TPS and ACN do not meet the requirements of vehicle overall design, a new iteration should be started from step 1 to 3. Finally, an appropriate TMS should be established after several iterations.

## 3.4. TPS concept database

The basic TPS concepts that were employed on the Space Shuttle orbiter [4] are listed in Table 2, and those adopted in concept database in this work are indicated by numbers in the last column. The concepts are classified as flexible ceramic blankets which includes AFRSI (advanced flexible reusable surface insulation) and TABI (tailorable advanced blanket insulation), metallic panels which includes TIMW (titanium multiwall), TI-HC (titanium honeycomb metallic TPS), SA-HC (superalloy honeycomb), SA-HC2 (second generation superalloy honeycomb) and AMHC (advanced metallic honeycomb), and rigid ceramic tiles including LI-900 (LI-900 tiles), AETB-8 (alumina enhanced thermal barrier tiles-8), AETB-12 (alumina enhanced thermal barrier tiles-12) and UHTC (ultra-high temperature ceramics tiles).

The concepts distribution is determined based on the wall temperature and the operational temperature of each concept, thus for a certain temperature range only one concept should be adopted in the database. However, as shown in Table 2, some concepts have the same highest allowable temperature, e.g., 922 K for AFRSI, TIMW and TI-HC, 1366 K for SA-HC and SA-HC2, 1478 K for TABI and AMHC, 1644 K for AETB-8 and AETB-12. In this work, AFRSI, SA-HC2, TABI and AETB-8 are adopted in the database for their lower effective density (see Figs. 18 and 19 in Ref. [4]). Thus, the final database consists of AFRSI, SH-AC2, TABI, LI-900, AETB-8 and UHTC, and they are ordered by the increasing operational temperature as shown in the last column in Table 2. The further detailed structure, scale and physical properties of each concept can be found in Refs. [4,41,42].

For the vehicle studied in this work, the temperature at several critical regions (e.g., leading edge of fuselage head) is much higher than the maximum operational temperature of traditional concepts (1644 K for AETB-8). Thus, a new concept UHTC consists of AETB-8 with an additional out layer of UHTC material is developed in this work. UHTC material can withstand temperature of about 2200–2500 K or even higher and is widely used in component of high temperature in recent years, e.g., sharp leading edges of fuselage head [41,42].

Based on above rationale, process and methods, an incorporate program which can be called “TPSX” is developed to calculate aerodynamic heat, distribute passive TPS concepts, conduct heat transfer analysis, and compute scales of TPS and ACN, and finally accomplish design of the TMS.

## 4.4. Active cooling network

Fig. 14 shows an assumed cooling system for the whole vehicle: the coolant is pumped out from the fuel tank, then flows through two assumed cooling networks, head and rear networks, and finally returns to the fuel tank or flows into the combustor. The head network should be responsible for the vehicle head surfaces including $L _ {F} , L _ {C} , F _ {I - L} \sim F _ {I I I - L} , F _ {I -}$ $s \sim F _ {I I I - S}$ and $C _ {I} \sim C _ {I I I}$ (see Fig. 12), while the rear one is responsible for the rest except $P _ {I - W} , P _ {I I - W} , P _ {I - S}$ and $P _ {\pi s} ,$ which should be cooled by the active cooling of propulsion system. In this system, the aerodynamic heat is taken to the propulsion system for fuel preheating by ACN and finally transformed into propulsive force, the ACN will be considered as regenerative cooling system. In addition, for such a system, the mass flow rate and temperature rise of coolant are the basic input data of further specific design, $\mathbf {e . g . ,}$ , cooling channels and heat exchangers.

As discussed above, two final paths are planned for the heated fuel, flows into the combustor or back into the fuel tank. If the fuel flow rate required by the combustor is larger than the flow rate of regenerative cooling of combustor itself, the heated fuel from the ACN can flow into the combustor directly, otherwise, it should back into the fuel tank first.

For the back path, the temperature of heated fuel has to be considered. If the temperature of heated fuel exceeds the chemical limit (above 450 K), the coking reaction may cause the pipe blockage, and also it may heat the fuel in the tank. In this work, the heated fuel still has relative low temperature, thus it can be fed back into the fuel tank without coking.

In addition, the back path requires some relevant apparatuses. First, a temporary tank is needed to link the ACN and the fuel tank. The scale of temporary tank should be determined by the volume flow rate of coolant, which can be calculated by:

$$\nu _ {f} = m _ {f} {\cdot} \rho ^ {- 1}\tag{9}$$

where mf and are the mass flow rate and the density of coolant, respectively. Second, a pump is needed to pump the fuel from the temporary tank into the fuel tank. The required power of the pump can be determined by:

$$W = \Delta P {\cdot} m _ {f} {\cdot} \rho ^ {- 1} {\cdot} \eta _ {\nu} ^ {- 1} {\cdot} \eta _ {m} ^ {- 1}\tag{10}$$

where $\Delta P$ is the pressure difference between the temporary tank and the fuel tank, and $\eta _ {\nu}$ and $\eta _ {m}$ are the volume efficiency and mechanical efficiency of the pump, respectively.

For instance, if the coolant mass flow rate of the whole vehicle is ${\bf 10 k g} / {\bf s} ,$ the density of coolant (kerosene) is $800 \mathrm{kg/m} ^ {3} ;$ , the pressure of temporary tank and the fuel tank are 0.1 and 0.3 MPa, respectively, and $\eta _ {\nu} {\cdot \eta _ {m}} \mathrm{i} s 0.8$ . Then, the temporary tank should be able to support volume flow rate of $0.0125 \mathrm {{m}} ^ {3} / \mathrm {{s}} ,$ and the minimum power required for the pump should be 3125 W.

The black, blue, pink, dark yellow, dark cyan, green and purple bars indicate results for cases of $h _ {e q} = 0.1 , 1 , 2 , 5 , 10$ 20 and $30 \mathrm{W} / ( \mathrm{m} ^ {2} \mathrm{K} )$ , respectively. The largest value appears at windward surface of wings due to the wings’ large area (about 121 $\mathbf {m} ^ {2} )$ . The value of head network increases from 9.4 to 2565 kg·K/s, while the whole network increases from 30.6 to 7945 kg·K/s with the equivalent HTC increases from 0.1 to $30 \mathrm{W} / ( \mathrm{m} ^ {2} \mathrm{K} ) ,$ , respectively.

## 4.5. Influence of equivalent HTC

The TMS can be accomplished based on above results, especially Table 4 and Fig. 15, which show the detailed information of passive TPS and ACN, respectively. According to above discussions, one can find that the equivalent HTC, $h _ {e q} ,$ is the critical parameter of TMS design: it can be used to influence the aerodynamic heating, the distribution and weight of passive TPS and the capacity of ACN. Fig. 17 shows the weight of TPS and coolant mass flow rate of ACN by the left and right vertical axes, respectively, and the abscissa axis is equivalent HTC. The black solid, dashed and dotted lines are TPS weight of $T _ {b} = 330$ , 367 and 440 K, respectively, while the blue ones are coolant mass flow rate for cases of $\triangle T = 10$ , 30 and 50 K, respectively. The results show that: the lower constrained cold side temperature leads to higher weight of passive TPS; the lower coolant temperature rise leads to higher mass flow rate of ACN; the TPS weight decreases while the mass flow rate of ACN increases with the increasing equivalent HTC. According to Fig. 17 and above discussions, one can conclude that the higher equivalent HTC (greater capacity of ACN) will result in lighter TPS while higher product of coolant mass flow rate and coolant temperature rise for ACN. The real overall design of flight vehicle always provides certain constraint values for the TMS, e.g., weight for TPS and mass flow rate for ACN. Under this condition, the results obtained in this work, especially the curves in Fig. 17 can be referenced. For instance, if the overall design of a vehicle has a constraint of TPS weight of less than 3.5 tons, one can find corresponding design zone $( y < 3.5 )$ for capacity of ACN in Fig. 17.

## 5. Conclusions

In this paper, a design method of thermal management system (TMS) for reusable flight vehicles is developed. The system consists of a passive thermal protection system (TPS) and an active cooling network (ACN) with kerosene as coolant. Based on an equivalent heat equilibrium model, the TPS and ACN are designed in a coupled way. A parameter equivalent HTC, $h _ {e q} ,$ is proposed to indicate the capacity of active cooling. The design process includes four steps, the calculation of aerodynamic heat, the determination of TPS concept distribution, the calculation of scales of TPS and ACN, and the final design of TMS after several iterations. A reusable launch vehicle of 30 m long with a certain trajectory is studied in this work. The influence of equivalent HTC $( h _ {e q} = 0-30 \mathrm{W} / ( \mathrm{m} ^ {2} \mathrm{K} ) )$ and cold side temperature $( T _ {b} = 330 , 367$ and 440 K) on aerodynamic heat, TPS and ACN are studied. The results show some useful conclusions:

(1) The aerodynamic heat flux and the proportion of heat taken by ACN increase, while the wall temperature decreases with the rise of equivalent HTC.

(2) The higher equivalent HTC will result in lighter TPS while higher product of coolant mass flow rate and coolant temperature rise for ACN.

(3) Increasing the constrained cold side temperature is very useful for the weight reduction of passive TPS, and it is more efficient with small equivalent HTC. The weight of TPS is reduced by 30.1%, 16.9%, 8.5%, 7.3%, 0.9%, 0% and 0% with $T _ {b}$ increasing from 330 to 440 K, for $h _ {e q} = 0 , \ 0.1 , \ 1$ , 2, 5, 10, 20, and $30 \mathrm{W} / ( \mathrm{m} ^ {2} {\cdot} \mathrm{K} )$ , respectively.

(4) The ACN can reduce the TPS weight, and it is more efficient with low cold side temperature. The weight of passive TPS is reduced by 40.0% and 14.1% with the equivalent HTC increases from 0 to $30 \mathrm{W} / ( \mathrm{m} ^ {2} {\cdot} \mathrm{K} )$ for $T _ {b} = 330$ and $T _ {b} = 440 \mathrm{;}$ , respectively.

(5) The method proposed in this work can be used to design ACN for the whole vehicle, as well as specific regions like the head of fuselage. For ACN of the vehicle head, the required mass flow rate decreases from 9.4 to 0.188 kg/s, 93.4 to 1.87 kg/s, 187 to 3.73 kg/ s, 462 to 9.25 kg/s, 910 to 18.2 kg/s, 1765 to 35.3 kg/s and 2565 to 51.3 kg/s, when the coolant temperature rise increases from 1 to 50 K for $h _ {e q} = 0.1$ , 1, 2, 5, 10, 20 and $30 \mathrm{W} / ( \mathrm{m} ^ {2} {\cdot} \mathrm{K} )$ respectively. However, for the whole vehicle, the mass flow rate will decrease from 30.6 to 0.612 kg/s, 305 to 6.12 kg/s, 608 to 12.15 kg/s, 1499 to 30.0 kg/s, 2926 to 58.5 kg/s, 5560 to 111 kg/s and 7945 to 159 kg/s, when the coolant temperature rise increases from 1 to 50 K for $h _ {e q} = 0.1 , 1 , 2 , 5 , 10$ , 20 and $30 \mathrm{W} / ( \mathrm{m} ^ {2} {\cdot} \mathrm{K} )$
