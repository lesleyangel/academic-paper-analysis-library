## 1. Introduction

FRP composites are favored in thermal protection structures for many flight vehicles and nozzles of solid rocket motor, due to their superior mechanical and chemical properties, their high strength-toweight ratios, excellent thermal insulation property and low life-cycle cost [1–4]. However, there is a serious concern for the degradation-tofailure of FRP composite structures at high temperatures associated with aerodynamic heating when spacecraft flies at supersonic [5,6]. Therefore, it is necessary to study the degraded stiffness and strength properties of FRP composites, which is significant for further structure design, evaluation of integrity and reliability of thermal protection structures with this kind of composites.

Degradation behavior of high-temperature mechanical properties of FRP composites is a complex phenomenon and a great deal of research is performed on this subject [7,8]. The conclusions could be summarized from the reported literatures as follows: in the lower temperature range, thermal softening and viscoelastic creep can cause the reduction in the mechanical properties of FRP composite, while, when the temperature reaches and exceeds the glass transition temperature, the elastic modulus and strength drops significantly increase [9–12]. Dimitrienko [13–15] defined two degradation functions to describe the temperature dependence of elastic characteristics, stiffness and strength properties, and established a structural thermo-mechanical model of composite materials at high temperatures. Validation of the model was performed on glass fiber-reinforced epoxy composites and the agreement between predicted and experimental results was reasonably good. Liang [16,17] developed a further thermo-mechanical model in which the degradation functions were basically the same as in the Dimitrienko model, and employed meso-mechanical methods to predict ablation and phase transition, and thermo-mechanical behavior of matrix materials and reinforced fibers. For FRP composites, four different states (glassy, leathery, rubbery and decomposed state) and three transitions (glass transition, leathery-to-rubbery transition, and rubbery-to-decomposed transition) can be defined when temperature is raised [18,19]. Based on the Arrhenius equation, Bai and Keller [19] presented a model to determine the temperature-dependent E-modulus, G-modulus, and effective coefficient of thermal expansion of FRP composites during different temperature ranges.

Moreover, E-modulus at gathered key points, such as the glass transition temperature and the decomposition temperature, were obtained by Dynamic Mechanical Analysis (DMA), as introduced by Keller [20], Dao [21], and Bausano et al. [22]. Thus, temperature-dependent E-modulus was described as stepped functions achieved by connecting experimentally key points. Several mathematical models were proposed to calculate the compression properties of polymer laminates at high temperatures [11,12,23–27]. Feih, Mouritz, Gibson et al. [25–27] studied the thermal decomposition, softening and failure of polymer matrix laminates under combined compressive loading and one-sided heating to high temperature. A thermo-mechanical model was presented to predict the compression strength, time-to-failure of polymer laminates in fire and the accuracy of the model was evaluated using failure times measured in fire-under-compression load tests.

Actually, when FRP composites are exposed to high temperatures, pyrolysis reaction of polymer matrix occurs. As the pyrolysis reaction proceeds, porosity and permeability of the material become sufficiently large, and stiffness properties of the porous solid skeleton are reduced drastically. Additionally, accumulation of decomposition gases can cause internal high pore pressure, which in turn will affect the bulk modulus of the composite material. However, less information exists on the loss in compressive stiffness properties with increasing temperature due to the polymer decomposition. Also, the effect of gas pressure on stiffness properties was not considered in the above-cited work.

This study developed a mesomechanical model to predict the degraded stiffness properties of FRP composites supporting a static compressive loading during heating. High temperature compression experiment was carried out to validate the proposed model through comparing numerical results with corresponding experimental results.

## 2. Experimental procedure

In order to determine temperature-dependent behavior of FRP composites under high temperatures, a three-zone annular furnace was installed on the universal testing machine (Instron 5500R, America), as shown in Fig. 1a. Three thermocouples were placed in the topmiddle-bottom of the annular furnace, thus, air temperatures of three zones in the furnace could be controlled separately by temperature control systems, as shown in Fig. 1b and c. The specimen temperature was measured by thermocouple 2 which could be placed at the specimen surface. Although the temperature inside the chamber may in fact was not uniform as the temperature of the upper portion was higher than that of the lower portion, we assume that temperature near the specimen is uniform because the specimen size is small enough compared to the overall length of the furnace [28].

A silica fiber-reinforced phenolic resin composite, kindly provided by Aerospace Research Institute of Materials and Processing Technology (Beijing, China), was selected and characterized in this study. This SiFRP composite was 42 v/v.% silica fiber (Shaanxi HuaTek Fiberglass Material Group Co., Ltd., China), 58 v/v.% phenolic resin (Tongshan chemical plant, China). The manufacture process of the experimental material was as follows. Firstly, the short fiber plies were alternately stacked until the desired thickness. Then, the laminated SiFRP material was immersed with phenolic resin until the weight of the material did not change. The immersed SiFRP material was subsequently solidified at a temperature of $125~^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ for 6 h. Then the SiFRP material for this study was finally obtained. Specimens for high temperature compression experiment measure 10 mm in length, 10 mm in width, and 20 mm in depth. The compressive loading was applied perpendicular to the fiber plane and in the thickness direction of the specimen. The compression experiment was conducted by referring the People's Republic of China National Standard GB/T 1448-2005. The through-thickness compression testing and theory of FRP composites introduced by Thompson [29], Ferguson [30], Kim [31] were also referred.

To investigate the temperature effect on the compressive modulus, specimens were performed with 0.5 mm/min displacement rate at various temperatures in compression tests. 20 (room temperature), 100, 135, 200, 350, 400, 450, 500, 550, 600, 650, and $800~^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ were chosen as the key points and three or five specimens were tested for each temperature to check the repeatability. The elastic modulus and strength of FRP composites are reduced seriously during the thermal softening and thermal decomposition of polymer matrix. Therefore, to obtain relatively accurate temperature-dependent mechanical properties of the material, more tests were performed in the temperature ranges of $20-135~^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ and $350-650~^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ corresponding to the thermal softening and rapid thermal decomposition, respectively [32].

The procedure of the experiment is as follows. At first, temperature control systems of three zones in the furnace were set as the target temperatures. The furnace was heated up without the specimen until the air temperature approached the target temperatures. Then, a sufficient soak time was required until the temperature of the upper and lower loading fixtures were saturated and reached the air temperature. Moreover, to ensure the temperature in the internal composite specimen is uniform, the soak time for the specimen is also required. The soak time corresponding with a key temperature point can be estimated by using a thermal response model which has been reported in our previous work [33]. The calculated results were shown that specimens were exposed to the above-mentioned key temperature points for a period of 0, 25, 15, 15, 20, 20, 20, 20, 20, 20, 25, and 25 min in the furnace, respectively, so that temperatures in the internal specimens with silica/ phenolic composites became saturated. Thereafter, the specimen was placed between the loading fixtures and compressive stress was applied on the specimen. Load and stroke data were acquired using analog-todigital converter during the test. Strain was resolved from the stroke data after compliance of the loading fixtures, grips, and machine was compensated. Compliance of loading fixtures, grips, and machine was directly measured by compression test without the specimen. Using these load and strain data, compressive modulus of the specimen was calculated. Additionally, it is possible to reach the repeatability by averaging the calculated modulus values of three or five specimens at the same temperature.

## 3. Mesomechanical model for predicting the degraded stiffness properties

3.1. Degraded stiffness properties at temperatures lower than glass transition temperature

A large number of fire-under-compression load experiments were performed on FRP composites by Gibson, Feih, Mouritz, Kim et al. [11,25–27,34,35]. Also, a number of functions showing the necessary antisymmetric behavior were examined for fitting mechanical properties [11]. A hyperbolic tanh function was used to describe the degradation behavior of FRP composite materials at temperatures lower than glass transition temperature for this study. The formula of temperature-dependent Young's modulus is given in the following form [11],

$$E ( T ) = \frac {E _ {\mathrm{f}} + E _ {\mathrm{g}}} {2} - \frac {E _ {\mathrm{r}} - E _ {\mathrm{g}}} {2} \operatorname{tanh} ( \eta ( T - T _ {\mathrm{g}} ) )\tag{ð1Þ}$$

where $E _ {\mathrm{r}}$ and $E _ {\mathrm{g}}$ are the Young's modulus at room temperature and at glass transition temperature, respectively. η is a constant describing the breadth of the distribution and $T _ {\mathrm{g}}$ is the glass transition temperature of matrix material. In this paper, the glass transition temperature of phenolic resin was about 140 °C, which was provided by the manufacturer.

3.2. Degraded stiffness properties at temperatures higher than glass transition temperature

Generally, bulk modulus of porous medium depends on property of the matrix, size, distribution and saturated state of the pore. The polymer matrix degrades to form carbonaceous char and gaseous products when pyrolysis reaction occurs. Carbon residue from the pyrolysis reaction and silica fiber form porous char skeleton. Decomposition gases are trapped due to the low porosity and permeability of the material at early stage of decomposition. The pressure of the gas which occupies the pores of the ablation material increases with temperature. In order to examine the effect of gas pressure on effective properties of porous solid skeleton, the internal pressure was generated by applying Eigenstrain in the domain occupied by the gas phase [36]. Also, the effective bulk modulus was adopted in the formulation of the proposed model.

## 3.2.1. Stiffness properties of matrix materials and reinforced fibers

At sufficiently high temperatures, the resin system degrades to form gaseous products and carbonaceous char. The resin polymer phase can transfer into char phase and gas phase. The stiffness properties of polymer matrix can be reduced due to the thermal decomposition. When material temperatures are higher than $600~^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ the phase transition of silica fiber occurs [15]. The amorphous solid phase is converted into crystalline solid phase, and that does not generate gases during the phase transition. Changes in elastic properties of silica fibers have been attributed to two factors: (1) changes in elastic properties of the fiber in the amorphous state under relatively low temperatures; and (2) physico-chemical processes of crystallization under higher temperatures [15]. Similarly to multilevel structural model of fabric materials proposed by Dimitrienko in Ref. [14], the physical models in mesomechanical scale of polymer matrix and reinforced fibers are illustrated in Fig. 2. In the physical models, phase component materials can be considered as multiphase media during ablation and phase transition, which is consisted of a virgin material phase (virg), new solid phase (new) and gas phase (g). The new solid phase and gas phase generated in the ablation and phase transition can be recognized as generalized inclusion phases. Accordingly, the Eshelby–Mori–Tanaka method can be employed to determine the effective properties of phase component materials.

Based on the elasticity theory, the stress–strain relationships of phase components can be given. Volume average effective stress of virgin material phase is expressed as follows,

$$\Theta ^ {\mathrm{virg}} = K ^ {\mathrm{virg}} \widetilde {\theta}\tag{ð2Þ}$$

where $K ^ {\mathrm{virg}}$ is the bulk modulus of virgin materials. $\tilde {\theta}$ is the volume average perturbation strain caused by interfering with each other of all the inclusion phases. Combining Eshelby equivalent inclusion theory, volume average effective stress of new solid phase can also be given by the following form,

$$\Theta ^ {\mathrm{new}} = K ^ {\mathrm{new}} \Big ( \widetilde {\theta} + \theta ^ {1} \Big ) = K ^ {\mathrm{virg}} \Big ( \widetilde {\theta} + \theta ^ {1} - \theta ^ {*} \Big )\tag{ð3Þ}$$

The internal pore pressure was generated by applying Eigen bulk strain $\theta ^ {\mathrm{p}}$ in the domain occupied by the gas phase. Thus, the pressure of gas phase can be written as,

$$\Theta ^ {\mathrm{g}} = K ^ {\mathrm{g}} \Big ( \tilde {\theta} + \theta ^ {2} - \theta ^ {\mathrm{p}} \Big ) = K ^ {\mathrm{virg}} \Big ( \tilde {\theta} + \theta ^ {2} - \theta ^ {\mathrm{p}} - \theta ^ {* *} \Big ) = - P\tag{ð4Þ}$$

where $\boldsymbol {\theta} ^ {*}$ and $\boldsymbol {\theta} ^ {* *}$ are the equivalent Eigen strains induced by introducing solid inclusions and gas inclusions in the virgin materials, respectively. P is the gas pressure and can be obtained by the thermal response model in our previous work [33]. Θ1 and $\theta ^ {2}$ are the perturbation strain owing to presence of a single solid phase inclusion or gas phase inclusion, respectively, and they can be expressed in terms of $\boldsymbol {\theta} ^ {*}$ and $\boldsymbol {\theta} ^ {* *}$ through Eshelby equivalent inclusion theory.

$$\theta ^ {1} = S ^ {1} \theta ^ {*} \quad \theta ^ {2} = S ^ {2} \theta ^ {* *}\tag{ð5Þ}$$

where $S ^ {1}$ and $S ^ {2}$ are the fourth order Eshelby tensors of solid phase inclusion and gas phase inclusion, respectively, which depend on shapes of the inclusion and properties of the virgin material.

Substituting Eq. (5) back into Eqs. (3) and (4) to calculate the equivalent Eigen strains $\boldsymbol {\theta} ^ {*} , \boldsymbol {\theta} ^ {* *}$

$$\theta ^ {\ast} = \left[ K ^ {\mathrm{virg}} - \left( K ^ {\mathrm{virg}} - K ^ {\mathrm{new}} \right) S ^ {1} \right] ^ {- 1} \left( K ^ {\mathrm{virg}} - K ^ {\mathrm{new}} \right) \widetilde {\theta}\tag{ð6Þ}$$

$$\theta ^ {* *} = \left[ K ^ {\mathrm{virg}} - \left( K ^ {\mathrm{virg}} - K ^ {g} \right) S ^ {2} \right] ^ {- 1} \left( K ^ {\mathrm{virg}} - K ^ {g} \right) \left[ \tilde {\theta} + ( S - I ) \theta ^ {\mathrm{p}} \right] .\tag{ð7Þ}$$

According to the condition of static equilibrium, the following relationship can be written for the ablative material consisted of multi-phases media,

$$\int _ {V ^ {\mathrm{virg}}} \Theta ^ {\mathrm{virg}} d V + \int _ {V ^ {\mathrm{new}}} \Theta ^ {\mathrm{new}} d V + \int _ {V ^ {\mathrm{g}}} \Theta ^ {\mathrm{g}} d V = 0\tag{ð8Þ}$$

where $V ^ {\mathrm {{u r g}}} ,$ Vnew and $V ^ {\mathrm{g}}$ represent volume fractions of phase media. The volume fraction of the ablative material depend on temperature and heating time, and can be calculated through the mass transforming relationships during volume ablation [33]. Combining Eqs. (2), (3) and (4), Eq. (8) becomes,

$$\tilde {\theta} + V ^ {\mathrm{new}} \Big ( \theta ^ {1} - \theta ^ {*} \Big ) + V ^ {\mathrm{g}} \Big ( \theta ^ {1} - \theta ^ {\mathrm{p}} - \theta ^ {* *} \Big ) = 0\tag{ð9Þ}$$

Additionally, Eq. (4) can be rewritten as,

$$K ^ {\mathrm{virg}} \left[ \tilde {\theta} + ( S - I ) \theta ^ {p} + ( S - I ) \theta ^ {* *} \right] = - P .\tag{ð10Þ}$$

Thus, the equivalent Eigen strains $\boldsymbol {\theta} ^ {*} , \boldsymbol {\theta} ^ {* *}$ and $\theta ^ {\mathrm{p}}$ can be determined by Eqs. (6), (7), (9) and (10).

The total volume average strainθ of the phase component material is expressed as,

$$\overline {{\theta}} = V ^ {\mathrm{virg}} \theta ^ {\mathrm{virg}} + V ^ {\mathrm{new}} \theta ^ {\mathrm{new}} + V ^ {\mathrm{g}} \theta ^ {\mathrm{g}}\tag{ð11Þ}$$

It is assumed that there is little change in volume fractions of inclusion phase when a small bulk strain increment exists in the material. After the total volume average strain θ is obtained through considering the effect of internal pressure P on deformation and strain of the material, the volume average stress–strain relationship can be expressed as,

$${\overline {{\theta}}} = K ( {\overline {{\theta}}} ) \left( \theta - {\overline {{\theta}}} \right)\tag{ð12Þ}$$

where $K ( {\overline {{\theta}}} )$ corresponds to the bulk modulus of the material without internal pressure. Accordingly, the effective bulk modulus of phase

component materials containing solid and gas inclusions with internal pressure is given by the following expression.

$$\overline {{K}} = K ( \overline {{\theta}} ) \big ( 1 + \overline {{\theta}} \big )\tag{ð13Þ}$$

In addition, in order to calculate the temperature-dependent $K ^ {\mathrm{virg}} ,$ the following degradation function is modified and used, which was proposed by Dimitrienko [13,15].

$$K ^ {\mathrm{virg}} ( T , t ) = K _ {\mathrm{soften}} ^ {\mathrm{virg}} \exp \left( - a \left\{\frac {T - T _ {0}} {T _ {0}} + \xi \int _ {0} ^ {t} \exp [ - \zeta ( t - \tau ) ] \frac {T ( \tau ) - T _ {0}} {T _ {0}} d \tau \right\} \right)\tag{ð14Þ}$$

where the constants a, ξ and $\zeta$ are determined in experiments from the dependence of the elasticity modulus on temperature [15]. $K _ {\mathrm{soffen}} ^ {\mathrm{virg}}$ is the bulk modulus of phase component material when the thermal softening is completed.

## 3.2.2. Stiffness properties of FRP composites

FRP composite materials can be considered as two-phase media consisted of matrix phase (m) and fiber phase (f). After calculating the effective properties of matrix materials and reinforced fibers, the high-temperature effective properties of the composite material can be obtained by using the Mori–Tanaka method. The static equilibrium relationship of the material is given as,

$$\int _ {V ^ {\mathrm{m}}} \overline {{K}} ^ {\mathrm{m}} \tilde {\theta} d V + \int _ {V ^ {\mathrm{f}}} \overline {{K}} ^ {\mathrm{f}} \left( \tilde {\theta} + \theta ^ {1} \right) d V = 0 .\tag{ð15Þ}$$

The effective elasticity properties of FRP composites under high temperatures can be determined from Eq. (15).

## 4. Results and discussion

## 4.1. Stiffness degradation of matrix, fiber and silica/phenolic composites

The measured results for compression modulus of the silica/phenolic composites at key temperature points are illustrated in Table 1. The modulus values in Table 1 were determined by averaging the three or five tested values for each temperature point. These experimental compressive modulus values are reasonable when compared to the through-thickness compressive properties of fiberglass polymer composites reported in Ref. [29–31]. Fig. 3 presents the compression modulus-temperature plot. It can be seen that the compression modulus of silica/phenolic composites decreases with increasing thermal exposure temperature, which is caused by the degradation of the matrix property, fiber property and fiber-matrix interface property at high temperatures.

The variations of volume fraction of each phase media for phenolic resin with temperature are predicted by the physical model in mesomechanical scale and illustrated in Fig. 4. As the pyrolysis reactions proceed, the resin polymer phase transfers into char phase and gas phase, which results in decrease of the volume content of polymer phase and increase of volume contents of char phase and gas phase. However, the sum of volume contents of these three phases remains a constant at any time in pyrolysis reactions. At the final stage, the polymer phase does not exist in the ablative material and volume contents of char phase and gas phase eventually approach a constant value because of the complete carbonization of the resin polymer. Fig. 5 is the dependence of volume fraction of each phase media in silica fiber on temperature. The amorphous solid phase transfers into crystalline solid phase during the phase transition of silica fibers. Although the volume fraction of amorphous phase decreases and volume fraction of crystalline phase increases, the density of silica fibers remains practically constant up to the melting point $( 1800~^{\circ}\mathrm{C})$ , as there are no mass loss [15].

The basic material properties of silica/phenolic composites are listed in Table 2. The internal pressure during volume ablation can be calculated by the one-dimensional thermal response model in our work [33]. In order to obtain the evident effect of gas pressure on elastic property of the material, the peak pore pressure (6.5 MPa) was applied in the formula of the proposed model, which should change with thermal exposure time. The bulk modulus of decomposition gases was considered in this paper by referring to the value of atmosphere at adiabatic condition.

Using the material properties presented in Table 2, the mesomechanical model in Section 3.2 was used to calculate the temperature-dependent effective elasticity properties of phase component materials, as shown in Fig. 6. It is observed that the loss in elastic modulus of phenolic resin is quite small at the early stage of pyrolysis reaction, while the elastic modulus is reduced drastically at $400-650~^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ which corresponds to the intensive thermal decomposition because the residual mass of the material decreases significantly with increasing thermal exposure temperature [32]. When the pyrolysis reaction is completed $( 800~^{\circ}\mathrm{C}) ,$ , the elastic modulus of phenolic resin does not reduce to zero, but approaches a constant value. The reason is that the carbonaceous char is residual during the pyrolysis reaction of phenolic resin corresponding to the char phase as shown in Fig. 4. In the lower temperature range, the elasticity property of silica fiber remains practically constant. Once the material reaches sufficiently high temperature $( 500-600~^{\circ}\mathrm{C})$ , the phase transition of silica fiber occurs, which results in the reduction to the elastic modulus of reinforced fiber material. Similarly to phenolic resin, the residual properties of silica fiber also approach a really small value at about $1400~^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ In addition, it should be noted that in Fig. 6 the normalized elastic modulus is defined as the stiffness at elevated temperature divided by the stiffness measured at room temperature.

Fig. 7 compares the measured elastic modulus with the calculated temperature-modulus curve using the proposed model in Section 3 for silica/phenolic composite materials. It is clear that the elastic modulus of FRP composite rapidly degrades at $65-180~^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ due to thermal softening, and the loss in stiffness property of the material is about 52.3% at this stage. The pyrolysis reaction of the matrix material tends to reduce the effective properties, and the elastic modulus of FRP composites follows a gradual degradation starting from 180 °C and ending at $500~^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ As the temperature is raised further, the elastic modulus decreases strongly again due to the intensive thermal decomposition of the matrix material and phase transition of reinforced fiber. It is also revealed from Fig. 7 that there is reasonable agreement between the model predictions and experimental results, which can assess the accuracy of the proposed model. Nevertheless, the discrepancies which do exist between the calculated and experimental results at temperatures higher than $550~^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ are thought to be due to the fact that the elastic modulus of crystalline solid phase was underestimated in the model.

## 4.2. Fracture morphology analysis

Fig. 8 shows the fracture morphologies of silica/phenolic composite specimen after high-temperature compression experiment. It can be seen from the figure that the failure modes of the composite specimens are similar at different temperature environments. Also, it is possible to visually observe the color transition in specimens with increasing temperature. The silver silica fibers are evident at the specimen surface at 800 °C because the residual char was completely consumed by the oxidation reaction.

## 4.3. Compression modeling of silica/phenolic composite exposed to one-sided heating

To investigate stiffness properties of FRP composites simultaneously exposed to one-sided heating and compression loading, a thermalmechanical analysis is required. Fig. 9 presents the geometric model and load conditions of the solving problem. It is assumed that one side of the specimen is evenly heated at a constant heat flux, as shown schematically in Fig. 9. The problem also assumes that a static compression loading is applied in the lengthwise direction of the specimen. With these assumptions, the FRP composites can be modeled as a onedimensional system where the temperature and compression modulus vary only in the heating (x) direction.

Thermal analyses are performed numerically using a onedimensional thermal response model in our previous work [33] in which heat and mass transfer processes during radiation heating of FRP composites are considered. The temperature distributions of the material in heating direction can be obtained. Thus, elastic modulus of the composite at the spatial locations in x direction which corresponding to different temperature can be calculated using the proposed model in Section 3. Once the modulus at various locations is known, the residual stiffness can be determined for each time interval by integrating through-the-thickness (x direction) using Simpson integration method [25–27].

Fig. 10 is a variation of residual stiffness of silica/phenolic composite versus heating time when exposed to one-sided heat flux and compression loading. As expected, it reveals that the residual stiffness of the composite decreases with increasing heating time. Comparison of prediction profiles at $100 \mathrm{~kW} / \mathrm{m} ^ {2}$ and at $200 \mathrm{\kW/m} ^ {2}$ shows that the more intense the applied heat flux is, the more drastically the residual stiffness degrades. Moreover, the residual stiffness of silica/phenolic composite decreases to a minimum value over a period of about 140 s at 200 $\mathrm{kW/m} ^ {2}$

## 5. Conclusions

The mathematical formulas for the mesomechanical model were established to predict the degraded stiffness properties at high temperatures. Thermal softening, viscoelastic creep and thermal decomposition of matrix material can cause the reduction to the mechanical properties of FRP composites under high temperatures. These effects were considered in the proposed model. Comparing the numerical and experimental results of elastic modulus, the proposed model can satisfactorily predict the temperature-dependent effective properties of FRP composites. When temperatures are around the glass transition temperature of the polymer matrix, the loss in stiffness properties of FRP composites is largely dependent on the thermal softening effect. The elastic modulus of the material rapidly degrades at $65-180~^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ . At higher temperatures, the degradation in stiffness properties of the composite material occurs due to the thermal decomposition of polymer matrix and phase transition of reinforced fibers. The elastic modulus of FRP composites decreases drastically again at $500-1000~^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$
