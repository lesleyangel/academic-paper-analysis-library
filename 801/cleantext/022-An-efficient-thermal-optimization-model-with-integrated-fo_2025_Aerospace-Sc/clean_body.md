## 1. Introduction

High-speed flight vehicles generate large amounts of aerodynamic heat during long-time flights. The surface thermal radiation can only dissipate part of the heat, and it is essential to manage the considerable heat that enters the airframe, especially the heat in high-temperature regions that needs to be transported to low-temperature regions within a very short time. Such type of heat transport system usually arranges paths in specific directions in the heat concentration region, and adopts high thermal conductivity materials, heat pipes or convection cooling pipes as media to realize rapid heat transport.

Traditional high thermal conductivity material includes Al- and Cubased composites, and the effective thermal conductivities of Si/Al, SiC/

Al, Diamond/Al, Graphite/Al, Diamond/Cu and graphite/Cu composites reach 120–140 (W⋅m− 1 ⋅K− 1 ) [1], 140–200 $( \bar {\mathsf{W}} {\cdot} \bar {\mathsf{m}} ^ {- 1} {\cdot} \bar {\mathsf{K}} ^ {- 1} )$ [2,3], 200 $( \mathbf {W} {\cdot} \mathbf {m} ^ {- 1} {\cdot} \mathbf {K} ^ {- 1} )$ [4], 400 $( \mathbf {W} {\cdot} \mathbf {m} ^ {- 1} {\cdot} \mathbf {K} ^ {- 1} )$ [5], 700–900 $( \mathbf {W} {\cdot} \mathbf {m} ^ {- 1} {\cdot} \mathbf {K} ^ {- 1} )$ [6,7] and 600–700 $( \mathsf{W} {\cdot} \mathsf{m} ^ {- 1} {\cdot} \mathsf{K} ^ {- 1} ) \ [ 8 , 9 ]$ , respectively. However, the high density and low service temperature of above metal-based composites lead to their limited application in flight vehicles. The carbon fiber reinforced C/C and C/SiC composites have lower density and higher service temperature, and at the same time, due to the super high thermal conductivity of pitch-based carbon fiber (1000 (W⋅m− 1 ⋅K− 1 ) [10]), the effective thermal conductivities of 1D C/C, 2D C/C, 3D C/C and C/SiC composites reach 500–900 (W⋅m− 1 ⋅K− 1 ) [11–13], $300-500 ( \mathrm {W {\cdot} m ^ {- 1} {\cdot} K ^ {- 1}} )$ [14] and 200–400(W $\cdot \mathbf {m} ^ {- 1} \cdot \mathbf {K} ^ {- 1} )$ [15,16] and 150–350 $( \mathbf {W} {\cdot} \mathbf {m} ^ {- 1} {\cdot} \mathbf {K} ^ {- 1} )$ [17,18], respectively, thus can be adopted as the heat transport medium under high-temperature conditions. The concept of heat pipe, proposed by Grover et al. [19] in 1963, is a heat transfer element with extremely high thermal conductivity, which realizes rapid heat transport through the phase change and flow of the cooling medium, and the equivalent thermal conductivity can be high as 1000 to 20,000 $( \mathbf {W} {\cdot} \mathbf {m} ^ {- 1} {\cdot} \mathbf {K} ^ {- 1} )$ [20–22]. The heat pipe is widely tried as the thermal management solutions for the wing leading edges of the space shuttle [23,24] and other typical high-speed vehicles [25–29]. The convection cooling transports the heat by the cooling medium flow, and it is almost considered in all types of high-speed flight vehicles, for instances, the liquid hydrogen is used as the cooling medium in NASP [30], the metal- and composites-based cooling scheme is designed for a sharp leading edge by NASA [31], a thermal cycle based on the liquid hydrogen cooling is designed in MR-2 by ESA [32,33], and a heat transport system based on the water cooling is designed for the leading edge of SpaceLiner by DLR [34].

In above studies, the heat transport path is designed considering the aerodynamic heating effect, the force transfer path thus has to be designed separately considering the aerodynamic pressure effect, and leads to excessive redundancy of the total system. For high-speed flight vehicles, the integrated heat-force design can improve the path efficiency and reduce the system mass in theory. However, the heat and force path are closely dependent on aerodynamic heat and pressure distributions, respectively, thus the efficient integrated path should be obtained based on massive calculations and corresponding optimizations.

The path optimization consists of several sequential stages according to the variable hierarchies, and the topology is always optimized in the first stage. Topology optimization can be classified into continuous or discrete depending on the type of variables. For the continuous optimization [35–38], a density-like variable with value between 0 and 1 is used to represent the path presence or absence, and the value close to 1 indicates the presence while close to 0 indicates the absence. A large number of gray elements with median values will be created in such optimization process, thus very complex post-processing is essential. In practical flight vehicles, the rough heat path and force path are usually pre-determined by the vehicles’ configuration and primary structures, thus, the path topology optimization is always accomplished by creating a Ground structure containing all the possible paths and following path delete according to certain rules. Such Ground path method is widely used in the optimization of beam- and truss-type force-bearing structures [39,40], while the application in heat path and heat-force integrated path is very limited. After the topology optimization, the optimization of additional hierarchies, e.g., the path profile size and shape should be further conducted to obtain better results. However, the mutual influence among different hierarchies of variables is not considered in such sequential optimization method, and the so-called hybrid optimization method is a more effective alternate [41,42]. In this work, variables of different types, e.g., the discrete topology and continuous size are involved, thus, the key of hybrid optimization method is to form the unified expression $( \boldsymbol {\mathrm{e.g.}}$ , binary) of multiple variables to optimize them simultaneously.

The path optimization is also highly dependent on the optimization algorithm. Each optimization problem corresponds to a specific mathematic model which should be solved by the optimization algorithm. Generally, the optimization algorithm can be classified into local and global types, the former converges quickly but is prone to local optimal solutions, thus, in this work the genetic algorithm which is a widely used global optimization algorithm is adopted. The genetic algorithm is inspired by the population evolution in nature, $\mathrm{i.e.,}$ better genes are inherited or created by a series of selection, crossover and mutation processes. However, the population should contain plentiful enough individuals to acquire optimal results, thus the population size is always large and leads to enormous computational cost, especially for problems with variables of multiple hierarchies. Thus, a fast and effective genetic optimization algorithm with well individual screening and population size reduction strategy is essential.

In order to overcome the complex and time-consuming thermal optimization problem for high-speed flight wings due to the inconsistent heat-force transfer paths and multi-hierarchy path variables. In this work, an efficient thermal optimization model with integrated force paths, fully-decomposed hierarchies and hybrid genetic operations is developed. The path variable is fully decomposed into six hierarchies of topology, angle, heat width, force width, width factor and height factor to form a great optimization space; a hybrid strategy based on the genetic algorithm is proposed to consider the complex relevance and conduct the simultaneous optimization of multi-hierarchy variables; an excellent population pre-evaluation and individual screening strategy is constructed to improve the convergence speed; and the integrated heatforce system is finally optimized with objectives of the minimum path mass and aeroelastic influence quantity.

## 2. The integrated heat-force path and fully decomposed variable hierarchies

For a high-speed vehicle, the well-designed heat transport path will provide a more uniform temperature distribution and results into low thermal stress on the force path. However, inconsistent heat and force paths are usually achieved separately according to different objectives and constraints due to the different loading and transfer mechanism. Such non-integrated optimization will definitely lead to a high redundancy design, especially when I-type force path is adopted and the heat transport path can be easily integrated into its two web sides. In this work, the integrated heat-force path is pre-designed for a high-speed wing, then an efficient integrated optimization model is developed and the optimal system performance is achieved.

Fig. 1 shows the radial heat-force integrated path of the wing, and Fig. 1(a) and (b) are the top and profile views, respectively. As shown in Fig. 1(a), the wing has a root chord length of about 1000 mm, a tip chord length of about 360 mm and a spread length of about 540 mm. The radial black lines in the in-plane direction represent the integrated path, along which the aerodynamic heat and force are transferred to the heat sink and force axle, respectively. Fig. 1(b) shows the profile of the wing, in which black, red, yellow and pink parts represent the force transfer path, heat transport path, skin, and external thermal insulation layer, respectively; it is clear that the integrated path is formed based on the Itype force path and the web side rectangular heat path. In this work, it is assumed that the heat transport path consists of high thermal conductivity materials.

Normally, a greater optimization space will provide better results. The optimization space is closely dependent on the variable decomposition. Fig. 2(a) to (d) show the variable decomposition process, the left and right ones are the top and profile ones, respectively, and the red box indicates the existed hierarchy while the blue one indicates the newly decomposed hierarchy. The variable is decomposed from two hierarchies, i.e., topology XT and width $X _ {W}$ to six hierarchies, i.e., topoplogy $X _ {T} ,$ angle $X _ {A} ,$ force width $X _ {W} ,$ heat width $X _ {W H} ,$ height factor $X _ {H F}$ and width factor $X _ {W F}$ Additionally, in those figures H, $X _ {W H}$ XHH and XWW indicate the force path height, heat path width, heat path height and web width, respectively. Among above variables, force path height H is determined by the aerodynamic configuration of the wing, and the heat path is uniformly located on the two sides with width of $X _ {W H} / 2$ for each side, thus, the independent optimization variables are topology $X _ {T} ,$ angle $X _ {A} ,$ force width $X _ {W} ,$ heat width $X _ {W H} ,$ width factor $X _ {W F}$ and height factor $X _ {H F}$ . The two shape variables, i.e., width factor and height factor in Fig. 2(c) and (d) can be described as Eq. (1):

$$X _ {W F} = X _ {W W} / X _ {W}$$

$$X _ {H F} = X _ {H H} / H\tag{1}$$

As depicted in Fig. 2(a), in conventional optimization, the path topology and width are the two commonly decomposed variable hierarchies, in which the topology indicates the path presence or absence, while the width is the path in-plane size (the out-plane size is determined by the wing configuration). In conventional structural optimization, the optimal topology is obtained by deleting certain paths according to certain rules and no path is added, thus as a result the preset Ground path should be dense enough to find the optimal solution. During the process, the included angle between neighbouring paths remains unchanged (no path deleted) or increased (path deleted) after the optimization. In order to overcome such disadvantage, the angle is decomposed as a variable hierarchy as shown in Fig. 2(b) in this work and its value variation can be used to simulate the path adding. The path width in Fig. 2(a) indicates the in-plane size of path for which the profile shape is assumed as rectangle. However, in real vehicles the I-shape profile is commonly adopted to save the structural mass, thus in this work the width and height factors are decomposed as additional variable hierarchies as shown in Fig. 2(c) to consider the path I-shape effect. Finally, the heat width is considered and the path variable is fully decomposed into six hierarchies as shown in Fig. 2(d). The two-, three-, five-hierarchy as shown in Fig. 2(a), (b) and (c) are the variable decomposition of force-only path, while the six-hierarchy in Fig. 2(d) is that of heat-force integrated path. In this work, the three-, five- and sixhierarchy models are constructed to verify the effectiveness of the variable hierarchy decomposition, and it should be noted that the number of optimization variable for each model equals to the path number multiplied by three, five and six, respectively. It is clear that the topology variable is the discrete type while the angle, width and shape factors are continuous ones.

Take six-hierarchy model for instance, the chromosome of each individual involves six segments, which corresponding to each variable hierarchy, i.e., topology, angle, force width, heat width, width factor and height factor, as shown in Eq. (2):

$$\begin{array} {l} {{\displaystyle X _ {T 1} , \cdots , X _ {T n} | X _ {A 1} , \cdots , X _ {A n} | X _ {W 1} , \cdots , X _ {W n} | X _ {W n 1} , \cdots , X _ {W n} | X _ {W 1} , \cdots , X _ {W n} | X _ {H P 1} , \cdots , X _ {H P n}}} \\ {{\displaystyle X _ {T n} \in \{0 , 1 \} , X _ {A i} = \{a _ {1} , \cdots , a _ {i n} \} , X _ {W n} = \{b _ {1} , \cdots , b _ {i n} \} , X _ {W n} = \{c _ {i 1} , \cdots , c _ {i n} \} , X _ {W n} = \{d _ {i 1} , \cdots , d _ {i n} \} , X _ {H F} = \{e _ {i 1} , \cdots , e _ {i n} \}}} \\ {{\displaystyle a _ {i j} \in \{0 , 1 \} , b _ {i j} \in \{0 , 1 \} , c _ {i j} \in \{0 , 1 \} , d _ {i j} \in \{0 , 1 \} , e _ {i j} \in \{0 , 1 \}}} \\ {{\displaystyle i = 1 , 2 , \cdots , n \quad j = 1 , 2 , \cdots , m}} \end{array}\tag{2}$$

As discussed above, a greater optimization space will give better results, however, it will also lead to higher optimization cost. For optimization problems with five or six hierarchies of variables, the conventional sequential strategy would be impossible to obtain the optimal solutions, especially when the intricate relevance exists between different hierarchies. Therefore, the hybrid optimization strategy is a better alternative. In addition, the fully decomposed variable hierarchies lead to a large optimization space with a large number of variables, so efficient cost reduction is essential to solve such optimization problems.

## 3. Hybrid genetic optimization algorithm and population reduction method

## 3.1. Unified chromosome encoding of discrete and continuous variables

In genetic algorithm, the selection, crossover and mutation of individuals are operated based on the chromosome. Encoding is the process of mapping the optimization variables to a chromosome string with specific format. The binary encoding method can be used to uniformly describe the discrete and continuous variables since its value form is consistent with that of the discrete topology variables, i.e., 0 or 1. However, the natural binary code may lead to Hamming cliffs for the continuous variables, thus the Gray code is finally adopted in this work.

where $a _ {i j} , b _ {i j} , c _ {i j} , d _ {i j}$ and $e _ {i j}$ are the Gray codes of path angle, force width, heat width, width factor and height factor, respectively; n is the path number and m is the code width. From Eq. (2) one can find that during the optimization, $X _ {T i}$ is expressed as 0 or 1, which represent the absence or presence of the integrated path, respectively; $X _ {A} ,$ , XW, XWH, XWF and $X _ {H F}$ are indicated as strings composed by 0 and 1, and their real values can be obtained after certain deconding process. The hybrid optimization of different hierarchies can then be realized based on such unified encoding for discrete and continuous variables. It also should be noted that each chromosome segment is encoded by the variable value of corresponding hierarchy for all the paths, thus a very long string is always involved, especially for hierarchies of angle, force width, heat width, width factor and height factor.

## 3.2. Hybrid genetic operators of multi-hierarchy variables

The genetic algorithm includes three types of operators as shown in Fig. 3 from top to bottom, i.e., the selection of the fittest individuals, the crossover of selected individuals and the mutation of selected individuals, which correspond to the fittest survival, the individuals mating and the random modification in the natural evolution process, respectively, and are also visualised by several $\mathbf {\ddot {x}} ^ {,}$ chromosomes in the left part of Fig. 3. For the optimization of so many variable hierarchies, the key issue is to take into account the complex relevance between different hierarchies, which means that the genetic operation of these hierarchies should be conducted simultaneously rather than sequentially. Meanwhile, the crossover process which involves two different individuals should avoid the mismatched gene exchange, thus, each chromosome is designed to have six non-interfering segments that can be selected, crossovered and mutated simultaneously.

As shown in the upper part of Fig. 3, an individual indicates an integrated heat-force system with certain path topology, angle, force width, heat width, width factor and height factor, and the chromosome of each individual has six corresponding gene segments (take sixhierarchy model for instance). The selection of fittest individuals is based on the fitness value of each individual, and the stochastic universal sampling method which can keep relatively inferior individuals and higher efficiency is adopted in this work. In this work, the individual fitness is described by Eq. (3):

$$F ( N _ {i} ) = 2 - S P + 2 ( S P - 1 ) \frac {N _ {i} - 1} {N - 1}\tag{3}$$

where $N _ {i}$ represents the order number of the ith individual in the population, N is the total individual number in the population, and the individual order is specified according to its value of objective function; SP is the selective pressure with assumed value of $^ {2 ,}$ which indicates the bias towards the fittest individual. In this work, the objective function is the minimum system mass or the minimum aeroelastic influence quantity which will be discussed in the following sections.

As shown in the middle part of Fig. 3, the crossover of selected individuals is the exchange of genes (chromosome values) between two different individuals up to certain crossover points (black droplet arrows), specifically, the right part of genes (indicated by the dark yellow dashed rectangle) up to the crossover point exchanged to each other. For the multiple variables problem of this work, genes should be exchanged between the same segments (i.e., variables) of different individuals. In this work, a crossover point is randomly specified for each chromosome segment with a certain probability, six independent crossover processes are then conducted to avoid the mismatched gene exchange. The onepoint crossover operator with crossover rate of 0.8 is adopted for each variable. Taking Individual 1 and Individual 2 as an instance, the crossover operator will create Child 1 and Child 2.

As shown in the lower figure of Fig. 3, the mutation of an individual is the inversion of gene values (0 to 1 or 1 to 0) with certain low probability and the mutation bit is indicated by dark yellow dashed rectangle. The Simple mutation method with the bit mutation probability of 0.7 divided by the chromosome length is adopted in this work. The mutations of Child 1 and Child 2 will finally create New Individual 1 and New Individual 2.

During the above processes, the path and profile variables, i. e., topology, angle, force width, heat width, width factor and height factor are selected, crossovered and mutated simultaneously, which indicates a hybrid optimization of multi-hierarchy variables.

## 3.3. The population size reduction method

In the optimization of this work, take six-hierarchy model for instance, six hierarchies of variables are involved for each path and 12 paths are pre-specified in the Gound path. Such large number of variables leads to long chromosome, great population size and high optimization cost; thus, certain population size reduction is essential for a feasible optimization model. The surrogate model is one of the widely used population reduction method, in which a mathematical model with a finite number of samples is constructed to replace the original highprecision model. In this work, the Radial Basis Function Neural Network (RBFNN) surrogate model is adopted for the five- and sixhierarchy models due to its great capacity in high-dimensional optimization problems.

The RBFNN consists of input, hidden and output layers, which correspond to the data input, feature extraction and prediction output, respectively. In the hidden layer, the radial basis function is used as the activation function of the neurons, and the widely used Gauss function is adopted as the kernel function as shown in Eq. (4).

$$h _ {j} ( \pmb {x} ) = \exp \left( - \frac {\| \pmb {x} - \pmb {c} _ {j} \| ^ {2}} {2 \sigma _ {j} ^ {2}} \right)$$

$$\parallel \pmb {x} - \pmb {c} _ {j} \parallel ^ {2} = \sum _ {i = 1} ^ {n} \left( \pmb {x} _ {i} - \pmb {c} _ {j i} \right) ^ {2}$$

$$\pmb {c} _ {j} = \left[ c _ {j 1} , c _ {j 2} , . . . , c _ {j n} \right] ^ {\mathrm{T}}\tag{4}$$

where x is the input vector, subscript j indicates the jth neuron of hidden layer, hj(x) is the jth radial basis function, and cj and σj are the center and

width of the radial basis, respectively.

The final output approximate function can be described as:

$$\begin{array} {l} {{\displaystyle y _ {k} ( {\pmb x} ) = \sum _ {j = 1} ^ {J} w _ {j k} h _ {j} ( {\pmb x} )}} \\ {{\displaystyle j = 1 , ~ 2 , J}} \\ {{\displaystyle k = 1 , ~ 2 , K}} \end{array}\tag{5}$$

where J and K are the neuron number of hidden and output layers, respectively; wjk is the connection weight from the jth neuron of hidden layer to the kth neuron of output layer.

In this work, the input and output of the RBFNN are the value of the variables and the fitness value of the individuals, respectively. The RBFNN is used to pre-evaluate and screen the individuals, i.e., the individuals are firstly ordered by their fitness values that predicted by RBFNN model, a certain amount (assumed to be 50 % in this work) of individuals with low fitness values are then eliminated and a new population of smaller size is thus finally created.

## 3.4. The optimization process

Based on the above methods, an effective multi-hierarchy hybrid optimization process can be summarized as Fig. 4. Five steps of population initialization, genetic operation, population reduction, path analysis and convergence judgement are involved in the process:

(1) Population initialization: a population with specified number of individuals is created by certain arbitrary string generations based on the encoding rules and the Ground path.

(2) Genetic operation: 1st, individuals are selected based on the fitness values to form the paraent population; 2nd, the crossover and mutation of the topology, angle, force width, heat width, width factor and height factor are conducted to create new individuals; 3rd, the new offspring population with new individuals is formed.

(3) Population reduction: 1st, the RBFNN model is established based on the sample database which are formed by massive thermomechanical numerical simulations; 2nd, the fitness values of the new population are predicted by the RBFNN model, and the individuals are pre-evaluated and re-ordered by their fitness values; 3rd, a certain amount of individuals with lower fitness values are eliminated to form a population of smaller size.

(4) Path analysis: 1st, the geometric and numerical models of the heat-forth integrated path are created based on the variables’ values and thermomechanical boundary conditions; 2nd, the heat and force transfer analyses are performed, and the accurate fitness value, objective function and constraint values of each individual are obtained.

(5) Convergence judgement: 1st, the accurate fitness evaluation of the population is conducted, and meanwhile the individual with largest deviation in comparison with the RBFNN model is added into the sample database; 2nd, the convergence judgement is made based on the objective functions and constraints; if it is converged, the optimal path is then obtained, if it is not converged, the present population can be adopted as the initial population to continue the genetic optimization.

It should be noted that, during the above steps, the individual fitness is calculated twice: the first calculation is performed for the original large size population by the RBFNN model, and the new smaller size population is created based on such a fast individual pre-evaluation; the second calculation is performed for the smaller size population by the numerical analysis, and the individuals are finally evaluated based on such relatively accurate fitness values.

## 4.1.2. Governing equations

In this work, two types of objective functions, i.e., the minimum mass and minimum aeroelastic influence quantity are adopted to optimize the path. For the minimum mass problem, the heat conduction and statics processes are considered to evaluate the path performance. The aerodynamic heat is transported from the high-temperature region to the heat sink by thermal conduction in integrated paths. The governing equation of steady-state thermal conduction is shown in Eq. (6):

$$d i \nu ( \lambda \mathbf {g r a d} T ) = 0\tag{6}$$

where T is the temperature, λ is the thermal conductivity.

After the thermal conduction calculation, the temperature fields are used as the input of mechanical analysis with equilibrium and geometric equations shown in Eqs. (7) and (8), respectively.

$$\frac {\partial \sigma _ {x x}} {\partial x} + \frac {\partial \tau _ {x y}} {\partial y} + \frac {\partial \tau _ {x z}} {\partial z} = 0 ; ~ \frac {\partial \tau _ {y x}} {\partial x} + \frac {\partial \sigma _ {y y}} {\partial y} + \frac {\partial \tau _ {y z}} {\partial z} = 0 ; ~ \frac {\partial \tau _ {z x}} {\partial x} + \frac {\partial \tau _ {z y}} {\partial y} + \frac {\partial \sigma _ {z z}} {\partial z} = 0\tag{7}$$

$${\varepsilon} _ {x} = {\frac {\partial u} {\partial x}} ; \ {\varepsilon} _ {y} = {\frac {\partial v} {\partial y}} ; \ {\varepsilon} _ {z} = {\frac {\partial w} {\partial z}} ; \ {\gamma} _ {y z} = {\frac {\partial v} {\partial z}} + {\frac {\partial w} {\partial y}} ; \ {\gamma} _ {x z} = {\frac {\partial u} {\partial z}} + {\frac {\partial w} {\partial x}} ; \ {\gamma} _ {x y} = {\frac {\partial u} {\partial y}} + {\frac {\partial v} {\partial x}}\tag{8}$$

where $\sigma _ {x x} , \tau _ {x y} . .$ are stress components, $, \varepsilon _ {x} , \gamma _ {y z} \ldots$ are strain components, and u, v and w are displacement components, respectively.

The path strain can be described by Eq. (9):

$$\varepsilon _ {i} = \varepsilon _ {i} ^ {m} + \alpha _ {i} \Delta T\tag{9}$$

where εm represents the strain induced by aerodynamic force, αi is the thermal expansion coefficient in the i th direction, and ΔT is the temperature difference which can be obtained by the temperature fields. The second term of Eq. (9) is the thermal stress.

For the static aeroelastic simulation, the ZONA51 theory based panel method and the thin plate spline interpolation method are adopted to calculate the aerodynamic force and realize the data transfer between aerodynamic and structural grids, respectively. The relationship of aerodynamic pressure coefficients and downwash can be described as Eq. (11):

$$\{W \} = \{D \} \{C _ {p} \}\tag{10}$$

where $\{W \}$ is the downwash that dependent on the attack angle and wing surface displacement; [D] is the aerodynamic influence coefficient matrix that related with the Mach number; $\left\{C _ {p} \right\}$ is the aerodynamic pressure coefficient. The lift curve slope can then be obtained by solving Eq. (10).

## 4.1.3. Boundary conditions

For the thermal conduction calculation, the aerodynamic heat flux is applied on the wing surface and the temperature of the heat sink is assumed to be 300 K. For the mechanical analysis, the aerodynamic pressure is imposed on the wing surface, the nodal temperature that calculated in thermal conduction analysis is applied as the thermal load, and the displacement of the force axle $( \mathrm{i.e.,}$ heat sink) is assumed to be 0.

In this work, the aerodynamic heat and pressure under conditions of speed of 4Ma, attack angle of 10◦, altitude of 15 km are calculated based on the commercial software Fluent. The aerodynamic heat flux of the windward region is about $350 \mathrm{kW/m} ^ {2} ;$ , that of leeward region is about 10 $\mathrm{kW/m} ^ {2}$ , and the highest value of about 1200 $\mathrm {k W / m ^ {2}}$ appears at the wing leading edge. The pressure field has similar distribution, i.e., the pressure of the windward region is about 35 kPa and higher than the leeward region of about 5 kPa, and the highest pressure of about 65 kPa appears at the wing leading edge.

## 4.2. Mathematical description of optimization model

Take the six-hierarchy model for instance, the mathematical model of the minimum mass optimization problem is shown in $\operatorname{Eq.}$ (11):

Find $X _ {T} , X _ {A} , X _ {W} , X _ {W H} , X _ {W F} , X _ {H F}$

## 5. Results and discussions

## 5.1. The path mass minimum problem

## 5.1.1. The force-only path optimization

In order to verify the effectiveness of integrated path optimization, the force-only path optimization is conducted. The aerodynamic heat is assumed to be resisted by the thermal insulation layer and radiatively dissipated into the outer space, thus the thermal stress is not considered. In the optimization, only the stress and displacement constraints are considered and the heat mass is not included in the objective function. The optimization cost is determined by the number of individuals in a population, in this work, three optimization cases are conducted as listed in Table 3 to confirm the population reduction strategy. As shown in Table 3, the O40, O20 and O40-R20 indicate cases of 40 original individuals without population reduction, 20 original individuals without population reduction, and 40 original individuals with 20 remained individuals, respectively, in which the first one is adopted as the benchmark case. It is clear that the time consuming of each generation is equally for O20 and O40-R20, while that of O40 is about the double of O20 or O40-R20. In general, the number of individuals for a population should be specified considering the number of variables, i.e., the front one should be larger than the half value of the latter one to an appropriate extent. In these cases, XWH is not considered as the optimization variable, thus, 60 variables (12×5) are involved totally and the number of original individuals for the benchmark case is assumed to be 40. For the problem with heat transfer, 72 variables are involved in total thus the number of original individuals is assumbed to be 50.

Fig. 6 shows the optimization process in which the black solid line, dashed line and dotted line indicate the O40-R20, O40 and O20 models, respectively, while the blue solid line and blue dotted line show the relative deviation of O40-R20 and O20 models to O40 model, respectively. From Fig. 6 one can find that: the objective of all the models decrease sharply at the initial stage and then the trade slows down; at the initial stage, the O40 model obtains smaller mass and thus achieves better results than O40-R20 and O20 models, while the latter two models have intersecting lines and thus similar optimization effectiveness; for the O40-R20 model, the mass decreases from 2.05 kg to 1.14 kg with the generation increases from 1 to 30 (the first red drop line), further decreases to 0.77 kg when the generation increases to 51 (the second red drop line), and after that generation the O40-R20 obtains smaller mass than O20 and approximate values with O40; when the optimization generation proceeds to 130, O40-R20 and O40 obtain almost the same value (0.647 kg and 0.646 kg) with deviation of smaller than 0.2 % (the right top enlarged diagram), and the mass reductions are 71.5 % and 68.4 % in comparison with the Ground (2.274 kg) and the 1st generation (2.05 kg) path, respectively. It can be concluded that O40-R20 shows better performance than O20, and presents approximate optimization ability with O40 when the optimization proceeds to a certain generation, which indicates that 50 % reduction of optimization cost with limited effectiveness compromise is obtained by the population reduction method proposed in this work.

Table 4 lists the specific results and optimization costs of three models, and NS indicates the number of numerical simulations which determines the major part of the optimization cost. In Table 4, the O40 model with 150 generations is used as the benchmark case, one can find that the O40 model with 75 generations, the O20 model with 150 generations, and the O40-R20 model with 150 generations obtain higher masses of 4.8 %, 20.4 % and 0.2 % than the benchmark case, respectively. The O40-R20 model obtains the best results comparing with other two cases with the same optimization cost (total NS), and further reveal the great comprehensive performance of the RBFNN-based population reduction method. In the later heat-force optimizations of this work, the 50 % reduction type model, i.e., O50-R25 model is adopted.

## 6. Conclusions

In this work, a heat and force integrated path optimization model is developed for the radial-beam wing of a high-speed vehicle. The path variable is fully decomposed into six hierarchies of topology, angle, heat width, force width, width factor and height factor to form a great optimization space, and a hybrid strategy based on the genetic algorithm is proposed to consider the complex relevance and conduct the simultaneous optimization of multi-hierarchy variables. The RBF neural network is employed to reduce the population size and the optimization cost. The integrated heat-force system is then optimized with objectives of minimum mass and minimum aeroelastic influence quantity. Some conclusions are obtained:

(1) The fully-decomposed variable hierarchy results into great optimization performance. The mass reductions of five-hierarchy model compared with the two- and three-hierarchy ones are 31.6 % and 15.6 %, respectively; the aeroelastic influence quantity reductions of five-hierarchy model compared with the two- and three-hierarchy ones are 11.6 % and 6.1 %, respectively.

(2) The complex relevance among different hierarchies is effectively considered by the hybrid optimization strategy; the optimization cost with multiple-hierarchy variables is greatly reduced (50 %) with limited effectiveness compromise (0.2 %) by the developed population reduction method based on the RBF neural network.

(3) For the force-only path without consideration of aerodynamic heating effects, the path mass is 0.647 kg after the minimum mass optimization, and the relative mass reductions are 71.5 % and 68.4 % in comparsion with the Ground path and the 1st qualified path, respectively; the final aeroelastic influence quantity is 5.85 % after the minimum aeroelastic influence quantity optimization, and the relative quantity reduction is 37.6 % in comparsion with the 1st qualified path.

(4) For the heat-force integrated path, 9 paths with mass of 1.80 kg are reserved, and the relative mass reductions are 42.7 % and 35.7 % in comparsion with the Ground and the 1st qualified path, respectively, in the minimum mass optimization; the aeroelastic influence quantity is reduced by 19.3 % comparing with the 1st qualified path in the minimum aeroelastic influence quantity optimization.

(5) In comparison with the force-only path, a total mass reduction of 0.08 to1.54 kg in absolute terms and 4.1 % to 45.3 % in relative terms is obtained by the integrated heat-force path.
