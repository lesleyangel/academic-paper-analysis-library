## 1. Introduction

As human society and civilization continue to develop, the impact of the energy shortage is becoming increasingly significant. The use of fossil fuels, nuclear decay and other traditional methods of obtaining energy results in a significant loss of energy in the form of heat, which in turn exacerbates the energy crisis [1]. Thermoelectric materials have received increasing attention in recent years due to their unique ability to convert thermal energy directly into electrical energy [2]. The performance of thermoelectric materials is typically quantified by the dimensionless thermoelectric figure of merit ZT, which is expressed as ${Z T} \mathrm {{=}} S ^ {2} {\sigma T} / {\kappa}$ , where S is the Seebeck coefficient, σ is the conductivity. T is the thermodynamic temperature and κ is the thermal conductivity. In order to obtain a high ZT, the thermoelectric material is usually required to have a high S, σ, and a low κ. Nevertheless, the interconnectivity and constraints of each thermoelectric parameter render the achievement of the optimal ZT value a challenging endeavor.

Strontium titanate (SrTiO3) is a promising thermoelectric material [3], which is a metal oxide with a cubic perovskite structure and has the advantages of a high melting point and a high carrier effective mass (m\*). However, its high κ limits its application in the thermoelectric field. The common methods to enhance the thermoelectric properties of $\mathrm {{s r T i O} _ {3}} {\cdot}$ -based materials mainly include ion doping [4,5], second-phase composite [6–9] and defect engineering [10], etc. Due to the advantages of high entropy materials, such as low κ and high structural symmetry, high entropy design has attracted great interest in the field of thermoelectricity in recent years. Banerjee et al. [11], prepared a single-phase high entropy solid solution of $\mathrm {S r ( T i _ {0.2} F e _ {0.2} M o _ {0.2} N b _ {0.2} C r _ {0.2} )}$ O3. The presence of five transition elements resulted in a reduction of the κ due to the induction of more anharmonicities, which enhances phonon scattering and reduces the κ to an ultra-low value of 0.7 W/m/K at 1100 K. Lou et al. [12], successfully prepared stable single-phase $\mathrm {S r _ {0.9} L a _ {0.1} ( Z r _ {0.25} S n _ {0.25} T i _ {0.25} H f _ {0.25} ) O _ {3}}$ high entropy ceramics and filled them with $\mathbf {B i} _ {2} \mathbf {O} _ {3}$ as a sintering aid and reducing agent. Following annealing in an Ar+C atmosphere, the team observed an increase in S and a reduction in $\kappa \left( S {=} 393 \ {\mu} \mathrm{V} / \mathrm{K} , \kappa {=} 1.89 \ \mathrm{W} / \mathrm{m} / \mathrm{K} \right)$

The current approach to entropy engineering design for thermoelectric ceramics is largely focused on the high-entropy equimolar ratio composition. However, recent studies have demonstrated that the thermoelectric properties of materials do not exhibit a straightforward linear relationship with configurational entropy. In some cases, nonequimolar-ratio high-entropy materials or medium-entropy materials have been found to exhibit superior thermoelectric properties. Bi et al. [13]. synthesized a medium-entropy thermoelectric ceramic $( \mathrm{Sr} _ {1 / 3} \mathrm{Ba} _ {1 / 3} \mathrm{Ca} _ {1 / 3} ) \mathrm{TiO} _ {3}$ by solid-phase reaction using graphite layer buried burning, which produced high S due to its moderate carrier mobility resulting in flatter energy bands, thus obtaining a high $P F ( S ^ {2} {\sigma} )$ at 773 K (378.8 $\mu \mathrm{W} / \mathrm{m} / \mathrm{K} ^ {2} )$ . Zhu et al. [14]. used the conventional solid phase method to prepare $( \mathrm {C a _ {0.33} S r _ {0.33} B a _ {0.33}} ) ( \mathrm {T i _ {0.5 - x} Z r _ {0.5 - x} N b _ {x}} ) \mathrm{O} _ {3}$ in a reducing atmosphere and obtained an ultra-low glassy κ of 1.6–1.9 W/m/K from room temperature to 873 K, which is among the lowest reported κ for perovskite oxides.

Spark plasma sintering (SPS) represents an advanced sintering technology that introduces direct current (DC) pulse current into the sintering process. It features rapid heating, controllable microstructure, energy conservation, and environmental friendliness, and is often used to prepare special functional ceramics, including thermoelectric ceramics. In this work, non-equimolar high-entropy ceramics $\left[ \mathbf {C a} _ {\left( 1 - x \right) /} \right$ $\mathrm {3 {S r} ( 1 - x ) / 3 B a ( 1 - x ) / 3} {\mathrm{La}} _ {x} {\mathrm{]TiO}} _ {3}$ (CSBL) were prepared by a combination of solid-phase method and spark plasma sintering (SPS). The impact of configuration entropy variations on the microstructure and thermoelectric properties of ceramics was thoroughly investigated to clarify the relation between the thermoelectric properties and configurational entropy.

## 2. Material and methods

The general formula of the compounds is $[ \mathrm{Ca} _ {( 1 - x ) / 3} \mathrm{Sr} _ {( 1 - x ) / 3} \mathrm{Ba} _ {( 1 - x ) /}$ 3Lax]TiO3 (x=0.10, 0.13, 0.19, 0.22, 0.25, 0.40), which are designated as L10, L13, L19, L22, L25, L40. Firstly, the reagent-grade $\mathrm {C a C O _ {3}} ,$ SrCO3, Ba $\mathrm{CO} _ {3} , \mathrm{La} _ {2} \mathrm{O} _ {3} ,$ and $\mathrm{TiO} _ {2}$ were initially weighed according to the stoichiometric ratio, the powder was mixed with alcohol and ball-milled for 12 hours, then calcined in an $\mathrm {{A l} _ {2} \mathrm {{O} _ {3}}}$ crucible at 1200◦C for 4 hours. This was followed by ball-milling for a further 12 hours. The samples were then placed in the graphite mould and subjected to SPS at $1250~^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ under a vacuum environment with a pressure of 30 MPa. Finally, the samples were annealed using graphite powder for reduction under $\left( \mathsf{Ar} {+} \mathsf{C} \right)$ atmosphere at 1400 ℃.

The density (ρ) of the ceramic material was determined by the Archimedes drainage method. The phase composition was analyzed by X-ray diffraction (XRD, D8 DISCOVER). Scanning electron microscopy (SEM, GEMINI SEM) was used to observe the microstructure of the ceramics. A field emission high-resolution transmission electron microscope (TEM, Talos F200X) was used to study the microstructural features of the specimens. X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS, Axis Supra) was employed to elucidate the elemental composition and chemical valence of the ceramics. A micro confocal Raman spectrometer with a 532 nm laser (Alpha300R, WITec, Germany) was utilized to analyze the structural information of molecular vibrational and rotational energy levels in the ceramics. A thermoelectric parameter testing system (Namicro-03) was employed to concurrently assess the conductivity and Seebeck coefficient of the ceramics. The thermal diffusion coefficient (D) and specific heat capacity $( C _ {\mathrm{p}} )$ were evaluated utilizing a hightemperature laser thermal conductivity meter (LFA-1000) and a differential thermal analyzer (NETZSCH, DSC/404 C), respectively, and the total thermal conductivity $\mathrm {( \kappa _ {t o t a l} )}$ was determined by $\kappa _ {\mathrm{total}} {=} \rho D C _ {\mathrm{p}}$

## 3. Result and discussion

## 3.1. Microstructure of CSBL ceramics

In the case of ABO3-type perovskite structures, the Goldschmidt formula [15] is frequently employed in order to calculate the tolerance factor (t) with a view to predicting and characterizing the stability of the solid solution structure:

$$t = {\frac {R _ {\mathrm{A}} + R _ {0}} {{\sqrt {2}} ( R _ {\mathrm{B}} + R _ {0} )}}\tag{1}$$

where $R _ {\mathrm{A}} , R _ {\mathrm{B}}$ and $R _ {0}$ represent the radii of cations at the A and B sites and oxygen ions, respectively. The closer t is to 1, the more stable the structure is. Information on the cubic perovskite structure $\mathrm {M e T i O _ {3}}$ corresponding to $\mathrm{Ca} , \mathrm{Sr} ,$ Ba and La, respectively, is shown in Table S1, and all of them have t close to 1. Therefore, these four elements were chosen for the preparation of high-entropy ceramics. However, it is worth noting that $\mathrm {L a T i O _ {3}}$ exhibits a distorted cubic perovskite structure and Ti exhibits a substable +3 valence. Therefore, more intrinsic defects tend to exist in $\mathrm{LaTiO} _ {3} ,$ and these defects may also be reflected in high-entropy ceramics. As the A-site of the perovskite structure is occupied by a variety of cations in this work, therefore, when calculating t, the average radius A-site atoms $\overline {{R _ {\mathrm{A}}}}$ should be used in place of $R _ {\mathrm {{A}}}$ The t of the CSBL ceramic samples are presented in Table 1. It can be observed that the t decreases with the increase of x, which is due to the decrease o $\mathrm{:} \overline {{R _ {\mathrm{A}}}} ,$ The t of each component sample is close to 1. Therefore, although $\mathrm {L a ^ {3 +}}$ is a high valence ion, it is predicted that they can all form stable single-phase cubic perovskite structures. The configuration entropy of each ceramic is calculated using the following formula [16]：

$$S _ {c o n f} = \mathrm{~-~} R \sum _ {i} x _ {i} I n x _ {i}\tag{2}$$

Where R is the gas constant and $x _ {\mathrm{i}}$ is the proportion of each element. According to the calculation results, ceramics with equimolar A-site elements obtained the maximum configuration entropy, and the more deviated from equimolar components, the smaller the configuration entropy

Fig. 1(a) and (b) show the XRD patterns of sintered CSBL ceramics before and after annealing, respectively. It can be seen that the diffraction peaks are in good accordance with the standard card of perovskite $\mathrm {{s r T i O _ {3}}}$ phase (JCPDS card no: 84–0444), indicating that various elements have been incorporated into the perovskite lattice. Zooming in on the main diffraction peak around 32◦, a shift in the position of the main diffraction peak towards a large angle was observed. It is indicated that the lattice constant decreases due to the decrease in the $\overline {{R _ {A}}}$ from 1.453 Å to 1.422 Å as the lanthanum content is increased from 0.1 to 0.4. Samples are all showed pure perovskite phase even after annealing at $1400~^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ under in reduced $_ {\mathsf{Ar+C}}$ atmospheres, which demonstrates that the high entropy composition own stable perovskite phase structure.

Fig. 1(c)-(h) show the results of the Rietveld refinement of the annealed ceramics, and the $R _ {\mathrm{wp}}$ are all below 10 %, indicating the credibility of the refinement data. The structural parameters of the ceramics obtained from the refinement are shown in the Table 2, and the increased x leads to a contraction of the lattice, which is consistent with the direction of the shift of the main diffraction peak. After the highvalent $\mathrm{La} ^ {3 +}$ ion enters the low-valent A-site atomic position, charge compensation is appeared to maintain the overall electro-neutrality of the ceramics. The compensation mechanism for the substitution of the low-valent cation by $\bar {\mathrm{La}} ^ {3 +}$ in perovskite structures is mainly the A-site ion vacancy compensation mechanism [17], and the defect chemical reaction equations are shown as follows:

$$L a _ {2} O _ {3} {} 2 L a _ {A} ^ {.} + V _ {A} ^ {( \prime )} + 3 O _ {O} ^ {\times}\tag{3}$$

The densities of the CSBL ceramics before and after annealing are also shown in Table 2. It is clearly seen that the density reduced after the ceramics annealed. Fig. 2 shows the microscopic morphology of CSBL ceramics in the natural section before and after annealing, and the grain size distribution of each ceramic are summarized in Figure S1 and Figure S2. The ceramics have fine grains before annealing, with an average grain size in the range of 0.25–0.39 μm. After annealing, the average grain size increases dramatically (1.63–4.35 μm), mainly due to the high sintering driving force resulting from the high specific surface area of grains prior to annealing and the high diffusion activation energy resulting from the high annealing temperature. The delayed diffusion effect due to the high entropy design also has an impact on the microstructure of the material, both before and after annealing, the average size of the ceramics decreases with the increase in configurational entropy, leading to an increase in the density of boundaries. The SEM images of the ceramics after annealing show that the ceramics have more pronounced pores inside the ceramics, which is consistent with the results of the density tests. It is generally believed that the appearance of pores during heat treatment is mainly due to the recrystallisation, the rapid migration of grain boundaries to introduce gaseous impurities into the internal grain to form pores, but this will only lead to an uneven distribution of pores. The annealed ceramics prepared in this work have a diffuse and uniform distribution of pores, so that the pores appearing during the annealing process are caused by the aggregation of Schottky defects in the grain boundaries [18], and the higher porosity in L40 ceramics is consistent with this theory. These pores play a pinning role in the migration of grain boundaries [19], resulting in a smaller average grain size of L40 ceramics after annealing compared to other samples. 8

Fig. 3 shows the TEM analysis of L19 ceramics to characterize the microstructural properties of the perovskite CSBL high-entropy ceramics. Fig. 3(b) shows the HRTEM image taken at the thinner part of the sample edge. Fig. 3(c) presents a locally magnified HRTEM image, which illustrates that the ceramics produced in this experiment exhibit excellent crystalline properties. The crystal plane spacing measured on the (110) crystal plane is 0.279 nm, which is close to the (110) crystal plane spacing of cubic perovskite structure $\mathrm {{s r T i O _ {3}}}$ (0.276 nm, JCPDS card no: 84–0444). The entry of multiple ions into the crystal lattice results in a change to the lattice constant, which in turn causes a slight alteration to the spacing between the crystal plane. Fig. 3(d) presents the SAED image obtained along the [011] zone axis. It is indicated that the ceramic has a cubic structure with diffraction characteristic spots of the Pm-3 m space group. Additionally, superlattice diffraction spots marked by red arrows are observed, which result from the occupation of the Asite by four different cations, leading to a deformation of the neighbouring coordination environment of the $[ \mathrm{TiO} _ {6} ]$ octahedron and an inverse torsion [20]. Fig. 3(e) depicts the IFFT image of region III, which reveals a high density of edge dislocations (marked with yellow "T" symbols). It indicates the presence of microdisorder within the high-entropy ceramics. Due to the interaction between atoms at different A-sites, there are lattice distortions and violent chemical disorder fluctuations inside the ceramics. This can lead to the arrangement of grains deviating from the equilibrium position, resulting in the formation of dislocations and other structural defects. Furthermore, there is a stress field in the vicinity of the edge dislocations. The aforementioned inhomogeneous lattice stress fields, in conjunction with the dislocations, vacancies, and other defects present in the crystal structure, serve to act as an additional phonon scattering center, thereby impeding the transmission of medium/high-frequency phonons and effectively suppressing the κ [21]. Fig. 3(f) shows the EDX elemental area scans corresponding to the BF-TEM images. It can be observed that the elements of $\mathbf {C a} ,$ Sr, Ba,

La, ${\mathrm{Ti}} ,$ and O are uniformly distributed without agglomeration.

In order to gain further insight into the distribution of cations in the samples, atomic scale HAADF images were obtained, in which the atomic lining is proportional to the square of the atomic number $( \mathrm{I} \propto Z ^ {2} )$ as shown in Fig. 4. It is illustrated that the A-site elements are distributed hierarchically with the Ti elements, in accordance with the atomic arrangement of the perovskite structure. EDX area scanning of the HAADF images reveals that each cation enters the perovskite lattice sites after undergoing elemental precipitation and chemical ordering. The resulting elemental content is consistent with the component design of $( \mathrm{Ca} _ {0.27} \mathrm{Sr} _ {0.27} \mathrm{Ba} _ {0.27} \mathrm{La} _ {0.19} ) \mathrm{TiO} _ {3} ,$ thereby confirming the successful synthesis of perovskite structure single-phase solid solutions.

In order to characterize the internal symmetry of the samples as well as the variation in disorder, the samples were subjected to Raman spectroscopy, as shown in Fig. 5(a). In an ideal centrosymmetric perovskite structure, the first-order Raman scattering peaks do not appear [22], but the vibrational peaks corresponding to the TO1, TO2, TO4 and LO4 phonon modes were found in the Raman spectrum [23]. This suggests that the presence of defects, as well as stress fields, reduces the symmetry of the material at the atomic scale and interferes with the vibration and propagation of phonons. It is generally assumed that the peaks below $200 ~ \mathrm{cm} ^ {- 1}$ (TO1, TO2) reflect the vibration of the A-O bond (including $\mathrm{Ca} {\cdot} 0 , s \mathrm{r} {\cdot} 0 ,$ Ba-O and La-O), while the high frequency peaks (TO4, LO4) reflect the bending of the Ti-O-Ti bond and the stretching of the Ti-O bond, respectively [24]. Fig. 5(b) shows the results after the peak positions corresponding to the characteristic peaks of TO1, TO2, TO4, and LO4 phonon modes are counted (determined from the second derivative of the Raman spectrum), and it can be seen that the positions of the TO1 and TO2 peaks are redshifted (shifted to lower frequencies), which is related to the change of the grain size [25]. While the positions of TO4 and LO4 peaks were blueshifted (shifted to high frequencies), which represents a deepening of the [TiO6] octahedral [26] distortion. Taking the peak position of L10 as the initial standard position, the positions and shift of the Raman peaks of the other component ceramics were statistically calculated and listed in Table 3, and it is worth noting that the TO4 peak of L19 demonstrates the largest shift, which indicates that L19 ceramics has the largest distortion of the Ti-O-Ti bonding which will lead to a larger lattice stress field. In addition to the configurational entropy, the influence of factors such as charge and defects on the lattice distortion cannot be ignored. Huang et al. [27]. investigated the relationship between the electron concentration and the lattice distortion by means of DFT calculations, and the results showed that, according to the filling state of the electrons with respect to the conduction bands, the lattice distortion of the sample exhibits a maximum value as the electron concentration increases. Returning to the present work, the elevation of x changes the electron concentration within the system, and the carrier concentration corresponding to x = 0.19 is closest to the maxima of the Ti-O-Ti bond asymmetry. The above results indicate that the magnitude of the configurational entropy is not completely decisive for the degree of lattice distortion in ceramics.

Fig. 6 demonstrates the XPS spectrum of the CSBL ceramics, with the positions of the peaks corrected for C 1 s (284.8 eV), which shows the presence of characteristic peaks for each element such as Ca, Sr, Ba, La, Ti and O. It is indicated that these elements coexist in CSBL ceramics.

Fig. 7 presents the peak fitting results of the O 1S high-resolution spectra for each sample, the proportion of oxygen vacancies to the lattice oxygen can be derived from the fitting, and the results are presented in Table 4. The peak at 529.6 eV can be attributed to lattice oxygen $\mathrm {( O _ {l a t} )}$ , while the peaks at 531.1 eV and 532.3 eV are related to oxygen vacancies $\mathrm {( V _ {o} )}$ and physically adsorbed oxygen $\mathbf {\rho} ( \mathrm {O _ {a d s}} )$ [28], It can be observed that there is a high concentration of $\mathrm{V} _ {0}$ in each component sample, which is related to the low oxygen partial pressure environment during sintering and annealing. The positive correlation between $\mathrm{V} _ {\mathrm{o}}$ concentration and x is mainly due to the fact that the A-site vacancies generated in the system to balance the donor doping induce the generation of Schottky vacancy pairs $\left( V _ {A} ^ {( \prime )} - V _ {O} ^ {( \cdots )} \right)$ [29]. And the creation of oxygen vacancies greatly increases the carrier concentration of the material, improving the σ of the ceramics.

## 3.2. Electrical and thermal transport properties of CSBL ceramics

Fig. 8(a) illustrates the σ curves of the samples as a function of temperature. L40 ceramics exhibits markedly low σ due to its elevated porosity. The σ changes of the remaining samples are mainly divided into two stages, initially rising and subsequently declining with increasing temperature. This indicates that the conductive mechanism of samples undergoes a transition from semiconducting to metallic conductive properties as a result of the following factors: at lower temperatures, more carriers gain sufficient energy to cross the potential barrier, whereas at higher temperatures, the lanthanide ions and oxygen vacancy ionize and scatter the carriers, leading to a decrease in σ [30]. The σ among various samples exhibits a trend of initially increasing and then decreasing with the rise in configuration entropy. This is mainly due to the severe disorder in the high-entropy ceramics, which creates additional alloy scattering and Anderson localization for electrons [31], where the σ of L22 ceramics at 673 K is 32,146.9 S/m. Fig. 8(b) illustrates the variation of the S of the samples with temperature. All components of the samples exhibit negative S, thereby confirming that they are all N-type semiconductors. In the case of degenerate semiconductor, the S can be calculated according to the single parabolic band (SPB) model [32]:

$$| S | = {\frac {8 \pi ^ {2} k _ {\mathrm{B}} ^ {2}} {3 e h ^ {2}}} m ^ {*} T \Bigl ( {\frac {\pi} {3 n}} \Bigr ) ^ {2 / 3}\tag{4}$$

where $k _ {\mathrm{B} , \textit {e} , \textit {h} , m ^ {*} ,}$ , T and n are the Boltzmann constant, electron charge, Planck’s constant, carrier effective mass, thermodynamic temperature and carrier concentration. Consequently, the S is proportional to the m\* and inversely proportional to the n. On one hand, as the concentration of donor doping is increased, the n also rises. On the other hand, the lattice tends to contract due to the smaller radius of $\mathrm {L a ^ {3 +}}$ ions, resulting in a reduction in the spacing between Ti atoms and an intensification of Ti 3d interactions. This phenomenon leads to the steepening of the conduction band and a reduction in $m ^ {*}$ [33]. The superposition of the two effects results in a reduction in the S, which is 23 % lower at 1073 K for L25 ceramics compared to L10 ceramics.

In an ionic crystal, electrons attract the surrounding positive ions and repel the negative ions, and the whole of the distorted region with the electrons is called a polaron. When the radius of the distorted region is close to the lattice constant, it is called a small polaron. The minimum energy required for a small polaron to activate and cross the potential barrier is referred to as the conductive activation energy $\left( E _ {\mathrm{a}} \right)$ . This can be determined from the Arrhenius equation [34]:

$$\sigma = {\frac {\sigma _ {0}} {T}} \mathrm{exp} {\frac {- E _ {\mathrm{a}}} {k _ {\mathrm{B}} T}}\tag{5}$$

$$\mathrm{In} ( \sigma T ) = \mathrm{In} ( C ) \bigg ( - {\frac {E _ {a}} {k _ {\mathrm {{B}}} T}} \bigg )\tag{6}$$

where σ, $E _ {\mathrm{a}} ,$ kB and T are the conductivity, activation energy, Boltzmann constant and thermodynamic temperature, the σ0 and C are constants. In Fig. 8(c), the excellent linearity results indicate that the ceramic conducts by a small polaron conduction mechanism in the temperature range of 373 K to 573 K [35]. The activation energies of the L10–22 ceramics are all in the range of 1.5–1.6 eV, whereas L25 ceramics has an activation energy of $1.7 \mathrm{eV} ,$ and its larger activation energy explains the decrease in conductivity when the x increases to 0.25. The extremely high grain boundary density of L40 ceramics hinders the transition of the polaron and therefore it has the largest activation energy of 2.1 eV, The activation energies of all the samples are much larger than the $\left( \kappa _ {\mathrm{lat}} \right)$ can be found by the following equation [36]:

Due to the extremely low conductivity of L40, the PF is 1–2 orders of magnitude lower than the rest of the samples, and the trend of the PF curves is similar to the trend of the σ with temperature, with L22 ceramics obtaining the highest PF of 563 $\mu \mathrm{W/m/K} ^ {\bar {2}}$ at 823 K.

To further analyze the carrier transport properties, room temperature Hall effect measurements were carried out using the Van der Pauw method at a magnetic field strength of 1.0 T. The results are shown in Table 5. As the donor doping concentration increases, the n increases by an order of magnitude. However, at the same time, due to the increase in lattice distortion within high entropy systems and the rise in grain boundary density caused by delayed diffusion effect, the scattering of carriers is enhanced, resulting in a decrease of $\mu _ {\mathrm{H}}$ by an order of magnitude.

Fig. 9(a) shows the variation curve of total thermal conductivity $\mathrm {( \kappa _ {t o t a l} )}$ with temperature, and the $\kappa _ {\mathrm{total}}$ of each component sample is in the range of 4.7–2.2 W/m/K in the temperature range of 323 K to 1073 K, which is decreased by more than 60.8 % compared to the $\kappa _ {\mathrm{total}}$ of SrTiO3 (12 W/m/K). According to the Wiedemann-Franz law, the carrier thermal conductivity(κe) and the lattice thermal conductivity

$$\kappa _ {\mathrm{e}} = L T \sigma\tag{7}$$

$$\kappa _ {\mathrm{lat}} = \kappa _ {\mathrm{total}} - \kappa _ {\mathrm{e}}\tag{8}$$

where $L ,$ T and σ are the Lorentz constant, thermodynamic temperature and conductivity. The results of the calculation are shown in Fig. 9(b). Since all samples are semiconductors, the carrier contribution to the κtotal is minor, with $\kappa _ {\mathrm{e}}$ ranging from 0 to 0.5 W/m/K in the temperature range of 323 K to 1073 K. The $\kappa _ {\mathrm{total}}$ can be described by the relaxation time approximation under the Debye model [37]:

$$\kappa _ {l a t} = \frac {k _ {\mathrm{B}}} {2 \pi ^ {2} \nu} \left( \frac {k _ {\mathrm{B}} T} {\hbar} \right) ^ {3} \int _ {0} ^ {\frac {\theta _ {\mathrm{D}}} {T}} \frac {x ^ {4} e ^ {x}} {{\tau _ {\mathrm{c}} ^ {- 1}} \left( e ^ {x} - 1 \right) ^ {2}} d x\tag{9}$$

where $k _ \mathrm{B} , \tau _ {\mathrm{C} , \mathrm{~}} \Theta _ {\mathrm{D}} ,$ v and ℏ are the Boltzmann constant, relaxation time, Debye temperature, phonon velocity and simplified Planck constant. The relaxation time $\tau _ {\mathrm{C}}$ is the result of the combined effect of several scattering mechanisms and can be expressed as [38]:

$${\frac {1} {\tau _ {\mathrm{C}}}} = {\frac {1} {\tau _ {\mathrm{B}}}} + {\frac {1} {\tau _ {\mathrm{U}}}} + {\frac {1} {\tau _ {\mathrm{D}}}} + \cdots .\tag{10}$$

where the right-hand side represents interface scattering (B), phononphonon scattering (U) and defect scattering (D), respectively. The $\kappa _ {\mathrm{lat}}$ of the samples of each component shows a decreasing trend with increasing temperature, indicating that the thermal transport process is dominated by the U process. Owing to the impact of lattice distortion induced by high entropy, the $\kappa _ {\mathrm{lat}}$ exhibits a decreasing trend when the x ranges from 0.10 to 0.19. However, due to the complexity of the interaction of multiple ions in the lattice and the decrease in porosity, the $\kappa _ {\mathrm{lat}}$ increase dramatically when the x reaches 0.22, then begin to decrease as the x increases. Finally, L19 ceramics achieves a extremely low $\kappa _ {\mathrm{total}}$ (2.13 W/m/K) at 1073 K due to its maximum lattice distortion. It is noteworthy that L40 ceramics exhibits a temperature-independent glasslike $\kappa _ {\mathrm{total}}$ , which decreases by only 0.28 W/m/K over the entire temperature range. The results are not only because of the short-range chemical disorder and the high degree of structural disorder in the highentropy material matrix, but also because of the additional phonon scattering centers provided by the greater number of pores and the higher density of grain boundaries.

The abundant phonon scattering mechanisms in the material are shown in Fig. 10. The high-entropy design allows multiple elements to compete to enter the perovskite lattice to form a solid solution, producing dramatic lattice distortion effects and strain fields. This not only causes atoms to deviate from their normal equilibrium positions and increases the phonon scattering centers, but also creates structural defects at a variety of scales, including point defects, dislocations and subgrain boundaries. Among these, point defects such as vacancies scatter high frequency phonons (5–15 THz) with relaxation times dependent on both atomic size and mass fluctuations, and the rich variety of elements within the high entropy system provides sufficient size and mass disorder. Line and surface defects contribute to enhanced scattering of low and intermediate frequency phonons (<5 THz), and multiscale structural defects scatter phonons at all frequencies, reducing the phonon mean free range and thus affecting thermal transport in the material [39].

Fig. 11(a) illustrates the ZT curve of ceramics as a function of temperature, with L19 ceramics exhibiting the highest ZT (0.20) at 1073 K due to its combination of high PF and low $\kappa _ {\mathrm{total}} ,$ which is 19 % higher than that of equimolar ratio high-entropy ceramics. Due to the influence of the porosity of L40 ceramics on its thermoelectric performance, its thermoelectric performance is extremely low. The S, σ and ZT of L10- L25 ceramics at 1073 K is counted and plotted in Fig. 11(b) and (c) with the configuration entropy. Due to the trend of each thermoelectric parameter with the configuration entropy and the constraints on each other, the ZT value of the ceramics shows a parabola with the increase of the configuration entropy, and the ZT reaches a peak at $\Delta S _ {\mathrm{Conf}} {=} 11$ .483 J/mol/K. Fig. 11(d) compares the thermoelectric properties of the L19 ceramics prepared in this work with those of the previously published high entropy $\mathrm {{S r T i O _ {3}}} {\cdot}$ -based ceramics [11,12,40–43]. L19 ceramics exhibits superior thermoelectric performance with a moderate level of configuration entropy. This strongly indicates that high entropy design is an effective optimization strategy for thermoelectric materials, but it does not necessarily mean that higher configuration entropy leads to superior performance.

## 4. Conclusion

In this work, non-equimolar high-entropy $\mathrm {[ C a _ {( 1 - x ) / 3} S r _ {( 1 - x ) / 3} B a _ {( 1 - x ) /}}$ $\mathrm {_ {3 L a _ {x} ] T i O _ {3}}}$ thermoelectric ceramics (x=0.10–0.40) were prepared. The main phase of the component ceramics has a cubic perovskite structure and the grain size tends to decrease with the increase in configurational entropy. The elements within the ceramic conform to the design proportions and are evenly distributed. The high-entropy design results in the presence of high-density defects and stress fields within the ceramics. The symmetry of the ceramics reduces, and the [TiO6] octahedra undergo stretching and torsion, this microstructural disorder achieves broadband scattering of phonons and reduces the thermal conductivity of the material. SPS and annealing induction in a reducing atmosphere produced a high concentration of oxygen vacancies, which achieved semi-conductivity of the ceramics and contributed to the high electrical conductivity of the materials. The ceramic with x=0.19 $( \Delta S _ {\mathrm{Conf}} mathrm {=} 11.483 \mathrm{J/mol/K} )$ achieved excellent power factor (444.43 μW/ $\mathrm{m} / \mathrm{K} ^ {2} )$ and thermal conductivity (2.13 W/m/K) at 1073 K, resulting in the highest ZT value of 0.20. It can be found that when using entropy engineering to optimize the thermoelectric properties of materials, it is not necessary to blindly pursue high configuration entropy, but rather to find the most suitable configuration entropy.
