## 1. Introduction

Cross-domain vehicles combine the typical features of aviation and space vehicles, can fly remotely in the atmosphere and transatmosphere, and have the characteristics of a large airspace and wide velocity domain, which have shown great application value in both military and civil fields, and thus have received extensive attention from scholars at home and abroad [1,2].

However, since the flight of this type of vehicle covers both aerospace and aviation domains, the mission and flight environment are complex, and its multidisciplinary design needs to comprehensively consider the flight environment of in-orbit operation and re-entry flight, and the configuration is more complex, which will directly affect the aerodynamic, structural and trajectory disciplines with high timeconsumption and high computational cost, making it more difficult and longer to solve the problem.

In the design process of traditional aircraft, the system is divided into independent subsystems according to disciplines based on the design idea of multidisciplinary decoupling, and the serial iterative design relationship is maintained [3]. However, there is a strong nonlinear coupling between different disciplines of the cross-domain vehicle, and the traditional discipline decoupling design method makes it difficult to consider the synergistic effect generated by the mutual coupling between disciplines, which may lead to the loss of the overall optimal solution of the system, thus reducing the overall performance of the vehicle [4]. At the same time, for this kind of high-time-consumption-solving problem, serial iteration solving is even less efficient.

Parallel solving of subproblems can effectively improve the solving efficiency. Currently, Collaborative Optimization (CO [5,6]), Concurrent Subspace Optimization (CSSO [7,8]), Bi-Level Integrated System Synthesis (BLISS [9,10]), Analytical Target Cascading (ATC [11–13]), and other decomposition-based coordinated optimization techniques have been widely studied and applied. Among them, all three types of methods, CO, CSSO, and BLISS, decompose the original complex problems into two-level structural problems and then solve them, but their convergence has not yet been strictly theoretically supported; comparatively speaking, ATC methods have been widely used to deal with multilevel structural problems under strict mathematical convergence requirements [14].S. Tosserams has put the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) combined with Augmented Lagrangian (AL) coordination method and applied it to the solution process of the ATC method, which significantly reduces the solution cost of the ATC method [15], and based on this, he proposed the application of Augmented The Augmented Lagrangian Coordination (ALC) method for solving MDO problems with Lagrangian relaxation is proposed [16–18], which breaks the limitation that the problem must be decomposed according to the hierarchical structure in the ATC method, and can deal with both hierarchical and non-hierarchical problems, with better convergence and lower computational cost, and becomes the most efficient method for solving the problem. With better convergence and lower computational cost, it becomes one of the best methods for solving efficiency.

Early ALC methods require the subproblems to be solved consecutively in a specific order due to the coupling of each problem, and the degree of solution parallelization is low. Therefore, S. Tosserams [16–18] et al. proposed the method of introducing the master problem, which establishes the indirect coupling between the master problem and the subproblems, eliminates the direct coupling between the subproblems, and realizes the parallel solution of the problem. ALC can be classified into centralized ALC and distributed ALC based on whether the master problem is introduced or not.

Distributed ALC does not eliminate the direct coupling variables between subproblems, and the direct coupling and coordination between each subproblem requires each subproblem to be solved consecutively, which restricts the parallel solution of the subproblems and leads to low efficiency of problem-solving. Gui [19] et al. proposed a distributed parallel ALC method, using the coupling values obtained in the previous round of each subproblem for the next round of solving, which can realize the parallel solving of the subproblems, but all the subproblems are decoupled and solved in parallel, which leads to too many consistency constraints, and it is difficult to satisfy all the consistency constraints for the strongly coupled problems, which leads to the results failing to converge, and the study also shows that in the parallel solving, the efficiency of the current AL-AD algorithm, which has the best efficiency, will lead to information mismatch when updating the Lagrange multipliers, and the Lagrange multipliers may not be optimal; and the increase of the penalty weights leads to numerical difficulties when solving the optimization problem [20], which further aggravates the convergence difficulties.

Centralized ALC can eliminate the direct coupling variables between each subproblem by introducing the master problem, but it will increase the direct coupling relationship between the master problem and each subproblem, which will increase the amount of system coordination coupling, leading to the reduction of its execution efficiency, and it is difficult to select its master problem. While Nie [21] et al. proposed the dominant ALC method, selecting a subproblem with the highest number of coupling quantities with other subproblems as the master problem can reduce the copy of coupling variables between the master problem and other subproblems, and achieve a balance between the coordination cost and parallelization, but for the strong coupling problem, selecting a discipline as the master problem will create the same problem as the distributed parallel ALC method, consistency constraints are too many and difficult to satisfy all of them, resulting in the results not converging.

Therefore, based on the idea of parallel solving of subproblems, this paper focuses on the cross-domain vehicle and researches the optimization design method considering the coupling of aerodynamic, trajectory, and structural disciplines in response to the problem of low solving efficiency caused by its highly time-consuming solving disciplines. The method carries out integrated parametric modeling of aerodynamic structure through a 3D CST algorithm to realize the geometry/aerodynamic/structural joint optimization. Meanwhile, a distributedcentralized ALC method is proposed to realize the arbitrary selection of the master problem and establish a joint optimization architecture, which can realize a multidisciplinary optimization design of a two-layer coordination structure. Finally, based on the constructed optimization design method, an optimization design with the objective of mass optimization is carried out for the X37-like vehicle, to obtain the aerodynamic shape and flight trajectory scheme with optimal comprehensive performance that satisfies the structure/trajectory/thermal protection constraints, and then verify the effectiveness of the method.

## 2. Cross-domain vehicle

## 2.1. Mission requirements

There are two main mission profiles for the cross-domain vehicle. As a spacecraft, its orbit altitude between 320 and 400 km, this paper class X-37B vehicle has a high lift-to-drag ratio aerodynamic layout adapted to the flight in the thin atmosphere, can be a long time on-orbit flight, and with a high re-entry velocity in the near space for hypersonic longdistance gliding or even jumping maneuvering flights, the maximum flight speed of up to more than Mach 20. As an aircraft flying at an altitude of 30 to 100 km, this kind of vehicle can eventually evolve into a “falling from the sky” weapon for striking the ground. The mission profile is shown in Fig. 1.

Aiming at the above mission requirements, this paper carries out a multidisciplinary design optimization of the cross-domain vehicle, including six disciplines: geometry, aerodynamics, trajectory, thermal protection, structure, and mass.

## 2.2. Joint geometric/aerodynamic/structural modeling based on the 3D CST method

Traditional structural parametric modeling mainly relies on various CAD tools [22]; however, CAD-based parametric modeling is inefficient and costly, difficult to achieve changes in aerodynamic/structural topology and layout, and unsuitable for large-scale design space exploration. Analytical based parametric models can primarily be categorized into two types: shape-based parameterization and perturbation parameterization. Shape-based parameterization expresses the outer form through a finite number of parameters with geometric characteristics, such as the Hicks-Henne method and the parametric section (PARSEC) method. Disturbance parameterization typically employs weighted combinations of basis functions, offering high adaptability and precision, exemplified by methods like B-splines and NURBS (nonuniform rational B-splines). However, the geometric meaning of parameters in NURBS-based models remains ambiguous, hindering the derivation of universal principles. Compared to the aforementioned methods, the CST method proposed by Kulfan et al. [23–25] has the characteristics of fewer parameters, higher accuracy, and better expressive capability [26], and has been applied to structural parametric modeling in recent years. Feng et al. [27] generated a 3D model of an X-37-like carrier based on the CST method and proposed an equirectangular division processing mesh based on the vector release technique. Su et al. [28–30] established a complete 3D analytical aerodynamic structure model of the vehicle by overall combination based on the idea of component combination.

This section develops an integrated geometrical aerodynamic structural rapid modeling method that supports the analytic description of cross-domain vehicle geometrical configuration, structural layout, structural dimensions, material properties, and other parameters based on 3D CST functions. The method avoids tedious work such as data conversion and transfer between different disciplines, and greatly improves the modeling and solving efficiency.

## 2.2.1. Geometry

The CST method expresses the shape of a cross-section through a combination of a category function $C _ {N _ {1}} ^ {N _ {2}} ( \psi )$ , which is used to define the basic shape of the geometry, and a shape functionS(ψ), which adds a perturbation to the basic shape to modify the profile, as shown in the following equation.

$$\zeta ( \psi ) | _ {u p p , l o w} = C _ {N _ {1}} ^ {N _ {2}} ( \psi ) \cdot S ( \psi ) + \Delta \zeta _ {N} ( \psi )\tag{1}$$

where ψis the normalized relative length in the range [0,1], ζ(ψ) is the value of the normal height at the correspondingψ upper/lower surface of the section, and $\Omega _ {{\boldsymbol {\mathsf{N}}}} ( {\boldsymbol {\psi}} )$ is the normal eccentricity.

The category function $C _ {N _ {1}} ^ {N _ {2}} ( \psi$ )controls the basic type of the overall shape of the curve by two parametersN $_ {1} , N _ {2} ,$ , and the expression is as follows:

$$C _ {N _ {1}} ^ {N _ {2}} = \psi ^ {N _ {1}} ( 1 - \psi ) ^ {N _ {2}}\tag{2}$$

The B-spline function is selected as the Shape Function $S ( \psi )$ , and the expression is as follows:

$$S ( \psi ) = \sum _ {i = 0} ^ {n} b _ {i} N _ {k} ^ {i} ( \psi )\tag{3}$$

where biis a weight factor, $N _ {k} ^ {i} ( \psi ) \mathbf {i}$ s called a kth order (k-1 times) B-spline basis function, and each B-spline basis function is a kth order segmented polynomial defined in the non-decreasing sequence T determined by the parameter t.

Based on the above description of the cross sections, further combining a set of cross sections along the spreading direction yields a 3D CST-based elementary surface. When combining the sections, the transitions between the sections are considered, and the entire unit surface variation is expressed using the spreading guidelines and normal guidelines. The x-axis along the spreading direction and the y-axis along the chord direction are specified. The same CST function is used for the spreading and normal guidelines:

$$\left\{\begin{array} {l l} {\zeta _ {n o r m a l} ( \eta ) = C _ {M _ {1}} ^ {M _ {2}} ( \eta ) {\cdot} S ( \eta )} \\ {\zeta _ {s p a n} ( \eta ) = \pm C _ {T _ {1}} ^ {T _ {2}} ( \eta ) {\cdot} S ( \eta )} \end{array} \right.\tag{4}$$

whereη is the unit coordinate of the surface along the x-axis, M1and $M _ {2} \mathrm{i} s$ the normal control factor, $T _ {1}$ and $T _ {2} \mathrm{i}$ is the spreading control factor.

The final expression of the 3D CST basic surface in the Cartesian coordinate system is shown in the following equation.

$$\left\{\begin{array} {l l} {\displaystyle X ( \boldsymbol {\psi} , \boldsymbol {\eta} ) = \boldsymbol {\eta}} \\ {\displaystyle Y ( \boldsymbol {\psi} , \boldsymbol {\eta} ) = C _ {N _ {1} ( \boldsymbol {\eta} )} ^ {N _ {2} ( \boldsymbol {\eta} )} ( \boldsymbol {\psi} ) C _ {M _ {1}} ^ {M _ {2}} ( \boldsymbol {\eta} ) \sum _ {i = 0} ^ {n} \sum _ {j = 0} ^ {m} b _ {i , j} N _ {k} ^ {i} ( \boldsymbol {\psi} ) N _ {k} ^ {j} ( \boldsymbol {\eta} ) + \Delta \zeta _ {M , N} ( \boldsymbol {\psi} , \boldsymbol {\eta} ) \vert _ {\boldsymbol {u p p , l o w}}} \\ {\displaystyle Z ( \boldsymbol {\psi} , \boldsymbol {\eta} ) = \boldsymbol {\psi \cdot C _ {T _ {1}} ^ {T _ {2}}} ( \boldsymbol {\eta} ) \sum _ {t = 0} ^ {w} b _ {t} N _ {k} ^ {t} ( \boldsymbol {\eta} )} \end{array} \right.\tag{5}$$

where theψ, η value is in the range [0,1].

The structural model is generated based on the aerodynamic model, and the non-decreasing node vectors τwith values in the interval [0,1] are further introduced as design variables in the height direction based on the parameterized mesh of the shape, as shown in the following equation:

$$\pmb {\tau} = \left[ \tau _ {1} \tau _ {2} \cdots \tau _ {k} \cdots \tau _ {l} \right]\tag{10}$$

Define

$$\left\{\begin{array} {l l} {X _ {i j k} = X _ {u p p i , j} + \left( X _ {u p p i , j} - X _ {l o w i , j} \right) \tau _ {k}} \\ {Y _ {i j k} = Y _ {u p p i , j} + \left( Y _ {u p p i , j} - Y _ {l o w i , j} \right) \tau _ {k}} \\ {Z _ {i j k} = Z _ {u p p i , j} + \left( Z _ {u p p i , j} - Z _ {l o w i , j} \right) \tau _ {k}} \end{array} \right.\tag{11}$$

## 3.1. Distributed-centralized ALC model

From the comparative analysis of the two coordination methods of ALC methods, it can be seen that in the centralized ALC method, the introduction of the master problem eliminates the direct coupling variables between the subproblems, and the additional addition of the direct coupling relationship between the master problem and the subproblems increases the amount of the coordination coupling thus leading to a decrease in its execution efficiency. The direct coupling and coordination between subproblems in the distributed ALC method require that each subproblem be solved continuously, limiting the parallel computation of the subproblems.

Meanwhile, for the improved methods of these two ALC methods, the distributed parallel ALC method can realize the parallel solving of the subproblems, but it cannot transfer the coupling information in time, and too many coupled variables are decoupled, which may lead to the failure of problem-solving. The dominant ALC method selects a subproblem with the highest number of coupling variables with other subproblems as the master problem to reduce the copies of coupling variables when the master problem coordinates other subproblems, which can balance the execution efficiency and parallel computation. However, for most of the solved complex problems, it may not be possible to select the subproblem with the largest number of individual coupling variables, and then the method cannot be used to solve the problem.

Therefore, this paper proposes a distributed-centralized ALC method, which uses distributed subproblems as the master problems to solve complex problems.

Assuming that the subproblems $p _ {i} ( i = 1 , \cdots , n , n < M )$ are the master problems, which are modeled as follows.

$$\begin{array} {l} {{\displaystyle \operatorname* {m i n} _ {{\bar {\mathbf {x}}} _ {i} = \left[ {\mathbf {y}} ^ {\top} , {\mathbf {y}} _ {i} ^ {\top} , {\mathbf {x}} _ {i} ^ {\top} , {\mathbf {x}} _ {0} ^ {\top} \right] ^ {\mathrm{T}}} f _ {0} ( {\mathbf {y}} , {\mathbf {x}} _ {1} , \cdots , {\mathbf {x}} _ {M} ) + f _ {i} ( {\mathbf {y}} _ {i} , {\mathbf {x}} _ {i} ) + \displaystyle \sum _ {\frac {j = 1} {j \neq i}} {\mathbf {\xi}} ^ {M} \phi _ {c , j} \Big ( {\mathbf {c}} _ {j} \Big ( {\mathbf {y}} , {\mathbf {y}} _ {j} \Big ) \Big )}} \\ {~ + ~ \phi _ {g} \big ( \mathbf {g} _ {0} , {\mathbf {x}} _ {0} \big ) + \phi _ {h} ( \mathbf {h} _ {0} )} \end{array}$$

$$\begin{array} {r l} & {\mathrm{s.t.} \mathbf {g} _ {i} ( \mathbf {y} _ {i} , \mathbf {x} _ {i} ) \leq \mathbf {0} , i = 1 , \cdots , n , n < M} \\ & {\mathbf {h} _ {i} ( \mathbf {y} _ {i} , \mathbf {x} _ {i} ) = \mathbf {0} , i = 1 , \cdots , n , n < M} \end{array}\tag{21}$$

The other subproblems $p _ {j} ( j \neq i , j = 1 , \cdots , M )$ are modeled as:

$$\begin{array} {r l} & {\quad \underset {\mathbf {X} _ {j} = \left[ \mathbf {y} _ {j} ^ {\kappa} , \mathbf {x} _ {j} ^ {\kappa} \right]} {\mathrm{min}} f _ {0} ( \mathbf {y} , \mathbf {x} _ {1} , \cdots , \mathbf {x} _ {n} ) + f _ {i} ( \mathbf {y} _ {i} , \mathbf {x} _ {i} ) + \phi _ {c j} \Bigl ( \mathbf {c} _ {j} \Bigl ( \mathbf {y} , \mathbf {y} _ {j} \Bigr ) \Bigr ) + \phi _ {g} \bigl ( \mathbf {g} _ {0} , \mathbf {x} _ {0} \bigr )} \\ & {\quad} \\ & {\quad \quad + \phi _ {h} \bigl ( \mathbf {h} _ {0} \bigr )} \\ & {\quad \quad \quad \quad \quad \quad \quad \quad \quad \quad \quad \quad \quad \quad \quad \quad \quad \quad \quad} \\ & {\quad \quad \quad \quad \quad \quad \mathrm{s.t.} \mathbf {g} _ {j} \Bigl ( \mathbf {y} _ {j} , \mathbf {x} _ {j} \Bigr ) \le \mathbf {0} , j = 1 , \cdots , M , j \neq i} \\ & {\quad \quad \quad \quad \mathbf {h} _ {j} \Bigl ( \mathbf {y} _ {j} , \mathbf {x} _ {j} \Bigr ) = \mathbf {0} , j = 1 , \cdots , M , j \neq i} \end{array}\tag{22}$$

The distributed-centralized ALC method is selected to divide the subproblems into two parts from the relatively large number of coupling relations between two and two, and the distributed arrangement of the subproblems in the former part is treated as the centralized master problems, and the distributed arrangement of the rest of the subproblems is treated as the subproblems. By this method, the coupling relationship between the latter part and the former part can be specifically revealed, avoiding the copying of the coupling variables of the former parts in the transmission of the latter part in the distributed ALC structure, which in turn leads to an increase in the coupling variables. ## 3.2. Distributed-centralized ALC method-solving strategy

The steps for solving the distributed-centralized ALC method are as follows:

i) Initialize the parameters.

ii) Solve the master problems to obtain the optimal values of local and co-state variables.

iii) Judge whether the convergence condition is satisfied; if so, go to step iv); if not, update the parameters and go to step ii).

iv) Solve the subproblems and obtain the optimal values of the local variables.

v) Judge whether the convergence condition is satisfied; if so, go to step vi); if not, update the parameters and go to step ii).

vi) Output the optimal result.

The distributed solution of the master problems and subproblems is divided into two parts: the inner loop and the outer loop. In the inner loop, the Lagrange multipliers and penalty weights are fixed to facilitate the convergence of the individual distributed subproblems to their respective local optimal solutions through lag coupling information. In the outer loop, the Lagrange multipliers and penalty weights are changed to ensure consistent constrained convergence of the distributed subproblem. For the outer loop of the distributed master problems and subproblems, the following strategies are used to update the Lagrange multipliers and penalty weights, respectively.

For the subproblems, its distributed problem consistency constraint convergence depends on the master problems’ coupling transfer within the current loop, using the traditional Lagrange multipliers and penalty weights updating strategy can accelerate the convergence speed of the subproblems, and facilitate the convergence of the master problemssolving.

$$\begin{array} {r l} & {\nu _ {j} ^ {i + 1} = \nu _ {j} ^ {i} + 2 w _ {j} ^ {i} \circ w _ {j} ^ {i} \circ c _ {j} ^ {i}} \\ & {w _ {j k} ^ {i + 1} = \left\{\begin{array} {l l} {w _ {j k} ^ {i} , \left| c _ {j k} ^ {i} \right| \leq \gamma \Big | c _ {j k} ^ {i - 1} \Big |} \\ {\beta w _ {j k} ^ {i} , \left| c _ {j k} ^ {i} \right| > \gamma \Big | c _ {j k} ^ {i - 1} \Big |} \end{array} \right.} \end{array}\tag{23}$$

where $c _ {j} ^ {i} = S _ {j} y ^ {i} - y _ {j} ^ {i}$ denotes the consistency constraint value of the subproblemj.

For the master problems, the convergence of their distributed problem consistency constraints depends on the last round of loops, and if Eq. (23) is used directly to update the Lagrange multipliers and penalty weights will lead to less efficient computation of the master problem, and the existence of solution oscillations and so on.

In this paper, an adaptive multiplier updating strategy is used for updating. The traditional multiplier updating formula 2ωi ∘ωi is the updating step of the multipliers, but it is difficult to get the optimal Lagrange multipliers at the late convergence stage when the step is too large, so the adaptive updating strategy needs to set an independent multiplier updating step. At the same time, in the traditional parameter updating strategy, a large penalty weight will make the problem change and can not be solved, a small penalty weight will lead to the consistency constraint being difficult to reduce, so the adaptive updating strategy also needs to be updated in real-time for the penalty weight.

Referring to reference [19], the adaptive updating strategy divides the iterative process into a fast convergence phase and a slow convergence phase. When the absolute value of the consistency constraint decreases continuously, it is considered to be in the fast convergence phase.

In the fast convergence stage, the penalty weights need to be limited to avoid them being too large, and at the same time, when the reduction of the absolute value of the consistency constraints is greater than 50 % of the reduction in the previous round, the step length of the multiplier should be increased appropriately, as shown in the following equation.

$$\begin{array} {r l} & {\mathrm{if} ( | c _ {j k} ^ {i - 1} | - | c _ {j k} ^ {i} | ) > 0.5 ( | c _ {j k} ^ {i - 2} | - | c _ {j k} ^ {i - 1} | )} \\ & {\alpha _ {j k} ^ {i + 1} = \beta _ {1} \alpha _ {j k} ^ {i}} \\ & {\mathrm{else} a _ {j k} ^ {i + 1} = \alpha _ {j k} ^ {i}} \\ & {\nu _ {j} ^ {i + 1} = \nu _ {j} ^ {i} + \alpha _ {j} ^ {i + 1} \circ c _ {j} ^ {i - 1}} \\ & {w _ {j k} ^ {i + 1} = \operatorname* {m a x} ( 1 , \sqrt {\frac {\nu _ {j k} ^ {i + 1}} {2 ( S _ {j} \nu ^ {i} ) _ {k}}} | )} \end{array}\tag{24}$$

where $\beta _ {1}$ is usually greater than 1, and the minimum value of the penalty weight is 1, $\sqrt {| \frac {\nu _ {j k} ^ {i + 1}} {2 \left( S _ {j} y ^ {i} \right) _ {k}}}$ | can avoid the penalty weight being too large, and thus leading to an order of magnitude difference between the penalty weight term and the Lagrange multiplier term.

In the slow convergence stage, it is necessary to take a smaller step length to update the multipliers, and the penalty weight remains unchanged.

$$\begin{array} {r l} & {\mathrm{if} \Big ( \Big | c _ {j k} ^ {i - 1} \Big | - \Big | c _ {j k} ^ {i} \Big | \Big ) \left( \Big | c _ {j k} ^ {i - 2} \Big | - \Big | c _ {j k} ^ {i - 1} \Big | \right) < 0} \\ & {\alpha _ {j k} ^ {i + 1} = \beta _ {2} \alpha _ {j k} ^ {i}} \\ & {\mathrm{else} \alpha _ {j k} ^ {i + 1} = \alpha _ {j k} ^ {i}} \\ & {\nu _ {j} ^ {i + 1} = \nu _ {j} ^ {i} + \alpha _ {j} ^ {i + 1} \circ c _ {j} ^ {i - 1}} \\ & {w _ {j k} ^ {i + 1} = w _ {j k} ^ {i}} \end{array}\tag{25}$$

where $\beta _ {2}$ is usually <1.

## 5. Conclusion

To address the low computational efficiency of solving complex cross-domain vehicle configurations due to high computational time consumption, this paper focuses on the discipline coupling characteristics of cross-domain vehicles. Based on the 3D CST method, it implements geometric/aerodynamic/structural co-simulation. A distributedcentralized ALC method is proposed with convergence analysis. Through mathematical formulation, we evaluate the computational efficiency, parallel scalability, and solution accuracy of distributed, distributed parallel, centralized, dominant, and distributed-centralized ALC methods. Results indicate that compared to dominant ALC and centralized ALC, the proposed distributed-centralized ALC method minimizes both coupled and decoupled variables while achieving the highest computational efficiency. Relative to distributed ALC and distributed parallel ALC, it yields the smallest solution errors and exhibits reduced susceptibility to local optima. Finally, the feasibility and effectiveness of the proposed method were further validated by solving a cross-domain aircraft multidisciplinary optimization problem. The research findings provide new insights for multidisciplinary design optimization and offer important guidance for engineering practice applications.
