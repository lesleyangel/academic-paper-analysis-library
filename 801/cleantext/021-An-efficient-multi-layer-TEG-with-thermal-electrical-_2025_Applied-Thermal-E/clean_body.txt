## 1. Introduction

The Mars exploration mission is full of challenges, and the radioisotope is the most promising technology to supply stable and reliable power during the long-duration exploration mission for the Mars rover. At present, the radioisotope thermoelectric generator (RTG) is widely utilized in more than 50 deep-space exploration missions of NASA, e.g., the Voyager [1], Galileo [2], Cassini [3], Curiosity [4] and Perseverance [5]. All RTGs in these missions show great power-supply performance. The RTG is mainly composed of the radioisotope heat source and the thermoelectric generator (TEG), and its thermoelectric (TE) conversion efficiency is primarily determined by the TEG performance [6,7]. The TEG utilizes thermoelectric materials to directly convert radioisotope decay heat into electrical energy [8,9]. The TE conversion process involves no mechanical moving parts, physical phase changes or complex chemical reactions, thereby offering high reliability and making it ideal for long-life Mars exploration [6,10–12].

The primary limitation of the current TEG technology is the relatively low conversion efficiency, which is typically lower than 6 % that have been applied in Mars exploration engineering. However, for the latest RTG, i.e., RTG in Perseverance Mars rovers, the TE conversion efficiency is still lower than 6.3 %, and 7 % is the target efficiency for NASA’s next-generation RTG [13]. Therefore, the invention of TEGs with high conversion efficiency has become a pressing research priority. In the design and application of conventional TEG, the single-layer TE structure is widely used [14–20]. However, most of the TE material has a well-defined optimal operating temperature, thus such single-layer structure has a limited operating temperature range and weak performance to some extent, which gradually failed to meet the growing performance demands. The multi-layer structure TEG with appropriate temperature range for each layer tends to present better performance [21–24]. Therefore, in this paper, a multi-layer TEG is innovatively created under Martian environment and the dimension is optimized with the objective of maximum gravimetric power density to achieve higher output power and TE conversion efficiency.

TEG consists of several contact pairs, but due to the existence of microscopic asperity characteristics on the engineering surfaces, the imperfect contact between the two contacting interfaces will lead to the interfacial contact effects. The interfacial contact effects are manifested as a shrinkage effect of heat and electrical flows, and brings thermal contact resistance (TCR) and electrical contact resistance (ECR) to the TE transfer and conversion process [25–28]. Kim [29] developed a numerical modeling for the performance evaluation of a TEG with thermal and electric contact resistance, and built a computer program for the performance simulation. Liu et al. [30] proposed a geometricalmechanical-thermal interaction model to predict the TCR between the bearing inner ring and shaft journal. Most published reports of interfacial contact effects are limited to conventional environments, such as air under standard conditions. However, the development of efficient TEGs for Mars exploration requires accurate quantification of the TCR and ECR under interfacial Martian atmosphere clearance. In this paper, the TCR and ECR between all layers in TEG under Martian atmosphere are quantified, and compared with the vacuum, Earth’s atmosphere, and silver-epoxy adhesive ones.

The prediction of TCR and ECR involves three main steps: rough surface characterization, contact deformation, and TE transfer and conversion analysis. Majumdar et al. [31] developed a simple roughness characterization method by introducing the Weierstrass-Mandelbrot (W-M) fractal function. The W-M fractal method can effectively capture the geometric self-similarity and self-affinity of rough surfaces, allowing similar surface topographies to be observed at different magnification [32–34]. In this paper, fractal W-M function with contact-type measurement is employed to characterize the surface topography. Contact deformation affects the real contact area on the microscopic scale, and

Wang et al. [35] found that the contact assessment requires the use of the elasto-plastic deformation model. In TE transfer and conversion process, the TCR and ECR are influenced by interfacial pressure, temperature, clearance medium, etc. Chen et al. [36] investigated the influence of interfacial pressure and temperature between HTA and C/ $\mathrm{ZrB} _ {2}$ SiC on TCR. Li et al. [37] estimated the TCR and ECR under difference clearance mediums of air, graphene sheet and thermal grease, and revealed that the TEG system delivered better performance at relatively low TCR and ECR. In this paper, the influence mechanism of loading pressure, average interfacial temperature and clearance medium on TE transfer and conversion is investigated. For the experimental measurement of TCR and ECR, the steady-state method is utilized for evaluating TE bulk materials [18,38–41]. Liu et al. [42] experimentally measured the TCR between high thermal conductivity C/C material and superalloy Inconel 600, pointing out the necessity of accounting for TCR in the design of thermal structures. Bendada et al. [43] combined experimental and numerical approaches to evaluate the TCR between polymer and mold. In the authors’ previous work [44], a platform was developed to indirectly measure interfacial contact effects of contacting pairs. However, the output voltage of TEG remains to be measured.

In this paper, a novel three-layer TEG is designed and optimized with the objective of maximum gravimetric power density to convert the heat from isotope decay into electrical energy. The multi-layer TEG consists of high-, mid- and low TE materials formulated with optimal operating conditions. The prediction models of TCR and ECR are established and validated by experimental measurement, and the influences of loading pressure and temperature are clarified under interfacial Martian atmosphere clearance. The interfacial contact effects are equivalent to additional layers and considered in the performance prediction of TEG under typical isotope decay heat conditions, and a typical TEG is fabricated and the numerical model is validated by the experimental measured voltage.

## 2. The multi-layer TEG for Mars RTG

## 2.1. The RTG and Martian environment

Fig. 1 illustrates the TEGs in Mars radioisotope power supply based on previous Mars exploration systems. As shown in Fig. 1(a), the radioisotope power supply is composed of three subsystems from outer to inner layers: the cooling, TE conversion and heat source systems. The cooling system features a shell and fins designed for efficient heat dissipation, and TE conversion system mainly consists of multiple TEGs and the insulation structures. The TEGs are longitudinally arranged on each side of the power supply and are interconnected via wires to form a closed circuit, with dedicated potential input and output ports. As shown in Fig. 1(b), the TEG consists of several TE legs (N- and P-type), electrodes, insulation blocks, substrates and heterogeneous interfaces. The TE legs and electrodes (both upper and lower electrodes) are sandwiched between the upper and lower substrates. The insulation blocks are perforated with holes that accommodate the TE legs, and these blocks serve the purpose of blocking lateral heat conduction as well as maintaining one-dimensional heat transfer between the hot and cold sides of the TE legs. The cross-sectional dimension of the TEG is $50 \times 50$ mm, and the N- and P-type TE legs are connected through electrodes to form the closed circuit in the sequence $\mathrm{of~} ^ {\ast} N \mathrm{TE} \mathrm{leg} - \mathrm{electrode} - P$ TE leg − electrode − N TE leg − ”. It should be noted that, as shown in Fig. 1 (b), the single-layer TEG consists of eight couples of TE legs, sixteen couples of electrodes, one insulation block, two substrates and three types of heterogeneous interfaces.

The radioisotope power supply operates in Martian environment to power space equipment systems, so it is necessary to clarify the real application scenarios under interfacial Martian atmosphere to support subsequent temperature and potential field calculations. For the Martian environment, the factors of temperature, density, pressure, wind speed of Martian atmosphere and heat transfer characteristics of the interfacial clearance medium, which affect the performance of radioisotope power supply. In this paper, the temperature range of 190 K to 300 K, corresponding to the Jetzero Crater on Mars, is adopted [45]. Martian atmosphere consists primarily of 95 % carbon dioxide, 3 % nitrogen, 1.6 % hydrogen and few other gases. The average atmospheric density and pressure on Martian surface are about 1 % of those on Earth. In this paper, Martian atmosphere is approximated as pure carbon dioxide with a density equal to 1 % of the Earth’s atmosphere density. The wind speed of Martian atmosphere affects heat dissipation of the radioisotope power source, and the convective heat transfer coefficients ranges from 1.24 W/(K m2 ) to $2.4 ~ \mathrm{W} / ( \mathrm{K} ~ \mathrm{m} ^ {2} )$ at wind speeds of 4 m/s to 15 m/s [46].

The clearance medium influences the TE transfer and conversion between the contact interfaces. In terms of the carbon dioxide fluid on Mars in the interfacial contact clearance, the Knudsen number (Kn) is used to determine the state of the fluid. When $K n < 0.01$ , the fluid exhibits the continuous state; when $0.01 < K n < 10 ,$ , the fluid exhibits the slightly dilute state; when $K n > 10$ , the fluid exhibits the extremely dilute state (free molecular state). Kn is calculated as Eq. (1):

$$K n = \frac {{{\lambda _ {l}}}} {l}\tag{1}$$

where $\lambda _ {l}$ is the average molecular free path; l is the characteristic length in the fluid and equals the average distance of the clearance in contacting pairs. λl is calculated as Eq. (2):

$$\lambda _ {l} = \frac {k _ {b} T} {\sqrt {2} \pi \sigma _ {\ell} ^ {2} P}\tag{2}$$

where $k _ {b}$ is Boltzmann’s constant; $\sigma _ {\ell}$ is the average collision diameter of gas molecules. The average molecular free path of carbon dioxide molecules in Martian atmosphere is $5 \times 10 ^ {- 6}$ m [47]. The Kn of the carbon dioxide fluid ranges from 0.001 to 0.1, suggesting that the carbon dioxide on Mars exists in the slightly dilute state. The thermal conductivity of fluid is calculated as Eq. (3):

$$\kappa = \frac {\kappa _ {p}} {1 + \frac {2 - \alpha} {\alpha} \frac {4 \varepsilon _ {T}} {\gamma + 1} K n}\tag{3}$$

where $\kappa _ {p}$ is thermal conductivity at standard atmospheric pressure; α is thermal adaptation coefficient; εT is specific heat ratio. In this paper, the research of Bhandari et al. [48] is referenced (research considers the slightly dilute state of the carbon dioxide on Mars), the thermal conductivity of interfacial Martian atmosphere can be fitted with an experimental correlation of the temperature $T ,$ as shown in Eq. In the interface contact simulations of this paper, the mesh model exists only in the solid regions; no mesh is present in the interfacial contact clearances. The carbon dioxide is in a slightly dilute state and thus does not affect the mesh conditions. The slightly diluted carbon dioxide gas influences heat transfer solely by affecting the thermal conductivity of the clearance medium.

The RTG consists of a heat sink system, a TE conversion system, and a heat source system from the outside to the inside. Due to the long-term exposure of the heat sink surfaces to the Martian environment, the heat sink surfaces of RTG will be covered by dust. Two cases considering the dust influences, $\mathrm{i.e.,}$ dust on the heat sink surfaces affecting the thermal resistance and emissivity, are investigated. In the cases, the thermal resistance of the heat sink is increased by 10 %, and the emissivity are varied from 0.8 to 0.6.

## 2.2. The radioisotope heat source of TEG

The radioisotope heat source of TEG is illustrated in Fig. 1(a). Its cross-section is designed as a regular hexagon, which offers two main advantages. On the one hand, the temperature is more homogeneous on its sides than in the case of triangles, rectangles, square pentagons, irregular hexagons, etc. and so on. On the other hand, considering the dimension of the TEG and arranging the TEG as much as possible, the square hexagonal cross-section is more advantageous than the circular and elliptical cross-section. Regular polygons with more than six sides are unsuitable for TEG heat source cross-section because of the insufficient side length to match the TEG. The insulation is made of low-density carbon-bonded carbon fiber (CBCF), which prevents the decay heat from dissipating from the top and bottom of the heat source system, and blocks the inflow of external heat to increase the security. The transition layer is a three-dimensional carbon–carbon composite material designed to provide structural support and physical protection to the internal heat-generating regions. At the same time, the transition layer also satisfies the design of shielding radioisotope heat source to release particles. The heat distribution layer is made of high thermal conductivity carbon–carbon material with the side length of 66 mm, and the outer side is in contact with the hot side of the TEG. It is used to uniformly distribute the decay heat on the TEG, providing the continuous, stable and consistent hot side environment for TEGs.

Table 1 lists several radioisotope heat sources and their parameters. For Mars exploration, the radioisotope heat sources are required to exhibit high power density (>0.2 W/g), sufficiently long half-life (0.3 to 200 years), easy shielding of the generated radiation, and favorable physical and chemical properties (e.g., high melting point, corrosion resistance, easy availability and low toxicity). In early space exploration missions, 238Pu was widely utilized for radioisotope heat sources due to its moderate half-life (87.74 years), high power density (0.461 W/g), low radiation and low shielding system requirements. However, the melting point $\mathrm{of} ^ {238} \mathrm{Pu}$ is relatively low (913 K), and $^ {238} \mathrm{P}$ u melting into liquid at high temperatures can affect the safety and performance of radioisotope power sources. $^ {238} \mathrm {{P u O} _ {2}}$ is more suitable as the radioisotope heat source because of its extremely high melting point (2503 K) and highly chemical inertness. Although the power density of $^ {238} \mathrm {{P u O} _ {2}}$ is slightly lower than that of $^ {238} \mathrm{Pu} , \ ^ {\bar {2} 38} \mathrm{PuO} _ {2}$ enables operation at higher operating temperatures. Moreover, even if it is destroyed, the ceramic $^ {238} \mathrm {{P u O} _ {2}}$ tends to break into large pieces rather than inhalable radioactive particles, which substantially improves safety. In this paper, $^ {238} \mathrm {{P u O} _ {2}}$ is used as the fuel of radioisotope heat source. The thermal power $Q _ {H}$ of the radioisotope heat source is $Q _ {H} = m q _ {H}$ , where $q _ {H}$ is the radioisotope power density.

Fig. 2 shows the multi-layer TEG with multiple interfaces. As shown in left portion of Fig. 2, the TEG is composed of the high-, mid- and lowtemperature TE materials formulated with optimal operating conditions. The single-layer TEG configuration does not solve the problem of the limited applicable temperature range of the single TE material. The multi-layer TEG configuration not only solves the feasibility problem in wide temperature range, but also maximizes the use of temperature difference. The multi-layers TEG contains a total of twenty-one heterogeneous contacting interfaces (seven for each TE layer), and these interfaces are categorized into seven distinct types of contacting pairs: with seven types of contacting pairs: “S-E”, N-type of “E-TE-H”, P-type of “E-TE-H”, N-type of “E-TE-M”, P-type of “E-TE-M”, N-type of “E-TE-L” and P-type of “E-TE-L”. The temperature range of hightemperature, PbTe-based mid-temperature and BiTe-based low-temperature TE materials are about 200 to 650 K, 300 to 900 K and 500 to 1300 $\mathrm{K,}$ respectively. The properties of TE materials are shown in Table 2, and Seebeck coefficient, thermal conductivity, electrical conductivity and figure of merit (ZT) are shown in Fig. 3 [49]. In the calculation, for low-temperature TE materials within the 600–650 K range, the material properties corresponding to 600 K are employed. As shown in right portion of Fig. 2, for the contacting interfaces, the electrical flow is transmitted through the real contact area, and can also be transmitted through the silver-epoxy adhesive in the clearance medium. Fig. 5 shows the optimization of multi-layer TEG considering interfacial contact effects. In this paper, the maximum gravimetric power density of the multi-layer TEG is selected as the optimization objective.

On one hand, the output power is a core parameter for evaluating TE performance. It is directly influenced by the temperature difference between the hot and cold sides of the TE material and the internal electrical resistance. On the other hand, the design of radioisotope power supply for Mars exploration requires lightweight structures. The gravimetric power density provides a comprehensive metric considering both output power and structural mass. The lengths of TE legs $h _ {1} , h _ {2} , h _ {3}$ and the diameter of the TE legs d are taken as the optimization variables. The optimization model is described as Eq. (5):

$$\begin{array} {r l} {\mathrm{find}} & {\pmb {X} _ {C E} = \left[ h _ {1} , h _ {2} , h _ {3} , d \right] ^ {T}} \\ & {\operatorname* {m a x} \quad \varpi \left( \pmb {X} _ {C E} \right)} \end{array}$$

$$\begin{array} {r l} {\mathrm{s.t.}} & {T _ {A l} ( {\pmb X} _ {C E} ) \leq 570 \quad \mathrm{K}} \\ & {T _ {G W - T E} \leq 1300 \quad \mathrm{K}} \\ & {T _ {Z W - T E} \leq 900 \quad \mathrm{K}} \\ & {T _ {D W - T E} \leq 650 \quad \mathrm{K}} \end{array}\tag{5}$$

where $\varpi ( {\bf X} _ {C E} )$ is the gravimetric power density of TEG; $T _ {A l} ( {\pmb X} _ {C E} )$ is the temperature of Aluminum alloy shell; $T _ {G W - T E} , \ T _ {G W - T E}$ and TDW− TE are the maximum applicable temperature of high-, mid- and lowtemperature layers, respectively. The gravimetric power density of TEG is calculated as Eq. (6):

$$\varpi ( {\bf X} _ {C E} ) = \frac {P _ {o u t p u t}} {M _ {T E G - t o t a l}}\tag{6}$$

where $P _ {o u t p u t}$ is the output power of multi-layer TEG; $M _ {T E G \_ t o t a l}$ is the mass of multi-layer TEG, which equals the sum of the mass of the TE legs, substrates, insulation blocks and electrodes. The actual output power of the TEG varies with the external load resistance. In this work, the term “output power” refers to the maximum output power which represents the output capability of the TEG. The output power reaches its maximum value when the external load resistance equals the internal resistance, as shown in Eq. (7):

$$P _ {o u t p u t} = \frac {\left( \int _ {T _ {N h}} ^ {T _ {N h}} | S _ {N} | d T + \int _ {T _ {P c}} ^ {T _ {P h}} | S _ {P} | d T \right) ^ {2}} {4 R _ {i n t e r n a l}}\tag{7}$$

where $s _ {N}$ and $S _ {P}$ is the Seebeck coefficient of N-type and P-type TE legs, respectively; $R _ {i n t e r n a l}$ is the internal resistance of TEG. As shown in Fig. 5 (a), in this work, structural parameters with high sensitivity to the gravimetric power density of the multi-layer TEG are selected as optimization variables. The $h _ {1} ,$ h2 and h3 significantly affect the thickness of the high-, mid- and low-temperature TE layers, and the optimization interval of above are from 5 to 20 mm. The d affects the mass and internal resistance of the TE legs, and determines the volume of the insulation block. The optimization interval of d is from 5 to 10 mm. The thickness of the substrates and electrodes are determined by the machining process, and the thickness of the substrates and electrodes are 2 mm and 0.5 mm, respectively. In the optimization model, the working temperatures of TE materials have been limited, that $\mathbf {i} s ,$ the hot side temperature of high-, medium-, and low-temperature TE materials cannot exceed 1300, 900 and 650 K, respectively. As shown in Fig. 5(b), the optimization variables h1, h2, h3 and d in conjunction with equivalent interfacial layers for modeling. The model is subjected to the finite element calculation, focusing on the temperature range of the TE materials, the lower surface temperature of the TEG, the power supply and the structure mass.

For structural optimization, the Nelder-Mead algorithm in COMSOL Multiphysics 6.1 is used. The Nelder-Mead optimization algorithm is a method to solve single-objective and multi-parameter optimization problems with constraints. This algorithm the advantages of relatively simple algorithm programming, low computational cost and unlimited requirements for the derivability of objective function, making it particularly suitable for engineering problems. However, it has the disadvantage that it may fall into the local optimal solution. Therefore, different initial values need to be set during optimization process to ensure that the TEG configuration is the global optimal solution. The convergence criteria is indicated as the design tolerance and set to be 0.01, which means that the convergence requires the results (gravimetric power density) of two iterations to be within a 0.01 difference. The maximum number of model calculations is set to 1000. The optimization is calculated by the 12 cores with the CPU of 12th Gen Intel (R) Core (TM) i5-12500. In this work, the calculation time of structural optimization is about 0.5 to 1 h.

## 3.2. Interfaces reconstruction and TCR, ECR calculations

The microscopic roughness characteristic of engineering surfaces leads to the clearance between the contacting interfaces affecting the TE transfer and conversion process, thus the accurate description of rough surfaces is essential to the evaluation of interfacial contact effects. The

W-M fractal method has the advantage of effectively capturing the geometric self-similarity and self-affinity of rough surfaces, allowing the observation of similar surface topographies at different magnification. In this paper, fractal W-M function with contact-type measurement is employed to characterize the surface topography. The above method first requires measuring the average roughness values (Ra) of the actual surface. Subsequently, based on the measurement results, a rough surface with minimal deviation is constructed. A three-dimensional fractal surface topography can be reconstructed as Eq. (14) [50,51]:

$$\begin{array} {c} {{\displaystyle z ( x , y ) = L _ {0} ( \frac {G} {L _ {0}} ) ^ {D _ {f} - 2} \cdot ( \frac {\ln \gamma _ {0}} {M} ) ^ {1 / 2} \cdot \sum _ {m = 1} ^ {M} \sum _ {n = 0} ^ {n _ {\mathrm{max}}} \gamma ^ {( D _ {f} - 3 ) n}}} \\ {{\displaystyle \times \left\{\cos \varphi _ {m , n} - \cos \left\{\frac {2 \pi \gamma _ {0} ^ {n} ( x ^ {2} + y ^ {2} ) ^ {1 / 2} )} {L _ {0}} \cdot \cos [ \tan ^ {- 1} ( \frac {y} {x} ) - \frac {\pi m} {M} ] + \varphi _ {m , n} \right\} \right\}}} \end{array}\tag{14}$$

where $D _ {f} ( 2 < D _ {f} < 3 )$ is the fractal dimension of the profile, and determines the contribution of high and low frequency components; $L _ {0}$ is the sample length; G is the scaling constant;γ(γ = 1.5) is a scaling parameter; M is the number of ridges; n is a frequency index; $\varphi _ {m , n} ( 0 <$ $\varphi _ {m , n} < 2 \pi )$ is a random phase. The fractal dimension is a key parameter for characterizing the topography of rough surfaces. Fig. 6 shows the contact-type measurement of rough surfaces, and the rough surfaces of the substrates, electrodes and TE legs are measured by surface roughness tester TR200. In this work, the accuracy can be described by the deviation of Ra between the simulated surface and the real surface. The Ra of the substrates, electrodes and TE legs is 4.107, 3.215 and 1.799 μm, respectively. The Ra of the reconstructed surfaces of substrates, electrodes and TE legs is 4.402, 3.350 and 1.683 μm (with the deviations of Ra are 7.18 %, 4.20 % and 6.44 %), respectively.

The TCR can be calculated by Eq. (15):

$$\mathrm{TCR} = \frac {\Delta T} {q _ {c} + q _ {r}} = \sum _ {i = 1} ^ {n} \frac {1} {h _ {i}}\tag{15}$$

where $\Delta T$ is the average temperature difference of two contact interfaces; $q _ {c}$ is the heat flux of solid conduction; qr is the heat flux of thermal radiation; where hi is the thermal contact conductance (TCC) of each contact element defined by Eq. (16):

$$h _ {i} = \left\{\begin{array} {c} {{h _ {c c} ( d _ {i} = 0 )}} \\ {{h _ {m c} + h _ {r} ( d _ {i} \neq 0 )}} \end{array} \right.\tag{16}$$

where $d _ {i}$ is the distance of the corresponding real contact elements; where $h _ {c c}$ is the TCC for the real contact elements; $h _ {m c}$ is the TCC of clearance medium for the non-contact elements; and $h _ {r}$ is the TCC of equivalent thermal conductance for thermal radiation. In this work, the TCC for the real contact elements is assumed to be perfect and given as 1 $\times ~ 10 ^ {10} ~ \mathrm {W / ( m ^ {2} {\cdot} K )}$ . For the non-contact elements, $h _ {m c}$ equals to 0 when the clearance medium is vacuum and equal to the thermal conductivity divided by the clearance distance when the medium is gass or silverepoxy adhesive.

The ECR can be calculated by Eq. (17):

$$\mathrm{ECR} = {\frac {\Delta U} {j}}\tag{17}$$

where $\Delta U$ is the average electric potential difference of two contacting interfaces.

## 3.3. Roughness-scale contact model

Fig. 7 depicts the meshed models and boundary conditions of roughness-scale contact model. In this work, the computationally efficient strategy of combining hexahedral elements with tetrahedral elements is employed, with a preference for hexahedral elements where feasible. As shown in left portion of Fig. 7, the rough regions of contacting pairs are meshed with tetrahedral elements, while the others are meshed with hexahedral elements. The contacting pairs model of E-TE consists of 437,865 elements (367,945 tetrahedral elements and 69,920 hexahedral elements) and 157,806 nodes, and that of S-E consists of 406,043 elements (393,893 tetrahedral elements and 12,150 hexahedral elements) and 109,103 nodes. In the analysis of contact mechanics, heat transfer and electrical conduction, the tetrahedral and hexahedral element types are C3D4 and C3D8R for “3D Stress”, DC3D4 and DC3D8 for “Heat transfer”, and DC3D4E and DC3D8E for “Thermoelectric”, respectively. When the number of meshes increases or decreases by 5 %, the deviation of the dimensionless real contact area is less than 3.7 %, indicating that the results are mesh independent. As shown in right portion of Fig. 7, the boundary conditions of roughness-scale contact model are shown. For the contacting pair of E-TE, the simulation consists of three processes: contact mechanics, heat transfer and electrical conduction; for the contacting pair of $\mathrm {\mathbf {S} - \mathbf {E} ,}$ the simulation consists of two processes: contact mechanics and heat transfer, and no electrical conduction is involved due to the electrically insulating substrates in S-E. In this work, RP denotes a reference point located on surface P1, and an “MPC beam” constraint is established between $R P$ and surface P1. The side surfaces of the contacting pair are defined as surface P3. In terms of contact mechanics, due to the difficulty of convergence when directly applying loading pressure in contact calculations, specified displacement $w _ {c}$ is applied to “MPC beam” instead of loading pressure. The displacements u and v on surfaces P1 and P3, and displacements u, v, and w on surface P2, are all constrained to zero. In terms of heat transfer, temperature T1 is applied on surface P1 and temperature T2 is applied on surface P2, while surface P3 remains adiabatic. In terms of electrical conduction, the potential U1 is applied on surface P1, potential U2 is applied on surface P2, and surface P3 is kept insulative. ## 4.2. Measurement of TCR and TEG output voltage

An experiment platform in the authors’ previous work [44] was developed to indirectly measure the TCR. ## 5.1.2. Heat transfer and electrical conduction

Fig. 13 shows the temperature distribution of contacting pairs. Fig. 13(a) displays the temperature distribution of the “S-E”, “E-TE-H”, “E-TE-M” and “E-TE-L” under different interfacial clearances. Fig. 14 illustrates the potential distribution of contacting pairs. As shown in Fig. 14(a), the potential distribution between electrodes and TE legs is presented under different interfacial clearances. The potential distributions under vacuum, Earth’s atmosphere and Martian atmosphere clearances are identical. Similarly, under the vacuum clearance, the absence of the gaseous medium renders these regions non-conductive. The potential gradient under vacuum/atmosphere clearance is higher than that under silver-epoxy adhesive clearance. The above results indicate that ECR under vacuum/atmosphere clearance is higher than that under silver-epoxy adhesive clearance. As shown in Fig. 14(b), the potential distribution between electrodes and mid-temperature N-type TE legs is presented under different loading pressures. The potential gradient decreases with the increase of the loading pressure, thus ECR decreases with the increase of loading pressure. The potential gradient under silver-epoxy adhesive clearance is lower than that under vacuum/atmosphere clearance.

The cooling fins of the radioisotope power supply improve the performance of the radioisotope power supply by enhancing heat dissipation, but excessive dimension of the cooling fins leads to temperature drops significantly, resulting in a decrease in the performance of power supply. The optimal dimension of cooling fins of heat sink system is 50 × 4 mm, and the gravimetric power density and the conversion efficiency of designed RTG are 1.998 W/kg and 7.189 %, respectively. ## 6. Conclusion

In this work, a multi-layer TEG composed of high-, mid- and lowtemperature TE materials is formulated for the power supply of Mars exploration. A two-scale contact model is established to clarify the thermal-electrical interfacial effects between multiple layers. In the roughness-scale, the thermal and electrical contact resistances under interfacial vacuum, Earth’s atmosphere, Martian atmosphere and silverepoxy adhesive clearances are numerically calculated and the numerical results are experimentally validated. In the TEG-scale, the contact effect is equivalent to an additional layer and considered in the structural optimization with the objective of maximum gravimetric power density under typical radioisotope decay heat conditions. The following conclusions are drawn:

1. The TCR decreases with the increase of loading pressure and is influenced by the average interfacial temperature and interfacial clearance medium. Under Martian atmosphere, TCR decreases with the increase of the average interfacial temperature, and similar rules are also presented under Earth’s atmosphere and silver-epoxy adhesive ones. The TCR of contacting pairs between substrate and electrode decreases from $2.961 \times 10 ^ {- 4} \mathrm{to} 1.906 \times 10 ^ {- 4}$ in the range of 261.0 to 923.6 K under Martian atmosphere clearance.

2. The ECR decreases with the increase of loading pressure and is influenced by the interfacial clearance medium. When the loading pressure increases from 1.201 to 3.041 MPa, the ECR decreases from

$2.578 \times 10 ^ {- 8} \mathrm{to} 1.019 \times 10 ^ {- 8} \mathrm{and} 6.778 \times 10 ^ {- 10} \mathrm{to} 6.711 \times 10 ^ {- 10} \Omega$ $\mathtt {m} ^ {2}$ under vacuum/atmosphere and silver-epoxy adhesive clearances, respectively.

3. The numerical prediction models of TCR and ECR are validated by experimental measurement with the maximum deviations of 15.4 % and 4.59 %, respectively. Additionally, the TEG performance evaluation model is verified with maximum deviation of 12.1 % in output voltage.

4. The interfacial contact affects the TEG performance. Compared with the ideal model, the conversion efficiency considering the interfacial contact effects decreases by 63.28 %, 61.76 % and 61.89 % under vacuum, Earth’s atmosphere and Martian atmosphere clearances, respectively. The output power of TEG under Martian atmosphere is 3.803 % higher than that under vacuum, and 0.3352 % lower than that under Earth’s atmosphere.

5. The gravimetric power density and TE conversion efficiency of the multi-layer TEG are 3.514 W/kg and 8.444 % in Martian environment, respectively. The maximum gravimetric power density, conversion efficiency and output power of radioisotope power supply are 1.998 W/kg, 7.189 % and 107.8 W, respectively.

The limitations of this study are listed: Firstly, the proposed TEG configuration is conducted under the assumption of constant heat source power, without considering its attenuation over time. Additionally, this study focuses on designing for one combination of high-, mid, and lowtemperature TE materials. The optimal TEG configuration is sensitive to TE material properties, and variations in input parameters of TE material properties exceeding 10 % can lead to changes in the optimal TEG configuration.
