## 1. Introduction

Composites are widely used, for instance, in the aerospace industry, where they are frequently subjected to a harsh service environment, e.g. severe thermal conditions. The temperature of certain location in highspeed aerospace vehicles may increase from room temperature to above 1000 °C in a short time of 600 s [1]. Under this condition, the thermal expansion behaviors of relevant composites and structures should be carefully considered, since the mismatch of thermal expansion coefficient between different phases may lead to damages of the composite, and that between different composites may lead to final failures of the structure.

Composite consists of two or more phases and is always designed to have advantages of each phase. Before its application, the corresponding behaviors and effective properties should be well understood primarily. The effective properties of composites can be calculated by a representative volume element (RVE) model based on the known properties of each phase. The prediction method has mainly two types, the analytical method and the numerical method. Classical analytical methods includes the self-consistent method, the differential method, the Mori-Tanaka method, etc., in which the Mori-Tanaka method is widespread for its explicit formula [2]. In comparison with the numerical method like Finite Element (FE) method, such analytical methods are much more efficient in model reconstruction and computational cost saving. The FE method, the Mori-Tanaka method and the strong contrast method (a statistical method) are compared in Ref. [3] for two-phase random composites. The results of Ref. [3] show that the Mori-Tanaka method is more efficient, whereas has some limitations such as the inclusions should have ellipsoidal shapes of low aspect ratios; the strong contrast method has good performance for spherical inclusions; while the FE method is more capable in capturing details like the local physical fields. According to Ref. [3], excluding the larger computational cost, another disadvantage of FE method is its dependency on the size of RVE. This should be due to the complexity and the stochasticity of composite structures, for which only approximate RVE and boundary conditions (BCs) rather than accurate ones can be obtained. On the other hand, for composites with symmetric structures (ordered structures), an accurate RVE can be formulated based on the symmetries and precise boundary conditions can be derived based on additional physical rules. Under this condition, the FE result is independent of the size of RVE. Such RVE is always expressed as unit cell.

A unit cell model should contain enough mesoscopic structure information, while for the macroscopic characteristics such as the related macro physical field that have been abandoned in the RVE formulation, we need to derive appropriate BCs to keep enough representation. Therefore, a unit cell model has two essential elements, the identification of structure symmetries and the derivation of accurate BCs. Textile reinforced composite is a typical composite with symmetric structures and has extensive applications in the present aerospace industry [4]. According to the textile process, there are plain/twill/satin woven, multi-directional braided, knitted composites, etc. To be general, the weave process is always used to create two-dimensional (2D) laminate structures, while the knitting process is good at manufacturing three-dimensional (3D) structure, and the braiding process is in between. Although different textile technologies create entirely different structures, all the complex textile structure can be decomposed into simple unit based on three basic symmetries, i.e., translation along an axis, reflection about a plane and rotation about an axis, and thus we can propose some basic rules of unit cell formulation and BC derivation for all the textile reinforced composite.

Boundary conditions are the relations between the micro unit cell model and the macro real composite (essential conditions of homogenization). In fact, in the unit cell formulation, the most consideration should be paid on the derivation of BCs. In most literatures about the mechanical or thermal calculation, only a full unit cell formulated by translational symmetries is used, and for such a model the accurate BC is the so-called periodic BC, i.e., periodic relative nodal displacement for mechanical problems [5–12] and periodic relative nodal temperature for thermal problems [13–18]. Such type BC is relative simpler and easier to be imposed on. However, if the reflectional and rotational symmetries are involved, the BC would be more complex and its implementation would raise some particular requirement of mesh generations. On the other hand, a better understanding of the composite behaviors and the cost saving brought by such size-reduced unit cell model are very attractive.

In the authors’ previous works [19–22], structure symmetries of plain woven, satin woven, 3D four-directional braided composites are fully exploited, corresponding BCs for thermal calculations are derived and effective thermal conductivities for each composite are studied. On the other hand, for mechanical problems, in Li’s work [23], the unidirectional (UD) fiber reinforced composite with both square and hexagonal fiber packing patterns are studied, unit cells for each pattern are formulated by using only translational symmetries, and corresponding mechanical BCs are derived. Li and his team also contribute a lot of extraordinary works on the unit cell method for mechanical studies of various textile reinforced composite [24,25].

Thermal expansion is a thermal-mechanical coupling problem, a fully study and summary of unit cell method for such problem is still absent. Dasgupta et al. [26] studied the thermal mechanical and also thermal conductive properties of plain woven composites by both FE and analytical methods based on a periodic unit cell. Shabana et al. [27] studied the effective thermomechanical properties of functionally graded composites, in which both the square and hexagonal arrangements of fibers are simulated. Nasution et al. [28] studied global effective and micro local thermomechanical properties of 3D orthogonal interlock composites. One can also find a series of excellent previous works about UD, woven, braided composites and interpenetrating phase composites [7,29–37]. To be summarized, the homogenization and unit cell methods are the basic methods used in the above typical researches, and all the involved unit cell models are in fact formulated based on translational symmetries only and are expressed as periodic ones in [26–28]. This leaves some room for us to employ the structure symmetries completely, to further reduce the model size and to develop a general unit cell approach for thermal expansion calculations. It also should be pointed out that the authors’ previous work [19–22] are mainly focused on the effective thermal conductive properties, the BCs are derived based on thermal conduction theory, and the unit cell models can only be used to simulate thermal conduction problems. For the thermal expansion problem studied in this work, the same geometric configurations involved in the above references are available, however, the BCs need new derivations. $\mathsf{Also} ,$ for each typical composite studied in the present work, the influence of structure symmetries on the deformation pattern is clarified based on the investigation of displacement fields on the model boundaries.

In this paper, the displacement distribution rules that can be used in BC derivations are summarized based on basic symmetric structures (translational, reflectional and 180° rotational symmetric structures), and the application scope of such rules is defined according to the mechanical and thermal considerations. For UD composites, four unit cells are formulated by different symmetries, and then a general unit cell approach for thermal expansion calculation is developed. For plain and multi-harness (4HS, 5HS, 6HS, 7HS and 8HS) satin woven composites, three and two unit cells are formulated, respectively, and the thermal expansion behaviors are studied.

## 2. Mathematical formulation

## 2.1. Constitutive equations

For a thermal conduction problem, the constitutive relation between macroscopic heat flux and temperature gradient can be described as Eq. (1):

$$q _ {i} ^ {0} = - \lambda ^ {0} \nabla T _ {i} ^ {0} , ~ i = x , y , z\tag{1}$$

where $q _ {i} ^ {0}$ is the macroscopic (global) heat flux, $\lambda ^ {0}$ is the effective thermal conductivity matrix, $\nabla T _ {i} ^ {0}$ is the temperature gradient, and i is the component direction in x, y and z-directions. The superscript ${} ^ {\mathfrak {a}} 0 ^ {\mathfrak {n}}$ in this work represents the macroscopic variables or effective properties. For a specific composite model, if one of $q _ {i} ^ {0}$ or $\nabla T _ {i} ^ {0}$ is given as the loading, the other one will be obtained by an appropriate numerical calculation, and the effective thermal conductivities can then be obtained based on Eq. (1).

In a thermal expansion problem, the stress and strain due to the temperature change should be concerned. Similarly, the constitutive relationship between stress and strain can be described by Eqs. (2-1), and in a pure thermal expansion process of orthotropic materials, the thermal strain due to the temperature change is expressed by Eq. (2-2).

$$\sigma ^ {0} = \mathbf {C} ( \varepsilon ^ {0} - \alpha ^ {0} T )\tag{2-1}$$

$$\varepsilon _ {i t h} ^ {0} = \alpha _ {i} ^ {0} T , i = x , y , z\tag{2-2}$$

where $\sigma ^ {0}$ and $\varepsilon ^ {0}$ are the macroscopic thermal stress and strain, respectively, C is the stiffness matrix, $\alpha _ {i} ^ {0}$ is the thermal expansion coefficient, T is the temperature offset from the reference temperature at which no thermal stress is induced. The reference temperature in this work is assumed to be 0 °C. In case of pure temperature loading, the term $\sigma ^ {0}$ equals to zero, since in such a case, the macroscopic strain $\varepsilon ^ {0}$ equals the macroscopic thermal strain $\alpha ^ {0} T$ . For a specific numerical simulation, a uniform temperature T is imposed on the model, the thermal strain $( x - , y - ,$ z-) can be obtained by post-processing and the thermal expansion coefficient at T can be calculated based on Eq. (2-2).

However, for a unit cell, we need additional constraint BCs except for the macroscopic loading. As discussed above, the unit cell is established based on three basic structure symmetries, i.e., translation along an axis, reflection about a plane and rotation about an axis for a certain angle. Hence, before the derivation of such constraint BCs, the temperature and displacement distribution rules in these basic symmetric structures should be stated first.

## 2.2. Temperature and displacement distributions in symmetric structures

Three typical structures, i.e., translational, reflectional and 180° rotational symmetric structures are studied in this work, and the relative temperature relations between symmetric nodes and the relative displacement relations between symmetric nodes are stated. As discussed in the authors’ previous work [22], the relative temperature relations between symmetric nodes M and M’ under uniform temperature gradient field can be described as:

$$T _ {M} - T _ {O} = \pm ( T _ {M ^ {\prime}} - T _ {O ^ {\prime}} )$$

$${\mathrm {\Lambda ^ {\alpha} + \mathrm {\Lambda ^{\circ}} : \ s y m m e t r i c \ t h e r m a l \ s t i m u l u s}} ; {\mathrm{}} ^ {\alpha} - \mathrm {\Lambda ^ {\rho} : \ a n t i s y m m e t r i c \ t h e r m a l \ s t i m u l u s}\tag{3}$$

where O is the reference node and $o '$ is its symmetric node. The symmetric thermal stimulus (STS) is the macroscopic heat flux that parallel to the reflection plane or the 180° rotation axis, while the antisymmetric (ATS) one is that perpendicular to the reflection plane or the rotation axis. In addition, for a translational structure, all the macroscopic heat flux can be considered as STS. Based on Eq. (3), one can derive BCs of unit cells for thermal conduction calculations.

However, for a thermal expansion problem, the relative displacement distribution rules should be stated. Under a macroscopic uniform strain field, we can have relative displacement relations between M and M′ as:

$$\big ( x _ {M} - x _ {O} \big ) \big ( u _ {M} - u _ {O} \big ) = \big ( x _ {M ^ {\prime}} - x _ {O ^ {\prime}} \big ) \big ( u _ {M ^ {\prime}} - u _ {O ^ {\prime}} \big )\tag{4-1}$$

$$\big ( y _ {M} - y _ {O} \big ) \big ( \nu _ {M} - \nu _ {O} \big ) = \big ( y _ {M ^ {\prime}} - y _ {O ^ {\prime}} \big ) \big ( \nu _ {M ^ {\prime}} - \nu _ {O ^ {\prime}} \big )\tag{4-2}$$

$$\big ( z _ {M} - z _ {O} \big ) \big ( w _ {M} - w _ {O} \big ) = \big ( z _ {M ^ {\prime}} - z _ {O ^ {\prime}} \big ) \big ( w _ {M ^ {\prime}} - w _ {O ^ {\prime}} \big )\tag{4-3}$$

where u, v and w are displacements in $x \mathrm{,~} y -$ and z-direction, respectively. It should be noted that Eq. (4) only describes the loading conditions under a uniform temperature change (thermal expansion) without considerations of macroscopic shear stress or strain. The above equations can be used to derive BCs of unit cells for thermal expansion calculations, and they will be confirmed by numerical simulations for translational, reflectional and 180° rotational symmetric structures, respectively, in the following subsections.

## 2.2.5. Application scopes

As discussed above, all the displacement distribution rules (Eqs. (7), (8) and (9)) related to reflectional and rotational symmetries are based on UC1 with periodic BCs (Eqs. (5) and (6)), which means that the derivation of BC of smaller unit cells UC2, UC3… are based on the periodic BCs. However, an improper use of such periodic BCs can be found in some thermal expansion researches. In some works [7,13,33], the periodic relative displacement BC and the periodic relative temperature BC are imposed on the unit cell at the same time to simulate the thermal expansion behaviors. Unfortunately, this is inappropriate. Such two types of BCs enable the unit cell to meet the homogenization requirement of macroscopic uniform strain field and uniform temperature gradient field, respectively. However, a macroscopic uniform strain field and a uniform temperature gradient field could not appear at the same time, which means that such two BCs could not be imposed on the same model. A simple schematic of such statement is shown in Fig. 5, in which the two cases of thermal loadings, i.e., uniform T and uniform T gradient, and the induced x-displacement distributions for a macro structure are displayed. In Fig. 5, ΔT and Δu are the relative temperature and relative displacement, respectively. In Fig. 5(a), the two diagrams are the uniform T and the induced u distribution, respectively. The left one is the uniform $T = 20~^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ , and the induced Δu distribution has a periodic pattern as the right diagram (same with Fig. 2(a)) shows. On the other hand, in the left diagram of Fig. 5(b), a uniform T gradient in x-direction is imposed on the structure. As shown in the enlarged two unit cells, the same temperature distribution pattern and the periodic ΔT can be found. However, as shown in the right diagram of Fig. 5(b), the relevant Δu will distribute in a totally nonperiodic pattern, and under this condition Eq. (6) is no longer an appropriate BC for those repetitive unit cells.

In a clear description, the periodic BC of relative temperature and the periodic BC of relative displacement should not be imposed on a model at the same time. In other words, a thermal-elasticity problem with temperature gradients calls for higher order homogenization theory and may be totally different BCs of the unit cell. Thus, to be summarized, the unit cells and the BCs that about to be derived in this work can be used to simulate the thermal expansion behaviors of composites under a uniform temperature change loading, such as the thermal residual stresses after a specific heat treating, and of course can be used to calculate the effective CTE of composites.

In the later sections, thermal expansion behaviors of several typical composites under uniform temperature change loadings are studied. The work is based on a two-scale consideration, the meso-scale fiber yarn and the macro-scale plain and satin woven composites. For both the yarn and the woven composite, unit cells of multi sizes are formulated based on the symmetries indicated in their structures, and corresponding BCs are derived.

## 3. Development of unit cells

It should be pointed out that all the unit cells’ formulations in this section are based on idealized fiber arrangements or textile structures disregarding flaws linked to the manufacturing processes. The flaw in textile composites has several types, such as the porosities in matrix, the interface between fiber yarns and matrix, the porosities between fibers in fiber yarns, etc. The quantitative descriptions of such flaws are difficult to obtain, thus in this work all the models are established with no considerations of them.

## 4. Numerical modeling

## 4.1. Application of boundary conditions

In order to investigate the thermal expansion behaviors under a specific temperature change T (the reference temperature is assumed to be $0 \ {} ^{\circ} \mathrm{C} ) _ {i}$ , the uniform T should be imposed on the model with appropriate BCs derived in this paper, and then the macroscopic stress or the macroscopic strain induced by the temperature change can be obtained by appropriate post-processing, and the effective thermal expansion coefficient can be calculated by Eq. (2-2).

As shown in Fig. $11 , \varepsilon _ {x} ^ {0} , \varepsilon _ {\nu} ^ {0}$ and $\varepsilon _ {z} ^ {0}$ in equations (Eqs. (6), (10), (11), (12), (13), (14), (15) and (16)) represent the macroscopic (average) strain of the macro structure induced by temperature loadings. If a macro structure is studied, the loading would be a pure temperature change with no concentrated forces. And after the calculation, the macroscopic strain $\varepsilon _ {x} ^ {0} , \varepsilon _ {y} ^ {0}$ and $\varepsilon _ {z} ^ {0}$ could be obtained and then the thermal expansion coefficients could be calculated.

However, for a micro unit cell model, we need additional $\mathtt {B C s}$ which are also the essential condition of homogenization to relate to the macroscopic physical fields. The BCs derived in this work are in fact functions of macroscopic strain vector $( \varepsilon _ {x} ^ {0} , \varepsilon _ {y} ^ {0} , \varepsilon _ {z} ^ {0} )$ . The values of $\varepsilon _ {x} ^ {0} , \varepsilon _ {y} ^ {0}$ and $\varepsilon _ {z} ^ {0}$ are unknown before the calculation, which means that those equations only describe the constraint relations between symmetric (translational, reflectional or rotational) nodes rather than the determined constraints. During the solution, $\varepsilon _ {x} ^ {0} , \varepsilon _ {y} ^ {0}$ and $\varepsilon _ {z} ^ {0}$ are performed as arbitrary degrees of freedom (e.g., x-displacement) for three extra nodes NN1, NN2 and NN3 (not included in the model), respectively. If the loading is a uniform temperature change, after the solution is converged, the values of $\varepsilon _ {x} ^ {0} , \varepsilon _ {y} ^ {0}$ and $\varepsilon _ {z} ^ {0} \left( \boldsymbol {\mathrm{e}} . \boldsymbol {\mathrm{g}} . , \right$ x-displacement of NN1, NN2 and NN3) can then be obtained as the reaction of the whole model to the temperature loading. The stress prediction is very important for damage analysis. As shown in Fig. 14(d), the stress concentration appears between fibers, which always leads to the initiation of cracks. ## 5.4. Normal displacement on boundary planes

In the above subsections, the “relative uniform” normal displacement field on boundary planes means the field seems to be uniform in a large range of legend. For UD composites, according to Eqs. For the other boundary planes, the equations have no such particular constraints. However, if the results of UC1 of UD are carefully investigated, one can find that the normal displacements on all the boundary planes also have uniform values. Considering the succession relations between unit cells, for all the boundary planes of UC2, UC3 and UC4 (excluding $P _ {4} )$ they will have uniform normal displacement distributions. That is, those boundaries without constant value constraints can also have uniform normal displacement distributions. In fact, if the structure in Fig. 6 is observed from a new point of view as shown in Fig. 21(a), UC1 can also be formulated by reflectional symmetries about $P _ {x} ^ {0} , P _ {y} ^ {0}$ and $P _ {z} ^ {0}$ based on a larger unit cell UC0. According to the BC derivations of UC2 and UC3, we know that the reflectional symmetries will result in uniform normal displacement distribution in boundaries. This means that the configuration of UC1 implies reflectional symmetric relations with adjacent unit cells in three directions, and thus will have uniform normal displacement distributions in all the boundaries.

For plain woven composite, according to Eqs. The other boundary planes have no such particular constraints. If the normal displacement fields of UC1 on boundary planes are observed separately, identical ones on $P _ {1} \sim P _ {4}$ can also be noticed. As shown in Fig. 21(b), the UC1 configuration also implies reflectional symmetric relations with adjacent unit cells in x- and y-directions, and thus the boundaries in x- and y-direction will have uniform normal displacement distributions. For the z-direction of plain woven composites, the laminate structure only has translational symmetry.

## 6. Conclusions

This work developed an approach to formulate unit cells to predict effective coefficient of thermal expansion of textile reinforced composites. The displacement distribution patterns in translational, reflectional and $180 ^{\circ}$ rotational symmetric structures are summarized as unified equations and are confirmed by typical calculations. The application scope of the present method is defined in the point views of both thermal and mechanical physics. Multi-size unit cells of unidirectional fiber reinforced, plain woven and multi-harness $( 4 , 5 , 6 , 7$ and 8HS) satin woven composites are formulated, the corresponding BCs are derived, and the thermal expansion calculations are conducted. Four, three and two size-reducing unit cells are formulated for each type of composite, respectively. The developed method can be referenced by any composites with corresponding symmetric structures. The specific novel studies involved in this work are: the summary of relative displacement distribution rules for three basic symmetric structures, four unit cells of different sizes are formulated for UD composite, two smaller unit cells for satin woven composite are formulated, and the deformation pattern on unit cell boundaries and its relations with structure symmetries are clarified.

Several following conclusions can be summarized:

1. For a composite with symmetric structures, the effective coefficient of thermal expansion can be efficiently calculated by a unit cell model. Such unit cell should be formulated based on structure symmetries, and relevant BCs can be derived based on displacement distribution rules summarized in this work. Such unit cell models can also be used to predict the local stress distributions in fiber yarns or textile reinforced composites and thus has potentials for damage analysis of these composites.

2. The boundary conditions of unit cells are derived based on the assumption of uniform macro strain field, which can be resulted in by a uniform temperature loading. Thus, the current unit cell method can only be used to simulate thermal expansion behaviors under a uniform temperature loading, such as the thermal residual stresses after a specific heat treating.

3. For boundaries that can be formulated by reflectional symmetries, the uniform normal displacement and the uniform deformation can be obtained in thermal expansion calculations; for boundaries that can only be formulated based on translational or rotational symmetries, the normal displacement and the relevant deformation are non-uniform.
