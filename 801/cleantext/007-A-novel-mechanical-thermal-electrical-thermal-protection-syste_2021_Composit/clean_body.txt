## 1. Introduction

Hypersonic flights withstand extremely aerodynamic heating during flight [1–4]. With the development of hypersonic engineering, thermal protection system (TPS) [5–8] encounters great challenges, especially from the point of view of energy utilization, the large amount of aerodynamic heat is wasted by the heat resistant and dissipation technologies and thus TPS is low‐efficiency. The improvement of the TPS and the development of heat reusing technologies become the key points of thermal management of hypersonic vehicles.

There are several types of TPS for hypersonic vehicles, such as integrated thermal protection system (ITPS), active cooling thermal protection system (ATPS), ultra‐high temperature ceramics (UHTC), ablative TPS, etc. The application of ITPS in hypersonic vehicles has been widely concerned by scholars, for it has the functions of loadbearing and thermal protection. Lin et al. [9] proposed a series of ITPS with different gradient hollows to improve the structural efficiency. Wei et al. [10] proposed a novel ITPS based on the lightweight C/ SiC pyramidal core lattice sandwich panel. Wang et al. [11] developed an ITPS which incorporates graded insulation materials and multilayer ceramic matrix composite cellular sandwich panels. Li et al. [12] designed a novel ITPS, which was consisted of C/SiC composite corrugated core sandwich plane and insulated aerogel. Xu et al. [13] proposed a multi‐layer ITPS consists of C/SiC panel, a glass wool layer and a Ti lattice sandwich filled with glass wool, and evaluated the heat transfer characteristics of the ITPS samples. Shi et al. [14] fabricated a novel all‐composite sandwich panel with corrugated core, and presented an approximate analytical model for predicting the coupled ablation and thermal response for the composite sandwich panel. The ATPS [15] applied to the hypersonic lunch vehicle can resist the long‐time heating of high heat flux, and can realize the integration of heat protection and heat insulation. Rong‐ et al. [16] Introduced a TPS that unites the opposing jet and platelet transpiration, conducted the cooling effect through numerical research method. Ferrari et al. [17] investigated a high temperature TPS with active cooling accomplished by flowing a gas into the sandwich’s core, and verified the research method by experiments. Lurie et al. [18] developed a TPS includes fibrous insulation and sprinklers, and used the analytic and numerical means optimized the structure. Xie et al. [19] investigated the performance of actively cooled pyramidal core sandwich panels. Besides, UHTC and ablative TPS are usually used in the position with the most severe heat load, such as the front end of the launch vehicle.

Raffaele et.al [20] investigated the thermal response of the UHTC of reusable re‐entry vehicles. For the TPS using ablative materials [21,22], part of the aerodynamic heat will be taken away by its ablation process. Kubota et al. [23] proposed a TPS employs an ablator as a thermal protection material, and used a high‐temperature polyimide carbon fiber‐reinforced plastic sandwich panel as a structure member.

Although the above TPS have their own advantages, some limitations have to be stated: for ITPS, it has thermal short‐circuit effect; for ATPS, it needs additional complicated system to supply consuming coolant and has high‐cost; for $\mathrm{UHTC} ,$ it has low toughness; for ablative TPS, it can only be used once and has the problem of aerodynamic shape change after ablation. What’s more, the above TPS cannot reuse the aerodynamic heat, which is inefficient from the point of view of energy. Thus, some energy reusing technology attracts researchers’ attention.

TE conversion technology [24] is a promising technology in energy reusing, by integrating TE module into TPS, a MTPS [25] can be established. The MTPS has some remarkable advantages: $1 ^ {\mathrm{st}} ,$ , the primary advantage is the integration of three effective functions of load bearing, heat protection and power supply; $2 ^ {\mathrm{nd}}$ , no additional consuming materials or coolants are needed, and it has the potential reusable features; 3rd, part of the power supply can be replaced by the MTPS, thus it can save the internal space of the vehicle to some extent. TE materials are the basis of TE conversion technology, and some scholars reviewed the research progress and applications of TE materials [26–28]. TE generator based on the TE conversation technology has the characteristics of environmental protection, long service life and simple maintenance, which has been concerned by scholars. Xiao et al. [29] established a TE module based on low‐temperature and mid‐temperature TE materials. Chen et al. [30] established a twostage semiconductor TE generator model with external heat exchange and analyzed the performance of the generator. Wang et al. [31] investigated the effective TE conversion behavior of cracked TE composites. In the aspect of study on the performance of TE device, Arora et al. [32] studied the optimization of the maximum output power and thermal efficiency of the two‐stage TE generator. Wang et al. [33] developed a general, three‐dimensional numerical model of TEs with consideration of temperature and electric potential field coupling. Meng et al. [34] developed a three‐dimensional model to investigate the dynamic response characteristics of TE generators. Cui et al. [35] solved the strong nonlinear thermal‐electric‐stress coupled field, and derived the analytical solutions of temperature fields, power output and energy conversion efficiency. Currently, TE conversion technology also has great application prospects in hypersonic vehicles. Cheng et al. [36] proposed a power generation scheme suitable for hypersonic vehicles, which is based on the multistage TE generator. Li and Wang [37] developed an integrated TE module with regenerative cooling system.

The authors also proposed a TE module based on the MTPS concept and evaluated its mechanical‐thermoelectric performance in [38], however, the three limitations of the structure and analysis should be mentioned here: $^ {1 ^ {\mathrm{st}}} ,$ the structure has only one TE stage of high‐temperature TE materials, which leads to low TE conversion efficiency; 2nd, the three‐layer structure is only schematically described and no more details are involved; $3 ^ {\mathrm{rd}}$ , in the thermomechanical analysis, only the thermal stress is simulated, and the influence of aerodynamic force loads is not considered. Thus, this leaves some rooms for this work.

In this paper, a MTPS unit with mesoscopic details is proposed. The MTPS unit is assumed to be the TPS of the second‐stage compression surface of a hypersonic launch vehicle. The aerodynamic force and heat flux at the specific position are obtained based on the whole vehicle analysis with a typical trajectory. The mechanical‐thermal‐electrical performance of the MTPS unit is then evaluated.

## 2. The vehicle and the MTPS

Fig. 1 is the schematic of the vehicle and the MTPS. The vehicle has a length of 32 m, a wingspan of 18.8 m, and a fuselage width of 5.1 m. The compression surface of the vehicle has three‐stage surfaces, and more detailed descriptions can be found for a similar vehicle in the authors’ previous work [39]. The MTPS is on the second‐stage compression surface of the vehicle, and the area of the MTPS is $8 \ \mathrm{m} ^ {2}$ The MTPS contains 8 MTPS plates, and each MTPS plate contains 36 MTPS units. 288 TE modules of the MTPS are connected in series to output electricity power.

Fig. 2 is the detailed structure of the MTPS unit proposed in the current work. Fig. 2(a), (b) and (c) show the CAD model, the structure cross‐section and the 3D printed model of the MTPS unit, respectively. As shown in Fig. 2(a), the MTPS unit is composed of a TE module and a gap TPS. The gap TPS is mainly composed of a heat‐resistant layer, two heat insulation layers, wall of the vehicle and a temperature control layer. Each layer is connected by the low‐modulus high‐temperature adhesives. The TE module is fixed on the wall of the vehicle by 12 screws, and the screws are arranged around by the high‐temperature rubber bushings. The TE module is composed of a load‐bearing frame, honeycomb cores, and TE layers. The TE layers include a hightemperature and a mid‐temperature TE layer. Each TE layer contains alumina ceramic layers, electricity conducting plates and TE materials. The high‐temperature and mid‐temperature TE layer share the middle alumina ceramic layer. The TE materials are embedded in the honeycomb cores, and the space between the TE materials and honeycomb cores is filled with fiber insulation material. Notably, the surface of the load‐bearing frame and honeycomb cores are coated with hightemperature resistant insulation coatings.

It should be noted that, the structure in Fig. 2 is an optimized structure based on several preliminary designs. During the evaluation of those pre‐design structures, it was found that the thermal stress concentration always occurs at the edge of the electricity conducting plates, the end face of the TE materials and the boundary surface of the alumina ceramic layers and honeycomb cores. Thus, in this work, the electricity conducting plates are embedded and welded into the TE materials to reduce the thermal stress concentration at the edge of the electricity conducting plate; the TE materials and alumina ceramic layers are connected by the low‐modulus high‐temperature adhesives to reduce the thermal stress concentration at the end face of the TE materials; The side walls of the alumina ceramic layers and honeycomb cores are fixed on the inner wall of load‐bearing frame by the lowmodulus high‐temperature adhesives to reduce the thermal stress concentration at the boundary surface of the alumina ceramic layers and honeycomb cores.

As shown in Fig. 2(b), A‐A and B‐B are sectional drawings along central axis direction of the MTPS unit. C–C and D‐D are sectional drawings along thickness direction of the MTPS unit. For the TE module, the materials of the load‐bearing frame, honeycomb cores and electricity conducting plates are TC11, Ti‐6Al‐4 V and QZr0.2, respectively. The selection of the TE materials mainly considers the operating temperature, thermal conductivity, electronic conductivity, ZT value and mechanical strength according to Refs. [40–46]. The hightemperature and mid‐temperature p‐type TE materials are PbTe‐4molSrTe‐2molNa and β‐Zn4Sb3. The high‐temperature and midtemperature n‐type TE materials are $\mathbf {A g P b _ {18} S b T e _ {20}}$ and (Pb0.95Sn0.05- $\mathbf {T e} ) _ {0.92} ( \mathbf {P b S} ) _ {0.08}$ For the gap TPS, the materials of the heat‐resistant layer, heat insulation layers, wall of the vehicle and temperature control layer are C/C‐SiC, Saffil Al‐fiber, Ti‐6Al‐4V and composite phasechange materials, respectively. The materials of the low‐modulus hightemperature adhesives, screws and rubber bushings are ZS‐1071, Inconel617 and G601, respectively.

Fig. 2(c) is a 3D printed structure of the MTPS unit, which verifies the rationality of the structure design through assembly process. As shown in Table 1, the mass of the MTPS unit is 1156.97 g, including

904.1 g for the TE module and 252.87 g for the gap TPS. In the TE module, the mass of the TE layer is 733.0 g. However, due to the large densities of TE materials, the present MTPS has large equivalent density, and sometimes will lead to relatively high weight. Such disadvantage is expected to be alleviated by the further structure optimization design considering weight reduction.

In summary, the model in this paper has three main improvements compared to the authors’ previous work $[ 38 ] : 1 ^ {s t} :$ , the structure has two TE stages of high‐ and mid‐temperature TE layers, which can bring high TE conversion efficiency; $2 ^ {\mathrm{nd}}$ , the details such as electricity conductive plates, load‐bearing frame, honeycomb cores, adhesive layers are considered in the structure; $3 ^ {\mathrm{rd}}$ , some thermal stress release designs are considered in the structure, such as the electricity conducting plates are embedded into the TE materials, the low‐modulus adhesives are used to connect different components.

The physical properties of C/C‐SiC and Ti‐6Al‐4V alloy can be found in Ref. [47], the physical properties of the composite phasechange materials can be found in Ref. [48], and the physical properties of other relevant materials are given in Table 2, respectively.

In the mechanical‐thermal‐electrical analysis of the MTPS, the multi‐scale method can be described as: we established three models of different scale for the vehicle, MTPS plate (1 $\mathbf {m} ^ {2}$ of the MTPS) and unit; for the top planes of the MTPS plate and unit, the mechanical boundary conditions are the aerodynamic force; for the four side faces of the MTPS unit, the mechanical boundary conditions are the displacements that obtained from the analysis of the MTPS plate, while that for the MTPS plate are those from the whole vehicle. The analysis steps are shown in $\mathrm{Fig.} 3 \colon 1 ^ {\mathrm{st}}$ , the aerodynamic force of the vehicle at its structural analysis points is calculated. The mechanical properties of the vehicle are analyzed, and the load and displacements of the second‐stage compression surface are obtained. $2 ^ {\mathrm{nd}}$ , the MTPS plate model $( 1 ~ \bar {\mathbf {m}} ^ {2}$ of the MTPS) is established. The structural design points of the MTPS are determined according to the load and heat flux of the second‐stage compression surface. The mechanical boundary conditions of the MTPS plate are obtained by displacement interpolation, and the mechanical performance of the MTPS plate is evaluated. $3 ^ {\mathrm{rd}}$ based on the CAD model shown in Fig. 2(a), the numerical model of the MTPS unit is established. The displacement boundary conditions are obtained from the results of the MTPS plate, and the aerodynamic heat flux data can be obtained from the author’s previous article [38], and then the mechanical‐thermal‐electrical performance of the MTPS unit can be evaluated.

The reliability of numerical results can be validated by experimental tests (relevant experimental studies are very limited) or guaranteed by rigorous simulation process. In the authors’ opinion, a valid numerical simulation can be guaranteed mainly from the accuracy of boundary conditions and the dependency of mesh size. MSC Patran/Nastran is used for analysis of the mechanical performance of the vehicle under the aerodynamic force. ABAQUS is used for analysis of the mechanical performance of the MTPS plate and the thermomechanical coupling performance of the MTPS unit. ## 3.4. MTPS unit

After analyzing the mechanical performance of the MTPS plate, the stress distribution of the MTPS plate can be obtained. Derive the displacements of all nodes on the four sides of the most severely loaded load‐bearing frame of the TE module, which are used as the mechanical boundary conditions for the thermomechanical coupling analysis of the MTPS unit.

The model of the MTPS unit is shown in Fig. 2(a), as described above. In ABAQUS, “Tie” constraint is used to bind the two parts with no relative motion. Assuming that all the welding and adhesive surfaces between components of the MTPS unit are in ideal state, “Tie” constraint is used to simulate the interaction between the welding and adhesive surfaces. As shown in Fig. 6(a), the model is meshed by the 3D solid element C3D8T and has 443,356 elements and 835,354 nodes. In ABAQUS, C3D8T possess both mechanical and thermal degrees of freedom and is intended for coupled thermal–mechanical simulations. As shown in Fig. 6(b), the O'x'y'z' coordinate system of the MTPS unit is parallel to the Oxyz coordinate system, and the origin O' is the center position of the bottom plane of the load‐bearing frame. The $O x _ {2} y _ {2} z _ {2}$ coordinate system is parallel to the $O x _ {1} y _ {1} z _ {1}$ coordinate system. P1 to P4 are the four sides of the load‐bearing frame. The position of P1 to P4 in the Ox2y2z2 coordinate system are shown in the upper right side of Fig. 6(b). The boundaries of the MTPS unit are composed of mechanical boundaries and thermal boundaries. For the boundary conditions, we need to consider the aerodynamic heat and force. The aerodynamic heat flux is applied on the top plane of the model, and its variation along with the flight time is considered. The in‐plane size is much larger than the out‐plane size, and the heat transfer in TPS can be considered as one‐dimensional heat conduction in out‐plane direction, thus the heat flux on the side planes can be ignored. However, the in‐plane influence of aerodynamic force has to be considered since the adjacent tension, compression and torsion could not be ignored. In this work, in order to obtain the accurate mechanical boundary conditions of the in‐plane side planes, a series of multi‐scale analyses have been conducted. Such types of boundary conditions provide reliability of the model to some extent. For the mechanical boundaries, the displacements of all nodes on the four sides of the most severely loaded load‐bearing frame are applied to the corresponding nodes on P1 to P4. It should be noted that the displacements of each node obtained from the analysis of the MTPS plate are in the Oxyz coordinate system, and the displacements applied to the corresponding nodes on P1 to P4 are in the O'x'y'z' coordinate system. The aerodynamic force is applied to the top surface of the MTPS unit. For the thermal boundaries, the aerodynamic heat flux is applied on the top plane of the model, and its variation along with the flight time is considered. The in‐plane size is much larger than the outplane size, and the heat transfer in TPS can be considered as onedimensional heat conduction in out‐plane direction, thus the heat flux on the side planes can be ignored, and the four sides of the MTPS unit are set to be adiabatic. The initial temperature is 300 K and the convective heat transfer coefficient of the bottom plane is $10 ~ \mathrm {W / ( m ^ {2} {\cdot} K )}$

It should be noted that in most previous researches, periodic displacements or zero force boundary conditions are imposed on transverse boundaries of MTPS unit, and such type of boundary conditions is only appropriate for units that formed based on translational symmetries of an infinite structure [49,50]. However, the displacement field in Fig. 10 obviously has no periodic pattern, this is because the MTPS plate could not be considered as infinite relative to the MTPS unit. Thus, the multi‐scale analysis is essential to ensure the accuracy of boundary conditions.

## 5. Conclusion

In this work, we developed a multi‐functional TPS concept with the integration of load bearing, thermal protection and power supply, the concept contains 8 MTPS plates in macroscale, and 288 MTPS units in mesoscale. The details including electricity conductive plates, loadbearing frame, honeycomb cores, adhesive layers are designed and their influences on the mechanical‐thermal‐electrical performance of the structure are studied, and a multi‐scale method for the acquirement of accurate mechanical boundary conditions is developed. The results show some conclusions:

1. In the mechanical‐thermal‐electrical studies of the MTPS unit, the load is non‐uniform in the time and space. The structural design points of the MTPS unit are obtained based on the analysis of the whole vehicle, and the proper displacement boundary conditions are essential to obtaining reasonable results.

2. The two‐TE‐layer design can convert aerodynamic heat into electric energy more efficiently, and the average output power and TE conversion efficiency of the MTPS unit are increased from 0.82 W to 0.88 W and 0.45% to 0.51%, respectively.

3. The embedded electricity conducting plates in the TE materials and low‐modulus high‐temperature adhesives can alleviate the thermal stress into affordable values, and the stresses of the bearing components and TE materials of the TE module are less than 650 MPa and 150 MPa, respectively.

4. For the MTPS unit proposed in this work, it has the functions of load bearing, heat protection and hear‐reusing. The MTPS unit is composed of a TE module and a gap TPS. The size of the MTPS unit, TE module and TE layers are 138 × 130 × 29.8 mm, $138 \times 130 \times 23.8$ mm and $108 \times 100 \times 23.8$ mm, respectively. The mass of the MTPS unit, TE module, TE layers and gap TPS are
