## 1. Introduction

For hypersonic vehicles, the long-time high-speed flight in dense atmosphere [1] and the relatively slender configuration bring about severe aerodynamic heating effects. The management of such a large amount of heat is one of the most critical issues to be solved for the development of hypersonic engineering [2]. In traditional design, the vehicle’s structure is insulated from most of the aerodynamic heat by thermal protection systems, whose weight and size could be unaffordable under hypersonic conditions. Thus, new heat management techniques [3] with potential high efficiency should be developed. The thermoelectric (TE) conversion techniques can convert the aerodynamic heat and engine combustion heat into electrical power, the thermal protection system that is integrated with TE generators (TEG) [4–7] has potential efficiency improvement in heat management and is one of the most prospective techniques for the heat management of hypersonic vehicles.

A TEG should consist of the N- and P -type TE legs to convert heat into electricity by Seebeck effect, the electrodes to connect TE legs and form an electric current path, and the insulation materials to reduce the thermal radiation. TE legs are TE materials with specific configurations. When the heat flows through a TEG, P - and N-type TE leg will form a certain temperature gradient, and it will generate a potential based on the Seebeck effect to promote the directional movement of carriers forming a stable current, thus generating electricity. The conversion efficiency of TE materials is usually measured by dimensionless TE merit $\begin{array} {r} {Z T = \frac {S ^ {2} k} {\lambda} T} \end{array}$ , where S represents Seebeck coefficient, k electrical conductivity, λ thermal conductivity, T absolute temperature, and $S ^ {2} \sigma$ power factor [8–11]. In general, TE materials can be divided into inorganic, organic, and hybrid (inorganic-organic), and the shape of the TE legs usually includes ingot-shaped, film-shaped, and etc. [12]. The new film-shaped TE materials have good TE properties and mechanical stability [13,14]. However, due to the low working temperature of the substrate material, the use of the film-shaped TE materials in hypersonic vehicles is limited. For the ingot-shaped TE materials, Skutterudite [15] has gained considerable attention because of its open structure and application potential. CoSb3 [16–18] based skutterudites are narrow bandgap semiconductors having high electrical conductivity and Seebeck coefficient, and the TE performance of bulk CoSb3 has been greatly improved by filling [19], doping [20] and nano-crystallization techniques [21,22]. The bulk CoSb3 has characteristics of non-toxic, cheap, stable and well TE properties under 800 K, thus has potential applications in leeward regions of hypersonic vehicles (wall temperature is smaller than 800 K [23]) to convert the aerodynamic heat into electric energy.

TEGs are widely used in industrial equipment [24], selfpowered devices [13,14,25,26], scramjet [27] and hypersonic vehicles [4–7,28,29]. For TEGs applied on hypersonic vehicles, it can be divided into three categories according to the heat source: $1 ^ {\mathrm{st}} ,$ aerodynamic heat; $2 ^ {\mathrm{nd}}$ , hot fuel heated by aerodynamic heat or combustion heat dissipation; $3 ^ {\mathrm{rd}}$ , engine combustion heat. For TEGs taking the aerodynamic heat as heat source, the authors of this manuscript have designed several configurations of TEGs [4,23,30] and found that the multi-stage structure has higher TE conversion performance. For TEGs taking the hot fuel as heat source, Ref. [6] developed a multi-stage TEG considering the flow in both heat and cold source, and optimized the maximum conversion efficiency to 18.38%. The amazing efficiency is mainly due to the large temperature difference of TEGs and advanced TE materials. For TEGs taking the engine combustion heat as heat source,

Cheng et al. [7] established a single- and a two-stage TEG, and found that the two-stage TEG has greater performance. Ref. [28] established a TEG with a hydrogen-fueled scramjet combustor as a heat source and wall channels filled with liquid sodium as a cold source. Besides, Ref. [29] established a closed-Brayton-cycle and TEG combined power generation system, and raised the electrical power of the TEG by 68.3%. In conclusion, TEG with a large temperature difference has a great application prospect for hypersonic vehicles, and which can be realized through multi-stage structure, improved heat source and application of cold source.

For a TEG, there are two types of TE materials, one electrode material and four key contact interfaces. The TE legs and electrodes have micrometer-scale rough surfaces, and such type of roughness will lead to very limited real contact region and a large proportion of contact gap for the contact interface. The electric current only flows through the real contact region and the current constriction results into the so-called electrical contact resistance (ECR) which will influence the performance of a TEG.

In recent years, a lot of research about theoretical, experimental measurements and numerical predictions have been carried out to study ECR. In terms of theoretical study of ECR, Xu et al. [31] established a dynamic model under vibration conditions, and Ta et al. [32] developed a volumetric contact theory. In terms of experimental measurements of ECR, for rough surfaces under static contact conditions, it can be directly measured by digital multimeters [33,34]; for rough surfaces under dynamic contact conditions, it is necessary to build complex combined sensing devices [35,36]. In addition, ECRs can also be indirectly measured by X-ray computed tomography [37]. The prediction of ECR mainly contains three steps: rough surfaces characterization, contact deformation analysis and electric conduction calculation. Wu et al. [38] established a single micro-scale contacting element model, and Qiu et al. [39] proposed a micro contacting model with more contact characteristics. Riba et al. [40] simulated the random distribution of micro convex bodies by generating a random array of a three-dimensional orthogonal parallelepiped. Atyabi et al. [41] established a three-dimensional multiphase model to predict the ECR between the gas diffusion layer and bipolar plate. Besides, Richard et al. [42] presented a finite element method without characterizing rough surfaces, while the accuracy needs a lot of simulation and test data to support. Nevertheless, the above numerical models of ECR have adopted some assumptions in the construction of rough surfaces, and thus it would be a better way to form rough surface models based on the measured real surface topography.

For the influence of the ECR on TEGs, Shittu et al. [43] and Zhang et al. [44] found that it is very important to consider the ECR in the stage of the design and optimization of TEG. Xu et al. [45] used two amplification factors to set ECR and thermal contact resistance in the interface layer of TECs and found that ECR may cause the most serious performance degradation. Besides, Kim [46] found that the ECR has a notable impact on TEGs with short length of TE legs. In addition, for thermoelectric coolers (TECs) with a similar TE conversion process, Su et al. [47] found that both ECR and thermal contact resistances are important for the cooling performance of the TEC. Especially, the reported research of TEG applied on hypersonic vehicles rarely considered the influence of the ECR. Thus, this leaves some room for this work.

In this paper, a TEG for hypersonic vehicles is established. The ECR and its influences on TEGs are studied by two-scale numerical simulations and experimental measurements. The micrometerscale ECR prediction numerical models are established based on the measured practical topography of TE legs and electrodes. An ECR measurement platform consists of a pressure device, power supply and data acquisition system is developed. The ECRs under different temperatures, loading pressures and gap mediums are predicted and the numerical models are validated by a series of measurements. The influence of ECR on the TE conversion process of the TEG is evaluated by establishing a millimeter-scale model including equivalent interfacial layers of ECR.

## 2. The TEG and material properties of its components

## 2.1. The TEG for hypersonic vehicles

Fig. 1 shows the TEG for the hypersonic vehicle. As shown in the left part of Fig. 1, the vehicle has a length of about 15 m, a wingspan of about 6 m, a height of about 3.2 m, and more detailed descriptions can be found in the authors’ previous work [23]. The TE component is located in the leeward regions of the vehicle. As shown in the A-A sectional drawing part, the TE component consists of a heat-resistant layer and a TE conversion layer. The trajectory of the vehicle is mainly composed of the climb and cruise phases, the working time of the cruise phase accounts for more than 85% of the total time, and the aerodynamic heating is mainly in the cruise phase. During the cruise phase, the temperature of the hot end of the TE conversion system is about 600 to 800 K (see Fig. 7 in the authors’ previous work [23]), and the heatresistant layer makes the TE conversion layer works in a suitable temperature range. In this work, the cold source of TEG is assumed to be kerosene which should be the fuel of hypersonic vehicles, although the detailed cooling process is not involved. Using the fuel as cold source has two potential advantages, i.e., no additional cooling medium means no additional weight, the heat brought by the fuel will be finally converted into propulsion energy and improve the energy efficiency of the whole vehicle. Considering the translational symmetry of the TE couples in the TE conversion layer, one of the TE couples is selected as a TEG for research. As shown in the macro-scale TEG part of Fig. 1, the TEG consists of two alumina ceramic substrates, one N- and one P -type TE leg, two copper electrodes and one poly-ether-ether-ketone (PEEK) shell. The envelope size of the TEG is $18 \times 5.5 \times 22$ mm. The size of the alumina ceramic substrates and the TE legs is 18 × $5.5 \ \times \ 2$ mm and $5.5 \ \times \ 3.5 \ \times \ 13$ mm, respectively. The thickness of the electrodes is 2.5 mm, and there is a hole with a diameter of 1.5 mm in the center, which is for the sensors of electric measurements. The PEEK shell plays the role of heat insulation, electrical insulation and lateral support of TE legs. In this work, the N-type TE material is $\Upsilon \mathsf{b} _ {0.29} \mathsf{Co} _ {4} \mathsf{Sb} _ {12}$ and the P -type TE material is $\mathrm{La} _ {0.1} \mathrm{Ce} _ {0.8} \mathrm{Fe} _ {3} \mathrm{CoSb} _ {12}$ , the fabrication process and physical properties are given in Appendix A. For the TEG, there are four key contact interfaces between TE legs and electrodes. As shown in the micro-scale ECR part of Fig. 1, the micrometer-scale rough surfaces for the TE legs and electrodes will lead to a very limited real contact region, and the electric current only flows through the real contact region. The constriction of interfacial electric current results into ECR which will influence the performance of the TEG. It should be noted that the configurations and geometric sizes of TEG, TE legs and electrodes shown in Fig. 1 are consistent with the samples used in the compression mechanic tests and the ECR measurement in this work.

## 3. Two-scale numerical model

The two-scale numerical simulation mainly includes two parts: $1 ^ {\mathsf{st}}$ , the contact mechanics and electric conductivity simulation are carried out for the contacting pairs model with micrometerscale rough surfaces, and the ECR under different loading pressures and temperatures between TE legs and electrodes are obtained; $2 ^ {\mathrm{nd}}$ , the TE conversion process simulation is carried out for the millimeter-scale TEG model with equivalent interfacial layers of

ECR. It should be noted that the thicknesses of the four equivalent interface layers are equal to the average distances of the corresponding four pairs of rough surfaces.

The two-scale numerical simulation is carried out by commercial software and self-programming, in which the ECR prediction of the micrometer-scale contacting pairs model is simulated by ABAQUS, the material properties of the equivalent interface layers are calculated by self-programming, and the TE conversion process of the millimeter-scale TEG model is simulated by ANSYS Multiphysics (ABAQUS cannot simulate the Seebeck effects). Besides, the measured rough surfaces of TE legs and electrodes are reconstructed by self-programming. The whole process contains three steps:

$1 ^ {\mathsf{st}}$ , the measured rough surfaces of the TE legs and electrodes are reconstructed by self-programming, and the micrometer-scale contacting pairs model is then established. The contact mechanics simulation is carried out for the contacting pairs model in ABAQUS, and the deformed contacting pairs model is obtained. The electric conduction simulation is then carried out for the deformed contacting pairs model. $2 ^ {\mathrm{nd}}$ , the material properties for the equivalent electrical conductivities of the equivalent interface layers are calculated by the ECRs and thicknesses between TE legs and electrodes. $3 ^ {\mathrm{rd}}$ , the millimeter-scale TEG model including equivalent interface layers is established In ANSYS Multiphysics, and the TE conversion process is carried out to obtain the influence of ECR on TEG.

## 3.2.2. Analysis method and boundary conditions

Fig. 4 shows the analysis method, boundary conditions and contact properties of the contact pairs. Fig. 4 (a) shows the surface definition, where RP is a reference point directly above the center of surface P 1, $P _ {\mathrm{r}} 1$ is the rough surface of TE legs, and $P _ {\Gamma} 2$ is the rough surface of electrodes. The “MPC beam” constraint is established between RP and surface P 1, and the loading pressure on surface P 1 is simulated by constraining the displacement $w _ {\mathrm{c}}$ of RP along O z direction. As shown in Fig. 4 (b), the first step is to carry out the contact mechanics analysis. In this step, the displacements of P 1 in x and y directions, the displacements of P 2 in x, y and z directions, and the normal displacements of P 3, P 4, P 5 and P 6 are constrained to be 0. After the contact mechanical analysis is accomplished, the deformed model can be imported to the next step, electric conduction analysis. In this step, the potential $U _ {1}$ and $U _ {2}$ are applied on P 1 and P 2, respectively, and the four sides (P 3, P 4, P 5 and P 6) are set to insulated. In ABAQUS, the contact type of “Surface to Surface” has the characteristics of high precision, smooth, stable, and insensitive to the exchange of master and slave surfaces. The rough surfaces of electrodes with large stiffness are selected as “main surface”, and the rough surfaces of TE legs are set as “slave surface”. As shown in Fig. 4 (c), in contact mechanics simulation, the tangential behavior of the rough surfaces contact is set to “penalty”, and the friction coefficient is set to 0.4. The normal behavior is defined as “hard contact” and allows separation after contact. In electric conduction simulations, air and silver-epoxy adhesive are considered as two types of gap mediums. For closed contact elements, the contact is assumed to be perfect and the electrical conductivity of the gap medium is given as $1 \times 10 ^ {12}$ S/m. In addition, the film resistance caused by pollutants and oxide film is not considered, and the medium between the two specimens should have some characteristics: it should be evenly distributed in the gap and there should be no extra electrical and thermal contact resistance between the medium and the studied surface. It should be noted that the contact mechanics and electric conduction analysis of the contact pairs are conducted in ABAQUS, and the TE coupling analysis of the TEG is completed in

The electrical contact resistance can be calculated by Eq. (1):

$$\mathrm{ECR} = {\frac {\Delta U} {j _ {z}}}\tag{1}$$

where $\Delta U$ is the potential difference of the average potential of two contact interfaces, while $j _ {z}$ is the current density in the direction of potential difference.

The silver-epoxy adhesive used in this study is 3702 from Shenzhen Xinwei Electronic Materials Co., Ltd. The density and specific heat capacity of the silver-epoxy adhesive are about 3.1 $\mathrm{g} / \mathrm{cm} ^ {3}$ and 504 J/(kg K), respectively. And the thermal and electrical conductivity are about 45 W/(m·K) and ${10 ^ {6} \ \mathrm{S} / \mathrm{m}} ,$ respectively. The silverepoxy adhesive can work below 470 K. The calculation conditions of the ECRs are shown in Table 3. First, the contact mechanics of the four contacting pairs under different compression displacements $w _ {\mathrm{c}}$ are analyzed. Then, the electric conduction analysis of the deformed contacting pairs model under air and the silverepoxy adhesive gap at several temperature points are studied. It should be noted that the range of the compression displacements of different contacting pairs given in Table 3 is the result of a large number of checking calculations in the early stage to ensure that the TE legs are not damaged, and the minus sign of the compression displacements indicates that the direction is downward. The calculated temperature points under air gap comprehensively consider the temperature of the leeward surface of the hypersonic vehicle (see Fig. 7 in the author’s previous work [23]) and the working temperature range of the TE materials.

## 3.3. TE conversion performance evaluation

Fig. 5 shows the geometric model and the mesh model of the TEG for the hypersonic vehicle. As shown in Fig. 5 (a), the influence of the interfacial ECR on TE conversion process is simulated by setting equivalent interface layers between TE legs and electrodes in the millimeter-scale TEG model. As shown in the B-B sectional drawing of the TEG, the thickness of the equivalent interfacial layers is the average distance of the corresponding rough surfaces (see the $5 ^ {\mathrm{th}}$ sentence in the first paragraph of 3.2.1), and the thicknesses of the upper and lower equivalent interfacial layers are set to 0.18 and 0.10 mm, respectively. It should be noted that the material property given by the equivalent interfacial layers is “equivalent electrical conductivity”, which is related to ECRs and thicknesses of equivalent interfacial layers. In terms of boundary conditions, the authors have developed a TEG concept for the leeward surface of the hypersonic vehicle, the temperature of the TEG concept is about 310 to 870 K (see Fig. 17 in the author’s previous work [23]). When the TEG is applied to the hypersonic vehicle, the TEG can work in a better temperature range by adding a heatresistant layer at the hot end and strengthening heat transfer at the cold end. In this study, observe the A-A sectional drawing of the TEG in Fig. 5 (a), the cold side (surfaces $P _ {0} 2$ and $P _ {0} 3 )$ of the TEG is set to 298 K, and the TE conversion performance of the hot side (surface $P _ {0} 1 )$ at 323, 373, 400, 423, 473 and 523 K is calculated. The other surfaces are set to be adiabatic. Besides, the potentials of all nodes on surface $P _ {0} 2$ and all nodes on surface $P _ {0} 3$ are coupled, and an external resistance is connected between the two coupling points to form a conductive circuit. As shown in

Fig. 5 (b), the mesh model of the TEG has about 19622 hexahedral elements and 85347 nodes. The equivalent interfacial layers are divided into five layers along the thickness direction. The model is meshed by the couple-field 3D solid element SOLID226 and has about 19622 elements and 85347 nodes. It should be noted that since the temperature boundaries are applied to the electrodes, the ceramic substrates needn’t be considered.

The electric potential U of the TEG can be represented by the product of temperature difference and Seebeck coefficient:

$$U = \vert S _ {N} \Delta T _ {N} \vert + \vert S _ {P} \Delta T _ {P} \vert\tag{2}$$

where $S _ {N}$ and $S _ {P}$ are the Seebeck coefficients of N- and P -type TE materials, respectively; $\Delta T _ {N}$ and $\Delta T _ {P}$ are the temperature differences of N- and P -type TE material, respectively.

## 3.4. Governing and constitutive equations

## 3.4.1. Mechanical contact process

The contact process of rough surfaces in this study involves the elastic and plastic mechanics. For the elastic stage, the governing and constitutive equations are Eqs. (3), (4) and (5); and for the plastic stage, the governing and constitutive equations are Eqs. (3), (4) and (8).

Equilibrium equation:

$$\begin{array} {r l} & {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial \sigma _ {x}} {\partial x}} + \frac {\partial \tau _ {y x}} {\partial y} + \frac {\partial \tau _ {z x}} {\partial z} + F _ {x} = 0} \\ & {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial \tau _ {x y}} {\partial x}} + \frac {\partial \sigma _ {y}} {\partial y} + \frac {\partial \tau _ {z y}} {\partial z} + F _ {y} = 0} \\ & {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial \tau _ {x z}} {\partial x}} + \frac {\partial \tau _ {y z}} {\partial y} + \frac {\partial \sigma _ {z}} {\partial z} + F _ {z} = 0} \end{array}\tag{3}$$

where $F _ {x} , \ F _ {y}$ and $F _ {z}$ are components of unit body force in x, y and z directions; $\tau _ {i j}$ is the shear stress; $\sigma _ {i} ( i = x , y , z )$ is the normal stress. The strain and displacement satisfy the geometric equations as Eq. (4).

Geometric equation:

$$\begin{array} {l} {{\varepsilon _ {x} = \displaystyle \frac {\partial u} {\partial x} ; \quad \varepsilon _ {y} = \displaystyle \frac {\partial v} {\partial y} ; \quad \varepsilon _ {z} = \displaystyle \frac {\partial w} {\partial z}}} \\ {{\gamma _ {y z} = \displaystyle \frac {\partial v} {\partial z} + \frac {\partial w} {\partial y} ; \quad \gamma _ {x z} = \displaystyle \frac {\partial u} {\partial z} + \frac {\partial w} {\partial x}}} \\ {{\gamma _ {x y} = \displaystyle \frac {\partial u} {\partial y} + \frac {\partial v} {\partial x}}} \end{array}\tag{4}$$

where $\varepsilon _ {i} ( i = x , y , z )$ is the strain; u, v and w are displacements in $x , y$ and z directions; $\gamma _ {i j}$ is the shear strain. The elastic stressstrain relation follows the Hooke’s law as shown in Eq. (5).

Constitutive equations for elastic stage:

$$d \sigma _ {i j} = D _ {i j k l} ^ {e} d \varepsilon _ {k l} ^ {e}\tag{5}$$

where $\varepsilon _ {k l} ^ {e}$ is the elastic strain; $D _ {i j k l} ^ {e}$ is the elasticity matrix. In isotropic case, Eq. (5) can be described with Eq. (6).

$$\begin{array} {l l} {\varepsilon _ {x} = \displaystyle \frac {\sigma _ {x} - \mu ( \sigma _ {y} + \sigma _ {z} )} {E} ; \quad \gamma _ {y z} = \displaystyle \frac {2 ( 1 + \mu )} {E} \tau _ {y z}} \\ {\varepsilon _ {y} = \displaystyle \frac {\sigma _ {y} - \mu ( \sigma _ {x} + \sigma _ {z} )} {E} ; \quad \gamma _ {x z} = \displaystyle \frac {2 ( 1 + \mu )} {E} \tau _ {x z}} \\ {\varepsilon _ {z} = \displaystyle \frac {\sigma _ {z} - \mu ( \sigma _ {x} + \sigma _ {y} )} {E} ; \quad \gamma _ {x y} = \displaystyle \frac {2 ( 1 + \mu )} {E} \tau _ {x y}} \end{array}\tag{6}$$

where E and μ are the Young’s modulus and Poisson’s ratio, respectively. γij is the shear strain.

In solving the plastic stress-strain relation, ABAQUS uses the “incremental” theory, in which the mechanical strain is decomposed into an elastic part and a plastic part, and the mathematical expression is shown by Eq. (7).

$$d \varepsilon _ {i j} = d \varepsilon _ {i j} ^ {e} + d \varepsilon _ {i j} ^ {p}\tag{7}$$

where $\varepsilon _ {i j}$ is the total mechanical strain; $\varepsilon _ {i j} ^ {e}$ is the elastic strain; and $\varepsilon _ {i j} ^ {p}$ is the plastic strain. Besides, the above equation follows the generalized Hooke’s law. The incremental stress-strain relation can be described with Eq. (8).

Constitutive equations for plastic stage:

$$d \sigma _ {i j} = D _ {i j k l} ^ {e p} \cdot d \varepsilon _ {k l} = ( D _ {i j k l} ^ {e} - D _ {i j k l} ^ {p} ) d \varepsilon _ {k l}\tag{8}$$

where $D _ {i j k l} ^ {e}$ is the elasticity matrix; $D _ {i j k l} ^ {p}$ is the plasticity matrix. And it can be expressed by Eq. (9) with matrix form under Cartesian coordinates.

$$\mathbf {D} _ {p} = \frac {9 G ^ {2} \mathbf {S} \mathbf {S} ^ {T}} {\sigma _ {s} ^ {2} ( 3 G + E _ {P} )} = \frac {9 G ^ {2} \mathbf {S} \mathbf {S} ^ {T}} {\sigma _ {s} ^ {2} ( 3 G + \frac {E E _ {t}} {E - E _ {t}} )}\tag{9}$$

where $E _ {p}$ is the plasticity modulus; $E _ {t}$ is the slope of the stressstrain curve in the plastic region and will in general change during a deformation; $\sigma _ {s}$ is the elastoplastic stress; G is the shear modulus, $\begin{array} {r} {G = \frac {E} {2 ( 1 + \mu )}} \end{array}$ . For deflection stress tensor S and its components, they have following expressions in Eqs. (10) and (11):

$$\mathbf {S} = \left[ s _ {x} \ s _ {y} \ s _ {z} \ \tau _ {x y} \ \tau _ {y z} \ \tau _ {z x} \right] ^ {T}\tag{10}$$

$$s _ {i} = \sigma _ {i} - \frac {1} {3} ( \sigma _ {x} + \sigma _ {y} + \sigma _ {z} ) ( i = x , y , z )\tag{11}$$

## 3.4.2. Heat conduction and TE conversion processes

The source term for the energy equation (heat conduction) of TE materials is the Joule heating and Peltier effect (see Eqs. (12)), while for the electric potential equation of TE materials it is the Seebeck effect (see Eqs. (13)), and the thermoelectric coupling analysis is realized by such terms.

$$\frac {\partial ( \rho T )} {\partial t} = d i \nu \bigg ( \frac {\lambda} {c _ {p}} \mathbf {g r a d} T \bigg ) + ( j \psi + j ^ {2} / k )\tag{12}$$

$$\frac {\partial ( \rho U )} {\partial t} = d i \nu \bigg ( \frac {k} {\varepsilon} \mathbf {g r a d} U \bigg ) + k S \Delta T\tag{13}$$

where $\rho$ is the density; λ is the thermal conductivity; $c _ {p}$ is the specific heat capacity; j is the electric current density; ψ is the Peltier coefficient and equals to Seebeck coefficient multiplying absolute temperature; U is the electric potential; k is the electrical conductivity; S is the Seebeck coefficient; T is the temperature difference.

## 4. Experimental measurement platform for ECR

In order to test the ECR, a test platform as shown in Fig. 6 is built in this paper. The platform is composed of a pressure device, power supply and data acquisition system which are used to provide loading pressures, voltage, and acquisition of electric current and voltage signals, respectively. The loading pressure is provided by the loading device and the weight of the mass block, and the mass block is used to ensure that the load is uniformly applied to the sample. The data acquisition system consists of a power supply, test wire and resistance box. The power supply can display the current in the circuit (with an accuracy of mA) while providing voltage (the minimum change is mV). The test wire can connect the circuit after touching different positions of the TE legs. The resistance box (the minimum change is 0.1 
) can increase the internal resistance of the circuit and improve the measurement accuracy of the ECR. The test principle can be summarized as follows: first, contact the test wire with the upper electrode (see the enlarged part of Fig. 6), and the internal resistance of the whole circuit can be calculated according to the voltage and current of the power supply; then, move the test wire to the position where the TE leg just contacts the upper electrode, and the calculated resistance minus the internal resistance of the circuit obtained in the previous step, which can be regarded as the ECR. It should be noted that the wire connecting the circuit is fixed in the hole of the upper electrode (see the enlarged part of Fig. 6).

As shown in the enlarged part of Fig. 6, the test sample is composed of alumina ceramic substrates, copper electrodes, TE leg and PEEK shell. The PEEK shell can ensure the verticality of the contact process between the electrode and TE leg during loading. The ECRs of the four contact pairs under the loading pressures of 0.1039, 0.1247, 0.1455, 0.1662 and 0.1870 MPa were tested, and the corresponding loading pressures are 2.0, 2.4, 2.8, 3.2 and 3.6 N, respectively. As shown in the data processing part of Fig. 6, the contact position between the electrode and the TE leg is defined as the position zero point. By moving the test wire, the resistance at 2.0, 4.0, 6.0 and 8.0 mm is calculated. The least square method is used for data fitting, and the intercept of the line on the longitudinal axis is the value of the ECR. In general, the ECRs between the TE legs and electrodes of the TEG are measured at 298 K and different loading pressures, and the micrometer-scale ECR prediction model is validated.

## 6. Conclusion

In this work, a TEG for hypersonic vehicles is established, and the influences of ECR on the TE conversion process and TE performance evaluation are studied by two-scale numerical simulations and experimental measurements. The practical topography of rough surfaces for TE legs and electrodes is measured, and the micrometer-scale ECR prediction models are established based on the reconstructed topography. An experimental apparatus for ECR measurement under different loading pressures is developed. The ECRs under different temperatures, loading pressures and gap mediums are then predicted and the numerical models are validated by a series of measurements. The TE conversion process of TEG is simulated by a millimeter-scale model, and the influence of ECR on the TE performance of the TEG for the hypersonic vehicle is thus clarified. The results show some conclusions:

1. The numerical models of the ECR prediction are established based on the measured practical topography of rough surfaces for TE legs and electrodes, and the proposed approach can be used to predict the electrical contact resistance under different temperatures, loading pressures and gap mediums.

2. The contact area increases approximately linear with the growth of loading pressure, while the maximum dimensional real contact area is less than 0.6% when the pressure is below 0.3 MPa.

3. With the growth of temperature, the ECRs between the Ntype TE leg and electrodes gradually increase, and the ECRs between the P -type TE leg and electrodes first increase and then decrease, which is mainly affected by the variation law of the electrical conductivity of the TE materials.

4. The ECR decreases while the tendency slows down with the growth of loading pressure. The ECR obtained in the silverepoxy adhesive condition (about $8.96 ~ \times ~ 10 ^ {- 10}$ to 10.2 × $1 {\dot {0}} ^ {- 1 {\dot {0}}} ~ \Omega \cdot \mathrm{m} ^ {2}$ at 400 K and 0.1 MPa) is much smaller and also less sensitive to the variation of loading pressure than that of air gap condition (about $2.70 \times 10 ^ {- 7} \mathrm {{\ t o \ 1.06 \times 10 ^ {- 6} \Omega ^ {.} m ^ {2}}}$ at 400 K and 0.1 MPa).

5. The developed experimental apparatus can measure ECRs under different loading pressures. The experimental and numerical results have a relatively good agreement with largest differences of 13.2%, 14.9%, 38.9% and 17.7% for “E-N\_l”, “E-N\_u”, $\ " \mathrm{E} \ / - \ : P \_ {\mathrm{l}} \ "$ and $\ " \mathrm{E} - P \_ \mathrm{u"}$ , respectively.

6. The influence of ECR on the TE conversion process of the TEG cannot be ignored. The ECR reduces the maximum output power of the TEG by increasing the internal electric resistance, and it decreases by about 83% and 2.0% under air and the silver-epoxy adhesive condition, respectively.

7. For the TEG applied to hypersonic vehicles, the negative influence of the ECR between TE legs and electrodes can be significantly reduced by using interfacial materials with high electrical conductivity, and can be slightly reduced by applying an appropriate pre-compressed force.
