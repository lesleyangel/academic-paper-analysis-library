## 1. Introduction

Online thermal monitoring of a vehicle relies primarily on sparsely placed discrete sensors. Full-field measurement techniques such as infrared thermography and temperature-sensitive paint are difficult to deploy in flight because of accuracy limits and the requirement for ground-based systems. As a result, thermal assessments are usually inferred from a few sensors placed in less extreme regions such as nonleading-edge areas. Reconstructing a global thermal field (i.e., distribution) from such limited sparse measurements constitutes an inverse problem.

Current research on such inverse problems generally follows three categories. The first is optimization-based: boundary conditions are iteratively adjusted so that the forward model's predictions at the sensor locations match the measurements as closely as possible, thereby yielding the boundary-condition distribution or field solution that best explains the observed data. The second is data-driven: a predictive model is trained to map measurements to the thermal field, using existing experiment or simulation data as training pairs. The third is physics-aware: a model is trained to produce fields that simultaneously satisfy measurements and the governing physical laws.

Optimization-based inversion can produce solutions that align closely with physical reality but is difficult to run quickly online. To ensure solution stability, these methods are commonly combined with regularization. Additional assumptions or constraints are added as penalty terms, for instance enforcing smoothness or minimizing energy, which converts an ill-posed problem into a well-posed one and suppresses spurious oscillations in the estimated field. Yang et al. (Yang and Fu, 2010) and Zhao et al. (Zhao et al., 2014) applied Tikhonov regularization to estimate boundary heat flux and demonstrated this stabilization in practice. To handle high-dimensional spatiotemporal thermal fields efficiently, conjugate-gradient sequential estimation with an adjoint model provides efficient gradients and makes the optimization fast. Huang et al. (Huang and He, 2022) perturbed the unknown heat flux and solved a linearized form of the governing equations to obtain temperature-to-heat-flux sensitivities and compute search step sizes, which reduced the cost of evaluating high-dimensional gradients. In addition, for the inverse prediction of heat flux on ablating external surfaces of flight vehicles, Islam et al. (Islam and Dutta, 2025) embedded nine equally spaced thermocouples through the structure and used their time-series measurements to estimate the heat flux on the moving boundary via repeated forward solves; they also trained a neural network on the measurement data to augment plausible sensor responses. Samadi et al. (Samadi et al., 2018) fitted the surface heat-flux history with polynomials up to eighth order and optimized the coefficients to match temperature measurements, further improving efficiency. A broad range of optimizers has been employed in optimizationbased inversion methods, including particle swarm optimization (Wang et al., 2018), gradient-based methods (Nair and Asok, 2020), genetic algorithms (Liu, 2008) (Vishweshwara et al., 2018), ant colony optimization (Mulani et al., 2015), and cuckoo search (Chen et al., 2018). Because traditional optimization-based approaches require repeated online calls to the forward solver, some works propose replacing the forward model with a data-driven surrogate to reduce the computational burden during iteration. Harsha et al. (Mk et al., 2018) first computed steady temperature fields for known heat-flux inputs and then trained a neural network to map the heat-flux input to the temperatures at nine sensor locations, thereby substituting for expensive computational fluid dynamics (CFD). The optimal heat flux was then found by minimizing the discrepancy between predicted temperatures and measured temperatures. For layered honeycomb composites, Chanda et al. (Chanda et al., 2017) trained a neural network whose inputs were the three thermal conductivities and whose outputs were the 19 sensor temperatures at the experimental locations, thus replacing the conduction forward solver. A genetic algorithm was then used to adjust the conductivities to match experiments by minimizing the temperature-prediction error.

Data-driven inversion trains a surrogate model on an offline dataset of measurement-field pairs. Once trained, it provides fast, accurate online estimates, but the offline cost is high and generalization beyond the sampled envelope is limited. Tamaddon et al. (Tamaddon-Jahromi et al., 2020) established a back-propagation neural network (BPNN) model from discrete temperatures to boundary temperatures for multiple conduction and convection problems and sensor layouts. They showed that near-boundary sensors carry the greatest value and that inference can be performed in milliseconds without repeatedly solving the forward model, though strongly nonlinear or non-unique problems may still demand more data or richer architectures. Yang et al. (Yang et al., 2025) proposed a multi-fidelity DeepONet with enhanced feature interaction, transfer learning, and physics-informed dynamic subsampling, reaching full-data accuracy with only 60% high-fidelity samples and enabling efficient flow-field modeling. For transient jet impingement cooling, Xie et al. (Xie et al., 2022) used gradient boosting to predict surface heat flux from experimental conditions and temperatures and validated superiority over several baselines. Zhu et al. (Zhu et al., 2022); (Zhu et al., 2022) combined convolutional neural network (CNN) with BPNN and long short-term memory (LSTM) to infer various boundary/parameter configurations from surface temperatures in cube and tank examples. Gu et al. (Gu et al., 2023) trained a CNN-based generative adversarial network that maps multi-time temperature fields to surface heat-flux distributions for 3D transient nonlinear inverse conduction, achieving more than a thousand times speedups over Tikhonov regularization with high accuracy.

Physics-aware approaches can be grouped into physics-guided, physics-informed, and physics-encoded classes (Faroughi et al., 2211). While effective at reducing data requirements relative to purely datadriven baselines, these methods often face limited generality, slow training, sensitivity to architectures and hyperparameters, and frequent retraining when conditions change (Koric and Abueidda, 2023). Cai et al. (Cai et al., 2021) trained a BPNN with spatiotemporal coordinates as inputs to predict temperature and pressure fields, optimizing it with a weighted composite loss that combines the equation residual, boundary and initial-condition consistency, and data-fit error. Jiang et al. (Jiang et al., 2023) ensembled multiple physics-informed neural networks (PINN) to quantify epistemic uncertainty and added a small number of virtual data in highly uncertain regions to boost accuracy, demonstrating success in heat-source, boundary-flux, and material-property inversion. Shang et al. (Shang et al., 2025) treated thermal conductivity as a trainable tensor optimized jointly with the network weights to reconstruct both temperature and conductivity. They proposed two strategies for boundary heat flux inversion: learning an unknown heat flux function through automatic differentiation of the temperature, and estimating the coefficients of an assumed functional form, which were defined as independent trainable parameters and optimized with the network.

Overall, thermal-field reconstruction from sparse measurements is well studied in inverse heat transfer. However, current methods still fail to balance real-time performance, high definition, and generalization from limited data, which are crucial for hypersonic onboard thermal management. Further, existing studies usually assume simplified boundary forms or predict boundary values only at given sensor locations, rather than reconstructing full spatial fields, which constrains their applicability. Optimization-based and physics-aware inversions are often too slow for real-time use; purely data-driven models can overfit when measurement data are scarce and not generalize to arbitrary sensor layouts. There is a need for an approach that incorporates physical information and still achieves high-accuracy, real-time reconstruction when measurements are taken at arbitrary locations. To overcome these limitations, we present a physics-guided, data-driven tensorized Fourier neural operator (TFNO) for real-time reconstruction of the aerothermal field from sparse and arbitrarily placed measurements. The operator performs global convolutions in the frequency domain and exploits the spatiotemporal structure of the field with a compact set of parameters, which delivers fast, accurate inference. Building on this foundation, the framework uses replication-masking (RM) strategy to generalize across sensor layouts, and fuses available flight state, low-fidelity predictions with sparse sensor data. For transient monitoring it further incorporates a short history of recent measurements to exploit temporal coherence. We evaluate the approach on heat-flux reconstruction for a blunt cone under hypersonic Mach 10.6 flow condition and temperature-field reconstruction for a C/SiC plate, and verify the contribution of each component. The contributions of this work are as follows:

(1) To address the limited generalization of thermal-field reconstruction under sparse measurements, we develop a TFNO-based method enhanced with a RM data-augmentation strategy. By repeatedly and randomly masking subsets of sensor readings during training, the model achieves strong generalization to arbitrary sensor layouts, learning to faithfully reconstruct global fields from sparse, irregular inputs while effectively expanding the small measurement dataset. Using only 9–126 sensors, the method reconstructs 2,400–3,700-node fields with mean relative errors below 2% (heat flux) and 1.5% (temperature), and achieves per-field inference within tens of milliseconds, substantially outperforming classical interpolation and conventional neural networks.

(2) To mitigate local overfitting and non-physical oscillations arising from insufficient sensor information, we introduce a multifidelity data-fusion mechanism that integrates flight-state parameters, low-fidelity field predictions, and high-fidelity sparse measurements. In the blunt-cone heat-flux reconstruction task, this fusion reduces the mean relative error of reconstruction from ≈27.5% (sensor-only) to 1.967%, where state parameters calibrate global magnitude and low-fidelity fields provide a spatially smooth prior.

(3) To overcome the poor temporal coherence of static reconstruction methods in transient scenarios, we propose a historyaugmented input strategy that incorporates several recent sensor readings to exploit temporal correlations. In the C/SiC plate temperature-field task, using three historical measurements at a 25-s interval reduces the mean relative error from ≈6.4% (no history) to below 1.5%, and markedly improves spatial smoothness in the reconstructed fields.

The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section 2 formulates the problem. Section 3 details the proposed framework. Sections 4 and 5 report results for the blunt-cone heat-flux and composite-plate temperature reconstructions, respectively, including comparisons to alternative methods. Section 6 concludes the paper.

## 2. Problem description

Hypersonic vehicles experience intense aerothermal loads due to the high-speed flow, which makes accurate, real-time access to the full-field thermal state essential for safe operation and agile thermal management. Heat-flux and temperature sensors provide the most timely and accurate surface measurements. However, installing sensors degrades surface smoothness, structural strength, and thermal protection, and it is infeasible to instrument ablation regions or areas with extreme heating. Accordingly, we aim to build a model that predicts the complete field from only a small number of discrete sensor measurements, namely a mapping from J sparse sensor measurements $[ {\pmb y} _ {1 k} , {\pmb y} _ {2 k} , . . . , {\pmb y} _ {J k} ]$ at time/ state k to a P− node full-field prediction $[ \widehat {\pmb {y}} _ {1 k} , \widehat {\pmb {y}} _ {2 k} , . . . , \widehat {\pmb {y}} _ {P k} ]$ , as schematized by Equation (1).

$$[ \widehat {y} _ {1 k} , \widehat {y} _ {2 k} , . . . , \widehat {y} _ {P k} ] = F ( [ y _ {1 k} , y _ {2 k} , . . . , y _ {J k} ] )\tag{1}$$

In many practical cases, in addition to sensor readings, the state variables $( \mathbf {e . g .}$ , freestream conditions) X are available, and there may be auxiliary data generated under those states by theory/empirical formulas or numerical simulation. Such data are typically less accurate than measurements but are advantageous in terms of sample size, resolution, and coverage. We refer to these auxiliary and measurementdomain sources as low-fidelity $( \pmb {y} ^ {\mathrm{L}} )$ and high-fidelity $( \pmb {y} ^ {\mathrm{H}} )$ data, respectively. With these definitions, the mapping in Eq. (1) can be extended to a multi-source fusion form in Eq. (2), where the high-fidelity field at P mesh nodes is modeled as a function of the available state $X ,$ the highfidelity sensor subset $\pmb {y} ^ {\mathrm{H}}$ at J sensors, and the low-fidelity data $\mathbf {y} ^ {\mathrm{L}}$ at P nodes.

$$[ \widehat {y} _ {1 k} ^ {\mathrm{H}} , \widehat {y} _ {2 k} ^ {\mathrm{H}} , . . . , \widehat {y} _ {P k} ^ {\mathrm{H}} ] = F ( X , [ y _ {1 k} ^ {\mathrm{H}} , y _ {2 k} ^ {\mathrm{H}} , . . . , y _ {J k} ^ {\mathrm{H}} ] , [ y _ {1 k} ^ {\mathrm{L}} , y _ {2 k} ^ {\mathrm{L}} , . . . , y _ {P k} ^ {\mathrm{L}} ] )\tag{2}$$

A further practical scenario is transient monitoring, where sensors stream data over time. The most recent history carries trends useful for improving reconstruction accuracy. In that case, Equation (2) is generalized to Equation (3): the predictor takes a short window of recent measurements together with the current inputs, enabling time-aware reconstruction.

$$[ \widehat {y} _ {1 k} ^ {\mathrm{H}} , \widehat {y} _ {2 k} ^ {\mathrm{H}} , . . . , \widehat {y} _ {p k} ^ {\mathrm{H}} ] = F ( X , \left\{{y} _ {j , \tau} ^ {\mathrm{H}} \right\} _ {j = 1 , \tau = k - d} ^ {J , k} , [ \widehat {y} _ {1 k} ^ {\mathrm{L}} , \widehat {y} _ {2 k} ^ {\mathrm{L}} , . . . , \widehat {y} _ {P k} ^ {\mathrm{L}} ] )\tag{3}$$

where d denotes the number of recent measurements, a parameter optimized to enhance transient reconstruction accuracy.

Formally, reconstruction from sparse local observations to a global field is an underdetermined inverse problem. To ensure uniqueness and stability, regularization must be introduced. This is expressed in Equation $( 4 ) ,$ which defines a composite objective that balances measurement fidelity at instrumented locations with regularity of the reconstructed field over the entire domain, controlled by a regularization strength α.

$$\begin{array} {c} {\mathrm{given} y _ {j k} j \in [ 1 , J ] , k \in [ 1 , K ]} \\ {\operatorname* {m i n} \left\| \widehat {y} _ {j k} - y _ {j k} \right\| + \alpha R \left( \widehat {y} _ {p k} \right) j \in [ 1 , J ] , k \in [ 1 , K ] , p \in [ 1 , P ]} \end{array}\tag{4}$$

Insufficient regularization (α too small) overfits sensor points and produces spurious oscillations; excessive regularization (α too large) yields smooth fields that deviate from the true values. The goal is to match sensor values while producing a physically reasonable distribution across the domain. In this work, such regularization is not merely implemented via an explicit penalty term with a fixed strength α; instead, it is primarily encoded through the model architecture and the multi-source input strategy. Specifically, the TFNO backbone performs global convolutions in the spectral domain, where high-frequency oscillations are inherently suppressed through spectral mode truncation to capture the underlying physical smoothness. Furthermore, by incorporating history-augmented measurement windows and fusing low-fidelity physical fields, we introduce strong spatiotemporal priors that effectively constrain the solution within physically plausible bounds.

## 3. Methods

## 3.1. Data-driven reconstruction

To achieve accurate and real-time reconstruction of full thermal fields from sparse sensor measurements, we adopt a data-driven framework comprising three sequential stages, as illustrated in Fig. 3.1.

(1) Low-fidelity model construction based on state information. When flight or experimental state variables are available, auxiliary lowfidelity thermal field data can be generated in advance using methods such as computational fluid dynamics (CFD) or engineering aerodynamic heating algorithms. After obtaining the low-fidelity thermal field, the data are partitioned into training, validation, and test sets following a standard 8:1:1 ratio. Using the training set statistics, the input and output variables are then standardized. For high-dimensional thermal fields, proper orthogonal decomposition (POD) may be applied for dimensionality reduction. Subsequently, a low-fidelity surrogate model is trained on low-fidelity training dataset (Equation (5) to rapidly predict the low-fidelity field $\widehat {\mathbf {y}} ^ {\mathrm{L}}$ for any given state. The low-fidelity model is constructed offline prior to online deployment to provide a physically reasonable and globally consistent prior field. While regression models such as neural networks or response surfaces were considered, this study employs a random forest (RF) regressor (Breiman, 2001), as it has been validated to offer superior generalization stability and effectively constrains predictions within physically plausible bounds, thereby avoiding the extrapolation errors common in other methods (Huang et al., 2024). The hyperparameters are tuned using the low-fidelity validation set, and the model's precision is evaluated on the low-fidelity test set to ensure an accurate low-fidelity surrogate is available for online calls.

$$[ \widehat {\boldsymbol {y}} _ {1 k} ^ {\mathrm{L}} , \widehat {\boldsymbol {y}} _ {2 k} ^ {\mathrm{L}} , . . . , \widehat {\boldsymbol {y}} _ {P k} ^ {\mathrm{L}} ] = {\cal F} ( X )\tag{5}$$

(2) Data preprocessing and high-fidelity model train. Once sparse sensor data $q _ {j k} ^ {\mathrm{H}} ( j \in [ 1 , J ] , k \in [ 1 , K ] )$ are acquired via wind tunnel or flight tests at current time/state k, the dataset is first split into highfidelity training, validation, and test sets.

(2.1) Sensor data augmentation. To overcome the practical challenges of few discrete measurement points, limited sample sizes, and the lack of high-resolution ground truth, we employ a replication-masking strategy for data augmentation, inspired by techniques in image superresolution (Zhang et al., 2024). Random masking involves randomly shielding S sensor readings based on a predefined masking ratio. The masking ratio is defined as the number of masked sensors divided by the total number of sensors. The values of these masked points are set to zero, while the remaining $J - S$ sensors serve as observable inputs. The full set of measurements $y _ {j k} ^ {\mathrm{H}} ( j \in [ 1 , J ] , k \in [ 1 , K ] )$ is divided into two subsets: the masked measurements $y _ {j k} ^ {\mathrm{H}} ( j \in [ 1 , S ] , k \in [ 1 , K ] )$ , which are withheld from the model, and the unmasked measurements $y _ {j k} ^ {\mathrm{H}} ( j \in [ 1$ , $J - S ] , k \in [ 1 , K ] )$ , which serve as the sparse inputs for reconstruction. The RM strategy combines random masking with sample replication: the original dataset is duplicated multiple times, and a different random mask is applied to each copy. As illustrated in Fig. 3.2, this strategy significantly expands the training set under limited experimental data conditions. No replicated or masked variants of test cases are used during training. It forces the model to rely on a subset of sparse measurements to perform global field reconstruction, thereby enhancing generalization performance across small sample sizes and arbitrary, unobserved sensor layouts.

(2.2) Input tensor construction. To facilitate learning within the TFNO framework, the input features are organized into a multi-channel tensor defined on the dense reconstructed grid:

(i) State channels store the state variables, broadcast as a constant field across the dense grid, providing global condition information, if states is available.

(ii) Geometry channels corresponding to the number of spatial dimensions store the dense grid coordinates, encoding geometric information to introduce spatial correlations.

(iii) Low-fidelity channel: One channel stores the predicted lowfidelity field, which is projected onto the dense mesh to eliminate geometric inconsistencies between grids.

(iv) Measurement channels: d channels store the history of unmasked measurements $\left\{y _ {j , \tau} ^ {\mathrm{H}} \right\} _ {j = 1 , \tau = k - d} ^ {J - S , k}$ . For static problems without history, this reduces to a single channel storing the current sparse sensor measurements mapped to their nearest dense grid nodes. Locations without sensors or those that are masked are padded with zeros.

(v) Mask channel: The final channel is a binary mask, using 1 to indicate the presence of an active sensor and 0 for unmeasured/ masked nodes. This input specification distinguishes zeropadding from physical zero values.

(2.3) Training. The multi-channel input tensor constructed above serves as the input, and the complete thermal distribution $\widehat {y} _ {p k} ( p \in [ 1 , P ] ;$ $k \in [ 1 , K ] )$ is the target output

$$[ \widehat {y} _ {1 k} ^ {\mathrm{H}} , \widehat {y} _ {2 k} ^ {\mathrm{H}} , . . . , \widehat {y} _ {P k} ^ {\mathrm{H}} ] = F ( X , \left\{{y} _ {j , \tau} ^ {\mathrm{H}} \right\} _ {j = 1 , \tau = k - d} ^ {J - S , k} , [ \widehat {y} _ {1 k} ^ {\mathrm{L}} , \widehat {y} _ {2 k} ^ {\mathrm{L}} , . . . , \widehat {y} _ {P k} ^ {\mathrm{L}} ] )\tag{7}$$

The model minimizes the data loss function calculated between the predicted values $\widehat {y} _ {j k} ( j \in [ 1 , J ] , k \in [ 1 , K ] )$ and the actual measurements $y _ {j k} ^ {\mathrm{H}} ( j \in [ 1 , J ] , k \in [ 1 , K ] )$ at all sensor locations:

$$\mathcal {L} = \frac {1} {J K} \sum _ {j = 1} ^ {J} \sum _ {k = 1} ^ {K} \left| \widehat {\boldsymbol {y}} _ {j k} ^ {\mathrm{H}} - \boldsymbol {y} _ {j k} ^ {\mathrm{H}} \right| ^ {2}\tag{8}$$

If the loss has not converged, backpropagation updates the model parameters. Upon convergence, the parameters are saved. Hyperparameters, such as the number of history steps and measurement intervals, are tuned based on validation set performance.

Prediction. In the online phase, inputs are constructed from the test set using the same channel organization as in training. Parameters are frozen, and only forward inference is performed, meeting realtime application requirements.

On one hand, the model takes the state, the low-fidelity field, and the full set of measurements $\left\{y _ {j , \tau} ^ {\mathrm{H}} \right\} _ {j = 1 , \tau = k - d} ^ {J , k}$ as inputs to observe the reconstructed complete thermal field. The thermal field predictions extracted at J total measurement points can reflect the model's fitting accuracy on known sensor locations.

On the other hand, the model can use the state, the low-fidelity field, and the unmasked subset $\left\{y _ {j , \tau} ^ {\mathrm{H}} \right\} _ {j = 1 , \tau = k - d} ^ {J - S , k}$ as inputs. The thermal field predictions are extracted at the $J - S$ unmasked measurements, the S masked measurements, and the J total measurements. These respectively reflect: the model's fitting accuracy on known sensor locations; the reconstruction accuracy at unmeasured (masked) locations; the overall accuracy across all observation positions. Under the RM strategy, the actual measurements $T _ {i k} ( i \in [ 1 , S ] , k \in [ 1 , K ] )$ at S masked points in the test set serve solely as supervision labels and are not provided as input to the model. Optionally, this step allows for manually specifying the masked subset to assess reconstruction performance in continuous regions.

To evaluate the reconstruction performance, we report three metrics: root mean square error (RMSE) ε, coefficient of determination $R ^ {2}$ , and mean relative error $\delta ,$ as shown in Equations (9)-(11)

$$\varepsilon = \sqrt {\frac {1} {J K} \sum _ {k = 1} ^ {K} \sum _ {j = 1} ^ {J} \left( \widehat {y} _ {j k} - y _ {j k} \right) ^ {2}}\tag{9}$$

$$\mathrm{R} ^ {2} = 1 - \sum _ {k = 1} ^ {K} \sum _ {j = 1} ^ {J} \left( \widehat {y} _ {k j} - y _ {k j} \right) ^ {2} / \sum _ {k = 1} ^ {K} \sum _ {j = 1} ^ {J} \left( y _ {k j} - \overline {{y}} \right) ^ {2}\tag{10}$$

$$\delta = \sum _ {k = 1} ^ {K} \sum _ {j = 1} ^ {J} \left| \widehat {y} _ {j k} - y _ {j k} \right| / \sum _ {k = 1} ^ {K} \sum _ {j = 1} ^ {J} y _ {j k} \times 100 \%\tag{11}$$

where ${\widehat {\boldsymbol {y}}} , {\boldsymbol {y}} ,$ and $\bar {y}$ stand for the predicted value, actual value, and the mean of the actual value.

## 3.2. Tensorized Fourier neural operator

While the RM strategy effectively expands the limited dataset, it necessitates a model architecture capable of handling irregular, sparse sampling. Traditional CNNs and BPNNs, which rely on local inductive biases, are ill-suited for global field reconstruction from such data. Consequently, we adopt TFNO, a neural operator that maps between infinite-dimensional function spaces. By performing global frequencydomain operations, TFNO captures physical smoothness and robustly handles missing values, making it an ideal match for the RM strategy. Additionally, it integrates physics guidance through spectral low-pass truncation and conditions the operator on global geometry via coordinate embeddings.

Let D be a bounded open subset of the reals that defines the spatial domain, with points $x \in D$ . Let B denote a Banach space of input functions $b ( \boldsymbol {x} )$ on $D ,$ and $Y$ a space of target functions y(x). Classical neural networks interpolate every input and output to a common set of coordinates and then learn a finite-dimensional mapping $[ b ( x _ {b 1} ) , b ( x _ {b 2} ) ,$ ... $] {} [ y ( x _ {y 1} ) , y ( x _ {y 2} ) , \ldots ] ,$ . By contrast, a neural operator learns a grid-agnostic mapping $B {} Y ,$ so that once trained it can produce $y ( x )$ at any x $\mathbf {\epsilon} : \in D$ with resolution-invariant behavior and super-resolution capability. Compared with PINNs that are trained for a single parameter setting, neural operators learn mappings between function spaces and thus handle families of conditions efficiently. For our inverse task—reconstructing complete thermal fields from sparse sensors under varying states—this yields lower computational cost and faster inference. A neural operator first lifts the input function $b ( \boldsymbol {x} )$ to a feature embedding $\nu _ {0} ( x )$ via a fully connected map $C _ {1}$

$$\nu _ {0} ( x ) = C _ {1} ( b ( x ) )\tag{12}$$

A stack of Fourier layers then applies global frequency-domain convolutions to update the features

$$\nu _ {0} {} \nu _ {1} {} \cdots {} \nu _ {z} {} \nu _ {z + 1} {} \cdots {} \nu _ {Z}\tag{13}$$

where z is Fourier layer index and $\nu _ {z + 1}$ satisfies

$$\nu _ {z + 1} ( \boldsymbol {x} ) = \sigma ( W \nu _ {z} ( \boldsymbol {x} ) + ( \mathcal {K} ( b ) \nu _ {z} ) ( \boldsymbol {x} ) )\tag{14}$$

where σ and W denote the activation function and the weights, respectively. The kernel integral operator is defined as

$$( \mathcal {K} ( b ) \nu _ {z} ) ( x ) = \int _ {D} \mathbb {k} ( x , \xi , b ( x ) , b ( \xi ) ) \nu _ {z} ( \xi ) d \xi\tag{15}$$

where $\xi \in D$ is the integration variable introduced to traverse the entire domain. k is the kernel function and is trained to learn pairwise interactions between spatial points. Its value depends on their relative positions and on the local values of the input function at those locations. Finally, $\nu _ {Z}$ is mapped by a fully connected layer $C _ {2}$ to the target output function

$$y ( x ) = C _ {2} ( \nu _ {Z} ( x ) )\tag{16}$$

In a generic neural operator, evaluating a learned kernel integral in the physical domain entails all-to-all interactions across the grid, which is time-consuming. The Fourier neural operator (Li et al., 2010) addresses this by constraining the kernel to be translation-invariant and parameterizing the operator in the frequency domain. Concretely, we expand the feature field into Fourier modes—discrete frequency components indexed by k—and learn complex multipliers only on a retained low-frequency set of modes. The update takes the form

$$( \mathcal {K} \ominus \pmb {\nu} _ {z} ) ( \pmb {x} ) = \mathcal {F} ^ {- 1} ( \pmb {R} _ {z} ( \mathbb {k} ) {\cdot} \mathcal {F} ( \pmb {\nu} _ {z} ) )\tag{17}$$

where $\mathcal {F}$ and $\mathcal {F} ^ {- 1}$ are the Fourier and inverse Fourier transforms. $R _ {z} ( \mathbb {k} )$ denotes a learnable complex coefficient for Fourier mode k. Parameterizing the operator in the spectral domain makes the mapping discretization-agnostic: because the parameters act on spectral coefficients rather than on grid samples, the same learned operator can be evaluated on finer meshes to produce higher-resolution outputs with comparable accuracy, provided most target energy lies within the retained bandwidth. This enables super-resolution evaluation across differing input and output resolutions. Computational efficiency stems from using fast Fourier transforms to shuttle between the physical and spectral domains and from truncating the spectrum to a prescribed lowfrequency band, which reduces computational and parameter cost and suppresses high-frequency noise.

(a)

Building on FNO, the TFNO (Kossaifi et al., 2310) introduces a higher-order analogue of PCA, the Tucker tensor decomposition, to approximate the spectral weight tensors with a lower-rank representation. By compressing the dimensionality of the spectral weight tensors, TFNO further improves efficiency, reduces the number of parameters, and alleviates overfitting, making it well suited to small-sample modeling with high-resolution outputs. This matches the characteristics of this study, where the number of discrete measurements is limited but the desired distribution is high resolution. The tensor $\mathcal {X}$ can be expressed in the following form (Fig. 3.3), consisting of a core tensor $\mathcal {C}$ and a set of projection (factor) matrices U

$$\mathcal {X} _ {\mathcal {L}} = \mathcal {C} _ {\mathcal {R}} {\times} _ {1} \pmb {U} ^ {( 1 )} {\times} _ {2} \pmb {U} ^ {( 2 )} {\cdots} {\times} _ {N} \pmb {U} ^ {( N )} + \mathcal {E} _ {\mathcal {L}}\tag{18}$$

where $\mathcal {L}$ and $\mathcal {R}$ denote the tensor mode dimensions and ranks, respectively; N is the number of projection matrices; $\mathcal {E}$ is the decomposition error tensor. As shown by Kossaifi et al. (Kossaifi et al., 2310), such compression reduces the parameter count without limiting model expressiveness, while mitigating overfitting and improving generalization. Fig. 3.4 outlines the TFNO model. Each Fourier layer consists of two main parts: $\textbf {a 1} \times \textbf {1}$ convolution in the physical domain, which preserves and mixes input features to mitigate information degradation with depth, and a spectral integral operator computed via fast Fourier transforms.

## 4. Heat flux distribution reconstruction for a blunt cone

## 4.1. Physical model

To assess the proposed method, we reconstruct the surface heat-flux distribution on a three-dimensional blunt cone. The physical model is a spherical-nose cone with a half-cone angle of 15◦, a base shoulder angle of 75◦, and a base radius of 152.4 mm (Cleary, 1969). Interchangeable noses provide three leading-edge radii $R _ {n}$ (0, 9.525 mm, and 27.940 mm), as sketched in Fig. 4.1. A cylindrical coordinate system is adopted, with the x-axis along the cone's centerline from the nose tip. The circumferential (azimuthal) angle φ is defined on the cross-section, with $\varphi = 0 ^{\circ} \mathrm{at}$ the leeward ray (top symmetry line) and $\varphi = 180 ^{\circ} \mathrm{at}$ the windward ray, as illustrated in Fig. 4.1. The model is electroformed from high-purity nickel with a wall thickness that tapers from 2.54 mm at the nose to 0.762 mm at the base. Available experimental data include discrete surface heat-flux measurements on models with the three nose radii at multiple angles of attack and unit Reynolds numbers. The number of sensors and their layout vary from 9 to 126 across models with different nose sizes and under different flight states (Cleary, 1969). After Chromel-alumel thermocouples were installed on the model surface, the model was rapidly injected into the wind tunnel’s steady-state flow with an insertion time of approximately 0.2 s. The resulting timeresolved temperature transients were recorded and subsequently used to infer the instantaneous surface heat flux. Only the steady-state heatflux values, derived from these transient temperature histories, are reported. The temperature measurements are regarded as accurate within the limits of the experimental setup, and the resulting heat-flux data were assessed through NASA theoretical analysis and deemed to be of high quality. This provides a reliable basis for the high-fidelity training labels employed in the present study. It is noted, however, that the original experiments did not explicitly report sensor accuracy or quantify measurement uncertainty.

## 4.2. TFNO model configuration and training

Computations are run on a workstation with an Intel Xeon Silver 4112 CPU at 2.60 GHz and 64 GB RAM. The software stack is Python 3.8 with TensorFlow. The baseline TFNO consists of four Fourier layers, each followed by a multi-layer perceptron (MLP). As an initial baseline, each Fourier layer retains five spectral modes and the associated MLP uses 10 neurons. These settings are later refined via the hyperparameter study summarized in Table 4.5. Although neural operators can map between continuous spatial positions, we discretize the output for visualization on a polar grid: 100 nodes in the axial (streamwise) direction and 37 nodes uniformly covering the 180◦ meridional plane at 5◦ increments. Features are z-score standardized. Early stopping is used, whereby training is halted if the validation metric does not improve within 500 iterations and the best checkpoint is retained. We adopt a five-channel input on the dense 2-D grid: (i) two channels store the dense grid coordinates to encode geometry; (ii) one channel stores the low-fidelity prediction evaluated at the dense grid coordinates; (iii) one channel stores the sparse sensor measurements, with uninstrumented locations padded with zeros; and (iv) one binary mask channel flags sensor availability (1 at measured nodes, 0 otherwise). To evaluate the effectiveness of low-fidelity fields in multi-source reconstruction, we utilize RF feature importance as a quantitative metric. The procedure is as follows: Based on the TFNO training data, we construct RF training/validation subsets. The outputs are the measurements at the full set of sensor locations. A regression RF model is trained to map these inputs to the outputs, and the feature importance ranking is extracted. The results show that as the distortion parameter λ increases, the importance score of the low-fidelity CFD features exhibits a global downward trend. This reflects that as the low-fidelity error increases, its contribution to complementing sparse sensor information and constraining the global spatial structure diminishes. Critically, this enables a rapid usability assessment of a new low-fidelity data source—typically requiring only a few minutes of RF modeling—before determining whether to include it in the computationally expensive TFNO training. Experimental results (Table 4.8) reveal a monotonic deterioration in multi-source reconstruction error as λ increases. When the feature importance of the lowfidelity field is not lower than approximately 0.176, the field provides valid spatial priors, helping the model supplement reasonable spatial structural information in sensor-sparse regions and thereby reducing the overall reconstruction error. Conversely, when the importance falls below 0.17, the low-fidelity field should no longer be used as an input. In such cases, it is advisable to discard the data or switch to a higherfidelity source or perform error correction to ensure the stability and interpretability of the multi-source reconstruction.

Sensors on the irradiated side are closer to regions dominated by boundary heat flux input and radiative non-uniformity. Consequently, when observations are missing, predictions rely more on the model learning implicit patterns of boundary conditions, leading to greater uncertainty. Conversely, sensors on the cooling side are dominated by internal thermal conduction. Their temperature changes are smoother and better constrained by thermal coupling with adjacent regions, maintaining low RMSE even when masked.

The high $\mathrm{R} ^ {2}$ values in Fig. 5.7 demonstrate the model's excellent capacity to fit known input features to the target field. However, the reconstruction of masked points in Fig. 5.8 reveals a magnitudedependent performance profile. High-temperature regions (② and $\textcircled{4}$ up to about 1000 K) maintain high $\mathrm{R} ^ {2} {\mathrm{:}}$ , indicating that TFNO effectively captures dominant transient dynamics even without direct observation. Conversely, lower-magnitude regions (① and ③) show a decline in $\mathrm{R} ^ {2}$ (0.659 and 0.894) and flatter predicted trends. This discrepancy is primarily due to the smoothing effect in global optimization, where highmagnitude gradients from points ② and ④ dominate the training process, causing the model to prioritize global temporal context over subtle local nonlinearities in regions with minor variations (about 6 K). Despite these localized deviations, the consistently low global relative error confirms TFNO's overall efficacy for large-scale transient field reconstruction.

In summary, the TFNO framework enables high-precision reconstruction of 3D temperature distributions. The model does not simply fit known points but infers missing information at unknown locations by leveraging spatial coupling relationships. Even if a sensor is damaged during operation or prediction is required at a location without sensors, the model can provide relatively accurate temperature predictions with reasonable trends and controllable errors, demonstrating robust engineering performance.

To demonstrate the benefits of historical measurement inputs, various historical settings were evaluated by randomly masking sensor points across multiple trials, as summarized in Table 5.4. While maintaining the same total time horizon, replacing the $3 \times 25 :$ s configuration with a $25 \times 3$ s setup resulted in poorer performance in terms of overall accuracy (which includes known measurements). This suggests that an excessive number of features can degrade the fit even at known data points. Furthermore, the 25 × 3 s setting failed to improve reconstruction accuracy, indicating that a high density of closely spaced, highly collinear frames increases dimensionality and the risk of overfitting without providing additional informative value. Finally, removing historical data entirely caused the reconstruction relative error to surge from 1.495% to 6.443%, highlighting the critical role of temporal context in solving this transient problem.

## 6. Conclusion

In this work we formulated a physics-guided, data-driven inversion framework based on a TFNO for reconstructing complete thermal fields of hypersonic configurations from sparse measurements. The method integrates (i) replication-masking to harden the model against arbitrary sparse sensor layouts, (ii) multi-input fusion of discrete sensors with flight-state variables and low-fidelity (CFD) predictions for aerodynamic heating on a blunt cone, and (iii) short historical measurements to recover transient temperature fields on a radiatively heated C/SiC plate. Validation across these two representative scenarios demonstrates realtime inference and consistent improvements over traditional interpolants, conventional neural networks, and other operator backbones. The main conclusions are as follows:

• On the blunt-cone heat-flux case, fusing discrete measurements, flight-state variables, and low-fidelity CFD yields a mean relative error below 2%, and ablation studies confirm the contribution of each component.

• On the C/SiC plate temperature case, using three recent measurements at 25 s intervals reduces the mean relative error in transient temperature reconstruction to below 1.5%, whereas omitting history raises it to about 6.4%.

• The trained model infers one field in about 0.08 s, enabling online updates that meet real-time requirements. Compared with nearestneighbor interpolation, RBF interpolation, BPNN, BPNN + POD, and alternative backbones, TFNO attains the best balance between accuracy and spatial coherence on both tasks. Repeated tests with a fixed masking ratio show that the model remains accurate even when some sensors fail or at unsensed locations.
