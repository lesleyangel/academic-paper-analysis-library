## 1. Introduction

Thermoelectric (TE) materials have emerged as a research frontier in both academic and industrial sectors, owing to their unique capability of directly converting waste heat into electrical energy with high efficiency for facing traditional energy consumption and sustainable development [1–4]. The performance of TE materials is quantitatively evaluated by the dimensionless figure of merit ZT [5], defined as ${Z T} {=} S ^ {2} {\sigma} T / {\kappa} ,$ , where S, σ, and κ represent the Seebeck coefficient, electrical conductivity, and thermal conductivity, respectively [6]. This formula underscores three pivotal research directions in TE materials: enhancing the σ and S, while concurrently reducing the κ. The synergistic optimization of these three parameters constitutes a critical pathway toward breakthroughs in TE material performance.

SrTiO3-based TE materials have emerged as a pivotal research focus within the realm of oxide TE materials, owing to their high-temperature stability and high electron effective mass [7]. Addressing the critical challenge of the inherently high intrinsic lattice thermal conductivity in this system, researchers have introduced the entropy engineering strategy. This concept originally emerged in the field of metals [8]. In 2015, Rost et al. reported an entropy-stabilized high-entropy oxide, $( \mathrm {M g _ {0.2} Z n _ {0.2} C o _ {0.2} C u _ {0.2} N i _ {0.2}} ) \mathrm{O}$ [9], which inspired considerable interest in exploring the properties of high-entropy ceramics. In high-entropy systems, the long-range periodicity of atomic arrangements is disrupted by the random distribution of multiple elements, leading to significant alterations in macroscopic physical properties. Since phonon scattering is closely related to atomic mass and bond length, the aperiodic variations in these parameters can significantly impede phonon transport, promote phonon localization, and ultimately result in low thermal conductivity [10], offering a novel research paradigm to transcend the performance limits of conventional TE materials [2,11–16]. Zheng et al. [14] were the first to report a significant reduction in the thermal conductivity of $\mathrm {{\cal S r T i O}} _ {3}$ -based TE ceramics through A-site high-entropy strategy, achieving a $Z T$ value of 0.2 at 873 K. Nevertheless, while optimizing the thermoelectric performance of $\mathrm {{S r T i O} _ {3}} \mathrm {{.}}$ -based materials via the "entropy engineering" approach can effectively suppress thermal conductivity, it inevitably leads to a reduction in electrical conductivity [17]. To mitigate or even eliminate this adverse effect, two primary strategies have been proposed. Firstly, incorporating a secondary phase with high electrical conductivity into the material [18]. Qin et al. [19] fabricated $\mathrm{Sr} _ {0.9} \mathrm{La} _ {0.1} \mathrm{TiO} _ {3} / \mathrm{Ag}$ composite materials, where Ag aggregated in a metallic state at grain boundaries, forming conductive pathways. This optimization enhanced both the electrical conductivity and Seebeck coefficient of the material, resulting in a ZT value of 0.3 at 883 K. Secondly, leveraging the "defect engineering" concept, structural defects beneficial to electrical transport are introduced into $\mathrm {{S r T i O} _ {3}} \mathrm {{.}}$ -based materials to refine their energy band structures and enhance electrical performance [20,21]. Lou et al. [22]. employed a combination of cold isostatic pressing and the addition of nano-Ti powder to induce oxygen vacancies within the matrix, boosting the electrical conductivity of $\mathrm{Sr} _ {0.875} \mathrm{La} _ {0.1} \mathrm{TiO} _ {3}$

Graphene oxide (GO), a form of graphene derived from oxidation, exhibits a two-dimensional honeycomb lattice structure. In this structure, carbon atoms are densely packed in a single layer, and the material can extend laterally to tens of micrometers [23]. This unique property enables GO to surpass the scale range typical of traditional materials science. Upon reduction, it can act as a highly conductive secondary phase additive, significantly enhancing the charge transport capability of the composite material. [24,25]. Furthermore, as a carbon material, GO tends to scavenge lattice oxygen from oxide matrices at elevated temperatures. This characteristic, which is beneficial for enhancing the electrical transport properties of n-type TE materials, has been well-established in numerous studies. For instance, Feng et al. [26]. detected a high concentration of oxygen vacancies near GO regions using electron energy loss spectroscopy after incorporating GO into $\mathrm {{s r T i O} _ {3} .}$ Similarly, Lin et al. [27]. found that incorporating GO into La-doped $\mathrm {{S r T i O _ {3}}}$ reduced the lattice oxygen, thereby increasing the electrical conductivity. Beyond these systems, this effect has also been widely observed in Nb-doped $\mathrm {S r T i O _ {3}}$ [28], La/Nb co-doped $\mathrm{SrTiO} _ {3} \ [ 29 ]$ , and A-site-deficient $\mathrm {{s r T i O} _ {3}}$ ceramics [30]. In summary, GO not only forms conductive networks within the ceramic matrix but simultaneously promotes the formation of oxygen vacancies due to its reducing nature. This dual mechanism provides a synergistic optimization, improving the material’s electrical conductivity.

In this study, an optimized non-equimolar high-entropy ceramic composition $( \mathrm{Ca} _ {0.27} \mathrm{Sr} _ {0.27} \mathrm{Ba} _ {0.27} \mathrm{La} _ {0.19} ) \mathrm{TiO} _ {3}$ [31], was employed as the matrix material. GO with varying mass fractions $( 0.25 \sim 1$ wt%) was incorporated into the matrix, and the composite ceramics were subsequently subjected to annealing treatment. A systematic investigation was conducted to explore the influence of GO content on the microstructural evolution and thermoelectric transport properties of the ceramics. The key issue was focused on analyzing the compositional change of GO during the annealing process and its interaction mechanism with the high-entropy ceramic matrix. Furthermore, the relationship between the microstructural evolution and thermoelectric performance was elucidated based on these findings.

## 2. Methods

## 2.1. First-principles calculation

First-principles calculations were performed using the Materials Studio software. To model the chemical disorder at the A-site in the $( \mathrm{Ca} _ {0.27} \mathrm{Sr} _ {0.27} \mathrm{Ba} _ {0.27} \mathrm{La} _ {0.19} ) \mathrm{TiO} _ {3 - x} \ : ( x = 0 , 0.1 , 0.2 )$ high-entropy ceramic, a special quasirandom structure (SQS) approach was employed. A 100- atom SQS model, corresponding to a $2 \times 2 \times 5$ supercell, was generated. In this model, the occupancy of the 20 A-site cations was optimized so that its pair correlation functions up to the first four nearest-neighbor shells best approximate those of a perfectly random solid solution. Subsequently, oxygen vacancies were systematically introduced by searching for low-energy defect configurations—specifically, 2 and 4 vacancies for the $x = 0.1$ and $x = 0.2$ compositions, respectively. Geometric optimization was then performed based on the density functional theory (DFT), employing the Perdew-Burke-Ernzerhof (PBE) exchangecorrelation functional within the generalized gradient approximation (GGA). The cutoff energy was set to 400 $\mathbf {e V} ,$ , and the convergence criterion for the total energy was established at 1 $. . 0 \times 10 ^ {- 6}$ eV/atom. The Brillouin zone was sampled using $\texttt {a 5} \times \texttt {5} \times \texttt {2}$ k-point mesh. The maximum stress deviation was constrained to less than $0.03 \mathrm{eV} / \mathring {\mathrm{A}} ^ {3}$ , and the maximum atomic displacement was limited to less than $1.0 \times 10 ^ {- 3}$ $\mathring \mathrm{A}$

## 2.2. Experimental procedure

In this work, ceramic powders were first prepared using the solidstate reaction method. Analytical-grade $\mathrm{CaCO} _ {3} ,$ $\mathrm {\bf S r C O _ {3}} ,$ Ba $\mathrm{CO} _ {3} ,$ La3 $O _ {3} ,$ and $\mathrm{TiO} _ {2}$ were accurately weighed according to the stoichiometric formula of $( \mathrm{Ca} _ {0.27} \mathrm{Sr} _ {0.27} \mathrm{Ba} _ {0.27} \mathrm{La} _ {0.19} ) \mathrm{TiO} _ {3}$ (detailed information on the raw materials is provided in Table S1). Subsequently, these raw materials were mixed with $\mathrm {Z r O _ {2}}$ milling balls, using absolute ethanol as the ballmilling medium. The mixture was ball-milled at a rotation speed of 300 r/min for 24 h. After the initial ball-milling, the resulting slurry was dried and ground. It was then calcined in a box furnace at $1300~^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ for 4 h. A second ball-milling process was then carried out, maintaining the same ball-milling conditions as the first. After drying and grinding the powder obtained from the second ball-milling, a single-phase perovskite-structured high-entropy powder was successfully synthesized.

This study utilizes commercially purchased GO powder as an additive, with its SEM image presented in Figure S1. The surface of the GO sheets exhibits wrinkles, primarily caused by the introduction of oxygencontaining functional groups. GO was then precisely weighed according to predetermined weight ratios, ranging from 0.25 wt% to 1 wt%. It was then dispersed in absolute ethanol together with the high-entropy ceramic (HEC) powder. Subsequently, the mixture was subjected to ultrasonic treatment for 0.5 h, followed by 0.5 h of magnetic stirring. Another 0.5-hour ultrasonic treatment was applied, and finally, the mixture was ball-milled for 2 h using the same ball-milling process as before, resulting in the HEC/GO composite powder.

The composite powder was loaded into a graphite die and placed in a spark plasma sintering (SPS) furnace under a vacuum. The sintering assembly was then held at $1250~^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ under a pressure of 25 MPa for 5 min to consolidate the HEC/GO composites. Subsequently, the SPS-sintered sample was placed in an $\mathrm {{A l} _ {2} \mathrm {{O} _ {3}}}$ crucible, with carbon powder added to the opposite side of the crucible. The sample was then annealed in a tube furnace under an argon protective atmosphere at a temperature of 1250 $^ \circ \mathrm{C}$ for 8 h. The overall sample preparation process is illustrated in Figure S2.

The HEC/GO composite materials prior to annealing were designated as 0.25GO, 0.5GO, 0.75GO, and 1GO, where the numerical values in the names represent the GO addition content. The ceramic sample prepared by sintering the powder without any GO addition served as the control group and was named 0GO. For the annealed materials, a prefix $" \mathrm{A} "$ was added to their original designations. A detailed breakdown of the material naming conventions is provided in Table S2.

## 2.3. Characterization method

The particle size of the calcined powder was measured using a laser particle size analyzer (Winner2005). The ceramic density (ρ) was determined by the Archimedean displacement method. The phase composition and lattice information of the ground powder of the materials were analyzed through Rietveld refinement using an X-ray diffractometer (XRD, D8 DISCOVER) in conjunction with GSAS II software. The microstructure of the material’s cross-section was examined using a scanning electron microscope (SEM, GEMINI SEM), and the grain size was statistically analyzed with the aid of Nano Measure software. The microstructural characteristics of the samples were investigated using a field-emission high-resolution transmission electron microscope (TEM, Talos F200X). The elemental composition and valence states of the ceramic samples were studied using an X-ray photoelectron spectrometer (XPS, Axis Supra). The Raman spectra of the samples were tested using a micro-confocal Raman spectrometer (Alpha300R). The carrier concentration and mobility of the material were measured using a van der Pauw configuration transport measurement system (ET9105-HS). The diffuse reflectance spectra were measured using a UV-Vis-NIR spectrophotometer (Cary 5000) coupled with an integrating sphere on a Lambda 1050 + system, and the optical bandgap of the samples was calculated using the Kubelka-Munk function and the Tauc plot [32]:

$$F ( R _ {\infty} ) = \frac {\left( 1 - R _ {\infty} \right) ^ {2}} {2 R _ {\infty}}\tag{1}$$

$$[ F ( R _ {\infty} ) h \nu ^ {1 / 2} = B ( h \nu - E _ {g} )\tag{2}$$

where $R , h , \nu ,$ and $E _ {\mathrm{g}}$ represent the reflectivity, Planck’s constant, the frequency of incident light, and the bandgap energy, respectively. The electrical conductivity (σ) and Seebeck coefficient (S) of the materials were measured simultaneously using a thermoelectric parameter testing system (Namicro-03). The thermal diffusivity (D) and specific heat capacity $( C _ {\mathfrak {p}} )$ were measured using a high-temperature laser flash apparatus (LFA-1000) and a differential scanning calorimeter (NETZSCH, DSC/404 C), respectively. The total thermal conductivity $( \kappa _ {\mathrm{tot}} )$ electronic thermal conductivity $\left( \kappa _ {\mathrm{e}} \right) _ {:}$ , and lattice thermal conductivity $\left( \kappa _ {\mathrm{lat}} \right)$ were calculated using the following equations [33]:

$$\kappa _ {\mathrm{tot}} = D C _ {\mathrm{p}} \rho\tag{3}$$

$$\kappa _ {\mathrm{e}} = L T {\sigma}\tag{4}$$

$$\kappa _ {\mathrm{lat}} = \kappa _ {\mathrm{total}} - \kappa _ {\mathrm{e}}\tag{5}$$

## 3. Results and discussion

## 3.1. Microstructure of HEC/GO composite materials

In order to explore the dynamic evolution of the material composition in subsequent experiments, the basic raw materials used for the composite material— HEC calcined powder and GO powder—were first subjected to structural characterization analysis to clarify the initial state of the raw materials.，Detailed analysis can be found in the supplementary information. The results indicate that the ceramic calcined powder consists of submicron-sized particles with a single-phase perovskite structure. Meanwhile, EDX area scanning and Raman spectrum reveal that GO contains a high content of oxygen-containing functional groups (with a C/O atomic ratio of 2:1 and an $I _ {\mathrm{D}} / I _ {\mathrm{G}}$ value of 0.89).

Fig. 1(a) presents the XRD phase structure analysis of the HEC/GO composite material before and after annealing, prepared by SPS sintering. Since the GO content is controlled below 1 wt%, no characteristic diffraction peaks of GO were observed in the resulting XRD spectrum. Instead, a pure perovskite phase structure is exhibited, indicating that the low addition of GO in this study has no significant impact on the primary phase structure of the sample. Rietveld refinement was performed on the XRD data of the ceramic samples, and the results are shown in Figure S8. The $R _ {\mathrm{wp}}$ values of all samples are below 10 %, demonstrating the accuracy of the Rietveld refinement process [34]. Table S3 presents the lattice constants obtained from the Rietveld refinement. Both the lattice constants of the samples before and after annealing are increased with the addition of GO. Furthermore, after annealing, the increase in lattice constants indicates further lattice expansion.

Figure S9 presents the microstructural characteristics of the grains in each sample and their corresponding grain size statistical analysis, with the average grain sizes listed in Table S3. The grains of all samples exhibit an equiaxed shape, which is consistent with the typical grain morphology of cubic perovskite structure ceramics. This suggests that the incorporation of GO did not induce any orientation changes in the grains. No significant correlation was observed between the average grain size and the GO content before annealing, with the sizes generally ranging from 0.2 to 0.3 μm. After annealing, the grains of all samples retained their equiaxed geometric shape, indicating the stability of the grains during the annealing process. However, it is noteworthy that the number of pores between the grains increased compared to the preannealed samples. Additionally, when the GO content reached 0.5 wt %, a noticeable grain growth occurred during the annealing process. EDX surface scanning analysis of the ceramic matrix was conducted, and the results shown in Figure S10 reveal a uniform distribution of cations with their contents matching the preset proportions (as shown in

Table S4).

Table S3 also lists the bulk and relative densities of the ceramics. In the pre-annealed materials, the samples without GO addition exhibited good densification (relative density greater than 95 %). As the lowdensity GO was added, the density gradually decreased. After annealing, the bulk density of all samples decreased, which corresponds to the increased porosity observed in the SEM images. The formation of pores can be attributed to two main factors. On one hand, the rapid migration of grain boundaries during the recrystallization process, which introduces gaseous impurities into the grains, forming voids. On the other hand, the primary cause is the accumulation of Schottky defects at the grain boundaries. The formation of Schottky defects can be attributed to the charge compensation effect that occurs when La3+ ions enter the Asites of the perovskite lattice. The defect chemical reaction equation for this process can be expressed as:

$$\mathrm{La} _ {2} \mathrm{O} _ {3} {} 2 \mathrm{La} _ {\mathrm{A}} ^ {.} + V _ {\mathrm{A}} ^ {\prime \prime} + 3 \mathrm{O} _ {0} ^ {\times}$$

Fig. 1(b) illustrates the distribution and microstructural characteristics of GO within the ceramic matrix, both before and after annealing for the 1GO sample. After SPS, GO retains its flake-like structure and is uniformly embedded in the ceramic matrix as a composite secondary phase. These GO flakes have length scales extending up to tens of micrometers, with widths ranging from 0 to 3 μm. During the annealing process, the majority of GO undergoes thermal decomposition, leaving behind distinct pores in the matrix. The presence of residual GO fragments within these pores provides strong evidence for the thermal

decomposition of GO. Notably, fibrous grains are observed within some of the pores. This phenomenon is closely associated with the gas-phase mass transfer process triggered by the thermal decomposition of GO. Under the influence of gas-phase mass transfer, gas molecules exhibit a stronger adsorption tendency towards specific crystal surfaces, which drives the preferential growth of grains in a particular direction, facilitating the formation of fibrous grains [35]. Moreover, the gas-phase mass transfer process plays a crucial role in accelerating mass transport and coalescence between grains during annealing, ultimately leading to an increase in grain size.

To further investigate the compositional evolution of GO during the sintering process, EDX point analysis was employed to determine its elemental composition. The specific locations and numbers of the selected points are clearly marked in the figure, and the resulting elemental content data are presented in Table S5. Based on the statistical data, it can be clearly observed that after SPS of the GO and calcined powder in vacuum, the oxygen content in GO is 37.7 %, showing no significant difference compared to the original GO material. However, after annealing the sample, the oxygen content of GO decreases to 16.9 %. This result indicates that oxygen - containing functional groups in GO are removed or reduced during the annealing process, resulting in a reduction in oxygen content and the transformation of GO into reduced graphene oxide (rGO).

Given the high likelihood of interactions between GO and the HEC matrix powder during the sintering process, which may lead to the reduction of oxygen in the ceramic lattice, the first-principles calculation methods were utilized to analyze the effects of oxygen vacancies on the ceramic lattice structure and the electronic band structure. As shown in Fig. 2, the crystal structure models of $( \mathrm{Ca} _ {0.27} \mathrm{Sr} _ {0.27} \mathrm{Ba} _ {0.27} \mathrm{La} _ {0.19} ) \mathrm{TiO} _ {3}$ with an ideal composition and (Ca0.27Sr0.27Ba0.27La0.19)TiO2.9 and $( \mathrm{Ca} _ {0.27} \mathrm{Sr} _ {0.27} \mathrm{Ba} _ {0.27} \mathrm{La} _ {0.19} ) \mathrm{TiO} _ {2.8}$ with varying degrees of oxygen vacancies are presented. After performing geometric optimization, the lattice constants for these three models are summarized in Table 1. It is observed that as the oxygen vacancy content increases, the ceramic’s lattice volume shows a tendency to expand. This phenomenon can be attributed to the absence of anions (oxygen ions) in the system, which allows the repulsive forces between cations to gradually dominate, causing the lattice to undergo expansion [36]. Based on the previously obtained ceramic lattice constants from Rietveld refinement, it can be inferred that the oxygen vacancy content within the composite materials increases with the addition of GO and the annealing treatment.

Fig. 2(a) not only displays the band structure of the three components but also presents the partial density of states (PDOS) for each element. More detailed PDOS plots for the individual orbital contributions of each element can be found in Figure S11. In these three ceramic materials, the electronic states at the bottom of the conduction band and the top of the valence band primarily arise from the contributions of O 2p orbitals and Ti 3d orbitals. For the model containing oxygen vacancies, the number of high-symmetry points in the band structure is reduced compared to the ideal model, indicating that the presence of oxygen vacancies leads to increased lattice distortion. This distorted lattice structure weakens the hybridization between the Ti 3d and O 2p orbitals, resulting in reduced band dispersion [37]. The model with oxygen vacancies shows a flatter band structure compared to the ideal model. This phenomenon can be attributed to the introduction of additional electrons due to the oxygen vacancies, which typically occupy localized states in the conduction band. This leads to a rearrangement of the band structure, ultimately making the conduction band flatter [38]. In the calculated band structures, the Fermi level is set as the energy reference (0 eV). For all three constructed models in our system, the conduction band minimum (CBM) lies below the Fermi level in the band structure, indicating that the Fermi level has entered the conduction band. This confirms that the system exhibits characteristics of a degenerate semiconductor with a high electron concentration. Moreover, as the concentration of oxygen vacancies increases, the band gap initially increases and then decreases. The band gap of $( \mathrm{Ca} _ {0.27} \mathrm{Sr} _ {0.27} \mathrm{Ba} _ {0.27} \mathrm{La} _ {0.19} ) \mathrm{TiO} _ {2.8}$ is only 2.17 eV.

Fig. 3(a) presents the peak fitting results of the high-resolution O 1 s spectra for each sample. The spectra can be decomposed into three components, with the binding energies corresponding, from low to high, to lattice oxygen $\left( \mathrm{O} _ {\mathrm{lat}} \right)$ , oxygen vacancies $\left( \mathrm{V} _ {\mathrm{O}} \right)$ , and physically adsorbed oxygen $\mathbf {\rho} ( \mathrm {O _ {a d s}} )$ [39]. The peak positions and corresponding peak areas of these components are summarized in Table S6. It can be observed that for the samples before annealing, as the amount of GO added increases, the proportion of oxygen vacancies rises. Moreover, after the samples undergo annealing, the content of oxygen vacancies further increases. This phenomenon can primarily be attributed to the reducing atmosphere during the annealing process. Secondly, the large-scale pyrolysis of GO during annealing leads to reactions between the carbon skeleton and the lattice oxygen in the ceramic matrix, generating CO or $\mathrm{CO} _ {2}$ This process promotes the formation of oxygen vacancies. The defect reaction equation can be expressed as follows:

$$C + O _ {o} ^ {\times} {} C O + V _ {o} ^ {\cdot} + 2 e ^ {\prime}\tag{7}$$

$$C O + O _ {o} ^ {\times} {} C O _ {2} + V _ {O} ^ {\cdot} + 2 e\tag{8}$$

$$C + 2 O _ {o} ^ {\times} {} C O _ {2} + 2 V _ {o} ^ {\cdot} + 4 e ^ {\prime}\tag{9}$$

Fig. 3(b) shows the peak fitting results of the C 1 s high-resolution spectrum, focusing on the characteristic peaks in the binding energy range from 282 eV to 292 eV. The peaks in this range can be decomposed into three components, with binding energies increasing from low to high corresponding to C-C and C––C bonds, C-O bonds, and C––O bonds, respectively [40]. The relative ratio of C-O and $\scriptstyle \mathrm{C=} 0$ bonds generally increases or decreases, which usually indicates the increase or decrease of oxygen-containing functional groups in the GO structure, directly related to the oxidation degree of GO. Detailed peak fitting data is listed in Table S7, from which it can be observed that as the amount of GO increases, the carbon-oxygen bond ratio in the pre-annealed samples increases. This phenomenon suggests a negative correlation between the amount of GO added and the reduction degree of GO in the material. In the post-annealed samples, the carbon-oxygen bond ratio is below 30 %, a finding that is highly consistent with the data obtained from the EDX point analysis. This further confirms that GO underwent reduction in all the samples. This reduction is primarily attributed to the thermally induced cleavage of carbon-oxygen bonds under high-temperature conditions. Furthermore, the increase in oxygen vacancy content provides additional reaction sites for the oxygen-containing functional groups in GO, promoting the interaction between the oxygen-containing functional groups and the ceramic matrix.

Fig. 3(c) displays the Raman spectra of the samples within the wavenumber range of $1000 \thinspace \mathrm{cm} ^ {- 1} \thinspace \mathrm{to} 2000 \thinspace \mathrm{cm} ^ {- 1}$ to observe the D peak and G peak. For the samples before annealing, as the proportion of GO addition increased, the peak intensities of both the D peak and G peak of the samples enhanced. In the annealed samples, when the GO addition amount was below 1 wt%, no significant D band and G band could be observed. This finding confirms the prevalence of the thermal decomposition reaction of GO. The $I _ {\mathrm{D}} / I _ {\mathrm{G}}$ ratio of the A1GO sample is 0.82, which shows a decrease compared to the $I _ {\mathrm{D}} / I _ {\mathrm{G}}$ ratio (1.21) of this component sample before annealing. This change reflects a reduction in the number of defects in GO, particularly a decrease in oxygencontaining functional groups.

To thoroughly investigate the interface-bonding characteristics between the ceramic and GO, TEM analysis was conducted on the A1GO sample, and the results are shown in Fig. 4. Fig. 4(a) presents the HAADF image at the interface between the ceramic and GO. It is observed that GO is in close contact with the ceramic matrix. Using EDX area scanning to study the distribution of C and O elements, the results reveal that in the direct-contact area between the innermost layer of GO and the ceramic, the O element is sparsely distributed, while the adjacent ceramic region is rich in the O element. This distribution difference indicates the migration of the O element from GO to the ceramic matrix. It should be noted that although the number of oxygen vacancies in the ceramic matrix near the two-phase interface decreases, since most of the GO has undergone thermal decomposition, the reduction of lattice oxygen by carbonaceous materials becomes dominant at this stage. As a result, the overall oxygen vacancies in the ceramic still exhibit a decreasing trend.

Fig. 4(b) shows the HRTEM image of the interface region between GO and the ceramic. In this image, Region I is GO, Region III is the ceramic matrix, and Region II, located between them, is the transition zone between GO and the ceramic matrix. Further analysis of the locally magnified HRTEM image of Region III reveals that the interplanar spacing of the ceramic’s (110) crystal plane is 0.275 nm, which is highly close to that of SrTiO3 (0.276 nm). No obvious lattice expansion phenomenon is observed, indicating a low content of oxygen vacancies in this region. This can be attributed to the effective filling of oxygen vacancies in the ceramic by the O element from GO. IFFT analysis of Region III shows the presence of a high-density of dislocations. The reason is that the interaction between GO and the ceramic generates an inhomogeneous stress field within the ceramic, which in turn induces the formation of dislocations. Since dislocations are inevitably accompanied by a stress field, geometric phase analysis was carried out on this region, and the results, as shown in the figure, indicate the presence of tensile (red and yellow regions) and compressive (blue region) stress distributions around the dislocations. These inhomogeneous lattice stress fields (±10 %) can act as additional phonon - scattering centers at the atomic scale, reducing thermal conductivity.

Fig. 4(c) displays the electron diffraction patterns corresponding to Region I, Region II, and Region III, respectively. The diffraction spots in Region I exhibit a circular pattern characteristic of a polycrystalline structure, indicating that GO retains its multi-layer structural properties. The diffraction spots in Region II show a complex pattern where circular and spot-like diffraction features are nested with each other, reflecting the phase-boundary characteristics of this transition region. In contrast, the diffraction spots in Region III completely transform into features typical of a perovskite lattice with a Pm3m space group. This demonstrates that even the part of the ceramic matrix closest to GO does not undergo a phase-structural transformation, thereby confirming that the interaction between the ceramic and GO is mainly confined to element - level exchange rather than deep structural integration. Fig. 4(d) depicts the structure of the ceramic matrix at the atomic scale, clearly showing the layered arrangement of A-site cations and $\mathrm{Ti} ^ {4 +}$ ions within the perovskite structure. The corresponding EDX images further corroborate the uniform distribution of each element.

In summary, during the annealing process, GO undergoes two parallel and mutually influencing chemical reaction pathways. On one hand, the carbon framework of GO undergoes thermal decomposition.

This reaction not only leads to the gradual removal of carbonaceous components but also promotes the generation of oxygen vacancies in the ceramic matrix. Through a gas-phase mass-transfer mechanism, it affects the growth kinetics and final morphology of the ceramic grains. As a result, fibrous grains are formed, and grain growth is promoted. Meanwhile, obvious pore structures are left in the ceramic matrix after the decomposition of GO. On the other hand, the oxygen-containing functional groups in GO detach during the high-temperature annealing process. This process not only further facilitates the reduction of GO but also influences the chemical composition and surface properties of its adjacent ceramic matrix. The detachment of oxygen-containing functional groups results in the reconstruction of chemical bonds at the interface between GO and the ceramic matrix, enhancing the interaction force between them. This, in turn, helps to form a more stable multiphase material structure.

## 3.2. Thermoelectric performance of HEC/GO composite materials

Figure S12 presents the electrical and thermal transport properties of HEC/GO composite material before annealing. The electrical conductivity increases with the GO content, underscoring the positive role of the highly conductive GO. However, the conductivity remains relatively low $( < ~ 600 ~ \mathrm{S/m} )$ , leading to a power factor below 16 $\mu \mathrm{W} / ( \mathrm{m} {\cdot} \mathrm{K} ^ {2} )$ Consequently, the unannealed material achieves a maximum ZT of only 0.0061 due to its low conductivity. Notably, this value is three times higher than that of the 0GO sample (0.0014), demonstrating the positive effect of GO on the thermoelectric properties of the composite.

Figure S13 presents the optical bandgaps of various samples estimated based on diffuse reflectance spectra, using the Kubelka-Munk function and Tauc plots. Materials with a high addition amount of GO exhibit a narrower bandgap, which can be attributed to the high oxygen - vacancy content promoting electron transitions.

Fig. 5 comprehensively illustrates the electrical and thermal transport properties of the annealed HEC/GO composite materials. Fig. 5(a) shows the temperature - dependent electrical conductivity curves of each sample. A0.75GO and A1GO achieve excellent electrical conductivity, which is consistent with the estimated bandgap widths. As the temperature rises, there is a transition in the conduction mechanism from semiconductor-like behavior to metal-like behavior [41]. This is because at lower temperatures, thermal excitation of electrons plays a dominant role, and more electrons gain the energy required to cross the potential barrier. In contrast, at high temperatures, ionized - impurity scattering becomes the dominant factor. The structural defects in the material enhance the scattering of electron, leading to a decrease in electrical conductivity.

The conduction activation energy (Ea) of small polarons can be determined using the Arrhenius equation [42]:

$$\sigma = {\frac {\sigma _ {0}} {T}} \exp {{\frac {- E _ {\mathrm {{a}}}} {k _ {\mathrm {{B}}} T}}}\tag{10}$$

$$\mathrm{In} ( \sigma T ) = \mathrm{In} ( C ) \bigg ( - \frac {E _ {a}} {k _ {\mathrm{B}} T} \bigg )\tag{11}$$

where $\sigma , k _ {\mathrm{B}} ,$ and T represent the electrical conductivity, Boltzmann constant, and thermodynamic temperature, respectively, while σ0 and C are constants. Figure S14 depicts the relationship plot between In(σT) and 1000/T for the HEC/GO composite material. It can be observed that In(σT) exhibits a good linear relationship with 1000/T, indicating that the composite material follows the small polaron conduction mechanism for charge transport. Linear fitting of the data for each sample yields the respective $E _ {\mathrm{a}}$ The $E _ {\mathrm{a}}$ for the A0GO sample reaches 2.27 eV, highlighting the difficulty of electron transition in this sample. In contrast, the $E _ {\mathrm{a}}$ for the A1GO sample is only 1.62 eV, confirming the effective enhancement of the electrical conductivity of the ceramic matrix by the addition of GO.

As the amount of GO added increases, the absolute value of the Seebeck coefficient for the samples decreases. Within the theoretical framework of degenerate semiconductors, the Seebeck coefficient can be expressed based on the single parabolic band (SPB) model as follows [43]:

$$| S | = {\frac {8 \pi ^ {2} k _ {\mathrm{B}} ^ {2}} {3 e h ^ {2}}} m ^ {*} T \Bigl ( {\frac {\pi} {3 n}} \Bigr ) ^ {2 / 3}\tag{12}$$

where $k _ {\mathrm{B}} , e , h , m ^ {\ast} , T ,$ and n represent the Boltzmann constant, elementary charge of an electron, Planck’s constant, the effective mass of electrons, thermodynamic temperature, and electron concentration, respectively. It is evident that the Seebeck coefficient is negatively correlated with the electron concentration and positively correlated with the effective mass of electrons. As the content of oxygen vacancies in the samples increases with the addition of GO, these oxygen vacancies ionize to produce free electrons, thereby increasing the electron concentration, which leads to a decrease in the Seebeck coefficient. However, the Seebeck coefficients among the samples are quite close to each other due to the compensating effect of an increase in the effective mass of electrons. Table 2 presents the electron concentration (n) and mobility $( \mu _ {\mathrm{H}} )$ of the material measured by the Hall test, and combines them with Eq. (12) to calculate the relative electron effective mass of the material. The results indicate that with the increase of GO addition, the electron concentration of the material rises, confirming the enhancing role of oxygen vacancies on electron concentration. However, the electron mobility shows a decreasing trend, which is primarily attributed to the increased porosity within the material and the enhanced scattering effect of oxygen vacancies on charge carriers. The calculated relative electron effective mass $( m ^ {*} / m _ {\mathrm{e}} )$ also increases. The expression for the effective mass of electrons is as follows [44]:

$$m ^ {*} = \hbar ^ {2} \left( \frac {d ^ {2} E} {d k ^ {2}} \right) ^ {- 1}\tag{13}$$

where, $\hbar , E ,$ and k denote the reduced Planck’s constant, electron enthe energy band. According to the energy band structure diagram obtained from first-principles calculations, an increase in the oxygen vacancy content leads to a flattening of the energy bands, i.e., a decrease in the energy band curvature. Consequently, as the amount of GO added increases, the effective mass of electrons in the ceramic matrix also increases. This effectively mitigates the adverse impact of the increased electron concentration on the Seebeck coefficient.

To delve deeper into the investigation of electron mobility characteristics, the weighted electron mobility (μw) of the material is calculated using the following formula:

$$\begin{array} {r l} & {\mu _ {w} = \cfrac {3 h ^ {3} \sigma} {8 \pi e \left( 2 m _ {0} k _ {B} T \right) ^ {3 / 2}} \left\{\cfrac {\exp \left[ \frac {| S |} {k _ {B} / e} - 2 \right]} {1 + \exp \left[ - 5 \left( \frac {| S |} {k _ {B} / e} - 1 \right) \right]} \right.} \\ & {\left. + \frac {\frac {3 | S |} {\pi ^ {2} k _ {B} / e}} {1 + \exp \left[ 5 \left( \frac {| S |} {k _ {B} / e} - 1 \right) \right]} \right\}} \end{array}\tag{14}$$

where, σ and S represent the electrical conductivity and Seebeck coefficient, respectively, while h, π, e, m0, kB, and T correspond to Planck’s constant, circular constant, electronic charge, the rest mass of a free electron, Boltzmann constant, and thermodynamic temperature. The relevant calculation results are illustrated in Figure S15. The A0.75GO and A1GO samples exhibit enhanced electron transport due to their high oxygen vacancy contents. Notably, although the A1GO sample possesses the highest oxygen vacancy content, its weighted electron mobility is lower than that of the A0.75GO sample owing to its high porosity. Fig. 5 (f) presents a comparison of the power factors for all tested samples, with the A0.75GO sample demonstrating the optimal performance under testing conditions at 1073 K, achieving a high power factor of 556.25 μW/(m⋅K²).

Fig. 5(g), (i) and Figure S16 depict the temperature dependence of the total thermal conductivity $\mathbf {\Pi} ( \kappa _ {\mathrm{tot}} )$ , lattice thermal conductivity $\left( \kappa _ {\mathrm{lat}} \right)$ and electronic thermal conductivity $\left( \kappa _ {\mathrm{e}} \right)$ for each sample (the specific heat capacity and thermal diffusivity data are shown in Figure S17), respectively. Given that our system is a semiconductor, the contribution of $\kappa _ {\mathrm{e}}$ is relatively small (<0.35 W/(m⋅K)), thus our analysis primarily focuses on the lattice thermal conductivity. The $k _ {\mathrm{lat}}$ for all component samples decreases with increasing temperature, indicating that the heat transport process is predominantly scattered by the Umklapp process [45]. The high-entropy design and introduction of a secondary phase induce various types of lattice defects within the crystal lattice, achieving broad-frequency phonon scattering. Samples with lower GO addition amounts (A0GO and A0.25GO samples) exhibit higher thermal conductivities compared to those with higher GO contents. This phenomenon can be attributed to the thermal decomposition effect of GO during the annealing process, which leaves behind porous structures within the ceramic matrix. These pores act as obstacles along the heat conduction pathways, impeding the efficient transfer of heat.

Fig. 5(f) display the ZT values of the HEC/GO composite material after annealing., the A1GO sample within the HEC/GO composite material achieves a ZT value of 0.32. This indicates that rGO not only serves as a highly conductive secondary phase but also acts as a reducing agent, promoting internal chemical reactions and microstructural optimization of the material, thereby effectively enhancing the thermoelectric performance of the ceramic.

Figure S18 presents a schematic diagram of the transport mechanism by which GO enhances the thermoelectric performance of HEC. Before annealing, GO forms a three-dimensional conductive network, which enhances charge carrier mobility and thereby improves the overall electrical conductivity of the composite material. During the annealing process, GO undergoes partial pyrolysis: the decomposed GO reacts with matrix lattice oxygen through redox reactions, generating oxygen vacancy within the material. These oxygen vacancies release free electrons via ionization, resulting in increased carrier concentration. The porous formed by GO decomposition acts as phonon scattering centers, suppressing lattice thermal conduction and reducing thermal conductivity. Meanwhile, the residual undecomposed rGO continues to function as electronic transport pathways, maintaining the material’s overall conductivity. Ultimately, this results in a synergistic enhancement of multiple mechanisms, including high-conductivity phase compositing and defect engineering in HEC. While previous studies [26,27,29] have predominantly focused on the role of GO in enhancing oxygen vacancy concentration, its other interfacial reactions with the ceramic matrix remain underexplored. Addressing this gap, our work systematically investigates the effects stemming from GO’s compositional evolution, including its influence on ceramic grain morphology, porosity, and the interfacial migration of carbon and oxygen elements. This study thereby provides a multi-faceted supplement to the theoretical framework of using carbon materials to enhance the thermoelectric performance of oxides.

## 4. Conclusion

In this study, $( \mathrm {C a _ {0.27} S r _ {0.27} B a _ {0.27} L a _ {0.19}} ) \mathrm {T i O _ {3} / G O}$ composite materials were successfully prepared by SPS. We systematically investigated the compositional evolution of GO during heat treatment and its interaction mechanisms with the high-entropy ceramic matrix, along with the influence of these structural changes on the thermoelectric properties of the composites. The results indicate that GO undergoes thermal decomposition during the annealing process. The associated vaporphase mass transfer promotes grain growth and leads to the formation of fibrous grains. Furthermore, the carbon atmosphere generated by GO decomposition induces an increase in oxygen vacancy concentration within the ceramic matrix. This effectively reduces the bandgap of the material, enhances carrier concentration and mobility, and ultimately leads to a notable improvement in electrical conductivity. Additionally, the increase in electron effective mass compensates for the potential adverse effects on the Seebeck coefficient. In terms of thermal transport, the high porosity resulting from GO decomposition effectively impedes phonon transport, substantially reducing the lattice thermal conductivity. The residual GO that is not fully decomposed is reduced to rGO, which acts as a highly conductive secondary phase, further optimizing the electrical transport properties of the material. As a result, the composite with 1 wt% GO exhibits the optimal thermoelectric performance at 1073 K, achieving a ZT value of 0.32. Through the incorporation of GO, this study successfully realizes a multi-mechanism synergistic enhancement of the electrical and thermal transport properties in highentropy ceramics, providing new insights into the functional application of carbon materials in oxide-based thermoelectric.
