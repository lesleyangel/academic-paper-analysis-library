## 1. Introduction

Presently, the rapid advancement of space research and technology has facilitated the prolonged habitation of astronauts in the space environment [1]. Yet, the space environment, which includes microgravity, radiation, hypomagnetic fields and other conditions, can substantially affect astronauts’ health and well-being [2,3]. The microgravity environment, for instance, can impact human skeletal muscles, while the redistribution of body fluids and other factors affect the cardiovascular and cerebrovascular systems [4]. In addition, microgravity radiation, stress, and other factors impact the nervous and immune systems, and the confinement of space claustrophobic life affects mental health [5,6]. Currently, aerospace medicine research focuses primarily on the changes of the musculoskeletal [7,8], cardiovascular [9], and cerebrovascular systems [10] in microgravity, whereas the neurological and cognitive impairment of astronauts is also a major factor that directly impedes the successful completion of space missions. Hence, increasing focus is being placed on this area.

Studies have demonstrated that microgravity can diminish neuronal function and cognitive abilities by altering the microenvironment of the brain (BME) [11,12]. Changes in the structure of neurons in the brain stem nuclei and cerebral cortex of mice under microgravity result in decreased neuronal activity [13]. Microglia, the brain’s primary resident immune cells, play a pivotal role in regulating the brain microenvironment (BME) by exerting both pro-inflammatory and anti-inflammatory effects to modulate neuronal function, synaptic activity, and provide nutritional support for neurons and other cells within the microenvironment [14]. As specialized macrophages in the central nervous system (CNS), microglia share the fundamental immune characteristics of macrophages found throughout the body but are distinct in their origin, distribution, and function, particularly in their unique adaptation to the neural context. Upon detecting external stimuli such as pressure, damage, hypoxia, ischemia, infection, and oxidative stress, microglia primarily regulate the BME through shifts in the M1 (pro-inflammatory) and M2 (anti-inflammatory) phenotypes [15]. Rapid activation allows microglial cells to migrate to damaged regions, where they produce anti-inflammatory factors and phagocytose debris [16]. However, persistent external stressors can lead to microglial overactivation, escalating the inflammatory response. This feedback mechanism enhances chemokine synthesis, drawing more microglial cells to the site of injury, potentially leading to excessive phagocytosis, neuronal damage, or even severe neurodegeneration and death, thereby contributing to inflammatory damage in brain neurons [17]. Such dynamics are closely associated with neuroinflammatory disorders, including Alzheimer’s disease, traumatic brain injury, Parkinson’s disease, and autoimmune encephalomyelitis [18]. Understanding the nuanced interplay between microglial and macrophage cells underscores the complexity of CNS immunity and highlights the critical role of microglia in maintaining CNS homeostasis and responding to pathological conditions. In recent years, it has been discovered that microglia may be the primary factor in microgravity-induced neurological decline. Lin et al. discovered that microgravity simulation might boost the activity of microglia, the secretion of INF- and TNF, and the neurogenesis in the hippocampus [19]. Another study indicated that unloading the hind limbs of mice can result in decreased gene expression alterations in the gray and white matter of the spinal cord of mice, as well as phenotypic changes in microglia and astrocytes [20]. Overall, the current understanding of microglial responses to microgravity is still limited, and additional investigation is required in order to comprehensively clarify the mechanisms that underlie these alterations. Thus, exploring the chemical mechanism by which microglia respond to changes in microgravity to regulate the BME will provide novel perspectives on strategies for mitigating and managing the cognitive decline observed in astronauts due to the effects of microgravity experienced during space travel.

The present study aims to provide an understanding of the molecular process underlying the behavior of microglia under conditions of microgravity. In this study, BV2 cells, representative of microglia, were cultivated in a Random Positioning Machine (RPM) system, specifically a 3D-clinostat to simulate microgravity. This RPM system, featuring software-controlled, independently rotating frames, continuously alters speed and direction to effectively simulate microgravity by neutralizing Earth’s gravitational force. These cells were then compared to a control group under normal conditions. The objective was to examine alterations in cell viability, cell morphology, and cell apoptosis, as well as to analyze the polarization of microglia in their initial state and following exposure to simulated microgravity. Flow cytometry was employed for this analysis. The gene expression of microglia in response to microgravity was further analyzed by RNA-seq transcriptome sequencing, and the key differentially expressed molecules were validated by quantitative polymerase chain reaction (qPCR) following an in-depth analysis of signal pathways associated with its function. This study provides a theoretical foundation for elucidating the impact of microgravity on the nervous system, establishing countermeasures against functional damage to the nervous system caused by space flight, and serving as a reference for future deep space exploration and long-term manned space medical support.

## 2. Materials and methods

## 2.1. Cells

BV2 cells were obtained from the Chinese Academy of Sciences $( \mathsf{CA} \mathsf{S} ,$ Shanghai, China). Cells were maintained in DMEM/F-12 (Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle Medium F-12, Gibco, Carlsbad, USA) at a temperature of

$37~^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ in an environment containing 5% $\mathrm{CO} _ {2}$ The medium was added with 10% fetal bovine serum (04-001-1ACS), 2.2 g/L of sodium bicarbonate, 2 mmol/L of L-glutamine, and 100 IU/mL of penicillin and 100 μg/mL of streptomycin.

## 2.2. RPM system

The RPM system, centered around the 3D-clinostat model SM-31 from the Center for Space and Applied Research at the Chinese Academy of Sciences, employs a software program to control its rotating mode as illustrated in Fig. 1A. This system is uniquely designed with inner and outer frames that attach to separate rotating shafts, enabling them to operate independently. This design allows for the continuous adjustment of speed and direction, facilitating a stochastic rotation pattern. Such dynamic operation effectively neutralizes the resultant vector of gravitational force exerted by the Earth. By doing ${\bf {s o}} ,$ the clinostat replicates the microgravity conditions akin to those experienced in outer space, offering a precise and controllable environment for conducting microgravity research. This simulation capability makes the RPM system an invaluable tool for exploring the effects of microgravity on biological and physical processes, providing insights that are otherwise challenging to obtain in Earth-bound laboratories. Prior to the simulation of microgravity conditions using the RPM system, cells were seeded at a specific density onto carrier slides that had been thoroughly sterilized. These slides were then strategically positioned within a sixwell plate, prepared for the experiment. Subsequently, these slides were secured in designated slots within tailor-made culture flasks, which were then meticulously filled with media devoid of air bubbles. The design of these bespoke culture flasks is a cuboid (dimensions 30 L × 20 $\mathrm{W} \times 35 \mathrm{H} \mathrm{mm} )$ , featuring three slots for slides, and each flask presents a sampling area of roughly 3000 $\mathrm{mm} ^ {2}$ accommodating about 7 million cells (as illustrated in Fig. 1A). Ultimately, these culture flasks were affixed to the RPM system set to a stochastic mode, allowing for random changes in direction and speed (ranging from 0 to 8 rpm). The system was then incubated at $37~^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ within a 5% $\mathrm{CO} _ {2}$ incubator. For comparative purposes, a control group of cells was cultured under standard gravitational conditions, without the application of the RPM system’s random flipping mechanism, to serve as a baseline for normal cellular growth and behavior [21,22].

## 2.3. Observation and identification of cell concentration and phenotype

After 24 h of treatment by RPM, BV2 cells were collected under a microscope at 20 × and 40 × to examine and document changes in the different samples after treatment. Cell concentration was measured and analyzed by using the manual method and the Beckman Coulter Vi-Cell XR (Product No: 383,556, Software version: Vi-CELL XR 2.06.2, BeckmanCoulter, Inc., USA). The phenotype of BV2 cells may be altered in response to stimulation after being subjected to RPM, for which we used the antibodies CD86 and CD80 on a flow cytometric platform.

## 2.4. BV2 cell viability

The approach employed for the detection of cell viability in BV2 cells involved the utilization of the CCK-8 (Cell Counting Kit-8). A total of 1.8 $\times 10 ^ {7}$ cells were introduced into a 6-well plate, with each well initially holding $2 \times {10} ^ {4}$ cells and a carrier slide. The BV2 cells were pre-cultured in an incubator set at a temperature of $37~^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ and $\mathsf{a} \mathrm{CO} _ {2}$ concentration of 5%. Once the cellular density reaches 75%, the carrier slide holding the cells is transferred to a rotating box for a 24-h treatment. Following the treatment in RPM, the culture medium is substituted with a fresh solution including 10% CCK8. The specimens were subjected to incubation for a duration of 2 h under conditions of darkness. Following incubation, the absorbance at 450 nm was quantified using the Synergy HTBio-Tek Corporation USA instrument.

## 2.5. BV2 cell apoptosis

The BV2 apoptosis was assessed by the Annexin VFITC kit (C1062 LHaimen China, Bosera Institute of Biotechnology, Beijing). Prior to experimentation, BV2 cells were grown and subjected to rotation for a duration of 24 h. Subsequently, the previously collected culture supernatant was introduced to the digested cells and subjected to centrifugation at 1000g for a duration of 5 min. The experimental group underwent treatment with Annexin V-FITC and PI solution, whereas the control group received treatment with a non-staining solution (specifically, Annexin V-FITC bonding solution only). Additionally, there were two single staining groups, one using PI staining and the other using Annexin V-FITC staining, as well as a double staining group utilizing both dyes. Subsequently, the cells were subjected to incubation in a light-restricted environment at ambient temperature for a duration of 10–20 min, followed by detection using flow cytometry (FACSCalibur, BD Company, USA).

## 2.6. Transcriptome sequencing

Transcriptome sequencing refers to the procedure of sequencing the complete set of RNA molecules transcribed from a particular tissue or cell at a specified temporal and physiological state, and consists of two main steps: library building and sequencing and bioinformatics analysis.

The library construction consists of total RNA extraction, mRNA isolation and fragmentation, cDNA synthesis, repair and final library enrichment, followed by initial quantification using a Qubit 2.0 Fluorometer, detection of library insert size by Agilent 2100 bioanalyzer and effective library concentration by q-PCR. After reaching the standard, the library was sequenced and the data was analyzed. In this study, the transcriptome sequencing of BV2 cells was mainly performed by Bemac (Beijing, China), and the sequencing platform was Illumina. The sequencing was quality tested to ensure the reliability of the data via FastQC program, with the percentage of Q30 bases for each sample being no less than 95.88%, which is higher than the required 85%.

## 2.7. Differential expression analysis and enrichment analysis

Cleaned reads to the mouse reference genome (version: GRCm38. p6) were aligned by using hisat2 software. Then the expression volume was performed using packages such as Rsubread, limma, degeR, etc. The variance analysis of the data was conducted using the DESeq2 software. DESeq2 adjusts p-values using the Benjamini-Hochberg procedure to control the false discovery rate (FDR), with DEGs identified based on an adjusted p-value threshold. The genes exhibiting differential expression (DEGs) were visually represented as volcano plots and heat maps. Furthermore, the GO/KEGG enrichment analysis was performed by using packages enrichGO and enrichKEGG.

## 2.8. Quantitative real-time PCR analysis

The BV2 cells were injected and pre-cultured in an incubator, followed by a 24-h rotation period, as previously mentioned. The extraction of total RNA from cells was performed using the HiPure Total RNA Mini Kit (Magen Institute of Biotechnology, R4111-02, Guangzhou, China). Subsequently, the extracted RNA was reverse transcribed into cDNA using the HiScript® II QRT SuperMix (Vazyme Institute of Biotechnology for qPCR, R223-01, Nanjing, China). The PCR instrument used for gene amplification was the Applied Biological System Gene Amplification PCR System 9700 (USA). The detection of gene expression was performed via quantitative polymerase chain reaction (qPCR) with the ChamQTM SYBR ® qPCR Master Mix (Vazyme Institute of Biotechnology, Q311-02, Nanjing, China) and Real-Time PCR Instruments (Bio-Rad company, CFX 96, USA), including Arg1 (Arginase 1), Ptgs2 (Prostaglandin-Endoperoxide Synthase 2), Src (Src Proto-Oncogene), Fos (Fos Proto-Oncogene), Il1b, (Interleukin 1 Beta), Clec7a (C-Type Lectin Domain Family 7 Member A), F10 (Coagulation Factor X) and 3 new genes (Gm44851, Gm22748, Gm9831. All pcr primers are showed in Table 1. The expression data of these genes were analyzed by $2 ^ {- \Delta \Delta \mathrm{Ct}}$ method, with GAPDH serving as the internal reference gene.

## 2.9. Statistical analysis

The biological experimental results were reported as the mean value plus or minus the standard deviation (SD). Statistical analysis and graphing were performed using GraphPad Prism 8.0.2 software. The significance of differences was assessed using an unpaired t test. A significance level of $p < 0.05$ was deemed to indicate a statistically significant difference and was denoted by the symbol “\*”. Similarly, a significance level of $p < 0.01$ was regarded as indicative of a highly significant difference and was denoted by the symbol “\*\*”.

## 3. Results

## 3.1. Reduced cell activity under simulated microgravity

The micrographic and Vi-CELL analysis are shown in Fig. 1B. The percent concentration of BV-2 cells revealed a reduction in the identical visual field following a 24-h treatment of RPM, in comparison to the control group. According to the results of CCK-8 activity of BV2 cells following RPM treatment, additionally, it was observed that the administration of RPM resulted in a considerable inhibition of BV2 cell viability (Fig. 1C). Preliminary results at cytological level showed that microgravity will negatively affect the activity of BV2 cells.

## 3.2. Activation M1 polarization of BV2 cells under simulated microgravity

BV2 cell function significantly depends on their phenotypic changes. Following RPM treatment, cell morphology transformed, adopting an amoeba-like appearance: cells became round and enlarged, resembling soft gelatin; their capsule walls turned transparent, with a wrinkly outer layer (Fig. 1B). These observations suggest that microgravity might influence BV2 cell polarization. Consequently, further investigations were conducted to explore these phenotypic alterations using flow cytometry. Results showed a marked increase in M1 phenotype BV2 cells compared to controls, indicating that microgravity can indeed alter macrophage phenotypes, triggering M1 polarization activation (Fig. 2).

## 3.3. Induced late apoptosis of BV2 cells under simulated microgravity

The study also detected BV2 cell apoptosis under simulated microgravity using the Annexin V-FITC/PI double staining kit. The results of the study revealed that there was no statistically significant difference in the early apoptosis of BV2 cells compared to the control group. However, it was noted that simulated microgravity could significantly induce late apoptosis in BV2 cells, resulting in a significant decrease in the proportion of viable cells. Consequently, this process causes considerable harm to BV2 cells (Fig. 3).

## 3.4. Transcriptome sequencing for differential expression analysis

To further investigation, transcriptome sequencing was conducted on cells from both the control and the simulated microgravity groups. Differential analysis was conducted using a fold change threshold of ≥2 and an adjusted p-value cutoff of <0.05. This analysis identified a total of 142 differentially expressed genes (DEGs) in the BV2 cells transcriptome between the RPM group and the control group. Of these DEGs, 121 were up-regulated, and 21 were down-regulated. The volcano plots (Fig. 4A) displayed differentially expressed genes (DEGs) that were either up-regulated or down-regulated. Subsequently, hierarchical cluster analysis was utilized to graphically represent these genes in a heatmap (Fig. 4B), effectively visualizing the expression patterns.

## 3.5. Enrichment analysis of DEGs

The Gene Ontology (GO) function annotation and Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) pathway databases provide essential insights into the classification and investigation of gene functions. To better understand the biological processes associated with the DEGs, GO analysis was performed on the screened genes, of which the up-regulated genes were enriched by bar graphs and chord plot to show the BP (biological processes) (Fig. 5A and B), MF (molecular functions), and CC (cellular composition), including cytokine-mediated signaling pathway, positive regulation of defense response, regulation of apoptotic signaling pathway, positive regulation of inflammatory response, fever generation, lymphocyte proliferation, negative regulation of synaptic transmission, heat generation, mononuclear cell proliferation, regulation of inflammatory response are among the most prominent biological processes. Subsequently, KEGG analysis was performed and the enrichment results were presented by bubble plots and bar plot (Fig. 5C and D), of which IL-17 signaling pathway, NF-kappa B signaling pathway, Neutrophil extracellular trap formation, TNF signaling pathway, Viral protein interaction with cytokine and cytokine receptor, Cytokine-cytokine receptor interaction, Estrogen signaling pathway are the most significant pathways. DEGs that were implicated in various biological processes were Arg1, Il1b, Ptgs2, Src, Fos, Clec7a and F10. The significance of these genes in the regulation of microglia has been acknowledged. Therefore, we have selected these particular genes as potential candidates for the purpose of validating the results obtained from RNA sequencing. In addition to the aforementioned established genes, the three most prominent novel genes among the DEGs were also chosen as candidate genes for validation.

## 3.6. Validation of the DEGs

Based on the analysis of DEGs, GO analysis, and pathway analysis, ten candidate genes were selected for further qPCR validation, as outlined in Table 1. Of these, 7 genes were found to be up-regulated, while 3 were down-regulated. The qPCR assay was utilized to validate the findings of RNA sequencing by examining these messenger RNAs (mRNAs). ## 4. Discussion

The cognitive abilities of astronauts may suffer in the microgravity environment of spaceflight, potentially compromising their capacity to carry out tasks crucial to the success of their missions. Previous studies have demonstrated that the exposure to microgravity has the potential to induce modifications in both the structure and function of the brain. These variations encompass a decrease in the amount of gray matter as well as changes in the flow of blood within the cerebral region [23]. The aforementioned alterations have been linked to deficits in attention, memory, spatial orientation, and various other cognitive domains. Cognitive impairments possess notable ramifications for the achievement of objectives, as they have the potential to undermine the safety and effectiveness of crew operations, resulting in errors and delays in the process of making decisions. Addressing these risks is vital, requiring effective strategies to maintain cognitive function in microgravity. Potential interventions to enhance brain health and function in space may encompass several approaches, such as cognitive training, pharmaceutical interventions, and other tactics.

Structural studies have provided confirmation of the neurological and cognitive deterioration induced by microgravity [24]. Recent research highlights the role of microglia, key in regulating the brain’s response to stress and inflammation, as significant contributors to microgravity-induced neurological damage [25]. The investigation pertaining to the interaction between macrophages and microgravity has experienced a surge in popularity in recent times. A search utilizing the terms “microgravity” and “macrophage” yields a total of 172 articles available on the Pubmed database. Recent scholarly articles have extensively explored the impact of microgravity on cellular behavior and its subsequent consequences, showcasing remarkable advancements across several domains. Multiple research investigations have demonstrated that the absence of gravity, known as microgravity, has the potential to expedite the process of programmed cell death, sometimes referred to as apoptosis, in various cell types including bone cells and human lymphocytes [26,27]. In our study, the results also demonstrate that microgravity can increase macrophage apoptosis (Fig. 3), with significantly more apoptotic cells in microgravity culture compared to the control group. This increased apoptosis could be one of the causes of neuroinflammation.

Moreover, our research observed a detrimental impact of microgravity on the proliferation and morphology of BV2 cells. Specifically, the cells exhibited an inability to maintain their typical shape within the RPM system, resulting in a decrease in cell concentration. The results of the cell morphology, apoptosis, and cell viability assays indicated that microgravity causes some harm to BV2 cells, and it is likely that this damage is intimately associated with disorders of the central nervous system that affect astronauts.

Microglia, by altering their polarity, are crucial in maintaining the nervous system’s stability, suggesting microgravity’s potential role in neurodegenerative disorders, a subject still in its infancy of research [25, 28]. NASA’s year-long spaceflight experiment highlighted cognitive and visual performance declines in astronauts, underscoring the importance of studying long-term space travel’s effects on the human brain, including microgravity’s impact on glial cell functions [29]. Recent studies, including investigations into space flight-related neuroocular syndrome (SANS) [30] and changes in visual perception [31], along with research on Drosophila melanogaster’s central nervous system in microgravity [32], highlight the growing interest in microgravity’s effects on the brain.

The M1/M2 phenotype switch of microglia is crucial for regulating the BME. Recent advancements have shown progress in enhancing microglial phagocytosis [33]. In our experiments, we collected the markers CD80 and CD86 on the cells to investigate their phenotype changes. We found that microgravity treatment mainly induced the M1 phenotype in BV2 cells, which secreted pro-inflammatory cytokines and chemokines and participated in positive immune responses. This phenotype also occurred when the infection or inflammation was very severe. Similar findings, such as ultrasound-driven piezoelectric material discharges causing macrophage M1 polarization, support the significance of managing microglial activity to prevent damage [34]. Understanding cell polarization is very important for disease treatment, and further research can focus on the role of some regulatory factors. Additionally, microglial apoptosis has been linked to changes in polarization phenotype, with certain drugs inducing apoptosis to mitigate neuroinflammation and promote neurorepair, and potentially enhancing neurogenesis and synaptic plasticity [35–37].

We sequenced the transcriptome of samples before and after microgravity treatment and found that abnormal cell polarization could cause brain diseases, especially neurodegenerative diseases. By conducting an examination of transcriptome data, it was determined that the expression trend of nine out of ten DEGs was confirmed. However, it was observed that the expression of gene F10 did not exhibit a statistically significant increase according to the qPCR findings. The observed challenges in replicating RNA-seq results may arise from variations in expression levels among replicates. This study aimed to analyze the relationship between cytokine receptors, the TNF signaling pathway, and the NF-kappa B signaling pathway, offering insights for future research.

All four genes that were identified as DEGs exhibited up-regulation in the RPM group including Il1b, Ptgs2, Src and Fos. Among them, Il1b in microglia can stimulate mitochondrial damage and cell death, resulting in neuronal death [38]. The up-regulation of Il1b may lead to neuroinflammatory responses in microgravity environment, and finally induced neuronal apoptosis. The gene Ptgs2, alternatively referred to as COX-2, is responsible for encoding the enzyme cyclooxygenase-2 (COX-2). This enzyme plays a role in the synthesis of prostaglandins, a class of lipid molecules that exhibit diverse physiological and pathological roles inside the human body [39]. Numerous research have been conducted to examine the involvement of Ptgs2/COX-2 in microglia, which are the immune cells residing in the central nervous system and are known to have a significant impact on neuroinflammation and neurodegenerative disorders [40,41]. Src is a constituent of the non-receptor type tyrosine kinase family, which plays a significant role in various signaling pathways within microglia. It is involved in crucial processes including phagocytosis, adhesion, activation, and the synthesis of inflammatory factors in microglia. Consequently, Src is intricately associated with neuroinflammation. Previous studies have demonstrated that the suppression of Src kinase activity can effectively impede the activation of microglia in the brains of various animal models of Parkinson’s disease. This inhibition consequently leads to neuroprotective outcomes. Additionally, it has been observed that the inhibition of Src kinase activity can also mitigate neuroinflammatory responses and cognitive impairments in transgenic mice with Alzheimer’s disease [42,43]. Fos is also an important gene in neuroinflammation, and there have been numerous reports on Fos and neurodegenerative diseases [44,45]. Its overexpression in microgravity may regulate the polarization state of microglia thus begin to affect the BME and contribute to the progression of diseases.

Two down-regulated DEGs were also verified in RPM group by q-PCR assay. Clec7a is a pattern recognition receptor that belongs to the non-Toll-like receptor family. It is expressed on several immune cells, including microglia. Existing evidence indicates that Clec7a has a role in regulating the phagocytic activity of microglia [46]. Arg1 is an enzymatic catalyst that facilitates the hydrolysis of arginine, resulting in the production of ornithine and urea. The aforementioned enzyme assumes a pivotal function in the process of cellular repair subsequent to an inflammatory response. The mechanism of action of this compound is in direct contrast to that of inducible nitrogen synthase (iNOS), which is responsible for the conversion of arginine to nitric oxide (NO) and is associated with oxidative stress and apoptosis [47,48]. The findings of our study align with previous research and provide further evidence that Arg1 may serve as a crucial molecular target under microgravity conditions. Hence, it is imperative to conduct further research on Arg1 within the context of this procedure.

In the following section, we outline the limitations of our study and highlight areas for future research. Initially, it should be noted that the data acquired from cytological assays exhibits certain limitations and deficiencies. To address these shortcomings and increase the understanding of the molecular mechanisms of microglia under microgravity conditions, more investigations involving in vivo research are warranted. Furthermore, it is imperative for future research to prioritize the investigation of these supplementary differentially expressed genes (DEGs) in conjunction with various processes. Furthermore, contemporary research has predominantly examined the temporal alterations of genes by utilizing RNA-seq technology. In order to enhance future research endeavors, it is imperative to employ more sophisticated methodologies, such as single-cell sequencing, to generate a gene expression map that offers a more thorough depiction of the intricate intracellular network. We possess a strong belief that the utilization of single-cell sequencing data will facilitate the development of more dependable and precise networks for subsequent scholarly investigations. Ultimately, there exist numerous disparities between simulated microgravity and the authentic space environment, which have the potential to impact the progression of diseases. In order to gain a thorough understanding of the mechanisms in question, it is imperative to conduct comprehensive and systematic studies, which will serve as the primary focus of future research endeavors.

In conclusion, our study suggests that microgravity-induced changes in microglia could affect neuroinflammation and neurodegeneration in the brain, potentially impacting critical cognitive functions such as attention and memory, essential for space missions. Our findings reveal potential mechanisms of microgravity’s effects on microglia, suggesting possibilities for future therapeutic interventions to protect cognitive functions. Further research will validate these initial results and explore effective protection strategies.
