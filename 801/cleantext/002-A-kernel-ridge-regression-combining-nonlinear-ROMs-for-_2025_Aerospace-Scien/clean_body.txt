## 1. Introduction

Accurate prediction of flow fields, particularly those containing discontinuities like shocks, is crucial for aerodynamic design. These discontinuities significantly impact the overall flow behavior and performance, demanding high-fidelity simulation tools. Although traditional computational fluid dynamics (CFD) methods have been developed rapidly in the past decades, their high computational expense limits their applications in design optimization processes especially for engineering problems. Therefore, the development of reduced-order models (ROMs) has been motivated in recent years, aiming to capture the essential characteristics of the flow fields using a significantlyreduced number of dimensions. In comparison with full-order CFD simulations, the ROMs can lead to substantial improvements in computational efficiency.

Traditional ROMs operate by projecting the high-dimensional flow fields onto a low-dimensional subspace spanned by a set of linear modes. These modes are typically obtained by proper orthogonal decomposition (POD) [1] or dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) [27]. In the existing studies, POD and DMD have been widely applied to flow-field modeling [10,21,25,28,36], prediction [12,23,33,36] and analysis [16,18,20,24, 32,34]. However, since the sharp gradients and nonlinear nature of discontinuities are poorly approximated by the smooth modes from POD or DMD, these POD-based or DMD-based methods often struggle to accurately capture the discontinuities in the flow fields. This leads to significant errors in the reconstruction of the flow fields, particularly in the discontinuous regions. Although methods such as local partitioning [14], weighted POD [2,5] and combinatorial method [15] have been proposed to address nonlinear flow-field prediction and analysis, the linearity of these methods limits their further application in modeling flow fields with discontinuities.

To address this issue, researchers began employing nonlinear ROMs, of which the most famous one is manifold learning (ML). The ML assumes that high-dimensional data is distributed on a low-dimensional manifold, which can capture the intrinsic features and relationships between the data. The pioneer works on the ML include the isometric mapping (ISOMAP) [30] and local linear embedding (LLE) [26] which were both published in 2000. The ISOMAP originates from the classical multiple dimensional scaling (MDS) [31]. It replaces the Euclidean distance used in MDS with the geodesic distance, allowing it to achieve a low-dimensional manifold from high-dimensional nonlinear data while simultaneously preserving both local linear structures and global nonlinear structures. Unlike the ISOMAP, which preserves the distances between nearby samples, the LLE extracts the manifold structure by maintaining the topological relationships among samples within a neighborhood. It assumes that each sample in the high-dimensional space can be reconstructed by a linear combination of its neighboring samples, and such a linear relationship can be preserved in the low-dimensional manifold space. These two ML methods have already been applied in the dimensionality reduction and analysis of nonlinear flow fields. Tauro [29] employed the ISOMAP to identify flow patterns while Marra [22] described the relationship between manifold coordinates and physical parameters. Farzamnik [7] have done a similar work, but found that there is a correlation between the manifold coordinates and dimensionless force coefficients. Ehlert [6] adopted the LLE to identify the reduced-order representations of cylinder wake transients. In the modeling of nonlinear flow, Franz [9] firstly applied the ISOMAP to predict the transonic steady flow fields. The prediction results for the airfoil and wing indicated that the pressure distribution obtained using nonlinear ROMs is more accurate, especially near the shock, compared to linear ROMs. This work demonstrated the potential of the ML-based ROMs in modeling flow fields with discontinuities. Thereafter, more flow fields modeling methods based on ML have been developed. For example, Decker [4] combined the ML and manifold alignment to build a multi-fidelity nonlinear ROM. Zheng [35] adopted constrained optimization genetic algorithm (COGA), using the distances between the sample and its neighbors as constraints to optimize the interpolated manifold coordinates, aiming to achieve more accurate prediction results with small sample size. Li [19] respectively used the ISOMAP to extract global features and the LLE to extract local features of the flow fields, achieving accurate prediction of transonic and supersonic flow fields.

## 2. Methodology

In this work, an accurate reconstruction method KRR-based discontinuity-capturing reconstruction (KRR-DCR) for flow fields with discontinuities is developed. The proposed method consists of three steps. First, we perform the dimensionality reduction of the flow fields by the ML, aiming to find the hidden low-dimensional nonlinear manifold of the original high-dimensional flow fields. Second, a set of mode coefficients and modes are obtained by the kernel ridge regression (KRR) with the manifold coordinates and the original flow fields. Lastly, the original flow fields are accurately reconstructed through a nonlinear combination of the manifold coordinates and the constructed modes. ## 2.1. Dimensionality reduction based on ML

Since flow fields with discontinuities exhibits strong nonlinearity, traditional linear dimensionality reduction techniques are not applicable. Therefore, it is necessary to use nonlinear methods, among which ML is one of the most representative approaches.

First, n flow-field samples are obtained by CFD simulations. Each sample is vectorized to an m-dimensional vector $\pmb {x} _ {i}$ (subscript i indicates the ith sample) and all of them form a samples matrix $\pmb {X} \in \mathbb {R} ^ {n \times m}$ . The ML assumes that X is embedded on a p-dimensional manifold. Generally, p is much smaller than m. Thus, if we can build a mapping f(⋅)between the original high-dimensional flow fields and low-dimensional manifold, the dimensionality reduction of the flow fields can be achieved, expressed as,

$$\pmb {Y} = \pmb {f} ( \pmb {X} )\tag{2.1}$$

where $\pmb {Y} {\in} \mathbb {R} ^ {n \times p}$ is the low-dimensional representation of X.

According to the principles of constructing $f ( \cdot )$ , the ML can be divided into various types. The LLE and ISOMAP are two of the most commonly used ML methods. The main processes of the LLE and ISO-MAP are described in Appendix A. The comparison of KKR-DCR using the LLE and ISOMAP is presented in Section 3.2.

## 2.2. KRR-based discontinuity-capturing reconstruction (KRR-DCR)

Since the low-dimensional representation Y are obtained by the ML, we need to build a reconstruction model to reobtain the original flow fields X.

Drawing inspiration from the flow reconstruction by the POD, we aim to find the mode matrix $\pmb {W} \in \mathbb {R} ^ {p \times m}$ that satisfies

$$\pmb {X} \approx \pmb {X} ^ {\prime} = \pmb {Y} \pmb {W} ^ {T}\tag{2.2}$$

where $\pmb {X} ^ {'} \in \mathbb {R} ^ {n \times m}$ is the reconstructed flow-field matrix.

Formula (2.2) is a linear reconstruction of X. Howeve $\mathbf {r} ,$ in order to achieve accurate reconstruction in nonlinear regions considering discontinuities, it is desirable to introduce a mode matrix $W _ {\mathrm{nonlinear}}$ for nonlinear reconstruction. Thus, we map Y from the manifold space to the kernel inner product space using a kernel function $\Phi ( \cdot )$ , and construct $\pmb {W} _ {\mathrm{nonlinear}}$ in this space by

$$\pmb {X} \approx \pmb {X} ^ {\prime} = \Phi ( \pmb {Y} , \pmb {Y} ) \pmb {W} _ {\mathrm{nonlinear}} ^ {T} = \pmb {Z} \pmb {W} _ {\mathrm{nonlinear}} ^ {T}\tag{2.3}$$

where Z is the kernel matrix, which represents the nonlinear similarity between training samples.

Then, we use ridge regression to solve $W _ {\mathrm{nonlinear}}$ by minimizing the following target function

$$\begin{array} {r} {\Vert \textbf {\em X} - \pmb {Z} \pmb {W} _ {\mathrm{nonlinear}} ^ {T} \Vert _ {F} ^ {2} + \lambda \Vert \textbf {\em W} _ {\mathrm{nonlinear}} \ \Vert _ {F} ^ {2}} \end{array}\tag{2.4}$$

where ‖ ⋅‖F represents the Frobenius norm; λ is the L2 regularization coefficient. Formula (2.4) consists of two parts: the first indicates the difference between the original flow fields and the reconstructed flow fields; the second prevents overfitting. The influence of λ is further discussed in Section 3.4.

Let formula (2.4) equals 0, and we can obtain the expression for Wnonlinear

$$\pmb {W} _ {\mathrm{nonlinear}} = \pmb {X} ^ {T} \pmb {Z} \big ( \pmb {Z} ^ {T} \pmb {Z} + \lambda \pmb {I} \big ) ^ {+}\tag{2.5}$$

where $\scriptstyle I \in \mathbb {R} ^ {n \times n _ {\mathrm{i}}}$ is an identity matrix, and $( \cdot ) ^ {+}$ represents pseudo-inverse. $W _ {\mathrm{nonlinear}}$ is an $m \times n$ matrix, where each column represents the mode corresponding to each training sample.

Assuming we have obtained the low-dimensional representations of k new flow fields in manifold space, which form a matrix $\pmb {Y} _ {\mathrm{new}} \in \mathbb {R} ^ {k \times p}$ , the reconstructed flow-field matrix $\pmb {X} _ {\mathrm{new}} ^ {\prime} \in \mathbb {R} ^ {k \times p}$ is

$$\pmb {X} _ {\mathrm{new}} = \pmb {Z} _ {\mathrm{new}} \pmb {W} _ {\mathrm{nonlinear}} ^ {T} = \Phi ( \pmb {Y} _ {\mathrm{new}} , \pmb {Y} ) \pmb {W} _ {\mathrm{nonlinear}} ^ {T}\tag{2.6}$$

where $Z _ {\mathrm {{n e w}}}$ is the kernel matrix between the new samples and the

training samples.

## 2.3. Kernel method

During the construction of the mode matrix $W _ {\mathrm{nonlinear}}$ in the above section, we use a kernel function to project manifold space into the kernel inner product space. In this section, the basic idea of the kernel method is detailed.

The mathematical foundation of the kernel method lies in the concept of mapping data into a higher-dimensional space $\mathcal {H}$ and then leveraging inner products in that space to solve linear problems in place of solving the nonlinear problem in the original space $\mathcal {X}$ by the mapping function $\phi ,$ expressed as,

$$\phi : \mathcal {X} \to \mathcal {H}\tag{2.7}$$

Since most of the mapping functions cannot be explicitly computed, the kernel method uses a kernel function $K ( \cdot ) \mathrm{to}$ directly compute the inner product of two samples in $\mathcal {H}$

$$K ( x , y ) = \langle \phi ( x ) , \phi ( y ) \rangle\tag{2.8}$$

which represents the similarity between x and y.

For two data matrices $\pmb {A} \in \mathbb {R} ^ {r \times d}$ and $\pmb {B} \in \mathbb {R} ^ {q \times d}$ , which contain r and q samples respectively, with each data point in d dimension, the similarity between the data in A and B can be represented using a kernel matrix $z ,$ expressed by

## 3. Method validation and parameter analysis

To validate and evaluate the proposed KRR-DCR method, we perform the flow field reconstruction of the RAE2822 airfoil with steady transonic flow. First, the generation of the dataset is introduced, as well as the accuracy criterion. Then, the transonic flow fields are reconstructed by the proposed KRR-DCR method, and key parameter analysis is performed. At last, the proposed KRR-DCR method is compared with 2 commonly-used reconstruction methods and the physical significance of the modes constructed by our method is explained. In addition, to validate the applicability to time-dependent problems of the KRR-DCR method, a brief analysis of transonic buffet case on OAT15A airfoil is also presented.

Unless otherwise stated, all reconstructions are directly based on the pressure fields and all the contours shown are the normalized pressure. The normalization is implemented according to

$$\pmb {p} _ {\mathrm{Norm}} ^ {( i )} = \frac {\pmb {p} ^ {( i )} - \operatorname* {m i n} \left( \pmb {p} _ {\mathrm{test}} ^ {( i )} \right) \cdot \mathbf {1}} {\operatorname* {m a x} \left( \pmb {p} _ {\mathrm{test}} ^ {( i )} \right) - \operatorname* {m i n} \left( \pmb {p} _ {\mathrm{test}} ^ {( i )} \right)}\tag{3.1}$$

where $p _ {\mathrm{Norm}}$ is the normalized flow-field vector; $\pmb {P} _ {\mathrm{test}}$ is the original flowfield vector of a testing sample; 1 is an all-ones vector of the same length as $\mathbf {\phi} _ {p _ {\mathrm{test}}} ;$ superscript (i) represents the ith testing sample.

## 3.1. Dataset generation and accuracy evaluation criterion

The samples of the transonic flow fields are generated under the flight condition of $M _ {\infty} \in [ 0.734 , 0.85 ]$ and $\alpha \in [ 2 ^{\circ} , 6 ^{\circ} ]$ . Within this range of flight conditions, the position and intensity of shocks in the flow fields change drastically. The Latin hypercube sampling (LHS) is employed to select 900 samples. Since this work only focuses on the process of flowfield reconstruction and does not involve flow field prediction, all 900 samples are used for dimensionality reduction, while 50 of them are randomly selected as testing samples for flow-field reconstruction. Among the testing samples, 5 reconstruction samples are selected. These reconstruction samples are located in different regions of the sampling space to assess the performance of the reconstruction model at the boundaries and within the sampling space. The distribution of all the 900 samples in the parameter space is shown in Fig. 3. CFD simulations are performed to obtain the flow fields of all samples. The computational mesh, solver settings and accuracy validation of CFD solver is presented in Appendix B.

The accuracy of the flow-field reconstruction is qualified by measuring the mean relative reconstruction error $E _ {\mathrm{mean}}$ and the maximum relative reconstruction error $E _ {\mathrm{max}}$ on the testing samples. $E _ {\mathrm{mean}}$ represents the ability to reconstruct the global flow field, while $E _ {\mathrm{max}}$ shows the capability of capturing the local flow structures. The definition of $E _ {\mathrm{mean}}$ and $E _ {\mathrm{max}}$ are given as follows

b) Absolute error

$$E _ {\mathrm{mean}} = \frac {\sum _ {k = 1} ^ {n} \sum _ {i = 1} ^ {m} e _ {i} ^ {( k )}} {n m}\tag{3.2}$$

$$E _ {\mathrm{max}} = \frac {\sum _ {k = 1} ^ {n} \operatorname* {m a x} _ {i \in [ 1 , m ]} ( e _ {i} ^ {( k )} )} {n}\tag{3.3}$$

$$e _ {i} ^ {( k )} = \left| \frac {x _ {i} ^ {\mathrm{real}} - x _ {i} ^ {\mathrm{recons}}} {x _ {i} ^ {\mathrm{real}}} \right|\tag{3.4}$$

where n is the number of testing samples; m is the number of mesh cells; $e _ {i} ^ {( k )}$ represents relative reconstruction error on the ith cell of the kth testing sample.

Additionally, the correlation coefficient R between the reconstructed flow fields and the original flow fields is also adopted as one of the metrics for evaluating the reconstruction accuracy of the global flowfield structures. In subsequent comparisons, to highlight the differences in $R ,$ we define the values of 1 minus the correlation coefficient as $R _ {\mathrm{rs}}$

$$R _ {\mathrm{rs}} = 1 - R = 1 - {\frac {\mathrm{cov} \bigl ( {\pmb x} ^ {\mathrm{recons}} , \ {\pmb x} ^ {\mathrm{real}} \bigr )} {\sigma _ {{\pmb x} ^ {\mathrm{recons}}} \sigma _ {{\pmb x} ^ {\mathrm{real}}}}}\tag{3.5}$$

where $\operatorname{cov} ( \cdot , \cdot$ )represents the covariance and σ is the variance.

In all the expressions mentioned above, the superscripts "real" and "recons" represent the original flow-field data and the reconstructed flow-field data, respectively.

## 3.3. Parametric analysis of λ and $\gamma$

In the proposed method, there are two key hyperparameters: the L2 regularization coefficient λ and the scale factor γ, which greatly affect the reconstruction accuracy. In this section, we carry out parametric analysis of these two key hyperparameters.

The L2 regularization coefficient λ can control the influence of the regularization term on solving the mode matrix $\mathbf {\Delta} \mathbf {W} _ {\mathrm{nonlinear}} ,$ thereby balancing the fitting capability and complexity of the model. The $E _ {\mathrm{mean}} ,$ $E _ {\mathrm{max}}$ and $R _ {\mathrm{rs}}$ on the testing samples under different λ are shown in Fig. $^ {9 ,}$ where the x-axis uses a base-10 logarithmic coordinate. $E _ {\mathrm{mean}}$ decreases as λ increases and reaches minimum when λ is around $10 ^ {- 5}$ . This is because when λ is too small, the regularization term loses its effect, and is overfitted under the influence of noise. When $\lambda > 10 ^ {- 5} ,$ , as λ continues to increase, $E _ {\mathrm{mean}}$ begins to grow. While $E _ {\mathrm{max}}$ keeps increase but it is not significant before λ reaches $10 ^ {- 5} . R _ {\mathrm{rs}}$ reaches its minimum when λ is near $10 ^ {- 4}$ . However, since the increase in $R _ {\mathrm{rs}}$ from $\lambda {=} 10 ^ {- 4}$ to $10 ^ {- 5}$ is less than $10 ^ {- 5} ,$ it can be considered practically unchanged. Therefore, λ is set to $10 ^ {- 5}$

γ determines the extent to which the inner product of samples affects the polynomial kernel. When $\gamma$ is large, the inner product between samples is amplified, and the kernel function becomes more sensitive to neighboring samples with high similarity. The variations in $E _ {\mathrm{mean}} , E _ {\mathrm{max}}$ and $R _ {\mathrm{rs}}$ for different $\gamma$ are shown in Fig. 10. When $\gamma > 1.1 , E _ {\mathrm{mean}}$ is basically flat (the difference between the maximum and minimum values is less than 0.00025). While $E _ {\mathrm{max}}$ slightly increases when $\gamma {>} 1.4$ It is because the number of neighboring samples with high similarity is limited, and excessive sensitivity to these samples prevents the model from obtaining sufficient discontinuity information. Similarly, $R _ {\mathrm{rs}}$ exhibits minimal variation when γ ranges from 0.6 to 1.2. Hence, it is more rational to set $\gamma = 1.1$

The reconstructed wall pressure distributions through different methods are compared in Fig. 11. The wall pressure distribution of all samples using the POD show strong oscillations around the discontinuities. The reasons of this phenomenon could be attributed to two aspects. First, the truncation of the high-order modes in the POD leads to the loss of discontinuous information, making it inaccurate to reconstruct the details of the discontinuities in the flow fields. Second, the POD modes exhibit globally smooth characteristics, and the linear combination of these finite-order smooth modes is insufficient to accurately capture and reconstruct the local discontinuities. These result in oscillations similar to the Gibbs phenomenon observed in signal processing [11].

In comparison with the POD, both the NPBM and KRR-DCR demonstrate to be more accurate in reconstructing wall pressure, especially near the discontinuities. This is due to the ML, which is a kind of nonlinear ROM and can effectively preserve the discontinuous information in the flow fields. However, at higher Mach numbers, as is shown in Fig. 11d) and e), the NPBM begins to exhibit obvious errors in reconstructing the discontinuities on both the upper and lower surfaces, while the KRR-DCR can still maintain high accuracy. The increase in shock intensity at high Mach number leads to an enhancement of nonlinear effects, resulting in substantial variations in the manifold’s curvature near these samples beyond the applicability of local linear approximations by the NPBM. However, for the KRR-DCR, since the original manifold coordinates are nonlinearly mapped into the kernel space by the kernel function, the changes in the manifold’s curvature can be preserved. This allows the modes constructed by the KRR-DCR to capture the nonlinear discontinuous information precisely, leading to an improvement of reconstruction accuracy near the discontinuities.

However, for the reconstruction sample at $M _ {\infty} = 0.843 ~ \mathrm{and} \alpha = 5.55 ^{\circ}$ , the KRR-DCR exhibits large reconstruction errors in the discontinuous region away from upper surface with an $E _ {\mathrm{max}} {=} 27.64$ %, as shown in Fig. 16. At the same time, the isobars in this region also show oscillations similar to those observed in the POD. The reason could be insufficient number of training samples around the sampling bounds which cannot provide sufficient modes containing similar discontinuity information. Nevertheless, the KRR-DCR is still more accurate than the POD and NPBM in capturing the discontinuity of the flow fields.

To enhance the reconstruction accuracy of reconstruction sample at $M _ {\infty} = 0.843 \mathrm{and} \alpha = 5.55 ^{\circ}$ , We supplement 10 additional samples in the sampling bounds near this reconstruction sample and reconstruct the flow field accordingly. The distribution of the additional samples is shown in Fig. 17, while the reconstructed pressure field and absolute error are shown in Fig. 18. After supplementing the additional samples, the reconstruction accuracy of the flow field has been improved significantly, with $E _ {\mathrm{mean}}$ decreasing to 0.04 % and $E _ {\mathrm{max}}$ dropping to 9.95 %. This demonstrates that increasing the number of boundary training samples can effectively enhance the reconstruction accuracy of the flow fields near the bounds.

We further analyze the accuracy of physical consistency in flow fields reconstructed by different methods. We separately reconstruct the pressure, temperature and velocity fields of the reconstruction sample at

$M _ {\infty} = 0.755 , \alpha = 2.33$ . Then, the dimensionless entropy and vorticity in z-direction are calculated.

Since the freestream is modeled as the ideal gas, the dimensionless entropy ̂s simplifies to

$${\widehat {s}} = \ln \left( {\frac {T} {T _ {\infty}}} \right) - {\frac {\gamma - 1} {\gamma}} \ln \left( {\frac {p} {p _ {\infty}}} \right)\tag{3.6}$$

where specific heat ratio $\prime = 1.4 ; T$ is the temperature and $p$ is the pressure; subscript $\ " \infty \ '$ refers to freestream parameters.

The entropy isolines of flow fields reconstructed by different methods are plotted and compared with the CFD result, as is shown in Fig. 19, Fig. 20, Fig. 21. All methods exhibit significant errors in entropy near discontinuity. The POD fails to produce reconstructed flow field with physically reasonable entropy distributions. The flow fields reconstructed by both the KRR-DCR and the NPBM correctly capture the post-shock entropy rise location, but demonstrate anomalously high entropy values—a non-physical phenomenon—in the upstream pre-shock regions. However, the entropy distribution in the shock-wave/boundary-layer interaction (SBLI) region of the flow field reconstructed by the KRR-DCR shows good agreement with the CFD result. This demonstrates that the reconstructed flow fields maintain excellent physical accuracy within this region.

The vorticity isolines are shown in Fig. 22, Fig. 23, Fig. 24. The vorticity distributions near the leading-edge stagnation point obtained by all three methods show good agreement with the CFD result, indicating that these methods achieve high accuracy in flow-field reconstruction for smooth regions near the leading edge. However, significant difference exists near the discontinuous and SBLI regions. The flow field reconstructed by the POD exhibits large underpredicted vorticity production downstream of the shock. The NPBM can accurately reconstruct the vorticity production locations, though some errors persist in the magnitude. The KRR-DCR demonstrates high reconstruction accuracy for vorticity production in both locations and magnitude.

Furthermore, we set a monitoring line at y = 0.2c and $\pmb {x} = 0.4 c . 0.8 c$ as is shown in Fig. 25, and present the distributions of static pressure, density, velocity magnitude and static temperature along this line. The comparison between the results of different methods and CFD are shown in Fig. 26. The POD significantly smooths the sharp gradient of the discontinuity, and all physical quantities exhibit non-physical oscillations across the discontinuity due to the Gibbs phenomenon. Meanwhile, the NPBM and KRR-DCR effectively preserve the physical jump location at the discontinuity. However, the NPBM exhibits large errors in the magnitude of physical jumps for static temperature and velocity, while the KRR-DCR maintains satisfactory accuracy across all physical quantities.

To further validate the physical consistency of the KRR-DCR at the discontinuities, we also present the variation of total temperature along the monitoring line, as shown in Fig. 27. The POD exhibits intense oscillations throughout the entire monitoring line. Due to the inaccurate reconstruction of velocity magnitude and static temperature, the NPBM demonstrates a large oscillation amplitude at the discontinuity. While the KRR-DCR also shows some variation in total temperature, the absolute amplitude of this variation remains within 1 K.

All analyses above indicate that the KRR-DCR possesses the highest level of physical consistency.

A comparison of total time required for flow-field reconstruction across different methods is shown in Table 2 and Table 3. According to the time consumption of different methods, the KRR-DCR requires a relatively long time to solve the modes matrix $\mathbf {\Delta} \mathbf {W} _ {\mathrm{nonlinear}} ,$ but its computational efficiency in the flow-field reconstruction stage is comparable to that of the NPBM method. The computational efficiency of the POD method is significantly higher than that of the other two methods, as the POD only involves a simple linear matrix transformation during flow-field reconstruction. However, considering the reconstruction accuracy improvement achieved by the KRR-DCR, the average time consumption of 0.02 s for reconstructing a single sample is acceptable.

The first 9 modes of the POD and the 9 modes corresponding to the first 9 training samples of the KRR-DCR are compared in Fig. 28. All modes presented in the figures are constructed exclusively from the pressure fields. The modes constructed by these two methods exhibit different physical characteristics, resulting in significant difference in reconstruction accuracy in discontinuous regions.

For the POD, the modes mainly represent the energy characteristics of the flow fields. Low-order modes focus on the global characteristics, including the pressure gradient at the leading and trailing edges of the airfoil, as well as in the region with discontinuities on the upper and lower surfaces. As the order increases, the modes begin to gradually concentrate on the local discontinuity information in the flow fields as shown in mode 5–9. The values of these higher-order modes alternately exhibit maxima and minima near the discontinuities. When reconstructing the flow fields, the maxima and minima compensate for each other to capture the sharp gradient at the discontinuities. It is observed that the frequency of this alternation gradually increases as the order rises. This indicates that the lower-order modes primarily capture the overall variation of the discontinuities, which are low-frequency components. While the higher-order modes can capture the discontinuities through high-frequency components. Truncating these high-order modes could lead to the significant loss of discontinuity information, resulting in large reconstruction errors and significant oscillations in the local region near the discontinuities.

Compared to the POD, the KRR-DCR modes reflect the similarity among the training samples in the kernel inner product space. For flow fields with discontinuities, the differences among these training samples are manifested in the location and intensity of the discontinuities. Thus, each mode constructed by the KRR-DCR can precisely capture the shock’s location and intensity of its corresponding training sample, as show in Fig. 28b). Each mode of the KRR-DCR represents the energy characteristics and contains significant discontinuity information of the flow field of each training sample. This enables the KRR-DCR to ensure the reconstruction accuracy of local discontinuities. Additionally, due to the use of kernel functions, the KRR-DCR can automatically select the modes that have a high similarity to the reconstruction sample, thereby reducing the reconstruction errors caused by incorrect truncation of the

## 3.5. Applicability to time-dependent problems

In this section, the applicability to time-dependent problems of the proposed KRR-DCR method is analyzed. We apply the KRR-DCR to a transonic shock buffet case involving the OAT15A airfoil at $M _ {\infty} = 0.73 \mathrm{,}$ $\alpha = 3.5 ^{\circ} , \mathrm{Re} = 3 ~ \times ~ 10 ^ {6}$ . The computational mesh and CFD solver settings are detailed in Appendix B as well.

We generate 855 flow-field snapshots for dimensionality reduction using LLE within 1.5 periods, and randomly choose 800 snapshots as training samples to construct KRR-DCR modes. 3 reconstruction samples are selected randomly from the remaining 55 snapshots, and their corresponding lift coefficients are shown in Fig. 29.

The reconstructed pressure fields of the 3 reconstruction samples using different methods are illustrated in Fig. 30, Fig. 31, Fig. 32. In this transient case, the reconstruction accuracy of the KRR-DCR method exhibits a noticeable decline. Although it remains significantly more accurate than the POD method around the discontinuities, the reconstruction error of the KRR-DCR method is larger compared to the NPBM method.

The inaccuracies could stem from two aspects: 1) the manifold coordinates obtained through LLE when preforming dimensionality reduction do not incorporate global temporal information; 2) the selected kernel function lacks temporal characteristics.

The first two dimensions of the manifold coordinates are shown in Fig. 33. To compare the relationship between the manifold coordinates and the flow-field dynamics, we normalize the lift coefficient to (− 1, 1) and also plot it in Fig. 33. It can be observed that although the manifold coordinates still exhibit an overall periodic variation trend with time, the lack of global temporal constraints results in non-smooth variations. This also reflects the characteristic of the LLE method for dimensionality reduction: it focuses only on relationships among samples within local neighborhoods while neglecting the preservation of global manifold structure.

We further plot all the kernel values for the reconstruction sample at time step=348, as is shown in Fig. 34. The indices of training samples are arranged according to the temporal sequence of the training samples corresponding to each kernel value. The magnitude of these kernel values represents the contribution of each training sample’s corresponding KRR-DCR mode to the flow-field reconstruction. It can be observed that the kernel values exhibit an anomalous peak within the training samples at time step between 200 and 300, with its temporal position precisely coinciding with a local peak in the manifold coordinates. This indicates that the inaccuracies in the manifold coordinates lead to errors in kernel calculations. Additionally, the kernel values are computed totally based on the manifold coordinates without incorporating temporal characteristics, which further exacerbates errors. As a result, certain modes with low similarity to the reconstruction sample participate in the reconstruction process with disproportionately large weights, consequently decreasing the reconstruction accuracy.

We also select four characteristic locations on the lift coefficient curve, as shown in Fig. 35, and plot the corresponding KRR-DCR modes for each location in Fig. 36.

The results demonstrate that the KRR-DCR modes successfully capture the shock’s movements at corresponding time instances. From location a) to c), the shock progressively moves downstream, resulting in an increase in lift coefficient; whereas from c) to d), the shock retreats upstream, accompanied by a gradual decrease in lift coefficient.

However, our current KRR-DCR modes can only obtain the flow-field dynamics of the discontinuities within the training snapshots. Consequently, during extrapolative prediction, the reconstruction process becomes compromised due to the absence of sufficiently similar modes. This represents a limitation common to all current data-driven models employed in this study. The POD modes, also derived from training snapshots, cannot accurately reconstruct flow fields when flow structures lie outside the training regime. Similarly, the NPBM relies on neighborhood samples and fails when no neighboring training samples exist. Therefore, future research should focus on appropriately incorporating temporal constraints and physical laws into the modeling process.

Based on the above analyses, the following limitations of the current KRR-DCR method are identified:1) Kernel sensitivity: reconstruction accuracy exhibits significant dependence on kernel selection, parameter tuning and method of dimensionality reduction, necessitating casespecific adjusting; 2) Absence of physical constraints: the modes’ construction process lacks physical laws and constraints; 3) Extrapolation vulnerability: performance degrades substantially in extrapolation beyond the training dataset.

Therefore, to ensure that the constructed KRR-DCR method accurately reconstructs flow fields around discontinuities, the key lies in: the manifold coordinates obtained through ML precisely preserve the discontinuity information present in the samples, and the kernel values quantitatively describe the similarity between samples. Selecting appropriate ML method and kernel functions regarding the specific problem is essential to meet the reconstruction requirements.

## 4. Conclusion

In this paper, a new reconstruction method KRR-DCR is proposed to enhance the reconstruction accuracy of the flow fields with discontinuities. The main conclusions are drawn as follows:

1. After dimensionality reduction by ML, we introduce the KRR to construct a set of mode coefficients and modes to nonlinearly reconstruct the flow fields from manifold coordinates. It can significantly improve the reconstruction accuracy in the discontinuous regions in comparison with the POD and NPBM.

2. The modes constructed by the KRR-DCR show strong physical interpretability, with each mode effectively capturing the discontinuity information of its corresponding training sample.

3. The LLE is recommended for dimensionality reduction of flow fields in the KRR-DCR, and the quartic polynomial kernel function with $\lambda {=} 10 ^ {- 5}$ and $\gamma = 1.1$ are suitable for flow-field reconstruction of the RAE2822 airfoil to achieve the minimum reconstruction error.

4. The analysis of applicability to time-dependent problems shows that main limitations of the current KRR-DCR method include kernel sensitivity, absence of physical constraints and extrapolation vulnerability.

5. Future works include leveraging conservation laws or governing equations into the construction of the KRR-DCR modes and exploring the feasibility of the KRR-DCR modes for building intrusive ROMs.
