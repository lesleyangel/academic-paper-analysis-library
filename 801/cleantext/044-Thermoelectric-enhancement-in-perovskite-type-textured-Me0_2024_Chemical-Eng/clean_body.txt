## 1. Introduction

The global energy system has begun to transition from fossil fuels to clean energy, thermoelectric conversion has attracted considerable attention for its potential to achieve effective, multi-level energy management, offering a promising solution to address the escalating energy crisis [1–3]. The thermoelectric performance can be effectively characterized by the figure of merit ZT, which is defined as $Z T = S ^ {2} {\sigma} T / \kappa$ There is a great demand for thermoelectric material candidates that possess high Seebeck coefficients (S), superior electrical conductivity (σ), lower thermal conductivity (κ), and can be applied to a wide temperature range with excellent high-temperature stability. ZT values have been substantially improved in bulk thermoelectric materials (TEs) such as $\mathrm {B i _ {2} T e _ {3}}$ [4,5], SnSe $[ 6 , 7 ] _ {\scriptscriptstyle \cdot}$ , and PbTe [8,9] systems which have been commercially available in the range of medium and low temperatures (＜900 K). Different from the high-performance of these alloy TEs, metal oxides such as $\mathrm {C a _ {3} C o _ {4} O _ {9}}$ [10], ${\mathrm{NaCo}} _ {2} {\mathrm{O}} _ {4}$ [11], BiCuSeO [12], Bi Sr $\mathrm {C o _ {2} O _ {y} [ 13 ] , Z n O [ 14 ] , C a T i O _ {3} [ 15 ] , C a M n O _ {3} [ 16 ] _ {\scriptscriptstyle 2}}$ , and $\mathrm{In} _ {2} \mathrm{O} _ {3} \ [ 17 ]$ have been found to exhibit good thermoelectric performance and attracted extensive interest of researchers due to their outstanding advantages of cost-effectiveness, environmental compatibility, robust chemical stability, and exceptional thermal stability, etc..

In thermoelectric metal oxides family, $\mathrm {S r T i O _ {3}}$ has garnered significant attention as a promising candidate for n-type TEs owing to its remarkably high power factor $( P F = S ^ {2} \sigma )$ of 36 mW/(m K2 ) [18]. The outstanding stability of $\mathrm {{s r T i O _ {3}}}$ with the perovskite structure over a broad range of temperatures and oxygen partial pressures, along with its lattice flexibility in accommodating substitute cations and diverse defects, and its potential to preserve the defect chemistry at high temperatures, have captured significant interest in researchers committed to in-depth explorations and striving for enhanced thermoelectric properties. However, its high thermal conductivity, specifically $\kappa = 11$ W/(m‧ K) at 300 K for single crystal [19] and ranges from 7.16 to 2.89 W/(m‧K) for polycrystalline bulk systems over 300 K to 1073 K [20], combined with the isotropic electron and phonon coupling relationship pose challenges in enhancing the ZT value. Strategies of nanostructuring [19,21], doping [20,22–24], defect chemistry [25,26], phonon engineering [27], and entropy engineering [28–32] have been employed to enhance the thermoelectric performance. Furthermore, it has been demonstrated that high-entropy engineering can effectively boost the absolute value of S and simultaneously lower κ in TE oxide ceramics, emerging as a promising approach for optimizing their thermoelectric performance through expanded composition design. Until now, highentropy perovskite has been confirmed can be used to significantly reduce the lattice thermal conductivity but also cause the deterioration of carrier mobility and restrain electron transport resulting in low electrical conductivity and low ZT for applications. Furthermore, the electrical conductivity can be greatly enhanced through the enhancement of carrier mobility without significantly affecting carrier concentration by introducing an appropriate concentration of cation vacancies in the disordered high-entropy system while maintaining low lattice thermal conductivity [32]. However, perovskite structure polycrystalline ceramics usually have equiaxed grains and isotropic physical properties. The strong coupling relationship between electrical and thermal transport properties, which depends on carrier concentration, it is often possible to improve electrical conductivity while deteriorating thermal conductivity and vice versa, making it became a huge challenge to achieve breakthroughs in ZT.

By contrast, textured ceramics with preferred grain orientation are anticipated to exhibit anisotropy in their physical properties, is expected to enable decoupling of the electron and phonon transport characteristic, ultimately leading to optimized thermoelectric performance. Dense and highly textured ceramics with typical perovskite-type structure can be successfully fabricated via the reactive templated grain growth (RTGG) method [33,34]. During the RTGG process, template seeds compatible with matrix materials are aligned within the green body serve as nucleation sites, and promote grain growth with preferred orientation. The RTGG technique has been effectively employed in the design and fabrication of crystallographically textured ceramics such as piezoelectric ceramics [33,35] and p-type thermoelectric materials [10,11,36]. In n-type TE ceramics, the textured $\mathbf {S r} _ {0.9} \mathbf {L a} _ {0.1} \mathrm{TiO} _ {3}$ -based ceramic exhibits a texture degree of 81 % [33], the textured $\mathrm{Sr} _ {3} \mathrm{Ti} _ {2} \mathrm{O} _ {7}$ sample possesses a maximum texture fraction of 75 % [37], and the textured $\mathtt {Z n} _ {1 - \mathbf {x}} \mathtt {A l} _ {\mathbf {x}} 0$ ceramic exhibits an orientation degree of 60 % [38]. Until now, preparing n-type cubic structured thermoelectric materials with a high texture fraction remains a significant challenge due to their isotropic growth rates in three crystallographic directions. It is highly worthwhile to investigate the evolution of their microstructure and its impact on electrical and thermal conductivity properties. It is therefore urgent to propose and explore a combined optimization strategy encompassing entropy engineering and interfacial engineering, aiming to decouple strong electron–phonon coupling influences thermoelectric performances in ceramics exhibiting a high texture fraction, specifically for thermoelectric applications applied in high-temperature

environments.

In this work, perovskite structure of high-entropy Me0 $. 85 \mathrm {T i O _ {3} ( M e =}$ $\operatorname{La} , \ s \mathbf {r} ,$ Ba, Ca) based textured ceramics (denoted as LSBC-T) were fabricated using a combination of the TGG and RTGG method along with a tape casting technique. A-site deficient $( \mathrm{L} \mathsf{a} _ {0.25} S \mathrm{r} _ {0.25} \mathrm{B} \mathsf{a} _ {0.25} \mathrm{C} \mathsf{a} _ {0.25} ) _ {0.8}$ 5- $\mathrm{TiO} _ {3}$ with the addition of $\mathbf {B i} _ {2} \mathbf {O} _ {3}$ (abbreviated as LSBC) served as the matrix powders and oriented plate-like $\mathrm {S r T i O _ {3}}$ with a (0 0 1) orientation was introduced as template seeds. A comprehensively investigation was conducted to reveal the phase transition, the potential mechanism of microstructural evolution, and thermoelectric performance of the textured LSBC-T ceramics. Reveal and clarify the evolution of oriented grain structure and epitaxial growth kinetics in high-entropy textured ceramics. Additionally, the underlying mechanism that facilitates the decoupling effect of electron–phonon interaction is discussed and illustrated.

## 2. Experimental section

## 2.1. Materials and method

The matrix powder of (La0.25Sr0.25Ba0.25Ca0.25)0.8 $\phantom {} _ {5} \mathrm{TiO} _ {3}$ was obtained by the solid phase reaction method. Start materials of $\mathbf {L a _ {2} O _ {3}}$ (50 nm), SrCO3, BaCO3, CaCO3, and $\mathrm{TiO} _ {2}$ (50 nm) powders (analytical pure, Sinopharm Chemical Reagent $\mathrm{Co.,}$ Ltd.) were weighed according to its composition of $( \mathrm{La} _ {0.25} \mathrm{Sr} _ {0}$ .25Ba0.25Ca0.25)0. ${\bf 85} \mathrm {T i O _ {3}}$ and then mixed for 24 h in ethanol using a planetary ball milling. After drying $( 60~^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ 6h), the mixed raw powders were calcined at 1473 K for 4 h. The obtained LSBC calcined powders were added with 10 wt% $\mathbf {B i} _ {2} \mathbf {O} _ {3}$ (AR, 99.0 %) which was used as a sintering aid to reduce the sintering temperature and introduce a liquid phase promoting the epitaxial growth of textured structures due to its low melting point of 825 ${} ^{\circ} \mathbf {C} ,$ and then underwent a second milling process by a high-energy ball milling system to obtain the fine matrix powder with the average particle size of 0.231 μm. Fig. S1 (Supplementary Information) shown the XRD pattern, morphology, particle size distribution, and TEM results of the as-synthesized A-site deficient high-entropy matrix material $( \mathrm{La} _ {0.25} \mathrm{Sr} _ {0}$ .25Ba0.25Ca0.25)0.85- $\mathrm{TiO} _ {3}$ The SAED pattern collected along the [0 01] zone axis demonstrated that the LSBC matrix powder used for the tape-casting process was a single-phase solid solution exhibiting a cubic perovskite structure.

The plate-like $\mathrm {{S r T i O _ {3}}}$ microplatelets were synthesized using a twostep molten salt technique based on topochemical microcrystal conversion (TMC) [39]. The average radial size of the $\mathrm {{s r T i O _ {3}}}$ microplatelets was 8.65 μm, having a thickness of 1.17 μm, resulting in an aspect ratio of 7.39. Subsequently, 10 wt% flake $\mathrm {{s r T i O _ {3}}}$ microplatelets were used as template seeds which were added to the LSBC matrix material to prepare $\mathrm{Me} _ {0.85} \mathrm{TiO} _ {3}$ based high-entropy textured ceramics by the (R)TGG method in conjunction with a tape casting technology and the preparation process was depicted in Fig. S2 (Supplementary Information). Specifically, the matrix material powders (90 wt%) were mixed with polyvinyl butyral binders (6 wt%), adjacent dioctyl phthalate plasticizer (4.3 wt%), and castor oil lubricant (0.75 wt%) in a xylene-ethanol solvent (30 wt%) by planetary ball milling for 24 h, and then 10 wt% $\mathrm {{s r T i O _ {3}}}$ template seeds were added into the slurry and milled for 1 h. The homogeneous slurry was taped through the gated doctor blade and the green tape was produced and cast to produce 100 mm × 100 mm × 36 μm ceramic tape, after laminated into $\Phi 30 \times 23$ mm green bodies, and then cut into rectangular columns of 3 $\mathrm{mm} \times 4 \mathrm{mm} \times 15$ mm and disks of Φ12.7 mm $\times 3$ mm. The photos of the prepared LSBC-T high-entropy textured green bodies were shown in Fig. S3 (Supplementary Information). These ceramic green bodies underwent a heat treatment at $650~^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ for 20 h to burn out the organic substances (Fig. S4) and then a sintering process at 1573 K soaking for 4, 8, and 16 h, respectively under an argon atmosphere. Subsequently, they were subjected to an annealing process for 1.5 h at 1673 K in an atmosphere of argon-combining graphite. Henceforth, these ceramics were labeled as LSBC-T-4, LSBC-T-8, and LSBC-T-16 for the characterization of their microstructure and performance. Furthermore, the LSBC-T ceramics perpendicular to the casting direction were abbreviated as LSBC-T(⊥), and the LSBC-T ceramics parallel to the casting direction were abbreviated as LSBC-T(‖).

## 2.2. Characterization

Phase identification was performed utilizing an X-Ray diffractometer (Bruker D8 Discover, Cu Kα radiation). The microstructure, chemical component, and elemental distribution were determined by dual beam scanning electron microscopy (FEI Helios G4 CX, USA) and EDX. Atomically unveiling the structure of the LSBC-T ceramic was performed using a double Cs corrected scanning transmission electron microscope (FEI Themis Z, USA). Under a $^ \mathrm{~1.0~T~}$ magnetic field, Hall effect measurements (the ET9105-HS) using the van der Pauw method was conducted to ascertain the carrier concentration (n) and mobility $( \mu _ {\mathrm{H}} )$

Under helium atmosphere, both the electrical resistivity and Seebeck coefficient were concurrently measured using a LINSEIS LSR-3/800 thermoelectric measurement system from RT to 1073 K. The total thermal conductivity $\mathbf {\Pi} ( \kappa _ {\mathrm{tot}} )$ was calculated by the equation $\kappa _ {\mathrm{tot}} = \rho D \mathsf{C} _ {\mathrm{p}} ,$ where $\rho$ is the bulk density measured by Archimedes method, D is the thermal diffusivity determined by a laser-flash apparatus (NETZSCH LFA1000, Germany), and $C _ {\mathrm{p}}$ is the specific heat capacity can be determined by the differential scanning calorimeter (NETZSCH STA 449C, Germany) using sapphire as a reference sample for calibration.

The XRD patterns shown in Fig. 1 indicated that the strongest diffraction peak of the LSBC-T(⊥) ceramics with different soaking times was still (1 1 0). With the continuous extension of soaking time, the diffraction peak intensity does not change significantly. In contrast, the {2 00} diffraction peaks of the LSBC-T(‖) samples show a remarkable enhancement in the intensities. The Lotgering factor (f) was introduced and defined to quantitatively assess the degree of texturing using the given equations: [10,37]:

$$f _ {( h 00 )} = \frac {P _ {( h 00 )} - P _ {0}} {1 - P _ {0}}\tag{1}$$

$$P _ {( h 00 )} = \frac {\sum \mathrm{I} _ {( \mathrm{h00} )}} {\sum \mathrm{I} _ {\mathrm{(hkl)}}}\tag{2}$$

$$P _ {0} = \frac {\sum \mathrm{I} _ {0 ( \mathrm{h00} )}} {\sum \mathrm{I} _ {0 ( \mathrm{hkl} )}}\tag{3}$$

where $\Sigma I _ {( h O O )}$ represents the total peak intensities for all peaks aligned along the (h00) direction (i.e., 100, 200…) in the XRD pattern of the textured sample. $\Sigma I _ {( h k l )}$ represents the sum of the peak intensities for all (hkl) peaks in the XRD pattern. $\Sigma I _ {O ( h O O )}$ and $\Sigma I _ {O ( h k l )}$ represents the corresponding XRD peak intensities of the non-textured sample serving as references for comparison. According to the diffraction peak intensity of $2 \theta = 20 ^{\circ} \sim 80 ^{\circ}$ listed in Table S1 (Supplementary Information), f was calculated and obtained. As identified in Fig. 1, the intensities of the {2 00} diffraction peaks were significantly enhanced in all samples, the f ranges from 89.20 % to 92.18 % achieving a remarkable high texture degree for perovskite-structured ceramics. These results indicated that LSBC-T ceramics exhibit orientation grain growth and form textured microstructure. In addition, after the reduction annealing treatment, Bi/ BiO characteristic peaks were detected in all samples, which will facilitate the formation of conductive pathways, thereby contributing to enhancing electrical conductivity and acting as additional scattering centers to strengthen phonon scattering [40,41].

It can be speculated that these results were related to the mechanism of epitaxial growth of textured grains which were guided by a liquid phase of $\mathrm {\bf {B i _ {2} O _ {3} .}}$ When the sintering temperature exceeds the melting point of $\mathbf {B i} _ {2} \mathbf {O} _ {3}$ (1098 K), a liquid phase was generated promoting the diffusion and aggregation of high-entropy matrix particles on the surface of the template seeds.

Based on the above analysis of the microstructure characteristics, LSBC-T-8 textured high-entropy ceramic obtained an anisotropic microstructure with a texture degree of 92 $^ {\% ,}$ exhibiting the typical “brick-wall” morphology. The growth of the textured grain structure was analogous to the Ostwald ripening process, where large template seeds continuously consume small-size matrix high-entropy particles to complete the orientation growth. The driving force of the textured grain growth comes from the disparity in surface energy between the matrix particles and the template seed $\triangle \mathtt {G} = \mathtt {A} [ \gamma _ {\mathrm{MP}} . ( \gamma _ {1} + \gamma _ {2} ) ]$ , where γM and γT corresponds to the surface free energy of the matrix material and the template seed, γMP represents the interfacial free energy, and A is the interfacial area of the two phases [43]. The finer particle size of the matrix material means a higher solubility, which leads to a greater driving force. The matrix particle size $( r _ {\mathrm{m}} )$ used in the LSBC-T-8 textured ceramic was 0.231 μm, and the average radial dimension $( r _ {\mathrm{T}} )$ of the (001) oriented flake $\mathrm {{s r T i O _ {3}}}$ was 8.65 μm. Therefore, $r _ {\mathrm{T}} / r _ {\mathrm{m}} = 37$ indicates that the LSBC high-entropy matrix material has a significant driving effect in oriented growth along $\mathrm {{s r T i O _ {3}}}$ seeds favoring the texture degree evolution. In addition, the thickness of the $\mathrm {{s r T i O _ {3}}}$ template seed ratio to the average particle size of the matrix power is $\sim 5$ which is much larger than the critical value of 1.5 for the dynamic requirement of textured ceramics. These factors described above provided favorable growth conditions for the development of texture fraction. Table S2 and Fig. S10 (Supplementary Information) provide the results of the grain growth kinetics in this LSBC-T textured ceramics revealing that the highentropy matrix materials were controlled by the mechanism of curvature-driven grain boundary migration, while the textured grains were dominated by the diffusion mechanism of grain boundaries.

Fig. 7 shows a schematic diagram of the mechanism of oriented grain growth and texture degree development in LSBC-T high-entropy textured ceramics. During the tape casting procedure, the (0 0 1) oriented $\mathrm {{S r T i O _ {3}}}$ plate-like template seeds were oriented and arranged in the direction of tape casting, under the effect of shear stress, and the smallsize matrix particles diffused to the three crystallography surfaces of the $\mathrm {S r T i O _ {3}}$ and nucleated on each surface, as depicted in Fig. 7(a). During the initial sintering stage, when the sintering temperature was below the melting point of $\mathbf {B i} _ {2} \mathbf {O} _ {3}$ (1098 K), only solid phase reactions and coarsening of the matrix materials occur, resulting in slow particle migration. When the sintering temperature exceeded 1098 K, and the liquid phase region generated with Bi2O3 dissolution, the dissolution-precipitation mechanism dominated [10,34] because the solid–liquid mass transfer plays a prominent role. The liquid phase played a dual role in promoting the densification of bulk ceramics and fostering an environment conducive to grain growth that accelerated the grain boundary segregation and facilitating the formation of brick-wall textured grains.

As displays in Fig. 7(b), atoms in the high-entropy matrix particles diffuse onto the surface of the (0 0 1) oriented $\mathrm {{S r T i O _ {3}}}$ template seeds. To minimize the overall grain boundary area and internal system energy, small-size matrix particles (0.231 μm) were consumed by large-size oriented template grains. Meanwhile, the formation of low energy grain boundaries $\lbrace h _ {1} k _ {1} O \rbrace _ {\mathrm{M}} / \lbrace h _ {2} k _ {2} O \rbrace _ {\mathrm{T}}$ and $\lbrace 00 l _ {\mathrm{l}} \rbrace _ {\mathrm{M}} / \lbrace 00 l _ {2} \rbrace _ {\mathrm{T}}$ by oriented plate-like grains parallel to the (00l) plane have the potential to grow into larger sizes and eventually remain in the textured ceramics. By contrast, when the grains of the small matrix that were arranged improperly to form high energy grain boundaries $\lbrace 00 l \rbrace _ {\mathrm{M}} / \lbrace h k 0 \rbrace _ {\mathrm{1}}$ and $\lbrace h k 0 \rbrace _ {\mathrm{M}} / \lbrace 00 l \rbrace _ {\mathrm{T}} ,$ they were not only easily absorbed by the oriented grains, but also readily consumed by the large high-entropy matrix grains. Along the c-axis of (0 0 1) orientated $\mathrm {{s r T i O} _ {3} ,}$ a large energy barrier was formed between the matrix particles and the oriented textured grains, resulting in sluggish grain growth and the formation of a plate-like textured grains exhibiting a preferred orientation, as depicted in Fig. 7(c).

In a word, the evolution of the microstructure can be divided into the above-described three stages, which depend on both TGG and RTGG grain growth mechanisms. The contribution of the first two stages to the texture fraction of LSBC-T ceramics was about 60 % confirmed by the XRD results of the sintered sample shown in Fig. S11 (Supplementary Information). During the third stage, as the temperature rose and the soaking time extended, the phase reaction was completed, and the RTGG textured grains grew at a faster rate than the non-textured grains. The textured grains formed during the TGG process continuously consume SrTiO3 template seeds through grain boundary diffusion and transform into RTGG, while the textured grains generated by RTGG at an early stage continued to grow and come into prominence. Ultimately, the LSBC-T-8 high-entropy textured ceramic with a texture fraction greater than 92 % was obtained.

In the perovskite structure of LSBC-T-8 high-entropy textured ceramic, a large number of multi-scale disordered microstructures such as lattice distortion, $[ \mathrm{TiO} _ {6} ]$ tilting, and strain fields coexistence under the influence of configuration entropy by the introduction of multiple elements. Base on the Debye-Callaway’s model, κ is closely determined by phonon relaxation time (τ) [45–47] which determines by different scattering mechanisms.

$$\frac {1} {\tau} = \frac {1} {\tau _ {G B}} + \frac {1} {\tau _ {P D}} + \frac {1} {\tau _ {U}} + \frac {1} {\tau _ {e p}}\tag{7}$$

$$\tau _ {G B} = \frac {\nu _ {p}} {L}\tag{8}$$

$$\tau _ {U} \propto \frac {\overline {{M}} \nu ^ {3}} {V ^ {1 / 3} \omega ^ {2} \gamma ^ {2} T}\tag{9}$$

$$\tau _ {P D} ^ {- 1} = \frac {V \omega ^ {4}} {4 \pi \nu _ {p} ^ {2} \nu _ {g}} ( \sum _ {i} f _ {i} \Big ( 1 - \frac {r _ {i}} {\bar {r}} \Big ) ^ {2} + \sum _ {i} f _ {i} \Big ( 1 - \frac {m _ {i}} {\overline {{m}}} \Big ) ^ {2} )\tag{10}$$

Where, $\tau _ {G B}$ represents grain boundary scattering and depends on the grain size (L), which mainly scatter on low-frequency phonons (<3 THz); τPD is point defect scattering, mainly effecting on intermediate frequency phonons at 3–5 THz; τU is phonon–phonon Umklapp scattering, which mainly contributing on high-frequency phonons at 5–15 THz; $\tau _ {e p}$ is electron–phonon scattering, contributing to low-frequency phonons of $< 3$ THz; $\gamma$ is the Gruneisen constant, M is the average mass, ω is the phonon frequency, $\nu _ {\mathrm{p}}$ is the group velocity of phonon, V is the volume of per atom $\mathbf {\Omega} , f _ {\mathrm{i}}$ is the fraction of the $\bar {i} ^ {\mathrm {{t h}}}$ atom with a radius of ri and a mass of $m _ {\mathrm{i}} , {\bar {r}}$ is the average radius and m is the average mass of atoms residing on a site. The combined effect of the above factors synergistically affects the transport behavior of electrons and phonons, decouple the electron–phonon coupling relationship. These multi-scale defects were not only act as an additional phonon scattering centers while affecting carrier transport, covering all frequency phonons (100 GHz-15 THz), enhancing phonon scattering and endowing LSBC-T-8 ceramic with low and glass-like thermal conductivity. In addition, multi-scale parallel interfaces were further introduced to generate anisotropy in electrical and thermal conductivity through textured microstructure.

Fig. 9(e)-(f) demonstrates that the LSBC-T ceramics with different soaking times exhibited higher $Z T$ values compared to non-textured ceramics in both perpendicular and parallel to the casting direction. The highest ZT value of 0.27 was achieved in the CSBL-T-8(‖) sample, which doubled the performance of non-textured ceramic as a result of the combined effect of increased PF and relatively decreased κ.

Fig. 10 illustrates that the optimal ZT value obtained in this work exhibits superior performance when compared to data from reported high-entropy $\mathbf {A B O _ {3}}$ -based TEs [28,29,31,32,41,48–50], indicating that the microstructure regulation strategy through textured engineering in the high-entropy system can effectively enhance thermoelectric performance.

In conclusion, this work outlines three key points for the electrical and thermal synergistic transport mechanism of the perovskite structured LSBC-T high-entropy textured ceramics. Firstly, the multi-scale defect structure introduced by the combination of non-stoichiometric and high-entropy component design, which can enhance the transport of carriers and improve the electrical conductivity of ceramics while holding on low thermal conductivity. Secondly, introducing sintering aids of $\mathbf {B i} _ {2} \mathbf {O} _ {3}$ with low melting point, liquid-phase sintering at high temperatures not only promoted the generation of multiple types and scales of microstructure defects in high-entropy system, but also further enhanced the electrical conductivity by forming conductive channels at the grain boundaries due to the reduction of metal Bi and Bi/BiOx clusters, without decreasing the Seebeck coefficient. Thirdly, high texture fraction in high-entropy ceramics with preferential grain orientation growth was obtained, and multi-scale parallel interfaces were constructed, including atomic scale of (00l) crystal plane in SrTiO3 template seeds, nano-scale sandwich layer interface of metal Bi particles in $\mathrm {{s r T i O _ {3}}}$ seed, “core–shell” structure interface and “brick-wall” textured grain boundaries, all of these factors combined to realize the electrical and thermal conductivity have obvious anisotropy, decoupling their coupled relationship and improving thermoelectric properties.

## 4. Conclusion

The perovskite $\mathbf {M e} _ {0.85} \mathrm{TiO} _ {3} ( \mathbf {M e} = \mathrm{La} , \mathbf {S r} ,$ Ba, Ca) based textured ceramics with $f = 92$ % were successfully prepared by (R)TGG combined with tape casting method through matrix materials of A-site deficient $( \mathrm {L a _ {0.25} S r _ {0.25} B a _ {0.25} C a _ {0.25}} ) _ {0.85} \mathrm {T i O _ {3}}$ and template seeds of 10 wt% (001) oriented $\mathrm {{s r T i O _ {3}} .}$ The unique grain-oriented microstructure, nano-sized $\mathrm {B i / B i O _ {x}}$ cluster, as well as multi-scale interfaces and hierarchical defects were built and manipulated in the A-site deficient high-entropy system. Therefore, the thermal conductivity of the LSBC-T samples was greatly decreased by the hindered transmission of transverse phonons through the combination of enhanced phonon scattering at multiscale parallel interfaces and the localization of phonons within the disordered high-entropy system. By employing a strategy that involves the delocalization of electrons and the localization of phonons, simultaneous optimization of both electron and phonon transport properties in LSBC-T high-entropy thermoelectric ceramics can be achieved to enhance their ZT value. At 1073 K, the low $\kappa _ {\mathrm{total}} = 1.79 \mathrm{W} \mathrm{m} ^ {- 1} \mathrm{K} ^ {- 1}$ was achieved and ZT values enhanced from 0.13 of non-textured ceramic to 0.27 in the LSBC-T-8(‖) sample. This study clarified the mechanism governing the evolution of the textured microstructure in the cubic perovskite crystal structure, along with its influence on electron–phonon interaction. Furthermore, it presented a viable approach to achieve anisotropy and decouple the electron and phonon coupling relationship.
