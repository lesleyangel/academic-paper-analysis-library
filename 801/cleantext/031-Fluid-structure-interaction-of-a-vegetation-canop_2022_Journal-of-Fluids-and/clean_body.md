## 1. Introduction

Vegetation canopies, such as seagrass meadow, crop field and dense forest, are abundant in nature and play an important role in a wide range of ecological services. These canopies can provide habitats for wildlife (Carpenter and Lodge, 1986), capture suspended sediment (Palmer et al., 2004), adjust temperature (Karlsson, 2000) and improve water/air quality (Moore, 2004). Most of these services are implemented through the fluid–structure interactions (FSI) between vegetation and the fluid flows they thrive in. Understanding the mechanisms of such interactions is crucial in predicting the flow stimulus on vegetation growth (Moulia and Combes, 2006) and avoiding the flow-induced damage to crops (Berry et al., 2004). In recent years, the relevant vegetated flows have been studied experimentally and numerically by researchers, from single vegetation (O’Connor and Revell, 2019; Zhang et al., 2020) to dual vegetation (Zhang et al., 2020) and to vegetation canopies (O’Connor and Revell, 2019; Ghisalberti and Nepf, 2006), as well as from terrestrial canopies (Belcher et al., 2012; De, 2008) to aquatic canopies (Ghisalberti and Nepf, 2002; Nepf and Vivoni, 2000). Some reviews in this subject can be found in Belcher et al. (2012), De (2008), Nepf (2011) and Finnigan (2000).

In the vegetated flows, the flow velocity above and inside the canopy varies greatly due to the vertical discontinuity of drag, causing an inflection point within the velocity profile (Nepf, 2012). In addition, such drag discontinuity also results in strong velocity shear at the top of the canopy, making the flow in this region roll up and form the coherent canopyscale vortices which propagate downstream through the canopy (Ghisalberti and Nepf, 2002). In certain circumstances, these coherent vortices have strong intensities to generate sufficient hydrodynamic forces on the vegetation below them, causing several local depressions in the canopy which propagate with the vortices synchronously. The wavelike motion of vegetation canopy, called honami in terrestrial flow or monami in aquatic flow, provides the striking visualization of such propagations, as shown in Fig. 1.

In recent studies, Ghisalberti and Nepf (2002) and Raupach et al. (1996) indicated that some characteristics of terrestrial/aquatic flows through and over vegetation canopies resembled those of a mixing layer (a free shear layer characterized by two regions of velocity separated by a confined region containing an inflection point, see Fig. 1), and the inflection point in the velocity profile caused the shear layer at the top of canopy to be subjected to the Kelvin–Helmholtz (KH) vortex instability. In addition, Ghisalberti and Nepf (2002) compared the predicted frequencies of KH instability with the observed waving frequencies of aquatic vegetation canopies and found the strong consistency between them. They suggested that the KH instability dominated the monami effect of aquatic vegetation canopy. However, it should be noted that when the vegetation canopy becomes sparse, the prominence of the inflection point in the mean velocity profile is reduced and the above KH instability will not be applicable anymore. The influence of canopy density on the mixing layer flow has been predicted by Belcher et al. (2003) and Nepf et al. (2007), but the detailed mechanism behind it still needs to be investigated further.

Except for the flow instability, researchers also focus on the dynamic motion of vegetation canopies. Through the observation of waving wheat crops, Finnigan (1979) found that the main waving frequency of wheat was very close to its free-vibration (or natural) frequency. This finding conflicted with the mixing layer model in which the main waving frequency of canopy should be dominated by the flow instability, and therefore indicated a noticeable influence of canopy motion on the flow dynamics. Ghisalberti and Nepf (2006) then performed a flume experiment with both rigid and flexible model canopies to study the structure of vegetated shear flow, they found the waving motion of canopy could improve the vertical momentum transport within the canopy and noted that the deflection of the canopy was more than just a passive response to the coherent vortices. A similar experiment was also conducted by Py et al. (2005, 2006), who used an image-correlation technique to analyze the waving motion of wheat and alfalfa fields. These two plants had similar geometric shapes but different material properties (natural frequencies). The results indicated that the waving motion of these two plants always occurred near their own natural frequencies. In addition, Py et al. (2006, 2004) proposed a simplified model to simulate the FSI of a flexible canopy in terrestrial flows, in which the hydrodynamic influence on the canopy was implemented through the drag load. This linear model predicted a so-called lock-in mechanism with increasing flow velocity, where the frequency of KH instability increased and locked onto the natural frequency of the canopy.

In order to further investigate the above lock-in mechanism, Gosselin and Langre (2009) improved the fluid model of Py et al. (2006, 2004) by removing the irrotationality assumption and investigated three different KH frequencies: the coupled KH frequency in flow with a flexible canopy, the pure KH frequency in flow without any canopy and the heavy KH frequency in flow with a still/rigid canopy. They found that the natural frequency of the canopy is closer to the pure KH frequency in the lock-in range. Recently, O’Connor and Revell (2019) numerically investigated the waving interactions of a flexible canopy with different material properties in an open channel flow, their results displayed a wide range of flap patterns of canopy and indicated that the lock-in effect occurred when the mean waving frequency of the canopy approached the coupled KH frequency. However, in their study the natural frequency of the canopy was estimated in the fluid, which is smaller than the coupled KH frequency in the lock-in range. Zhang et al. (2020) then studied the behaviors of dual wall-mounted flexible flaps in a channel flow and found that the lock-in effect occurred when the waving frequencies of flaps approached their natural frequencies. However, the frequency of KH instability was not considered in their study since the number of flaps was too few to generate the mixing boundary layer. The lock-in effect found in this instance was a result of vortex-induced vibration.

Considering the differing conclusions obtained in the above studies, it is necessary to further investigate the lockin effect in canopy flows and determine the relationship between the natural frequencies, the pure/heavy/coupled KH frequencies and the waving frequency of canopy. In addition, it should be noted that the difference of conclusions among the above studies is partly due to the different modeling approaches they use. In the studies of Py et al. (2006, 2004)

and Gosselin and Langre (2009), the fluid and structure solvers are simplified, whereby the velocity profile of flow is treated to be piecewise linear, the whole vegetation canopy is treated as a continuum and each vegetation element is assumed to be rigid. While O’Connor and Revell (2019) and Zhang et al. (2020) used fully coupled fluid–structure models in which individual vegetation are flexible and their dynamics are fully resolved. Relatively, the fluid–structure models used in O’Connor and Revell (2019) and Zhang et al. (2020) are more applicable and can reflect the realistic behaviors of vegetation canopies.

The present paper adopts the two dimensional (2-D) fluid–structure model used in O’Connor and Revell (2019), in which the fluid field is solved by the lattice Boltzmann method (LBM), the dynamic motion of vegetation is calculated using the finite element method (FEM), and the interaction between fluid and vegetation is handled by the immersed boundary method (IBM). In addition, a strong-coupling implicit scheme is also introduced in this model in order to improve the stability and accuracy of algorithm. In recent years, the present fluid–structure model has been widely used in many FSI simulations, such as flexible particle suspension (Krüger et al., 2011), passive flow control (Fang et al., 2019), energy harvesting (Dong et al., 2016), elastic lamina oscillation (De Rosis et al., 2014a), and flapping flight study (De Rosis et al., 2014b). The accuracy and reliability of the present model has also been demonstrated by these studies, mainly from the following three aspects: Firstly, the LBM used in the present model has been proven to be $\mathbf {a}$ feasible substitute over a Navier–Stokes (N–S) solver due to its simple formulations, high efficiency and high level of parallelizability (Shan et al., 2006). Secondly, the fluid grid in the IBM does not need to conform to the structure boundary, so the grid generation in the present model becomes much faster than that of the traditional Arbitrary Lagrangian Eulerian (ALE) method (Peskin, 2002), and the flow governing equations are solved very efficiently on the fixed regular fluid grid. Thirdly, the FEM in the present model has great capability to tackle the nonlinear deformations of complex structures, and therefore has broad applicability (Felippa and Haugen, 2005). Thanks to the above two advantages, the present fluid–structure model is especially suitable for solving FSI problems with highly deformable structures.

In the present paper, the dynamic response of a shallow submerged vegetation canopy is studied with different bending rigidities and canopy densities at Reynolds number 100, the detailed vortical structure in the mixing layer as well as its influence on vegetation behaviors are investigated. The lock-in effect between the frequency of the mixing layer instability and the natural frequency of the vegetation is also explored. In the rest of the current paper, the mathematical model for fluid–structure interaction is described briefly, then the case setup and the relevant parameter space are presented. Finally, the results and main findings are analyzed and conclusions are drawn.

## 2. Mathematical model

## 2.1. Lattice Boltzmann method

The flow field in the present paper is solved by the lattice Boltzmann equation (Chen and Doolen, 1998), given as:

$$\frac {\partial f} {\partial t} + \pmb {e} \cdot \nabla _ {\pmb {x}} f + \pmb {F} \cdot \nabla _ {\pmb {e}} f = \Omega _ {f}\tag{1}$$

where t is time, x and e are the location and velocity of fluid particles at time $t . f$ is the distribution function of fluid particles and the force term F represents the external force applied to the fluid. $\varOmega _ {f}$ represents the collision operator of fluid particles and it can be simplified using the Bhatnagar, Gross, and Krook (BGK) approach (Bhatnagar et al., 1954), the expression of $\varOmega _ {f}$ is given by

$$\varOmega _ {f} = \frac {1} {\tau} \left( f ^ {( e q )} - f \right) \tag{2}$$

where $f ^ {( e q )}$ represents the equilibrium state of the distribution function $f ,$ τ is the relaxation time parameter which is associated with the fluid kinematic viscosity $\upsilon _ {\star}$ and their relationship is given by,

$$\upsilon = \left( \tau - \frac {1} {2} \right) c _ {s} ^ {2}\tag{3}$$

while $c _ {s}$ is the lattice speed of sound.

After time and spatial discretization, each fluid particle in Eq. (1) is allocated a finite number of discrete velocity values, referred to by subscript $\alpha ,$ then the corresponding equilibrium distribution function $f ^ {( e q )}$ can be written as:

$$f _ {\alpha} ^ {( e q )} = \rho w _ {\alpha} \left[ 1 + \frac {{\pmb e} _ {\alpha} \cdot {\pmb v}} {c _ {s} ^ {2}} + \frac {( {\pmb e} _ {\alpha} \cdot {\pmb v} ) ^ {2}} {2 c _ {s} ^ {4}} - \frac {{\pmb v} ^ {2}} {2 c _ {s} ^ {2}} \right]\tag{4}$$

where $\rho$ is the fluid density, $\pmb {e} _ {\alpha}$ and $w _ {\alpha}$ represent the discrete velocities on fluid particle and the corresponding weight coefficients, respectively. v is the macroscopic fluid velocity.

The discretization form of external forcing term F in Eq. (1) is proposed by Guo et al. (2002), in which the external force has influence on both fluid momentum and momentum flux, its expression can be written as:

$$F _ {\alpha} = \left( 1 - \frac {1} {2 \tau} \right) w _ {\alpha} \left[ \frac {{\pmb e} _ {\alpha} - {\pmb v}} {c _ {s} ^ {2}} + \frac {{\pmb e} _ {\alpha} \cdot {\pmb v}} {c _ {s} ^ {4}} {\pmb e} _ {\alpha} \right] \cdot {\pmb f}\tag{5}$$

where f represents the force density act on the fluid. Then the macroscopic fluid quantities can be obtained by:

$$\begin{array} {l} {\displaystyle \rho = \sum _ {\alpha} f _ {\alpha}} \\ {\displaystyle \rho \pmb {v} = \sum _ {\alpha} \pmb {e} _ {\alpha} f _ {\alpha} + \frac {1} {2} \pmb {f} \delta t = \rho \pmb {v} ^ {\ast} + \frac {1} {2} \pmb {f} \delta t} \end{array}\tag{6}$$

(7)

here $v ^ {*}$ is called the predicted velocity.

## 2.2. Finite element method

The dynamics of individual vegetation is calculated by the nonlinear finite element method, in which each vegetation element is discretized by a series of two-node Euler–Bernoulli beam elements. The dynamic equation of vegetation can be written as:

$${\pmb M} {\ddot {\pmb U}} \left( t \right) + {\pmb F} _ {i n t} \left( {\pmb U} \right) - {\pmb F} _ {e x t} \left( t \right) = 0\tag{8}$$

where M is the mass matrix of vegetation, U and $\ddot {\pmb {U}}$ are vegetation displacement and acceleration, respectively. $F _ {i n t}$ represents the internal force of vegetation while $\pmb {F} _ {e x t}$ represents the external forces act on vegetation. Based on Newton’s third law, the expression of $\pmb {F} _ {e x t}$ can be written as:

$$\boldsymbol {F} _ {e x t} = - \sum _ {m} \int _ {V _ {m}} \boldsymbol {N} ^ {T} \mathbf {f} ^ {\prime} d V\tag{9}$$

where $V _ {m}$ is the volume of the mth beam element. N represents the shape function matrix while $\pmb {f} ^ {\prime}$ represents the force density acting on the element, its expression can be found in Section 2.3.

Eq. (8) can be discretized in time and solved using the Newmark- $- \beta$ method with the Newton–Raphson method, thus its discretized form can be written as:

$$\left[ {\pmb {K}} _ {T , t + \Delta t} ^ {i} + c _ {0} {\pmb {M}} \right] \Delta {\pmb {U}} ^ {i + 1} = {\pmb {F}} _ {e x t , t + \Delta t} - {\pmb {F}} _ {i n t , t + \Delta t} ^ {i} - {\pmb {M}} \left[ c _ {0} \left( {\pmb {U}} _ {t + \Delta t} ^ {i} - {\pmb {U}} _ {t} \right) - c _ {1} {\dot {\pmb {U}}} _ {t} - c _ {2} {\ddot {\pmb {U}}} _ {t} \right] .\tag{10}$$

where $\varDelta t$ is the time step in the Newmark- $- \beta$ method while i represents the iteration step in the Newton–Raphson method, ${\pmb K} _ {T}$ is the tangent stiffness matrix of vegetation and $c _ {0} \sim c _ {2}$ are variables determined by the accuracy and stability requirements of Newmark- $- \beta$ method. More details about Eq. (10) can be found in Doyle and Desantiago (2001).

## 2.3. Immersed boundary method

The exchange of interface data between the flow and vegetation is handled by the immersed boundary method, in which the fluid field is discretized by a set of fixed Eulerian points while the vegetation is discretized by several Lagrangian markers. The data exchange between these two grids is implemented by two operators: the interpolation operator Π and the spreading operator $\psi$ , their expressions are given by:

$$\begin{array} {l} {{\displaystyle \phi ( {\pmb X} ) = \pi [ \phi ( {\pmb x} ) ] = \sum _ {\varOmega} \phi ( {\pmb x} ) \tilde {\delta} ( {\pmb x} - {\pmb X} ) \Delta x \varDelta y \varDelta z}} \\ {{\displaystyle \phi ( {\pmb x} ) = \varPsi [ \phi ( {\pmb X} ) ] = \sum _ {\varGamma} \phi ( {\pmb X} ) \tilde {\delta} ( {\pmb x} - {\pmb X} ) \epsilon \varDelta q \varDelta r \varDelta s}} \end{array}\tag{11}$$

(12)

where $\phi$ and $\varPhi$ are quantities defined in Eulerian and Lagrangian frames, respectively. $\pmb {x} = ( x , y , z )$ is the position of Eulerian point while $\pmb {X} = ( q , r , s )$ is the position of Lagrangian marker. Ω represents the computational domain while $\varGamma$ represents the boundary of structure. ϵ is a scaling factor for the reciprocity between Π and Ψ . δ˜ is the three-point discrete Dirac delta function defined in Peskin (2002).

As described by Li et al. (2016), the fluid velocity v at Cartesian mesh point x obtained in LBM can be interpolated onto the location of Lagrangian marker with coordinate X as:

$${\pmb v} \left( {\pmb X} , t \right) = \varPi \left( {\pmb v} \left( {\pmb x} , t \right) \right)\tag{13}$$

According to the non-slip boundary condition, this interpolated velocity must equal the boundary velocity V (X, t) calculated in FEM, given as,

$$\pmb {V} \left( \pmb {X} , t \right) = \pmb {v} \left( \pmb {X} , t \right)\tag{14}$$

Substituting Eqs. (13) and (14) into Eq. (7) and converting Eq. (7) into the Lagrangian frame as:

$$\pi [ \rho ( {\pmb x} , t ) ] {\pmb V} ( {\pmb X} , t ) = \pi \left[ \rho {\pmb v} ^ {\ast} ( {\pmb x} , t ) \right] + \frac {\Delta t} {2} {\pmb f} ^ {\prime} ( {\pmb X} , t )\tag{15}$$

Solving Eq. (15) to obtain the corrective force density $\pmb {f} ^ {\prime}$ and then transforming it into the fluid mesh by spreading operator Ψ as:

$${\pmb f} \left( {\pmb x} , t \right) = \psi \left[ {\pmb f} ^ {\prime} \left( {\pmb X} , t \right) \right]\tag{16}$$

Finally, the obtained force density f (x, t) is taken back into the Eq. (7) to update the flow field.

## 2.4. fluid–structure coupling

The coupling of the fluid and structure in the present model is handled by a partitioned method, whereby the flow and structure are solved sequentially and the necessary interfacial data at the fluid–structure interface are exchanged between these two solvers. However, for problems with a low structure-to-fluid density ratio, this coupling strategy can lead to a boundary condition mismatch at the fluid–structure interface, which can adversely impact the stability and accuracy of the solution. The theoretical analysis of this issue can be found in Zheng et al. (2010). To overcome this, a strong-coupling block Gauss–Seidel implicit scheme is introduced in the present work. In this coupling scheme, the fluid and structure solvers are iterated over multiple times within a single time step until the interface compatibility conditions are met. At each internal iteration, the displacement of structure is relaxed based on the previous iteration, given by

$$\tilde {\pmb {U}} = \omega \pmb {U} + ( 1 - \omega ) \tilde {\pmb {U}} ^ {k - 1}\tag{17}$$

where $\omega$ is the relaxation factor, U is the displacement of structure calculated in FEM, $\tilde {\pmb {U}}$ and $\tilde {\pmb {U}} ^ {k - 1}$ represent the relaxed displacement of structure in current and previous iteration, respectively.

The Aitken’s $\varDelta ^ {2}$ relaxation method (Kuttler and Wall, 2008) is adopted here to speed up the convergence rate of the above coupling scheme, in which the relaxation factor ω is calculated dynamically through:

$${\boldsymbol {\omega}} ^ {k} = - {\boldsymbol {\omega}} ^ {k - 1} {\frac {\left( {\pmb {r}} ^ {k - 1} \right) ^ {T} \left( {\pmb {r}} ^ {k} - {\pmb {r}} ^ {k - 1} \right)} {\left| {\pmb {r}} ^ {k} - {\pmb {r}} ^ {k - 1} \right| ^ {2}}}\tag{18}$$

where $r ^ {k}$ is the residual vector, given by

$$\pmb {r} ^ {k} = \pmb {U} ^ {k} - \tilde {\pmb {U}} ^ {k - 1}\tag{19}$$

Typically, a lower structure-to-fluid density ratio requires more sub-iterations to ensure the convergence of the coupling. Although this implicit sub-iteration scheme increases the computational cost, the present FSI simulation framework has much greater numerical stability and algorithmic robustness than other conventional explicit methods.

The present fluid–structure model has been thoroughly validated and used in our previous studies (O’Connor and Revell, 2019; Favier et al., 2017; Harwood et al., 2018), the readers are referred to these studies for more details.

## 3. Numerical settings and parameter space

As shown in Fig. 2, the vegetation canopy has height h and it is embedded into a rectangular open-channel with height $H = 3 h$ . Each vegetation element in the canopy stands upright and has equal spacing ∆S. The whole canopy is placed in the so-called canopy section of the channel with a fixed length of $L _ {c} = 64 h$ In order to limit the interference of the channel boundaries with the canopy behavior, the canopy section has a 10h distance from the channel inlet and a 30h distance from the channel outlet, thus the total length of the channel is $L _ {t} = 104 h$

The velocity on the channel inlet has a parabolic profile, given by

$$u ( 0 , y ) = 1.5 \overline {{u}} \frac {y ( 2 H - y )} {H ^ {2}}\tag{20}$$

where u is the mean flow velocity while 1.5u is the maximum velocity. Compared with the piecewise linear velocity profile used in Py et al. (2006) and Gosselin and Langre (2009) which has an inflection point itself, the present velocity profile used here has no inflection point and is just used to mimic the open-channel flow. This allows us to capture the detailed development process of the mixing layer instability. In addition, the inlet velocity is set to increase nonlinearly from zero over a short of time and then keep constant, this is used for suppressing the density waves generated by the weak compressibility of LBM. The channel outlet is set to a fixed pressure boundary condition, while the non-slip and free-slip conditions are imposed on the bottom and top boundaries of the channel, respectively. Based on the mean inlet velocity and vegetation height, the Reynolds number of the present set of flow conditions is 100.

The material properties of the vegetation are described by the dimensionless mass ratio M and bending rigidity K, which are given by:

$$M = \frac {\rho _ {s} b} {\rho _ {f} h} , K = \frac {E I} {\rho _ {f} \overline {{u}} ^ {2} h ^ {3}}\tag{21}$$

where $\rho _ {f}$ and $\rho _ {s}$ are the fluid and vegetation densities, b represents the thickness of vegetation, E and I are the Young’s modulus and the second area moment of vegetation. As described in O’Connor and Revell (2019), typical values of the dimensionless mass ratio M for vegetation range from O(0.01) for aquatic vegetation to O(10) for terrestrial vegetation. Even so, the main characteristics of the waving instability are known to be similar for both aquatic and terrestrial vegetation (Gosselin and Langre, 2009). Since the mass ratio of vegetation is not the focus of our research, in the present paper a typical value of $M = 1.0$ is selected. Physically, $M = 1.0$ corresponds to a terrestrial vegetation canopy, in which the air density is $\rho _ {f} = 1.29 \mathrm{kg} / \mathrm{m} ^ {3}$ , the vegetation density is $\rho _ {s} = 64.5 ~ \mathrm{kg} / \mathrm{m} ^ {3}$ (very close to the density of wheat straw of $\rho _ {s} = 65.0 ~ \mathrm{kg} / \mathrm{m} ^ {3}$ reported in Ebeling and Jenkins, 1985), the height of vegetation is $h = 0.3$ m and the thickness of vegetation is $b = 0.006 \textrm {m}$

The dimensionless bending rigidity of vegetation K is measured to be O(0.01) for aquatic vegetation and $O ( 0.1 )$ for terrestrial vegetation. In order to cover most of the parameter space that governs the behavior of both aquatic and terrestrial vegetation, twelve different values of $K \ ( K 0 \sim K 11 )$ are tested in the following tests, which are distributed in powers of ten as

$$K i = 5 \times 10 ^ {- 3 + ( 2 ( i - 1 ) / 9 )} , i = 0 \sim 11\tag{22}$$

Aside from the material properties of individual vegetation, the spatial density of the vegetation canopy is also an important parameter to be investigated. Generally, canopy density is described by the solid volume fraction occupied by the vegetation, for the 2-D case its expression is given by,

$$\varPhi = b / \varDelta S\tag{23}$$

where ∆S is the spacing between vegetation elements. In the present paper, the values of spatial density are chosen from 0 to 0.1, given by,

$$\phi j = j \times 10 ^ {- 2} , \quad j = 0 \sim 10\tag{24}$$

Preliminary tests indicated that the above range of Φ was sufficient to yield the typical solid volume fractions for various species of real aquatic vegetation (Chandler et al., 1996). In our model, the number of vegetation elements present in the canopy is then obtained by $n = L _ {c} / \Delta S + 1 = L _ {c} \phi / b + 1$ . Here $\varPhi = 0 \left( \mathrm{or} j = 0 \right)$ represents the case of a single vegetation element which is used to provide a benchmark for comparison with results for a larger canopy. The set of values for j in Eq. (24) is listed in Table 1.

In the above cases, each vegetation element is discretized with 50 IBM markers/lattice sites $( \varDelta x = 0.02 h )$ and 20 FEM elements. To ensure that the results of FSI simulation are independent of the mesh resolution, a mesh independence test is carried out in Appendix. In this test, the deflection of vegetation is described by the deflection angle, which is measured from the vegetation root to its tip with respect to the vertical direction, given as $\theta _ {t i p} = \arctan \left( \delta _ {x} / \left( h + \delta _ {y} \right) \right)$ , where $\delta _ {x}$ and $\delta _ {y}$ are the tip deflections in the streamwise and vertical directions, respectively.

## 4. Results and discussion

## 4.1. Dynamic behaviors of vegetation canopy

Initially, the dynamic behavior of a vegetation canopy with different bending rigidities $( K 0 \sim K 11 )$ and canopy densities (Φ0 ∼ Φ10) is investigated. Through observation of the dynamic response, the behavior of the vegetation canopy can be classified into five modes: static, flapping, regular waving, irregular waving and dual. The first four behavior modes as well as their corresponding flow characteristics have been reported in a previous study (O’Connor and Revell, 2019), while the dual mode is a natural combination of both flapping and waving states. Before conducting further investigation, it is instructive to present the main characteristics of the different behavior modes. Fig. 3 provides snapshots of the flow field and vegetation deflections of six representative cases, which cover all behavior modes of the vegetation canopy mentioned above. As shown in Figs. 3a and 3f, in the case of the static mode, no instability is observed in the flow field and the vegetation is shown to be static. While for the flapping mode and the waving mode, the flow instability emerges and develops along the canopy. For these states, the behavior of the vegetation is dominated by mode-one deformation (see Figs. 3d and 3e) and mode-two deformation (see Fig. 3b) respectively. In addition, the flow-induced oscillation of the vegetation in the waving mode can be further categorized into the regular waving mode and the irregular waving mode. The distinction between these two modes has been reported in O’Connor and Revell (2019), in which the vegetation in the regular waving behavior settles into a regular periodic motion with constant amplitude and frequency, while the vegetation in irregular waving behavior has an irregular motion with an amplitude that varies in time and space. The dual mode, which exposes an instance where the canopy is exhibiting both flapping and waving (see in Fig. 3c), can be seen as a transitional state between the flapping mode and regular waving mode. More details about this mode will be discussed in the next section.

Fig. 4 displays a parameter map for the observed behavior modes for the vegetation canopy for the full range of cases examined in this study. The figure reveals several clearly banded regions, whereby the static behavior is observed at the four corners as well as the four coordinate limits of parameter map; corresponding to large and/or low bending rigidity and/or canopy density. While not apparent from the figure, the tests exhibiting a static behavior present with a range of tip deflection angles; e.g. small deflection in Fig. 3f and large deflection in Fig. 3a. The flapping behavior of vegetation canopy is limited to a small region at low bending rigidity (K 1), while the regular waving behavior is identified in a far larger, central region of the parameter space for bending rigidities $( K 2 \sim K 9 )$ and canopy densities $( \phi 1 \sim \phi 9 )$ . The irregular waving behavior and the dual behavior are restricted to narrower regions of the parameter map, with the former at the top for relatively large values of rigidity $( K 9 \sim K 10 )$ , and the latter at the bottom for low (K 2 ∼ K 3) values of bending rigidity.

The results in Fig. 4 clearly demonstrate that the behavior mode of the vegetation canopy is highly dependent on both bending rigidity and the canopy density. While the influence of bending rigidity was investigated qualitatively in a previous study by the co-authors (O’Connor and Revell, 2019), the structure of the mixing layer and its influence on the dynamics of the vegetation was not investigated at length. A more detailed investigation into the latter is the focus on the next section.

## 4.2. Vortical structures in different vegetation behaviors

As mentioned in the Introduction section, the dynamic behavior of the vegetation canopy is a collective result of the mixing layer and vegetation, whereby the mixing layer acts as an excitation source to the vegetation while the vegetation works to resist such excitation. In this section, the detailed unsteady flow structures present in different behavior states are investigated to determine the interaction mechanism between the mixing layer and the vegetation.

Fig. 5 shows snapshots of the flow vertical velocity $u _ {y}$ and streamlines of the same six cases in Fig. 3, which all belong to the Φ4 branch (see the blue band in Fig. 4). The x-axis scale of the subfigures has been adjusted to facilitate comparison of different scales of flow structure. Figs. 6a and 6b show the mean drag coefficient $\overline {{C}} _ {D} = \left( \operatorname* {m a x} \left( C _ {D} \right) + \operatorname* {m i n} \left( C _ {D} \right) \right) / 2$ and the amplitude of the drag coefficient $C _ {D} ^ {\prime} = \operatorname* {m a x} {( C _ {D} )} - \operatorname* {m i n} {( C _ {D} )}$ of the vegetation along the canopy, respectively. Here the drag coefficient is defined as: $C _ {D} = F _ {D} / \left( 1 / 2 \rho _ {f} \overline {{u}} ^ {2} h \right)$ , where $F _ {D}$ represents the force acting on the vegetation in the x-coordinate direction. Since the amplitude of the drag coefficient in the irregular waving case (Φ4K 10) varies in time and space, the relevant result is not included in Fig. 6b. The amplitude of the drag coefficient $C _ {D} ^ {\prime}$ is non zero only where the mixing layer instability emerges, so it can be used to measure both the onset and scale of the coherent vortices. While the mean drag coefficient $\overline {{C}} _ {D}$ reflects the intensity of coherent vortices, so it can be used to measure the intensity of the mixing layer.

Static state with large deflection. It can be seen from Fig. 5a that when the bending rigidity of the vegetation is small (K0), the resistance of the vegetation canopy against the mixing layer is weak and the vegetation canopy exhibits a static drag-reducing reconfiguration with large deflection. The flow structures generated at the vegetation tips are small and all ensuing vortical motion remains restricted to the interspacing of vegetation, the vertical velocity of the interstitial flow within the canopy is almost zero (see Fig. 5a). The deflected vegetation creates a virtual barrier at the top of canopy, which prevents the vertical exchange between the interstitial flow within the canopy and the bulk flow over the top of the canopy. In addition, after the first several vegetation, the intensity of the mixing layer (the mean drag coefficient of the vegetation) decreases slightly along the canopy due to the dissipation of the canopy (see Fig. 6a), while the amplitude of the drag coefficient remains zero (see Fig. 6b), which means the mixing layer instability does not appear in this case.

Flapping state. As the bending rigidity increases (K 1), the resistance of the vegetation canopy is increased while the deflection of the vegetation is decreased. The virtual solid barrier at the top of the canopy is broken and the flow within and over the canopy start to exchange. Furthermore, the vertical velocity of flow within the canopy is no longer zero (see Fig. 5b). The mixing layer instability emerges in this case and the coherent vortices present larger scales than those in the static behavior, which expand across several vegetation spacings. It should be noted that the resistance of vegetation canopy remains relative low to the intensity of the mixing layer. As such, the coherent vortices have sufficient energy to penetrate into the whole canopy entirely. Furthermore, the lower extent of these vortices expand up to the channel bed and a non-zero vertical velocity within the canopy is present up to the wall. The coherent vortices highly depress the vegetation immediately below them as the travel downstream, and the rotation centers of these vortices are lower than the tip of vegetation around them. As such, the forces generated by these vortices on the upper half and lower half of the vegetation act in opposing directions (see the yellow arrows in Fig. 5b), causing the vegetation to bear strong shear stress and display mode-two (flapping) deformations.

As observed in Fig. 6b, the mixing layer instability (amplitude of drag coefficient) in the flapping case develops quickly along the canopy and then remains almost constant. However, the intensity of the mixing layer continues to decrease due to the dissipation of the canopy. This is demonstrated more clearly in Fig. 7 which plots the mean deflection angle $\overline {{\theta}} _ {t i p} = \left( \operatorname* {m a x} \left( \theta _ {t i p} \right) + \operatorname* {m i n} \left( \theta _ {t i p} \right) \right) / 2$ as well as the amplitude of angular oscillation $\theta _ {t i p} ^ {\prime} = \operatorname* {m a x} \left( \theta _ {t i p} \right) - \operatorname* {m i n} \left( \theta _ {t i p} \right)$ of along canopy for the K1 case. The mean deflection angle is shown to decrease over the first 9 elements, while the angular amplitude increases, this is because the intensity of mixing layer is reduced while its instability is developing. The mixing layer instability is initiated after the 9th vegetation element (point A in Fig. 7). At this point the mixing layer intensity and thus the mean deflection angle of the vegetation rises slightly before a gradual decrease over the full length of the canopy, while the mixing layer instability (the angular amplitude of the vegetation) continues to increase up to the 12th element (point B in Fig. 7) and then varies slightly along the canopy. Since the scales of coherent vortices are small and their rotation centers are lower than the surrounding vegetation, the flow continually sweeps the canopy forwards and so the deflection angles always remain positive.

Dual state. When the bending rigidity of the vegetation is increased (K2), the dual behavior emerges, as shown in Fig. 3c. Compared to the case where flapping behavior is present across the whole canopy, the flapping motion of vegetation in the dual behavior exhibits for only a small region of the canopy (around a quarter of the canopy in Fig. 3c), after which a moderate waving motion is observed. This is because the resistance of the vegetation against the mixing layer increases with increasing bending rigidity. As a result, the coherent vortices cannot penetrate into the whole canopy anymore and their rotation centers move upward (see the streamline and flow vertical velocity in Fig. 5c). When their rotation centers are higher than the tip of surrounding vegetation, the flapping motion of the vegetation terminates. It can be indicated from Fig. 6 that the intensity of the mixing layer (the mean drag coefficient) decreases in the flapping behavior due to the dissipation of the canopy, while the mixing layer instability (the amplitude of drag coefficient) increases rapidly and then decreases. There is a V-notch on the peak of the amplitude of drag coefficient which can be attributed to the influence of the subsequent waving behavior.

In the subsequent waving behavior, the mixing layer instability develops slightly along the canopy and the amplitude of the drag coefficient is increased. However, the vegetation still has dissipation on the intensity of the mixing layer, causing the mean drag coefficient of the vegetation to decrease further. Over the last third of the canopy, the coherent vortices are generated with large scales (see Fig. 5c). The detailed development process of the mixing layer will be discussed in the next section. In this case, the rotation centers of vortices remain above the vegetation and impose hydrodynamic forces on the canopy which all act in the same direction. As a result, the shear stress of the vegetation is negligible and the deformation of the vegetation returns to mode-one. In addition, since the vegetation at two sides of the vortices bear the opposite hydrodynamic forces (see the yellow arrows in Fig. 5c), the passage of these vortices deflect the vegetation forward (downstream) and downward (upstream) (see Figs. 3c and 5c).

Regular waving state. Further increasing the bending rigidity (K4 ∼ K10) eventually eliminates the flapping behavior entirely, and the vegetation canopy returns to the regular/irregular waving behavior instead. The development of the mixing layer instability in the regular/irregular waving cases is similar to that in the dual state. From Fig. 6 it is observed that the mixing layer instability first increases gradually over the first section of the canopy, up to around the 40th element, while the mixing layer intensity (the mean drag coefficient) decreases over this section due to the canopy dissipation. With further increase of rigidity, the mixing layer instability develops more rapidly, leading to the production of coherent vortices with higher intensity which in turn further increasing the mean drag coefficient. For moderate values of bending rigidity (up to K8), there is a dynamic balance between the development of mixing layer instability and the dissipation of vegetation response. After an initial development length, the coherent vortices eventually establish fixed scales (see Fig. 5d), that is, the regular waving behavior. Both the mean and amplitude values of drag coefficient remain almost constant after the rapid increase (see Fig. 6). Over the last few elements of the canopy, the mean drag coefficient increases rapidly while the amplitudes of drag coefficient remain unchanged. This is because the lack of the downstream vegetation increases the pressure drag of these vegetation, but there is no notable impact on the mixing layer instability and the generation of the coherent vortices.

Irregular waving state. As the bending rigidity increases further (K 9 ∼ K 10), the dynamic balance presented in the regular waving state is disrupted, since the canopy dissipation becomes dominant over the mixing layer. As a result, the penetration scales of the coherent vortices dissipate gradually along the canopy (see Fig. 5e). The intensity of the mixing layer also decreases gradually, as shown by the mean drag coefficient of vegetation (see Fig. 6a). This imbalance causes temporal and spatial variations in the waving amplitude of vegetation which leads to the observed irregular waving behavior. The size of the coherent vortices is shown to decrease with increasing bending rigidity, as shown in Figs. 5c∼5e.

Static state with small deflection. When bending rigidity is increased further (K 11), the resistance of the vegetation to the mixing layer acts further to entirely eliminate the observed structural response. The mixing layer instability is thus suppressed and the vegetation returns to a static state but this time with significantly smaller deflection. As shown in Fig. 5f, the flow vortex structure between vegetation elements is reminiscent of flow over a lid-driven cavity, in which a recirculation vortex is induced at the top of canopy by the mixing layer while a smaller vortex is induced at the bottom of canopy.

Mixed crop. In the context of crop protection it is preferable to avoid the mode-two bending associated with the flapping state, given the dynamic loading this would impact on the vegetation. An interesting observation from the preceding section is that there may be instances where a dual flapping-waving state can be induced in preference to a state where flapping persists for the entire canopy. This may be achievable via the use of a cheaper ‘buffer’ vegetation, interspersed within a region higher value crop in order to offer resistance to the onset of the flapping mode. This buffer vegetation should have larger bending rigidity than the crop itself, so as to enhance the dissipation of the vortices. In order to investigate this, a preliminary test is carried out by replacing the first third of vegetation in Φ4K 1 with K 2 vegetation (called the mixed case). Fig. 8 shows a snapshot of the flow field and vegetation deflection for the mixed case, the legend in this figure is the same with that in Fig. 3. It can be indicated by comparing this figure with Fig. 3b that the K 2 vegetation (red in Fig. 8) eliminates the occurrence of the flapping mode in the K 1 vegetation downstream, which instead exhibits a moderate waving motion. From Fig. 6a it is observed that the K2 vegetation in the mixed case has almost the same mean drag coefficient with that of the Φ4K 2 case. When the vegetation switches from K 2 to K 1, there is a drop in both mean and amplitude values of drag coefficient (see black arrows in Figs. 6a and 6b), leading to a lower drag coefficient in the K1 region than that observed in the Φ4K1 case. The interval at which the more rigid buffer crop should be placed would likely be dependent on other parameters in this model, and is the subject of ongoing work, but the concept is demonstrated here for interest.

## 4.3. The influence of canopy density on mixing layer instability

As established in the preceding section, the mixing layer instability arises at the inflection point in the cross flow velocity profile and leads to the formation of coherent vortices on the top of the vegetation canopy. Previous studies (Belcher et al., 2003; Nepf et al., 2007) have demonstrated that the canopy density can modify the velocity profile and alter the location of the inflection point, which will therefore directly impact the intensity of the mixing layer instability. There have been relatively few studies employing a two-way fluid–structure interaction model to investigate the link between canopy density and the mixing layer instability. In this section, the unsteady flow over various different canopy densities are compared, with a focus on the generation mechanism behind the formation of the observed coherent vortices.

Figs. 9 and 10 present snapshots of the flow field, streamline and vegetation deflections for five representative cases with canopy densities (Φ0.5 ∼ Φ9) in the K 7 branch (see the red band in Fig. 4). For comparison, the single vegetation case (Φ0) is also included. Fig. 10 focuses on the foremost part of canopy, i.e. prior to the establishment of the mixing layer instability. Note that the streamwise extent of sub-figures in Fig. 10 has been adjusted to facilitate comparison of the different scales observed.

From Figs. 9a and 10a, for the case of a single vegetation element, a static drag-reducing reconfiguration with large deflection is observed. A large scale recirculation vortex is induced by the shear flow at the tip of vegetation, while a small secondary vortex is induced at the root corner of the vegetation element. In contrast to the instability generated in the mixing layer, both the recirculation vortex and the secondary vortex are the result of flow separation alone, the mixing layer instability is not observed in this case.

When the canopy density is increased (Φ0.5), the reduced space leads to a smaller flow recirculation and a lower deflection of each vegetation element due to the blockage of the adjacent elements. The flow remains static for this case. This is demonstrated clearly in Fig. 10b where the streamwise scale of the recirculation vortex is restricted to the spacing by the adjacent vegetation. The lower boundary of the recirculation flow appears to remain attached to the channel bed while its upper boundary is moving upward, causing the vertical scale of the recirculation vortex to expand gradually with the increasing canopy density. The vertical component of flow velocity at the top of the canopy is decreased compared to the Φ0 case, indicating that the blockage acts to reduce the vertical exchange of momentum, thus reducing the mixing of cross flow momentum over the canopy. The intensity of the mixing layer is then increased while the intensity of the recirculation vortex is decreased. As a result, the small secondary induced vortex is observed only in the first vegetation spacing.

Upon further increasing the canopy density $( \phi 1 \sim \phi 7 )$ , the deflection of the vegetation continues to decrease and the upper boundary of the recirculation vortex remains fixed at the top of the canopy. The intensity of the recirculation vortex continues to weaken, its lower boundary is unable to extend fully to the channel bed and the vortex center moves upward gradually (see Figs. 10d and 10e). The increased canopy density leads to a reduction of the vertical exchange between the canopy and the cross-flow, and further leads to an increased cross-flow gradient at the top of the canopy. Once established, the mixing layer instability gives rise the occurrence of a series of coherent vortices, and the wavelike motion of canopy emerges. The onset of this instability moves forward along canopy with increasing canopy density (see Figs. 9c, 9d and 9e). In addition, as the canopy density increases, the resistance of the canopy against the mixing layer increases, the coherent vortices move upward gradually and eventually establish fixed scales.

When the canopy density is increased further (Φ9), the vertical exchange between the canopy and cross flow is reduced to a negligible level (see Fig. 10f) and the coherent wavelike motion disappears. In this case, the top of the vegetation canopy presents as a solid boundary, and the canopy can be seen as a continuum. The dynamic behavior of vegetation returns to a static configuration with a small deflection (see Fig. 9f).

The variation of mixing layer intensity with canopy density is more directly assessed in Figs. 11a and 11b, which plot the variation of streamwise (x-) velocity $u _ {x}$ and its gradient $\partial u _ {x} / \partial y$ in the wall-normal (y-) direction at the location $x = 26.0$ . The flow at this point is essentially static for all values of $\Phi ,$ , since it lies prior to the onset of the mixing layer instability in all cases, see Fig. 9. Since the shear stress in Newtonian flow is proportional to the flow velocity gradient (Mark and S, 2015), the $\partial u _ {x} / \partial y$ is a suitable measure of the intensity of the mixing layer. As illustrated in Fig. 11, the inflection point of the mean velocity profile moves upward with increasing canopy density and rises to the height of canopy $( y = h )$ for the very dense canopy (Φ9). Both the streamwise velocity and velocity gradient drop towards zero in the region below the inflection point, which corresponds to the ‘‘wake zone’’ reported in Ghisalberti and Nepf (2006), where shear stress and flow renewal are negligible. Above the inflection point, the velocity $u _ {x}$ increases gradually up to a maximum value at the free surface of channel $( y = 3 h )$ , while the velocity gradient $\partial u _ {x} / \partial y$ increases rapidly at the inflection point, reaching a peak just above the canopy height $( y = h )$ , before decreasing gradually above the canopy height (y>h) and returning back towards zero at the free surface $( y = 3 h )$ . As expected, the maximum value of $\partial u _ {x} / \partial y$ is clearly observed to increase with canopy density.

As mentioned in Robert and Houze (2014), there is a qualitatively positive correlation between the mixing layer instability and the observed intensity of vorticity in the mixing layer. Therefore, when the canopy density increases, the vorticity intensity in the mixing layer (described by $\partial u _ {x} / \partial y )$ and the mixing layer instability both increase, the position of the onset of the mixing layer instability moves forward along the canopy. With increasing velocity gradient, the inflected velocity profile of the mixing layer becomes increasingly unstable to small perturbations (Finnigan, 2007), these perturbations can interact with the mixing layer flow and further exaggerated by it, the KH type instability is then induced in the mixing layer. The detailed formation of the KH instability can be found in Robert and Houze (2014). For the classic mixing layer in which two fluids move parallel at different speeds without any vegetation, such small perturbation can be induced by the gradient of flow velocity, the flow density or the potential flow temperature. However, in the present study when we replace the flexible vegetation with the rigid vegetation, the mixing layer instability does not emerge over any of the canopy densities tested $( \phi 0 \sim \phi 10 )$ , which indicates that the above sources of classic mixing layer instability are not the initial perturbation of the current vegetated flow, the initial perturbation is actually induced by the coupled oscillation of vegetation elements with the cross flow. More specifically, the mixing layer instability appears to arise from the small oscillations of vegetation elements in the foremost part of canopy, which are progressively amplified downstream by the mixing layer flow, and finally induce the KH type instability and the onset of wavelike motion of vegetation.

Based on the discussion above, it can be concluded that the instability of the mixing layer and the observed dynamics of vegetation are the collective response to the density and bending rigidity of the canopy. Increasing these two parameters can improve the resistance of the vegetation canopy. The first, by increasing the flow impediment from adjacent vegetation, while the second by improving the resistance of the vegetation itself. On the one hand, such increased resistance can enhance the mixing layer intensity on the top of the canopy, as analyzed above, which works to further accelerate the exaggeration of the initial perturbation generated by the vegetation and exacerbate the growth and development of the mixing layer instability. That is why when the canopy density and/or bending stiffness increases, the mixing layer instability starts to emerge, the motion of the vegetation breaks away from the static behavior and turns to the dynamic behavior including waving, flapping, or both (see the black dashed line in Fig. 4). On the other hand, such resistance enhancement can also suppress the initial oscillation of the vegetation, and further suppress the downstream development of mixing layer instability. This is why when the canopy density and/or bending stiffness increase to a very high value, the mixing layer instability does not emerge even though the vorticity intensity of mixing layer is very high (see the red dashed line in Fig. 4). The above two conflicted effects of bending rigidity and canopy density co-determine the behavior of the vegetation to be static or dynamic. While the detailed transition among different dynamic behaviors (the flapping, dual, regular waving and irregular waving behaviors) has been discussed in Section 4.2.

## 4.4. Lock in effect

In this section, the relationship between the waving frequency of the canopy, the natural frequency of the individual vegetation and the frequency of the KH instability is presented and discussed for the cases considered above.

Fig. 12 presents the response frequency of the vegetation canopy for dynamic behaviors (flapping, dual, regular waving and irregular waving) for different bending rigidities and canopy densities. Several important observations are apparent from this figure. First of all, for each dual behavior, the flapping motion of the foremost part of the canopy and the waving motion of the latter part of the canopy are computed separately since they present different frequencies, as indicated by the black doubled-headed arrows in Fig. 12. As such the observed response of vegetation forms two distinct branches: the waving branch (regular waving, irregular waving and the waving in dual behavior) and the flapping branch (flapping and the flapping in dual behavior). As shown in Fig. 12, there is a jump in the frequency as the behavior transitions from the waving branch to the flapping branch. This can be explained by the fact that the main deformation mode for the flapping state is the mode-two, which is associated with higher-frequency motion. Secondly, as bending rigidity increases, the frequencies in the waving branch and the flapping branch all increase significantly. This can also be indicated from Figs. 5c∼5e that the spacing between the coherent vortices becomes smaller as the bending rigidity increases, which means the frequency of vortex passage as well as the waving frequency of the vegetation are increased. Thirdly, for cases where bending rigidity is small $( K 1 \sim K 5 )$ , the observed frequencies in the same bending rigidity vary slightly with the canopy density, while as bending rigidity increases $( K 6 \sim K 10 )$ , such variation becomes more obvious. The variation of frequency with canopy density can also be indicated from Figs. 9c∼9e that the spacing between coherent vortices is almost the same in these cases, which means the waving frequencies of the vegetation vary very slightly with canopy density.

The variation of waving frequency with bending rigidity in Fig. 12 indicates that there is indeed a coupled dependence of the dynamic behavior of vegetation on its structural properties. To further elucidate this dependence we now consider the natural frequency of the individual vegetation element. Previous studies have generally described the response of a canopy in terms of either the vacuum natural frequency (Ghisalberti and Nepf, 2002), or the fluid natural frequency (the vacuum natural frequency modulated by the effect of fluid) (O’Connor and Revell, 2019). In the present investigation, both definitions of natural frequencies are computed for comparison.

The vacuum natural frequency of vegetation can be estimated using cantilever Euler–Bernoulli beam theory, in which the ith-order dimensionless natural frequency is given by:

$$f _ {n i} ^ {v} = \frac {f _ {n i} h} {\overline {{u}}} = \frac {k _ {i} ^ {2}} {2 \pi} \sqrt {\frac {K} {M}} , \mathrm{with~} \cos k _ {i} \cosh k _ {i} + 1 = 0\tag{25}$$

where $f _ {n i}$ represents the dimensional natural frequency, and $k _ {1} = 1.875$ and $k _ {2} = 4.694$ are two coefficients related to the first- and second-order natural frequencies, respectively. While the fluid natural frequency of vegetation is obtained through modulating the vacuum natural frequency by adding the equivalent mass in Eq. (25), given as:

$$f _ {n i} ^ {f} = \frac {k _ {i} ^ {2}} {2 \pi} \sqrt {\frac {K} {M + C _ {m} \cdot \pi / 4}} \nonumber\tag{26}$$

here $C _ {m} = 1.0$ is the added mass coefficient. As reported in Chung and Chen (1984), the value of $C _ {m}$ is dependent on the confinement of vegetation elements and increases with the canopy density $\varPhi$ . Since $C _ {m}$ is used to interpret the dynamics of vegetation as a posteriori estimation, it is not critical to consider its variation with Φ, $C _ {m} = 1.0$ is a suitable value for the vegetation element, and this value has been widely used in many relevant studies (Zhang et al., 2020; Luhar and Nepf, 2016).

The first- and second-order vacuum and fluid natural frequencies of vegetation are plotted in Fig. 12. As expected, the measured frequencies in the waving branch and flapping branch are broadly consistent with the first- and second-order natural frequencies, respectively. This indicates that there is a strong correlation between the dynamic behavior of the vegetation and its natural frequency, whereby the waving behavior is dominated by the first-order natural frequency while the flapping behavior is dominated by the second-order natural frequency. For the flapping cases, the frequencies measured from our numerical tests appear to follow the slope of the predicted frequencies, although the observed frequencies are consistently below those indicated by both $f _ {n 2} ^ {v}$ and $f _ {n 2} ^ {f}$ . Some deviation is also observed in Fig. 12 for the waving branch, the measured frequencies from our results do not follow the slope of either $f _ {n 1} ^ {v}$ or $f _ {n 1} ^ {f}$ , generally falling between these two curves in the figure. Indeed, when the bending rigidity is small, these frequencies are closer to the $f _ {n 1} ^ {v}$ . As the bending rigidity increases, these frequencies turn to be closer to the $f _ {n 1} ^ {f}$ . In Section 4.3 we have assumed that the initial perturbation of the mixing layer is generated by the oscillation of the vegetation, the mixing layer amplifies such perturbation and propagates it downstream. In this case, the waving frequency of vegetation should equal its natural frequency (oscillation frequency). However, the difference between the waving frequency of vegetation and its natural frequency indicates that the mixing layer not only propagates such initial perturbation (oscillation frequency) downstream, but also has a noticeable influence on it.

In order to elucidate the role the mixing layer plays in the dynamic behavior of the vegetation, the corresponding KH instability frequencies in different test cases are now investigated. As mentioned in the introduction, the inflection point in the velocity profile caused the mixing layer at the top of the canopy to be subjected to the KH instability, while the frequency of the KH instability is related to the momentum thickness of the mixing layer (Ghisalberti and Nepf, 2002; Ho and P, 1984), given as,

$$\begin{array} {l} {f _ {K H} = {S t _ {n}} \displaystyle \frac {\overline {{u}}} {\theta}} \\ {\displaystyle \theta = {\int _ {- \infty} ^ {+ \infty} {\left[ \frac {1} {4} - \left( \frac {u - \overline {{u}}} {\varDelta u} \right) ^ {2} \right] d y}}} \end{array}\tag{27}$$

(28)

where $S t _ {n} = 0.032$ is the natural Strouhal number associated with mixing layer, $u _ {1}$ and $u _ {2}$ are the low- and high-stream velocities (see in Fig. 1), $\overline {{u}} \ : = \ : \left( u _ {1} + u _ {2} \right) / 2$ is the mean velocity while $\varDelta u \ = \ u _ {2} \ - \ u _ {1}$ is the velocity difference, θ is the momentum thickness. The Eqs. (27) and (28) show that the KH frequency of the mixing layer is only related to the corresponding velocity profile, it is proportional to the mean velocity and inversely proportional to momentum thickness. Since the velocity in Eq. (28) has been normalized by ∆u, the momentum thickness is strongly linked to the height of infection point, the higher inflection point leads to the smaller momentum thickness.

Fig. 13 plots the frequencies of KH instability obtained through Eq. (27) for the test cases reported in this study. For comparison, the heavy KH frequencies (the KH frequencies of rigid vegetation cases) are also included. Similar to Fig. 12, different KH frequencies are obtained where dual behavior is present. Since the velocity profile of flow in the flapping part of dual behavior has a lower inflection point, the KH frequency in flapping part is smaller than that in the waving part. It can be seen from Fig. 13 that the frequency of KH instability increases with increasing bending rigidity, as observed and elucidated in Section 4.2, when the bending rigidity increases, the coherent vortices move upward, which with reference to Eq. (28), leads to an increase in both velocity $u _ {2}$ and the mean velocity u. On the other hand, as the penetration scale of coherent vortices is decreased, the inflection point in the velocity profile moves upward and the momentum thickness θ decreases. Both of these two changes lead to an increase in the KH frequency. However, as the bending rigidity increases further, and the canopy behavior switches from regular waving to irregular waving, the corresponding KH frequency decreases. At this point, the resistance of vegetation is great enough and the corresponding momentum thickness θ is almost unchanged. While the disappearance of instability vortices causes the high stream velocity u2 to decrease, and thus the KH frequency decreases as per Eq. (27). Finally, as the vegetation returns to a static state, the KH frequency has a very slight variation and approaches the values for the heavy (rigid) cases, since both of the mean velocity u and the momentum thickness θ are almost unchanged in this region. The similar variation of KH frequency can also be found for the increasing canopy density.

In addition, it can be seen from Fig. 13 that the lowest bound of all KH frequencies is approximately 0.06 (see the red dashed line in Fig. 13), which is the limiting case of the KH frequency calculated from the parabolic inlet velocity profile alone, defined in Eq. (20). It is also apparent that the KH frequencies of all dynamic states (flapping, dual, regular waving and irregular waving) rest in the range of 0.1∼0.15, i.e. the light-blue region in Fig. 13. In order to investigate the relationship between the natural frequency of the vegetation and the dynamic KH frequency, the corresponding region in the same range has also been marked in Fig. 12. It can be seen from Fig. 12 that the intersection of the vacuum natural frequency $f _ {n 1} ^ {v}$ and the dynamic KH frequency is approximately in the range of K4.6 ∼ K6.2. To explore this ‘cross’ region in more detail, additional numerical simulations were performed in this region for Φ2, Φ4 and $\Phi 7$ branches. As indicated by the light-red area in Fig. 14, the waving frequency of the canopy reaches a plateau in each branch. This plateau has also been reported in previous studies (O’Connor and Revell, 2019; Py et al., 2006; Gosselin and Langre, 2009) and been described as a lock-in effect. Paraphrasing these studies, when the frequency of the KH instability and the natural frequency of the vegetation approach each other, the waving frequency of the vegetation canopy deviates from its original trend and locks on to the synchronized frequencies of the KH instability and vegetation. While this is a generally accepted interpretation, the detailed mechanism behind such lock-in effect has not been explained in depth.

In the present paper, the obtained results suggest that the lock-in effect is actually a manifestation of the attraction effect of the mixing layer on the waving frequencies of the vegetation. As shown in Fig. 14, if the motion of the vegetation is governed by its structural properties alone, the waving frequency of the vegetation should strictly follows the curve of the vacuum natural frequency $f _ {n 1} ^ {v}$ or $f _ {n 2} ^ {v}$ since the mixing layer has no influence on it, while considering the added mass effect of fluid leads the waving frequency of the vegetation to follow the fluid natural frequency $f _ {n 1} ^ {f}$ or $f _ {n 2} ^ {f}$ However, for vegetation elements with a (first-order) fluid natural frequency $f _ {n 1} ^ {f}$ which is below the dynamic range KH frequencies (see the Region 1 in Fig. 14), the waving frequencies of the vegetation are observed to be slightly higher than $f _ {n 1} ^ {f}$ . Conversely, for elements with $f _ {n 1} ^ {f}$ higher than the KH frequency (see the Region 2 in Fig. 14), the observed waving frequencies are slightly below $f _ {n 1} ^ {f}$ . These findings demonstrate in more detail how the mixing layer acts to attract and ‘lock in’ waving frequencies of vegetation to its KH frequency (see the red arrows in Fig. 14). The reverse deviation of waving frequencies on both sides of the mixing layer (increasing in Region 1 while reducing in Region 2) further produces a plateau in the cross region (indicated by the light-red region in Fig. 14). This attractive effect of the mixing layer is even more pronounced in the flapping branch (see the Region 3 in Fig. 14), where the measured frequencies in the flapping branch are considerably lower than both the vacuum $f _ {n 2} ^ {v}$ and fluid $f _ {n 2} ^ {f}$ natural frequencies.

The above lock-in effect confirms that the governing mechanism for the dynamic motion of vegetation is indeed a coupling of the mixing layer and structural property, in which the structural property plays the dominant role while the mixing layer also plays a considerable role. It should be noted that when the Reynolds number of the flow increases further, the effect of the mixing layer on the dynamic motion of the vegetation is expected to be greater. As a result, the dominant role of the structural vibration of the vegetation element will be reduced and the waving frequencies of vegetation will become closer to the KH frequency. This helps explain why the observed waving frequency of the vegetation canopy was found to be more consistent with the predicted KH frequency in previous studies (Ghisalberti and Nepf, 2002).

## 4.5. Discussion on the effects of other parameters

In the present study, the effects of bending rigidity and canopy density on the dynamics of vegetation canopy are investigated by only varying these two parameters while keeping the other fixed. For instance, the Reynolds number of channel flow is fixed at Re = 100 while the mass ratio of vegetation is fixed at $M = 1.0$ . In this section, we will briefly discuss the effects of these parameters on the dynamics of vegetation canopy.

Reynolds number Based on our previous study (O’Connor and Revell, 2019), the Reynolds number is chosen to be Re = 100 as this was found to be close to the minimum Reynolds number capable of exhibiting waving instabilities. This Reynolds number is far from the real conditions since the Reynolds number of aquatic vegetation flow ranges $0 {\sim} O ( 10 ^ {5} )$ , while the Reynolds number of terrestrial vegetation flow is even higher. However, the dynamic motion of the vegetation elements as well as the variation of the response with bending rigidity and canopy density, is still relevant to the real vegetation canopy. When the Reynolds number is increased, two problems will occur. Firstly, the increased Reynolds number will induce the transverse coherent vortices which have the same scales as the streamwise coherent vortices. The coherent vortices in these two different dimensions will interact with each other and form the kinking and pairing vortices eventually (Finnigan, 2007). In this case, three-dimensional (3-D) physical effects become very important and the current 2-D model will be insufficient. Secondly, the increased Reynolds number will amplify the deflections of the vegetation elements and decrease the drag of them (Poggi et al., 2004), this will cause the contact between adjacent vegetation elements and lead to a stability problem since no contact model is incorporated in our numerical methods.

Mass ratio: The effect of the mass ratio M on the dynamics of the vegetation canopy has been investigated in our previous study (O’Connor and Revell, 2019). In that work, the value of the mass ratio is chosen from O(0.01) for aquatic vegetation to O(10) for terrestrial vegetation. The results show that the increase of mass ratio can increase the inertial forces of vegetation elements and reduce their deflections. In addition to this the instability of the mixing layer is further suppressed. This conclusion is still useful for the present study. When the mass ratio of the vegetation M increases, the dynamic states (flapping, dual and regular/irregular waving) of the vegetation canopy will be decreased. Furthermore, the area of these dynamic states in the mapping of Fig. 4 will shrink gradually and the area of the static state will expand. When the mass ratio meets a threshold value, the states of vegetation canopy will become static in the whole mapping.

Gravity/Buoyancy: In the present study, the dynamics of the vegetation canopy is dominated by the fluid load, and the gravity/buoyancy effects are disregarded. However, these gravity/buoyancy forces do have influences on the dynamics of vertical flow currents and vegetation elements. For instance, the gravity force of fluid flow will increase the penetration scales of the coherent vortices and further enhance the vertical exchange between the flow within and over the canopy. Therefore, the gravity force of fluid can exaggerate the instability of mixing layer.

While for a 2-D vegetation element, the gravity/buoyancy force acts on it can be expressed by Ghisalberti and Nepf (2002):

$$F _ {B} = \left( \rho _ {f} - \rho _ {s} \right) g h b = \rho _ {f} g h \left( b - M h \right)\tag{29}$$

where $g$ represents the gravitational acceleration and the direction of $F _ {B}$ is along y axis.

When the gravity/buoyancy force $F _ {B} = 0$ , the corresponding value of mass ratio is $M ^ {*} = b / h = 0.02$ . The mass ratio used in the present study is $M = 1.0 > M ^ {*}$ , which means the gravity force of the vegetation element is larger than its buoyancy force. This downward gravity/buoyancy force will increase the deflection of vegetation element and improve the penetration scales of the coherent vortices. The instability of the mixing layer is then further exaggerated.

2-D approximation Compared to the real 3-D vegetation canopy, the drag of the canopy in the present 2-D model is increased since the out-of-plane flow around the canopy is not considered. Such limitation is significant for a deeply submerged canopy, in which the coherent vortices are highly three-dimensional owing to their interaction with the larger boundary-layer turbulence. However, for shallow submergence like the configuration presented in this work the boundary-layer turbulence is not present and the coherent vortices remain two-dimensional in nature (Nepf, 2011). Therefore, the restriction to two dimensions here is a reasonable simplification to understand the interaction between the canopy and the mixing layer, and the same assumption has been widely used in previous studies (Zhang et al., 2020; Nepf et al., 2007; Gosselin and Langre, 2009).

In addition, one possible compromise for the real 3-D vegetation canopy would be to consider permeable vegetation elements in the current 2-D simulations. These permeable vegetation elements can mimic the out-of-plane flow around the canopy by allowing the flow to pass through them, and avoid the high computational cost of 3-D simulations. In this case, the confined recirculation zones in Figs. 5 and 10 will disappear, which is more accordant with the real vegetation canopy. Even when the vegetation elements become rigid, the permeability of these vegetation elements can also lead to the mixing layer instability in some conditions (see Fig. 13). In recent years, the flow over permeable or porous structures has been widely studied and many conclusions have been drawn (Pluvinage et al., 2014; Pepona and Favier, 2016; Natali et al., 2016). However, how to tune an empirical permeability to represent the 3-D flow around a slender structure still needs to be further investigated.

## 5. Conclusions

A direct fluid–structure modeling approach realized via a lattice Boltzmann-immersed boundary-finite element model is used to investigate the interaction between a vegetation canopy and the mixing layer. The dependence of vegetation behaviors on the bending rigidity and canopy density is explored and five representative response modes are observed: static, flapping, dual, regular waving and irregular waving. Through comparison of the vortical structures associated with different behavior modes, the influence of bending rigidity on the vortical structures in the mixing layer is investigated. In the flapping behavior, the centers of coherent vortices are lower than the tip of the vegetation, causing the vegetation to exhibit strong shear stress and to undergo mode-two deformation. While in the waving behavior, the rotational centers of coherent vortices are shifted upwards as the resistance of vegetation against the mixing layer is increased. As a result, the shear stress of the vegetation is negligible and its deformation transits to mode-one. The imbalance between the mixing layer production and canopy dissipation causes temporal and spatial variation in the waving amplitude of the vegetation; the waving motion of vegetation transits from a regular state to an irregular one. In addition, a spontaneous vertical transport from the canopy to the mixing layer is found in the flapping behavior, which is associated with the sandification of land. This finding indicates that increasing the canopy density and bending rigidity of the vegetation are both able to protect land from sandification. Finally, inspired by the dual behavior, a protection strategy for fragile crops is proposed by interspersing crop of different rigidity.

The influence of canopy density on the development of the mixing layer instability indicates that increasing the canopy density can decrease the vertical flow exchange between the canopy and the overlying flow, and further increase the intensity of the mixing layer. As a result, the onset of the mixing layer instability moves forward along canopy. The dynamic response of the canopy is the collective result of canopy density and bending rigidity of the vegetation. The increase of these two parameters can improve the resistance of the vegetation canopy, but also acts to increase the mixing layer intensity above the canopy. However, increasing these two parameters further acts to suppress the initial oscillation of vegetation, arresting the development of the mixing layer instability altogether.

The measured frequency response of the vegetation canopy is found to follow the first-order natural frequency in the waving branch and the second-order natural frequency in the flapping branch. The lock-in effect is observed in the cross region between the KH frequency and the natural frequency of the vegetation. These findings reveal an attraction effect of the mixing layer on the waving frequencies of vegetation and indicate that the governing mechanism for the dynamic motion of the vegetation is a coupling of the mixing layer and structural properties. The structural properties play a dominant role on the dynamic motion of the vegetation, while the mixing layer causes the waving frequency to display a deviation on its original trend. As the Reynolds number of the flow increases further, the attractive effect of the mixing layer is expected to become more dominant.

Although further insight and some meaningful conclusions are obtained in the present work, several areas remain for further investigation. Firstly, the spontaneous vertical transport found in the flapping behavior of the vegetation has a great significance for the control of the local wind erosion and/or the sandification. In further studies, the influence of gravity and buoyancy on this vertical transport should be incorporated. The proposed protection strategy for fragile crops also warrants further investigation. Finally, although physical insight gained from two-dimensional study is expected to be instructive, a fully three-dimensional analysis should be undertaken to confirm the extent of this approximation, exploring the impact of reduced blockage and an assessment of the transverse motion in the vegetation canopy.
