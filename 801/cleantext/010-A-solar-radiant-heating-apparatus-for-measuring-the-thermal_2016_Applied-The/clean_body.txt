## 1. Introduction

In order to protect the surfaces of the hypersonic vehicle and keep the underlying structure within safe operating temperatures, thermal protection systems with simultaneous lightweight and perfectly thermal protection/insulation properties are desired [1–3]. However, the thermal protection material experiences an irreversible physico-chemical process of ablation under elevated and high temperatures [4]. To provide insight into this complex high-temperature behavior, a controlled heating environment with well defined boundary conditions which can simulate the actual operating conditions of the thermal protection material is necessary. Moreover, many mathematical models for predicting the thermal behavior of these materials also require accurate experimental data. Therefore, there is a need to design a novel test bench which produces easily a high heat flux in a short time and simultaneously measures temperature profiles and thickness of heat affected zone of the material.

The types of high-temperature effects on thermal protection materials are aerodynamical heating, gas-dynamical heating, heating in an energetic device, action of a fire and technological heating [5]. The experimental apparatus for aerodynamical heating mainly involve arc heated wind tunnel, high frequency plasma wind tunnel, and oxyacetylene torch, et al. These apparatus can produce steady flowfield with high heat flux and were widely used to investigate the ablation properties, thermal shock, temperature resistance, and heat sealing performance of thermal protection materials [6–8]. The arc-jet wind tunnel can reproduce thermalfluid dynamic conditions typical of an atmospheric re-entry through converting the electric energy to thermal energy. Inside the arc heater pressure and temperature of the air reach 1– 17 atm and 2000–10,000 K, respectively [9,10]. However, fabrication and operating costs of this equipment system is higher because of a number of sophisticated components, also adjustment and calibration of the heat flux parameters is difficult [11,12]. Although oxyacetylene torch test could be utilized to study the high temperature oxidation performance and ablation behavior of ablative materials, the oxyacetylene flame may alter the flowfield around the ablator, which, in turn, affects the measured results [13,14].

In particular, induction heating and electric resistance heating devices were developed and used to examine the thermomechanical behavior of thermal protection materials with electrical or magnetical performances [15–17]. Meng et al. [15,16] used a home-made high current electrical source to conduct the repeated thermal shock test in which the test specimen was fixed between two Cu electrodes. Nevertheless, the heat fluxes produced by these heating devices are not uniform, and the one-sided heating boundary condition cannot be applied. Besides, the additional electric or magnetic fields may affect the microstructures of the experimental materials. Therefore, the interferences mentioned above should be avoided when we theoretically test the various responses of the thermal protection materials with temperature, and the radiant heating is thought to be the desired heating method.

Moreover, the high temperatures result in large changes in the thermal and transport properties, pyrolysis reactions, and thermochemical expansion of the thermal protection material. A significant number of mathematical models have been developed to investigate these complex material responses [18–23]. Henderson and Wiecek [18] proposed a one-dimensional transient thermal model to predict the thermal response of a decomposing, expanding polymer composite. In the model, solution of the system of governing differential equations was accomplished using the numerical control volume technique. Bai [22] and Keller and Bai [23] presented a thermo-chemical model in which an implicit finite differential method was used to solve the governing equation. The structural fire endurance experiments on cellular fiber reinforced polymer (FRP) panels were performed to validate the model. Gibson [24], McGurn [25], Mouritz [26], Tranchard [27], Anjang et al. [28] studied the fire response and post-fire structural properties of thermoplastic and thermosetting matrix composites under a static mechanical loading in fire environments.

This paper developed a solar radiant heating experimental apparatus which produces high heat flux boundary conditions. The various responses of a silica fiber reinforced phenolic composite exposed to high surface heat fluxes were characterized using this test bench. Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) was used to examine the microstructure and organizations of silica/phenolic composites before and after thermal exposure. A model was proposed to simulate the heat transfer, thermo-chemical decomposition and gas diffusion process of polymer matrix composites at high temperatures. The accuracy of the model was accessed using the experimental data.

## 2. Design of the novel solar radiant heating test

## 2.1. Apparatus

Radiant heating devices have generally utilized a variety of radiant energy sources such as lasers, carbon arc, resistance furnace, tungsten filament lamps, and various types of high temperature heating elements to heat test specimens. These apparatus are capable of producing high heat flux with well-defined boundary conditions. Henderson and Hagen [29] built an experimental apparatus to measure the thermal response of polymeric materials, in which a silicon carbide heating element was powered by the power supply. These radiant energy sources, however, do not produce full spectrum radiation. Because surface absorptivity of test materials is different for radiation of each band, these heating devices produce somewhat lower heat fluxes and heating rates.

A full spectrum solar radiant heating apparatus which produces high heat fluxes was developed to investigate the high temperature behavior of polymer matrix thermal protection material. A schematic description of this radiant heating apparatus and instrumentation is shown in Fig. 1. To succeed in the development of this versatile radiant heating test, we need to get a Fresnel lens (80 cm  80 cm, focal length of 130 cm), a prism (made of K9 glass), a thermocouples temperature measurement system, a heat flux calibration system, and an insulation which ensures the onesided heating boundary conditions. This test bench is capable of producing radiant heat fluxes up to $4 \ : \mathrm{MW} / \mathrm{m} ^ {2}$ in a short time. The fabrication and operating cost of this equipment system is about \$25,000, while numerical values about the cost for the oxyacetylene torch, arc-jet wind tunnel and high frequency plasma wind tunnel are about \$30,000, \$380,000, and \$770,000, respectively. Therefore, this apparatus is more inexpensive than the others mentioned above and the functional parameters are also controllable. The sunlight, which firstly focused by Fresnel lens and then secondly focused by prism, was used as the energy source. The high radiant heat flux generated by the apparatus was applied perpendicular to the test specimen surface and then ablation and thermal shock behavior of thermal protection materials can be measured and evaluated.

The photograph of the apparatus is shown in Fig. 2. The prism is mounted vertically below the Fresnel lens, while the test specimen is held vertically, by the carriage mechanism, above the base plate (Fig. 2(a)–(c)). Adjusting handles are also mounted to ensure the heating surface of the test specimen with uniform heat flux. Thermocouples implanted in the specimens are used to measure the indepth temperature profiles. The specimen is thermally insulated by a ceramic tube which is placed in the hole of the graphite fixture (Fig. 2(d)). Additionally, a thin sheet of aluminum is attached on the external graphite fixture to reflect the incident light on the graphite fixture as much as possible. The experimental apparatus can adjust the distance of the prism and the specimen to produce strong radiant heat fluxes. The full spectrum radiation heating process produced by the apparatus is beneficial to theoretically test the various responses of the thermal protection materials with temperature.

## 2.2. Experimental procedure

The apparatus should be adjusted before test to make the sunlight vertical incidence to the specimen surface. Firstly, the Fresnel lens was adjusted to focus on the sun. Position of the prism and specimen were adjusted simultaneously. To prevent the specimen from heating during the adjustment process, an insulated plate with the specimen location mark was mounted vertically above the specimen. The sunlight converged twice coincided with the mark on the insulated plate after adjusting. Subsequently, the insulated plate was removed to start test. In order to estimate the heat flux during test, a subsequent experiment was performed to provide the temperature data for a small-scale specimen (5 mm 5 mm 3 mm, as shown in Fig. 2(e)). The subsequent test was within 10 s to assure that heat transfer in the material is a result of pure conduction, and the maximum temperature of the small-scale specimen was less than 100 C. These temperature data were required as input to the inverse heat transfer problem (IHTP) to estimate the heat flux during test.

A silica fiber reinforced phenolic resin composite, kindly provided by Aerospace Research Institute of Materials and Processing Technology (Beijing, China), was selected and characterized in this study. This silica/phenolic composite was 24 wt% silica fiber (Shaanxi HuaTek Fiberglass Material Group Co., Ltd., China), 20.4 wt% phenolic fiber (Tongshan chemical plant, China), 55.6 wt % phenolic resin (Tongshan chemical plant, China). It should be noted that the basic properties of the phenolic fiber were similar to the phenolic resin. The silica/phenolic composite specimens measure 10 mm in length, 10 mm in width and 15 mm in depth. The basic material properties, temperature-dependent thermal and transport properties of silica/phenolic composites summarized in our previous work [30] were also used.

## 3. Formulations of the coupled temperature-diffusionexpansion problem

Schematic of various responses of FRP composites under high temperatures is presented in Fig. 3. When FRP composites are subjected to intensive high-temperature heating, thermal decomposition reactions occur and the resin system degrades to form gaseous products and carbonaceous char. The decomposition gases are trapped in the internal material with low porosity and permeability and this may cause high internal pressures. Large thermochemical expansion of the solid material is attributed to high pore pressure. As the pyrolysis reactions proceed, the decomposition gases begin to flow through the porous solid skeleton. Furthermore, the heat transfer, thermochemical expansion and diffusion of decomposition gases are generally coupled and associated with the thermal decomposition of polymer matrix. The thermal behavior of FRP composites under high temperatures can be treated as a coupled temperature-diffusion-expansion (TDE) problem of the porous materials.

## 3.1. Energy conservation equation

For simplicity, the thermal analysis is restricted to the onedimensional heat and mass transfer process and there are the following assumptions. (1) The decomposition gases are non-reactive, and only flow through the thickness direction of materials. (2) The thermal equilibrium exists between the solid matrix and the decomposition gases. (3) The gas flow is steady, incompressible and irrotational, and is governed by Darcy’s law. With these assumptions, the energy conservation can be given by a modified Laplace’s equation,

$$\nabla \cdot q _ {\mathrm{w}} \vec {i} + \rho _ {s} C _ {\mathrm{ps}} \frac {\partial T} {\partial t} = \nabla \cdot \left( k \frac {\partial T} {\partial x} \vec {i} \right) - \rho _ {\mathrm{g}} C _ {\mathrm{pg}} \vec {\nu} _ {\mathrm{g}} \cdot \nabla T - Q \rho _ {s} \frac {d c} {d t}\tag{ð1Þ}$$

where $q _ {\mathrm{w}}$ is the radiant heat flux; $\therefore C _ {\mathfrak {p}}$ and Q are the thermal conductivity, specific heat and heat of decomposition, respectively. The subscripts s and g represent the solid phase and gas phase, respectively. $\vec {\upsilon} _ {\mathrm{g}}$ is the volume average gas velocity vector and can be expressed in terms of pore pressure p, permeability of material k and gas viscosity in pores $\mu$ through Darcy’s law,

$$\vec {\upsilon} _ {\mathrm{g}} = - \frac {\lambda} {\mu} \frac {\partial p} {\partial x} \vec {i}\tag{ð2Þ}$$

In Eq. $( 1 ) ,$ c is the conversion degree of the pyrolysis reaction of polymer matrix and generally depends on temperature and activation energy of materials. We use the modified Arrhenius kinetic reaction equation to determine the rates of decomposition, which can be expressed as follows,

$$\frac {d c} {d t} = - A _ {0} c ^ {n} \exp \left( - \frac {E _ {\mathrm{a}}} {R T} \right)\tag{ð3Þ}$$

where $A _ {0} , n ,$ , and $E _ {\mathrm{a}}$ are pre-exponential factor, order of reaction and activation energy, respectively; R is the ideal gas constant. These kinetic parameters can be calculated using the Kissinger method from an analysis of the thermogravimetry (TG) data for polymer matrix at a heating rate of 5 C/min in nitrogen atmosphere.

## 3.2. Mass transforming relationship

Similarly to the multilevel structural model of fabric materials proposed by Dimitrienko [5], a physical model in mesomechanical scale of FRP composites during decomposition is developed. The model consists of fiber phase (f), polymer phase (p), char phase (c) and pore and crack phase filled with gaseous pyrolysis products (g). We assume that the density of each phase component is constant during pyrolysis reactions. Using this assumption, mass transforming relationships can be expressed by the following forms,

$$\rho _ {\mathsf{p}} {\frac {\partial \varphi _ {\mathsf{p}}} {\partial t}} = {\frac {d c} {d t}}\tag{ð4Þ}$$

$$\rho _ {\mathrm{c}} \frac {\partial \varphi _ {\mathrm{c}}} {\partial t} = - \frac {d c} {d t} ( 1 - \Gamma ^ {\mathrm{p}} )\tag{ð5Þ}$$

$$\varphi _ {\mathrm{g}} = 1 - \varphi _ {\mathrm{f}} - \varphi _ {\mathrm{p}} - \varphi _ {\mathrm{c}}\tag{ð6Þ}$$

where $\rho , \varphi$ are the density and volume content of phase component. $\Gamma ^ {\mathfrak {p}}$ is the gasification coefficient of the resin polymer determined in TGA experiments.

The density of FRP composites with thermal decomposition is described by the equation,

$$\rho = \rho _ {\mathrm{f}} \varphi _ {\mathrm{f}} + \rho _ {\mathrm{p}} \varphi _ {\mathrm{p}} + \rho _ {\mathrm{c}} \varphi _ {\mathrm{c}}\tag{ð7Þ}$$

It should be noted that the density of gas phase is ignored in Eq. (7).

## 3.3. Formulations for thermochemical expansion

Henderson [18] used an expansion equation proposed by Buch [31] to calculate the rate of length change of the solid material during thermal decomposition. In this study, an equation similar to Henderson’s work [18] is used to calculate the thermochemical expansion of FRP composites at high temperatures. It is assumed that the initial thickness of the specimen in heating direction is $h _ {0}$ The rate of thickness change of FRP composites is given by the following expression,

$$\frac {1} {h _ {0}} \frac {\partial h} {\partial t} = [ \beta _ {\mathrm{virg}} ( 1 - c ) + \beta _ {\mathrm{char}} c ] \frac {\partial T} {\partial t} + \frac {\xi} {m _ {0}} \frac {\partial m} {\partial t}\tag{ð8Þ}$$

where $\beta _ {\mathrm{virg}}$ and $\beta _ {\mathrm{char}}$ represent the coefficient of linear expansion of the virgin and char materials, respectively; n is the thermal decomposition expansion factor determined by experiment, which can evaluate the effect of pyrolysis reactions on the thermal expansion of FRP composites. For silica/phenolic composites, the value of n is 0.88. In Eq. $( 8 ) , m _ {0}$ is the initial mass of the material; m is the instantaneous mass of the material during thermal decomposition and can be given in terms of the conversion degree, c, as follows,

$$m = c m _ {0} + ( 1 - c ) m _ {\mathrm{final}}\tag{ð9Þ}$$

where $m _ {\mathrm{final}}$ is the final mass of the material when the pyrolysis reaction completes and is determined by the TG analysis.

The change in thickness of the composite during heating causes the change in geometry boundary of the heat transfer problem. The moving boundary must be considered in the calculation of temperature profiles. The coordinate system is established in the initial state and does not change during the thermal analysis in this study. The amount of node and the node number also do not change as same as the initial state, whether the thermal expansion occurs or not. However, the node coordinate as a function of heating time is updated when the thickness changes. Schematic of the implementation of moving boundary due to thermal expansion is shown in Fig. 4. By updating the coordinate of each node at time $t + \Delta t ,$ , the temperature of node i with new coordinate at time t, denoted by $T _ {i ^ {\prime}} ^ {0} ,$ , is calculated using the Newton’s interpolation method from the temperature results at time t.

## 3.4. Gas diffusion equation

The mass transfer process of pyrolysis gases through a porous solid material is analyzed using a control volume model. The differential equation governing the diffusion of pyrolysis gases through the porous solid skeleton, according to the principle of mass conservation of gas phase, is given as,

$$\frac {\partial m _ {\mathrm{g}}} {\partial t} = \frac {\partial m _ {\mathrm{pg}}} {\partial t} - \nabla \cdot ( \rho _ {\mathrm{g}} \vec {\upsilon} _ {\mathrm{g}} )\tag{ð10Þ}$$

where $m _ {\mathrm{pg}}$ is the gas mass generated in pyrolysis reactions and can be determined using Eq. (3). The left term is the time rate of gas storage inside the control volume. The first term on the right side gives the time rate of gas generation and the second term defines the rate of change of the gas mass flux across the control volume boundaries.

## 4. Results and discussion

Using the developed solar radiant heating apparatus, solar radiant heating tests for silica/phenolic composites were performed. The surface morphology of the specimen after thermal exposure is given in Fig. 5. As can be seen, the surface materials are charred completely and there are only silica fibers and inert char left on the heated surface. An incremental change in degree of decomposition of the post-test specimen is evident in the figure. The thickness of the char layer, pyrolysis zone and virgin material may be estimated by distinguishing the color of the specimen profile. However, there are large errors in that estimation.

In this study, the post-test specimen was sectioned in millimeter segments firstly. Fig. 6 presents a series of images of segments of a silica/phenolic composite specimen after thermal exposure. The segments 1–9 were sectioned successively along the heating direction. Then, the density of the segment was calculated from its mass and volume and the location of the segment was documented from the heated surface simultaneously. The measured density profile is plotted in Fig. 7. The density of the post-exposure composite in the through-thickness direction is not uniform, but of course is lowest at the char layer and highest at the virgin material. Thus, the thicknesses of the char layer, pyrolysis zone, and virgin material can be determined from the density profiles.

The mass transforming relationships (Eqs. (4)–(7)) were used to calculate the density profile of the silica/phenolic composite specimen during heating. The predicted density profile is presented as a solid line in Fig. 7. As evident from the figure, the calculated density profile roughly coincides with the experimental results and the density curve roughly divides into three regions. The densities change slightly from 0.97 to 1.12 g/cm3 at depths of 0–1.84 mm where recognized as the char layer, while at depths of 1.84– 6.40 mm, the densities change greatly from 1.12 to 1.36 g/cm3 , where recognized as the pyrolysis zone. The densities retain a constant of 1.37 g/cm3 at subsequent location where recognized as the virgin material. Furthermore, comparison of Figs. 6 and 7 reveals that slice 1 and 2, 3–5 as well as 6–9 are recognized as the char layer, pyrolysis zone and virgin material, respectively. Also, the density profiles are in good agreement with visual observations on transitions in segments shown in Fig. 6.

The mathematical model developed in Section 3 was used to calculate the thermal response of silica/phenolic composites exposed to one-sided radiant heating. The heat flux during test estimated by the IHTP technique was applied to the composite surface, which was 235 kW/m2 . Also, the same boundary and initial conditions were utilized to validate the model by comparing the predicted results with experimental values. Fig. 8(a) shows the comparison of experimental and calculated time-dependent temperature profiles. The thermocouples were embedded at initial depths of 2.5, 8.0, and 13.5 mm to monitor the time-dependent temperatures presented as data points in the figure. Comparing the measured temperature values with the predicted temperature curves at the same spatial locations, the agreement is good. This demonstrates that the proposed model gives a good prediction of the thermal response of FRP composites. Comparison of calculated temperature profiles with and without moving boundary is shown in Fig. 8(b). The maximum temperature difference is 50 C between considering moving boundary and without. This suggests that the thermal expansion tends to reduce the temperatures.

Fig. 9 shows the spatially dependent calculated conversion degree of the pyrolysis reaction. The value 1 of degree of decomposition indicates that the polymer has completely decomposed, while the value 0 indicates that the pyrolysis reaction of the material has not happened. As expected, the degree of decomposition decreases with increasing distance from the heated surface. Because the critical value 0 of conversion degree coincides with the depth of heat affected zone, the experimental conversion degree was determined through measuring the depths of heat affected zones of specimens after thermal exposure which also presented in the figure. The measured depth of the heat affected zone is about 8.27 mm at heating time of 100 s, and this thickness increases with increasing heating time to about 11.97 mm at 200 s.

Fig. 10 illustrates the spatially dependent mass flux profiles of gas of silica/phenolic composites during heating. It can be seen that the absolute mass flux of gas decreases with increasing distance from the heating surface. This suggests that most of the decomposition gases flow through the char layer toward the heated surface attenuating the conduction of heat to the pyrolysis zone [18]. Also, examination of Figs. 9 and 10 shows that the initiation of the decomposition gas flow toward the heated surface occurs immediately after the onset of the pyrolysis reactions.

The representative microstructures of silica/phenolic composites after thermal exposure at known locations are presented in Fig. 11. Variation in the composite material morphology from char layer to pyrolysis zone to virgin material is evident in the figure. As shown from Fig. 11(a), the morphology of charred material (slice 1) does not contain the phenolic resin phase the resin polymer has completely decomposed. There are parts of phenolic resin for the composite at pyrolysis zone, as shown in Fig. 11(b) and (c). A typical virgin silica/phenolic composite microstructure is presented in Fig. 11(d). It also can be found that the porosity of the composite material increases with decreasing distance from the heated surface.

## 5. Conclusions

The full spectrum solar radiant heat flux apparatus was developed in which the sunlight was converged twice. The temperature measurements were conducted to provide the experimental data necessary to evaluate the accuracy of the numerical model. The specimen after thermal exposure was sectioned in millimeter segments to determine the thickness of the heat affected zone. Considering the coupled heat, mass transfer and thermochemical expansion processes, the mathematical model was proposed and temperature profiles, degree of decomposition and mass flux of gas of silica/phenolic composites were predicted. The main conclusions were as follows,

(1) The apparatus can produce high radiant heat fluxes. The full spectrum radiation heating process is beneficial to theoretically test the high temperature behavior of the thermal protection materials.

(2) The device is inexpensive and easy to construct and maintain.

(3) The mathematical model satisfactorily predicts the thermal response of FRP composites exposed to one-sided heat fluxes.

(4) Variation in the thermal behavior from char layer to pyrolysis zone to virgin material for silica/phenolic composites after thermal exposure is evident.
