## 1. Introduction

With the retirement of Space Shuttle, several subsequent hypersonic and space vehicle programs are proposed, such as NASP and succedent Hyper-X of USA, Skylon of UK, LAPCAT of EU, Space-Liner of Germany. In comparison with the space shuttle which is a rocket-launched vehicle, the thermal condition for these subsequent hypersonic vehicles is more severe for two reasons: 1st, the current space vehicles flight in atmosphere for a longer period, thus the accumulation of aerodynamic heat over time is enormous; 2nd, for the current space vehicles, the air-breathing or combined cycle engines are widely adopted, the aerodynamic performance is more concerned and slender configurations are preferred, and thus the reduced bluntness of the fuselage leads to more severe heating effect.

The techniques of thermal protection system (TPS) are developed and improved as responses to the severe aerodynamic heating with the progress of these programs. The passive TPS concepts that developed for NASP and Hyper-X by NASA are lighter, stronger and more reliable than those for Space Shuttle. For instance, the concept alumina enhanced thermal barrier (AETB) that developed for X-43 (vehicle of Hyper-X project) is much stronger and has better performance of rain erosion. The active TPS techniques like the convective cooling are more widely considered in current hypersonic vehicles. For instances, as early as NASP, the cryogenic hydrogen is selected as the reused heat sink [1]; for MR-2 which is designed in LAPCAT, a cooling cycle with fuel hydrogen is proposed, and the whole system considers the cooling of fuselage, fuel tanks, cabin, etc. [2]; for SpaceLiner, the cooling systems with water and fuel-hydrogen are designed and evaluated for the nose and leading edge [3].

In general, the thermal environment of current hypersonic vehicles is extremely severe, and meanwhile the slender configuration means the vehicle is more sensitive to the scale including the weight and the bulk of TPS. Under this condition, the TPS with higher efficiency and the TPS design method with lower redundancy are required. Therefore, the concept of thermal energy management (TM) is proposed. The TM system is to manage the production, dissipation, transport and reuse process of the aerodynamic heat.

The management of aerodynamic heat production mainly refers to the heat reduction. A variety of techniques have been developed in regard of aerodynamic heat reduction. For instance, the blunt forebody is the most-preferred configuration although it will increase the aerodynamic drag [4], and it is always combined with spikes and jets, i.e., opposing jet [5], rear jet [6] or lateral jet [7] to reduce both aerodynamic heat and drag. However, all the above heat reduction techniques will influence the vehicles’ aerodynamic performance for certain, thus, developing techniques to efficiently dissipate, transport and reuse the aerodynamic heat is another promising technology path.

The aerodynamic heat dissipation is mainly dependent on the thermal radiation at the surface of passive TPS, and a qualified passive TPS should be capable of bearing high temperature as well as insulating the heat from entering the inner structure [8,9]. A traditional passive TPS is composed of a variety of TPS concepts with different sizes and weights, and each concept is composed of an outer high-temperature resistant layer such as ceramic coatings, one or several insulation layers such as insulation fiber felts, and an inner layer such as adhesive to attach to the vehicle structure. It should be noted that the present primary concepts are those developed by NASA in Space Shuttle and subsequent NASP and Hyper-X programs, and can be classified into flexible ceramic blankets, rigid ceramic tiles and metallic panels [10]. The key issue of such a passive TPS is to obtain optimum distribution, size and weight of TPS concepts according to the aerodynamic heating environment. Besides, such type of TPS concepts has single function of thermal resistant and relatively low structural efficiency, thus some additional components can be integrated to create more efficient multifunctional TPS concepts, such as the corrugated core integrated TPS of mechanical load bearing function [11,12] and thermoelectric (TE) material integrated TPS of TE conversion function [13].

Most of the aerodynamic heat is dissipated through thermal radiation on the passive TPS surface, for the part that enters passive TPS, it should be transported (“take-away”) to regions of low temperature or places of heat consuming. In hypersonic vehicles, such heat transport can be realized by heat pipe [14,15] and coolant convection [16–18] techniques. The heat pipe technique is widely used in high-temperature components like vehicle leading edges [15] to transport aerodynamic heat to low-temperature region in a short time, however, the final heat dissipation has to resort to other additional measures, thus heat pipes are not suitable for vehicle surfaces of large area. The coolant convection cooling is well-known as regenerative cooling (RC) when the fuel is adopted as coolant: the fuel flows through high-temperature region and transports the heat to propulsion system. It is obvious that the RC technique can realize the transport and reuse of aerodynamic heat at the same time, and attracts lots of attentions. At present, the primary application of RC technique is in specific components like the combustion chambers and nozzles for a variety of propulsion systems such as scramjets [19,20], rocket engines [21,22] and combined cycle engines [23,24]. In addition, for the well-known precooled hypersonic engines [25–27] such as SABRE, the heat energy of the in-take air is firstly cooled by the refrigerating medium (Helium for SABRE) and then the heated refrigerating medium is cooled by the fuel, from the point view of energy regeneration, such type of cooling technique can be also considered as RC technique. The RC technique is theoretically efficient due to its potential ability in both heat transport and reuse, thus deserves wider applications: a kerosene-cooled corrugated sandwich structure is proposed in [28], and the influence of coolant velocity and flow direction on thermal and thermomechanical performances are analyzed; in the authors’ previous work [29], the cooling capacity of RC networks of the head and whole vehicle are designed for a hypersonic launch vehicle. However, the research on this topic is still very limited.

As discussed above, the reuse of aerodynamic heat can be implemented by RC and TE conversion techniques. The thermal energy can be converted into electric energy based on the Seebeck effect of TE materials, and the conversion efficiency can be improved (5-20% or higher) by doping, nano-structuring, etc. [30]. Under certain temperature difference, n-type TE material has higher electric potential on the high-temperature side, while the p-type material has higher electric potential on the lowtemperature side, thus a TE module always consists of a “π -type” connected n-p to output the maximum electric power. At present, TE materials and modules of high-temperature (>900K, Oxide ones) [13,19], mid-temperature (500-900K, e.g., PbTe) [31,32] and lowtemperature (<500K, e.g., Bi2Te3) [33–35] are developed, and some multi-stage modules that available in a relatively wide temperature range are also proposed [36,37]. The above TE modules or generators are used in aerodynamic heat conversion of hypersonic vehicles [13,38], solar energy conversion [33], waste heat recovery of automobile [32], power supply in handheld electronic gadgets [34] and hybrid electric vehicles [37] and so on. In addition, some researches focus on the general factors that influence the TE conversion efficiency, e.g., the influence of Thomson effect [39], the porous annular TE connector type [40], and the heat transfer on the hot side [41], the cold side [42] and lateral side [43] of TE modules.

In this work, the TM system consists of passive TPS, RC network and TE component of a hypersonic cruiser is designed, and the design roadmap is developed. An equivalent thermal equilibrium model is developed to establish the mutual correlation between the aerodynamic heating and TM system. The aerodynamic heat is calculated based on an engineering algorithm with the consideration of heat capacity of TM system. For the passive TPS, the distribution and weight of the TPS concepts are determined; for the RC network, the hydrocarbon and liquid hydrogen fuel are considered as coolants, the mass-flow-rate and the allowable coolant temperature rise are obtained; for the TE component, a TE-AFRSI multifunctional concept is developed by integrating two TE stages into a type of passive TPS concept, i.e., advanced flexible reusable surface insulation (AFRSI). The performances of the RC network and TE component on specific subregions of the cruiser are numerically studied, and the heat reuse efficiency is analyzed.

## 2. The vehicle, TM system and the fuel-coolant

## 2.1. The hypersonic cruise vehicle and its TM system

In this work, the vehicle will encounter the most harsh aeroheating for a long period of time in the cruise stage, while the ascent stage has little influence, thus, only the cruise stage is considered.

## 3. Methodology of TM system design

In this work, the TM system consists of three subsystems, i.e., passive TPS, RC network and TE component. The design of such TM system involves the calculation of aerodynamic heat and the determination of subsystems’ scales.

## 3.1. The equivalent thermal equilibrium model and overall HTC

The TPS is involved with three heat transfer processes, the aerodynamic heating, i.e., the heat convection $\left( q _ {a e r o} \right)$ between the hypersonic airstream and the outer wall of TPS, the thermal radiation at the outer wall of TPS and the heat conduction into TPS. The thermal equilibrium equation should be:

$$\alpha ( T _ {r} - T _ {w} ) = \varepsilon \sigma T _ {w} ^ {4} + q _ {i n}\tag{1}$$

where α is the convective heat transfer coefficient of aerodynamic heating, $T _ {r}$ is the recovery temperature, $T _ {w}$ is the wall temperature, ε is the surface emissivity, $\sigma ~ = ~ 5.67 ~ \times ~ 10 ^ {- 8} ~ {\sf W} / ( {\mathrm m} ^ {2} {\cdot} {\bar {\mathrm{K}}} ^ {4} )$ is the Stefan-Boltzmann constant, and $q _ {i n}$ is the heat flux of the heat conduction into TPS. The term on the left side of Eq. (1) is the convective heat transfer that caused by aerodynamic heating (heat in the wall), and the right two terms are the heat radiation and conduction (heat out and through the wall), respectively. In addition, the emissivity of TPS surface is mainly dependent on the surface material, temperature and other characteristics. From Ref. [10], one can find that for C9 coating on the surface of TPS concepts AFRSI and TABI (Tailorable Advanced Blanket Insulation), the surface emissivity is between 0.87 to 0.66 with temperature range of 848 to 2098 $\mathrm{K} ;$ for IN617 on the surface of SA-HC and SA-HC2 (the second-generation superalloy honeycomb), the surface emissivity is 0.86. In Ref. [46], the surface emissivity of ceramic materials $( \mathsf{C} / \mathsf{C} , \mathsf{C} / \mathsf{SiC} . \hdots )$ is assumed to be 0.8. In this work, a unified surface emissivity is needed in the calculation and it is assumed to be 0.8 according to the above references.

It should be noted that $q _ {i n}$ will be finally taken away by active TM subsystems, i.e., transported or reused by RC networks and TE components, thus such amount of heat can be considered as a sort of “take-away” heat. In most previous studies, it is neglected to simplify calculations from the conservative point of view. However, in this work, the amount of take-away heat should not be neglected. The RC network is assumed to be under the bottom wall of passive TPS, while the TE component is assumed to be integrated into the passive TPS concepts (details can be found in Section 3.6).

In the authors’ previous work [29], an overall equivalent heat transfer coefficient (HTC) is proposed, and $q _ {i n}$ is specified by:

$$q _ {i n} = h _ {e q ^ {\prime}} ( T _ {w} - T _ {c} ) = h _ {l} ( T _ {b} - T _ {c} )\tag{2}$$

where $h _ {e q ^ {\prime}}$ is the overall equivalent HTC, $T _ {c}$ is the temperature of coolant, $h _ {l}$ is the local HTC of active cooling, $T _ {b}$ is the temperature of TPS bottom wall (a constrained design value). The middle term of Eq. (2) can be a substitute of $q _ {i n}$ in Eq. (1), and values will be specified for $h _ {e q}$ and $T _ {c}$ before the calculation, thus no unknown parameters are introduced to Eq. (1). Based on Eqs. (1) and (2), the influence of $q _ {i n}$ on aerodynamic heating is considered, and the influence is also dependent on the wall temperature reasonably. However, $T _ {c}$ is not a constant in real conditions, and it is assumed to be the temperature of heated coolant in [29], which is a conservative assumption. In addition, the heat take-away technique of $q _ {i n}$ is limited to coolant convection by $T _ {c}$ . Therefore, in this work, $T _ {c}$ in the middle term of Eq. (2) is eliminated, and Eq. (2) is modified as:

$$q _ {i n} = h _ {e q} T _ {w}\tag{3}$$

It is clear that $h _ {e q}$ in Eq. (3) can also be called overall equivalent HTC, and $q _ {i n}$ is only correlated with the first power of $T _ {w}$ . In this condition, $T _ {c}$ is not an input parameter, and the cooling techniques of $q _ {i n}$ will not be limited to coolant convection.

In summary, the model in this paper has two main improvements compared to the authors’ previous work [26]: 1st, the equivalent thermal equilibrium model and the overall equivalent HTC can deal with different heat take-away techniques rather than the convective cooling only; it can be detailed described as: the coolant temperature in the previous thermal equilibrium equation means that the model is only available to convective heat transfer; however, no such types of parameters are involved in the new thermal equilibrium equation, thus, the new model is more general and appropriate for different types of heat transfer and heat conversion ways. 2nd, for the cooling network, the initial coolant temperature is not required in this work, and the over conservative input is thus avoided in the preliminary design; it can be detailed described as: the previous work adopts the final temperature of the heated coolant as the initial temperature, which is obviously over conservative and weakens the heat take-away effect; at the same time, the new model is available for the preliminary design of cooling network, since it is factually the temperature difference rather than the initial temperature of the coolant that is concerned in the further design of cooling network.

## 3.2. Correlation between aerodynamic heat and TM subsystems

The TM system of this work contains three subsystems, the passive TPS, RC network and TE component. The passive TPS is

determined by the aerodynamic heat and the wall temperature, which will be influenced by $q _ {i n} ,$ i.e., the equivalent heat transfer flux that will be taken away by RC network or TE component.

If $q _ {i n}$ is transported by RC network, the product of coolant mass-flow-rate and temperature rise can be obtained by:

$$h _ {e q} T _ {w} A = m _ {f} c _ {p} \Delta T _ {c}\tag{4}$$

where A is the area of heat transfer, $m _ {f}$ is the mass-flow-rate, $c _ {p}$ is the specific heat capacity, and $\triangle T _ {c}$ is the temperature rise of coolant. In this work, $m _ {f} \cdot c _ {p}$ indicates the heat capacity that required by RC network. Two types of coolants are adopted, the JET-A 3638 and the liquid hydrogen $( \mathrm{LH} _ {2} ) ,$ , whose physical properties can be found in Section 2.2. It should be noted that the chemical heat sink of the coolant is not considered in this work. The coolant temperature should be lower than the constrained bottom wall temperature in this work, 440 K, which is lower than the chemical limit of both kerosene (450 K for autoxidation reaction and 700 K for pyrolysis reaction [47]) and LH2 (833 K for ignition point [45]).

$\operatorname{If} q _ {i n}$ is reused by TE conversion, the temperature difference between the two sides of the TE material will create electric potential and the phenomenon is the so-called Seebeck effect. The TE couple can be considered as a constant voltage power supply, and its internal resistance is the series electric resistance of TE materials R. If the electric current loop is linked by an external resistor $r ,$ the total electric power can be divided into Joule heating consumption on TE materials and power output on r. The Joule heating can be considered as the internal source of heat conduction, thus only the electric power that generated on r is considered as the output of TE conversion. When the external resistance equals to the internal one, i.e., $r \ : = \ : R$ , the power supply will output the maximum electric power theoretically. The real output power is dependent on the external resistor, thus, in this work, only the theoretical maximum one is considered. Then, $q _ {i n}$ can be represented as:

$$h _ {e q} T _ {w} A = U ^ {2} R / ( 2 R ) ^ {2}\tag{5}$$

where U is the total electric potential of TE couple and R is the series electric resistance of TE materials. The right term of Eq. (5) is the electric power that generated on the external resistor. The electric potential U can be represented by the product of temperature difference and Seebeck coefficient:

$$U = | S _ {n} \Delta T _ {n} | + \left| S _ {p} \Delta T _ {p} \right|\tag{6}$$

where $S _ {n}$ and $S _ {p}$ are the Seebeck coefficients of p- and n-type TE materials, respectively; $\Delta T _ {n}$ and $\Delta T _ {p}$ are the temperature differences of p- and n-type TE material, respectively.

If $q _ {i n}$ is taken away by the combined RC and TE conversion, then we can have:

$$h _ {e q} T _ {w} A = m _ {f} c _ {p} \Delta T _ {C} + ( | S _ {n} \Delta T _ {n} | + \left| S _ {p} \Delta T _ {p} \right| ) ^ {2} / ( 4 \mathrm{R} )\tag{7}$$

In addition, if a two-stage TE module is adopted, Eq. (7) will become:

$$\begin{array} {r l} & {h _ {e q} T _ {w} A = m _ {f} c _ {p} \Delta T _ {C} + ( | S _ {n m} \Delta T _ {n m} | + \big | S _ {p m} \Delta T _ {p m} \big | ) ^ {2} / ( 4 R _ {m} )} \\ & {\qquad + ( | S _ {n l} \Delta T _ {n l} | + \big | S _ {p l} \Delta T _ {p l} \big | ) ^ {2} / ( 4 R _ {l} )} \end{array}\tag{8}$$

where the subscripts $" m "$ and “l” indicate the mid-temperature and low-temperature TE materials, respectively.

In short, based on Eqs. (1) and (3), the heat flux that enters passive TPS, $q _ {i n}$ will be considered in the calculation of $q _ {a e r o}$ and $T _ {w} ,$ , which are used to determine the scale of passive TPS. For further design of TM subsystems, $q _ {i n}$ will be used to determine the parameters for RC network, TE component and the combination of both based on Eqs. (4), (5) and (7), respectively. Besides, $q _ {i n}$ is linearly dependent on $T _ {w}$ by a coefficient of overall equivalent HTC, this is reasonable from the point view of design, i.e., the active cooling should be enhanced in regions of severe aeroheating with higher $T _ {w}$

In summary, the primary function of the equivalent thermal equilibrium model is to establish the mutual correlation between aerodynamic heating and TM system rather than the one-way influence of aerodynamic heat on TM design, and have two specific aspects: 1st, the overall equivalent HTC is provided as an input design parameter in the aerodynamic heat calculation, thus the influence of TM system on the aerodynamic heat can be obtained; 2nd, all the TM subsystems will be influenced by the aerodynamic heating and is in accordance with the design principles, i.e., the passive TPS is dependent on the aerodynamic wall temperature and heat flux, while the RC network and TE component are mainly dependent on the wall temperature with a coefficient of $h _ {e q}$

## 3.3. Calculation of aerodynamic heat

In this work, the aerodynamic heat at the fuselage stagnation point is calculated based on the widely used Kemp-Riddell equation [48]. For other regions, it is predicted by an engineering-based program, which is based on the Prandtl boundary layer theory. The method has two basic assumptions:

a. The external flow field of the boundary layer is considered as inviscid flow, and the head, fuselage and wings of the vehicle are considered as flat for the calculation of aerodynamic force.

b. The internal flow field of the boundary layer is considered as viscous flow, and the reference temperature method is used to obtain a compressibility correction.

The inviscid flow is assumed to be the ideal gas and the aerodynamic parameters are calculated based on isentropic conditions. According to the vehicle’s configuration and speed, the calculation methods that adopted for the windward and leeward of the head, fuselage and wings are listed in Table 2 [13,29], and the detailed information can be found in Ref. [49]. The calculation of inviscid field will output parameters of the outer edge of the boundary layer, and those parameters will be used in the calculation of viscous flow field.

For the calculation of viscous compressible flow, the reference temperature is adopted to be a compressibility correction. The reference temperature $T ^ {*}$ is defined as:

$$T ^ {*} = 0.7 T _ {e} + 0.58 ( T _ {w} - T _ {e} ) + 0.23 ( T _ {r} - T _ {e} ) ( 5 < M a _ {e} < 10 )\tag{9}$$

where $T _ {e}$ is the temperature of the outer edge of the boundary layer, and the subscript $" e "$ indicates the outer edge of the boundary layer; $T _ {r}$ is the recovery temperature and can be obtained based on the parameters of the outer edge of boundary layer, which are calculated by the inviscid flow field. After $T _ {e}$ is obtained, the aerodynamic convection coefficient α in Eq. (1) will be determined based on the thermodynamic parameters under $T _ {e}$ The aerodynamic heat flux will be finally obtained based on the solving of Eq. (1) with the specified trajectory and overall equivalent HTC.

## 3.4. Design rationale of passive TPS

## 3.4.1. TPS concepts

The TPS concept database contains traditional concepts that employed in Space Shuttle and developed in subsequent NASP and Hyper-X engineering of NASA [10] and the new concept established in the authors’ previous work [29]. For the vehicle studied in this work, three concepts of the database, i.e., the ultra-high temperature ceramics tiles (UHTC), the second-generation superalloy honeycomb (SA-HC2) and the advanced flexible reusable surface insulation (AFRSI) are involved. The highest operational temperature of UHTC, SA-HC2 and AFRSI are 2500, 1366 and 922 K, respectively. More detailed description of the database, i.e., the structure, initial thickness and material physical properties can be found in Refs. [10,29].

## 3.4.2. Design rationale

The design of passive TPS contains two steps, the determination of TPS concept distribution and the calculation of the TPS concept scale including thickness and weight. After the calculation of aerodynamic heat, fields of the heat flux $q _ {a e r o}$ and the wall temperature $T _ {w}$ of the whole vehicle surface will be obtained. For specific subregions, the available TPS concept should meet the requirement of Eq. (10):

$$T _ {w} < T _ {T P S} < T _ {w} + T _ {t o l}\tag{10}$$

where $T _ {T P S}$ is the maximum allowable temperature of TPS concepts and it is included in the TPS concept database, $T _ {t o l}$ is the temperature tolerance. Then, the distribution of TPS concept will be determined based on $T _ {w}$ field.

After the TPS concept distribution is determined, the required thickness and weight of each concept can be obtained based on one-dimensional transient heat transfer analysis. For the external boundary of the heat transfer model, the boundary condition is the input heat flux $q _ {a e r o}$ and the radiation dissipation, while for the internal boundary, the boundary condition is the output heat flux $q _ {i n}$ . The heat transfer analysis is about to determine the temperature field inside the concept: if the temperature of each layer is lower than the materials’ highest operational temperature, and the temperature of the internal boundary is lower than the specified bottom temperature $T _ {b} ,$ , then the concept can be considered as meeting the thermal protection requirement, otherwise the thickness of the insulation layer should be increased. The scale of the TPS concept can be determined after an optimization process, and the mathematical description is shown in Eq. (11):

$$\begin{array} {r l} {\mathrm{Min}} & {\mathrm{Weight} ( x ) = \displaystyle \sum _ {i = 0} ^ {n} \rho _ {i} x _ {i}} \\ & {s . t . T _ {i} ( x ) < T _ {i \operatorname* {m a x}} , i = 1 , 2 , . . . , n} \\ & {T _ {n} ( x ) < T _ {b}} \end{array}\tag{11}$$

where n is the layer number of the TPS concept, x is the thickness of each layer, $T _ {i} ( x )$ and $T _ {n} ( x )$ are the calculated temperature of i and n−layer, respectively; $T _ {i \mathrm{max}}$ is the highest operational temperature of the material of i-layer. More detailed description can be found in the authors’ previous work [29].

The passive TPS of the vehicle can be finally determined with the obtained distribution, thickness and weight of TPS concept.

As shown in Fig. 4, the boundaries of the model can be classified as the upper wall, the lateral walls, the bottom wall, the inlet and the outlet. The boundary conditions are listed in Table 3. As shown in the table, the heat flux on the upper wall is the aerodynamic heat flux and the thermal radiation (emissivity of 0.8 and ambient temperature of 300 K); the temperature of the inlet coolant is 15 and 280 K for $\mathrm{LH} _ {2}$ and JET-A, respectively; the adiabatic thermal boundary conditions are imposed on the lateral walls and the bottom wall; the flow is mass flow inlet, and cases of different mass-flow-rate are studied in this work.

The flow condition, i.e., laminar or turbulent should be predicted before the calculation. For JET-A with 3 mm channel and 1 kg/s mass-flow-rate (larger than most key cases in this work), the Reynolds number of inlet is about 433 and smaller than the widely used critical Reynolds number, 2300; for LH2 with 3 mm channel, 0.055 kg/s mass-flow-rate (larger than most key cases in this work), the Reynolds number of inlet is about 2208 and also smaller than the critical number. Thus, the laminar viscous model is used in this work.

The Reynolds number 2208 for $\mathrm{LH} _ {2}$ case is close to critical number 2300 where the flow starts to transition to turbulence. In order to confirm the validity of the laminar viscous model, supplementary calculations with standard k-ω and SST k-ω turbulence models (available for low-Reynolds-number flows) are conducted. The results show that for LH2 with 3 mm channel and 0.055 kg/s mass-flow-rate, the average temperature of outlet is 143.6, 143.6 and 143.9 K for laminar, standard k-ω and SST k-ω models, respectively; the average temperature of TPS bottom line (RTV lower boundary) is 82.4, 82.1 and 82.4 K for laminar, standard k-ω and SST k-ω models, respectively. It is clear that the influence of the turbulence model is very limited.

## 3.6. TE concept and its optimization

## 3.6.1. TE-AFRSI concept and its numerical model

A TE-AFRSI concept in this work is developed by integrating TE materials into AFRSI concept (such type of concept is indicated as TE-TPS in this work). Fig. 5(a), (b), (c) and (d) show the TE-AFRSI, the unit cell, the geometric model and the meshed model, respectively. The two-stage of mid-temperature and low-temperature TE module is adopted in this work. For the TE-AFRSI concept, the mid-temperature TE stage is between the C9 coating layer and the upper AB312 layer, while the lower-temperature TE stage is between the lower AB312 layer and the RTV layer; the TE stages are attached to TPS concept layers by Ag paste in top and bottom sides; the TE materials are embeded in Q-fiber, and the Ag paste is used to connect p- and n-type materials by π -type and to conduct electricity. In this work, the low-temperature $p -$ and ntype TE materials are $\mathsf{Bi} _ {2} \mathsf{Te} _ {3}$ based compounds, and n-type one has additional 7% Cu2Te [51]; the mid-temperature p- and n-type TE materials are $\beta - Z {{\mathrm{b}}} _ {4} {{\mathrm{Sb}}} _ {3}$ [52] and $( \mathrm{Pb} _ {0.95} \mathrm{Sn} _ {0.05} \mathrm{Te} ) _ {0.92} ( \mathrm{Pb} \mathrm{S} ) _ {0.08}$ [53], respectively. The properties of TE materials are listed in Table 4, and the properties of materials for TPS concepts can be found in [10,50].

In this work, the thermal-electric analysis of the TE-AFRSI is carried out based on ANSYS Multiphysics. For the calculation, a unit cell including one couple of mid-temperature TE materials and one couple of low-temperature TE materials as shown in Fig. 5(b) is formulated. Based on the unit cell, the geometric model is established as shown in Fig. 5(c), in which the Ag paste is ignored for its high thermal and electric conductivity. The meshed model is shown in Fig. 5(d), the couple-field solid element SOLID226 and its thermal-electric analysis mode are adopted for TE material regions, the thermal solid element SOLID70 is adoped for other regions, the thermal surface effect element SURF152 is adopted to consider the thermal radiation, and the model has 21432 elements and 33281 nodes.

The boundary conditions are shown in Table 5. As shown in Fig. 5 (c), $A _ {1}$ and $A _ {2} , A _ {3}$ and $A _ {4} , ~ A _ {5}$ and $A _ {6}$ are boundary planes in x-, y- and z-directions, respectively. The unit cell is formulated based on translational symetries in x- and y-directions, thus $A _ {1}$ and $A _ {2} , \ A _ {3}$ and $A _ {4}$ should have periodic boundary conditions as shown in Table 5. Detailed descriptions for periodic boundary conditions can be found in the authors’ previous work [54]. For the bottom plane $A _ {5}$ , the convective heat transfer boundary condition is imposed on, while for the top plane $A _ {6} ,$ the aerodynamic heating and the thermal radiation boundary conditions are applied.

## 3.6.2. The concept optimization

In this work, the structure of TE-AFRSI is optimized, and the objective is to obtain the maximum electric power with minimum weight increment. The height of mid-temperature TE layer $t _ {1} ,$ the height of low-temperature TE layer $t _ {2} ,$ the length of TE material l (see Fig. 5), and the convective heat transfer coefficient $h _ {l}$ will influence the TE performance and thus are considered as optimization parameters.

In this work, three types of objectives are adopted in the optimization, i.e., the maximum TE conversion efficiency, the maximum electric power supply per unit volume density and the maximum amount of take-away heat flux. The mathematical descriptions can be described by Eqs. (12), (13) and (14), respectively.

$$\begin{array} {r l} {\mathsf{Max}} & {{} T E C = P / q _ {a e r o} / ( a b )} \end{array}$$

$$s . t . \quad 300 \mathsf{K} < T _ {b o t t o m} < 440 \mathsf{K}\tag{12}$$

$$\mathrm{Max} \quad P _ {D} = P / \rho _ {e}$$

$$s . t . \quad 300 \mathsf{K} < T _ {b o t t o m} < 440 \mathsf{K}\tag{13}$$

$$\begin{array} {r l} {\mathsf{Max}} & {{} H _ {r e u s e} = q _ {i n} / q _ {a e r o}} \end{array}$$

$$s . t . \quad 300 \mathsf{K} < T _ {b o t t o m} < 440 \mathsf{K}\tag{14}$$

where TEC is the TE conversion efficiency, P is the maximum electric power that the TE concept can supply, a and b are the boundary lengths of the unit cell and are assumed to be 14 and 7 mm, respectively, $P _ {D}$ is the power supply per unit volume density, $\rho _ {e}$ is the volume density of the concept, and $H _ {r e u s e}$ is the heat flux amount proportion that reused by convection and TE conversion. Therefore, the optimization problems can be stated as obtaining the maximum TEC, $H _ {r e u s e}$ and $P _ {D}$ , respectively, and meanwhile the temperature on the bottom surface is constrained between 300 and 440 K.

In this work, the Mixed Integer Sequential Quadratic Programming is adopted to do the optimization based on ISIGHT. The optimization scopes of geometric parameters are given as $1 \leq t _ {1} \leq 10$ mm, $1 \leq \ t _ {2} \ \leq 10$ mm, $1 \leq l \leq 6$ mm. The convective heat transfer coefficient is assumed to be $10 \leq h _ {l} \leq 25000 \mathrm{W/(m} ^ {2} {\cdot} \mathrm{K} )$ , and the minimum and maximum values are corresponding to the natural convection and phase-change heat transfer, respectively [55].

## 3.7. The unified governing equation

The governing equations of the heat conduction calculation of TPS, the CFD simulation of RC network and the thermal-electric simulation of TE-AFRSI can be described in a unified form as Eq. (15):

$$\frac {\partial ( \rho \phi )} {\partial t} + \mathrm{div} ( \rho \mathbf {\boldsymbol {v}} \phi ) = d i \nu ( \Gamma \mathbf {\boldsymbol {g} r a d} \phi ) + Q _ {s}\tag{15}$$

where $\rho$ is the desity, t is the time, V is the velocity vector, φ is the universal variable,  is the generalized diffusion coefficient and $Q _ {s}$ is the generalized source term. In this work, there are five types of equations are involved and the symbols for each equation are summarized in Table 6. It should be noted that for the first term of the electric potential equation, the density should be replaced by permittivity and such term is associated with the capacitive effect which is only appropriate in fast transient process, thus it has very limited influence and neglected in this work (no time-dependent change of heat flux). In Table 6, $V _ {i}$ indicates the velocity in i-direction; λ is the thermal conductivity; $\mu$ is the dynamic viscosity; j is the electric current density; k is the electric conductivity; $\psi$ is the Peltier coefficient and equals to Seebeck coefficient multiplying absolute temperature; S is the Seebeck coefficient; 
T is the temperature difference. It should be noted that, the source term for the energy equation (heat conduction) of TE materials is the Joule heating and Peltie effect, while for the electric potential equation of TE materials it is the Seebeck effect, and thus the thermal-electric coupling analysis is realized by such terms.

## 4.1.2. The reliability of aerodynamic heat calculation

During the cruise stage of the vehicle, the flight speed and altitude keep the same, thus the aerodynamic heat flux $\left( q _ {a e r o} \right)$ remains unchanged during the calculation time of 380 to 2738 s (see Fig. 1). For the case of $h _ {e q} = 0 ,$ the heat flux (aerodynamic heat in Fig. 8 dividing by trajectory time) on the leeward median line is between 19.6 to 15.4 $\mathsf{kW} / \mathsf{m} ^ {2}$ expect for the stagnation point of leading edge, and the reported results in Ref. [56] for X-34 with 6.3 Ma, 36 km altitude and $23 ^{\circ}$ attack angle are between 16 to 3.4 $\mathsf{kW} / \mathsf{m} ^ {2}$ (see “Fig. 15” in the Reference). Therefore, in consideration of the different configuration and trajectory between the vehicle and X-34, the aerodynamic heating calculation in this work is relatively reasonable.

It should be noted that the maximum aerodynamic heat flux at subregion $F _ {I I - L}$ increases from 19.1 to 22.2 $\mathrm{kW} / {\mathrm{m}} ^ {2}$ with $h _ {e q}$ increases from 0.1 to 20 $W / ( \mathrm{m} ^ {2} \cdot \mathrm{K} ) ,$ these values are used as the boundary conditions for CFD analysis of RC network and FEM analysis of TE-AFRSI.

The lines in Fig. 15 show that: 1st, for $\mathrm{LH} _ {2}$ , the $y$ coordinate and the channel size have very limited influence (channel size results into deviations of smaller than 1% in the enlarged segments) on the outlet coolant temperature; the outlet temperature decreases with the increase of $m _ {f}$ and the specific values are 434, 341, 279, 238, 208, 187, 170, 156 and 145 K for $m _ {f}$ of 0.015, 0.02, 0.025, 0.03, 0.035, 0.04, 0.045, 0.05 and 0.055 kg/s, respectively; 2nd, for JET-A, the outlet temperature increases with the decreasing $m _ {f}$ and increasing $y$ coordinate; the maximum local temperature appears at the TPS bottom and the minimum one appears at the channel bottom; the larger channel size leads to higher maximum local temperature $( T _ {3 \mathrm{mm}} > T _ {2 \mathrm{mm}}$ when $y > 0.001$ mm, T 3mm $> T _ {2 \mathrm{mm}} > T$ 1mm when $y > 0.002$ mm) while lower minimum one $( T _ {3 \mathrm{mm}}$ when $y \textless 0.001$ mm, $T _ {2 \mathrm{mm}}$ when 0.001 mm < $y \textless 0.002$ mm), however, it has very little influence on the average outlet temperature (between 360 to 320 K when $m _ {f}$ increase from 0.5 to 1 kg/s), which means that the heat amount (determined by $m _ {f} \cdot \triangle T _ {c} )$ that taken away by the coolant convection is independent on the size of cooling channels; 3rd, to be summarized, for $\mathrm{LH} _ {2} ,$ the channel size has very little influence on the cooling performance, while for JET-A, the increasing channel size leads to higher maximum local coolant temperature although it has little influence on the average temperature.

It can be found in Figs. 13, 14 and 15 that, for JET-A, the smaller channel results into better cooling effect (lower TPS temperature), although the heat that taken away by the coolant convection has the same amount for 1, 2 and 3 mm cooling channels. This can be due to the higher coolant velocity.

Considering the large size, multi structure layers and complex material composition of the CFD model, it is difficult to carry an experimental validation. Thus, the Nusselt number of fully developed fluid region is compared with values in Reference. In this work, the area of fully developed fluid region decreases with the increase of mass-flow-rate, e.g., $2.2 < x < 11.72$ m when $m _ {f} = 0.5$ kg/s and $10.9 < x < 11.72$ m when $m _ {f} = 2.5$ kg/s. As mentioned in Ref. [55] (page 250), under uniform heat flux, the Nusselt number of fully developed fluid inside a circular tube is 4.36. However, the heat flux in this work is not uniform. The heat flux at the solidfluid interface, $q _ {s - f}$ decreases with the increase of x (the radiation heat flux increases correspondingly), and the reduction trend slows down with the increase of $m _ {f} \colon$ the reduction scale $\scriptstyle ( x \ = \ 0 \ {\mathrm{~to~}} x \ =$ 11.72 m) decreases from 21% to 9.3% with $m _ {f}$ increasing from 0.5 to 2.5 kg/s. Thus, the cases with large $m _ {f}$ are used to compare with the reference value. For the cases of $\dot {m} _ {f} = 2.27 \ ( q _ {s - f}$ reduction scale is 9.7%) and 2.5 kg/s $( q _ {s - f}$ reduction scale is 9.3%), the Nusselt numbers of fluid region $x \ = \ 11$ to 11.72 m (fully developed) are 4.14 and 4.27, respectively. It can be found that, when the heat flux is close to a uniform distribution $( m _ {f} = 2.27$ and 2.5 kg/s), the calculated Nusselt number has a largest deviation of 4% from the reference value, the CFD model established in this work is thus validated to some extent.

## 4.5. TM system and its design roadmap

The results in Sections 4.2 and 4.3, Table 7, Fig. 17 and Table 9 reveal some characteristics for the TM system: the weight of TPS decreases, while the capacity of RC network increases with the increasing $h _ {e q} ;$ in this work, with $h _ {e q}$ increasing from 0 to 20 ${\sf W} / ( \mathrm{m} ^ {2} \cdot \mathrm{K} )$ , the weight of TPS decreases from 820 to 299 kg, the maximum heat amount proportion that will be reused by TE-AFRSI concept and RC network can be 31.5%, the maximum electric power supply can be above 20 kW for the whole vehicle with surface area of $145.9 \ \mathrm{m} ^ {2} ;$ for real conditions, the specified $h _ {e q}$ and the aerodynamic heat determine the heat capacity requirement of the RC network, the additional CFD simulations and bottom temperature, $T _ {b}$ can be used to determine the minimum limitation of mass-flow-rate of the RC network; the equivalent convective heat transfer coefficient of the RC network is obtained and input as the boundary conditions of TE-TPS concept, and after the TE analysis and optimization, an optimal TE-TPS concept is obtained.

The TM system consists of passive TPS, RC network (indicated as TPS-RC) and TE component (indicated as TE-TPS), and its design process can be summarized as four steps as shown in Fig. 18: 1st, the calculation of aerodynamic heat based on the input vehicle configuration, trajectory and overall equivalent HTC, $h _ {e q} ;$ 2nd, the determination of passive TPS and capacity of RC network based on the aerodynamic heat flux, wall temperature, the input bottom temperature $T _ {b} ,$ and the database of TPS concepts and fuel-coolant properties; 3rd, the determination of the optimal TE-TPS based on the TE analysis and optimization of TE-TPS concept that formed by TPS and TE module; the determination of TPS-RC network based on the CFD analysis and the capacity requirement of RC network; 4th, the iterative design until the TPS, RC network and TE component meet the overall requirements of vehicle design.

In this work, a TM system design roadmap that can coupling consider the aerodynamic heat calculation, the design of passive TPS, the design of RC and TE conversion subsystems is developed. For the aerodynamic heat calculation, the TPS design and the preliminary computation of heat capacity of RC network, some “quick” models and methods are developed; for the final determination of the mass-flow-rate of RC network, a series of further CFD simulations is needed, and the CFD model needs aerodynamic heat as boundary conditions and TPS as solid structure. Although the CFD model is simple, the simulation cases should cover a range of conditions, thus the whole design is relatively time-consuming. On the other hand, if the “quick” models and methods are replaced by CFD ones, we need to simulate the aerodynamic heating, solid heat conduction, convective cooling at the same time, it will be very difficult to obtain a converged solution.

## 5. Conclusions

In this paper, a TM system is designed for a hypersonic vehicle with length of 15 m and wingspan of about 6 m under a certain cruise trajectory. The system consists of passive TPS, RC network and TE component, and the aerodynamic heat is dissipated by the passive TPS, transported by the RC network, and reused by the RC network and the TE component. An equivalent thermal equilibrium model is developed and the mutual correlation between the aerodynamic heating and TM system is established, an overall equivalent heat transfer coefficient is introduced to indicate the heat capacity of RC network and TE component, and the rationale of the design of such a TM system is stated. For passive TPS, the distribution, area and weight of relevant concepts are determined; for RC network, the distribution of heat capacity requirement for the whole vehicle is obtained, a CFD model for AFRSI-RC is established, the hydrocarbon and liquid hydrogen fuel are considered as coolants, and the minimum limitation of mass-flow-rate is determined; for TE component, a TE-AFRSI concept is introduced, the optimal concepts are obtained based on the optimization of different objectives. The influence of overall equivalent HTC $( h _ {e q} = 0$ to 20 ${\sf W} / ( \mathrm{m} ^ {2} \cdot \mathrm{K} ) )$ and cold side temperature $\left( T _ {b} \right. = \left. 300 \right$ , 367 and 440 K) on the aerodynamic heat and TM system are studied.

The results show some conclusions:

1. With the increasing of $h _ {e q}$ the wall temperature decreases, while the aerodynamic heat increases; the heat amount proportion that transported and reused increases, while that dissipated decreases; the required capacity of RC network and TE component should be enhanced, while the scale of TPS can be reduced.

2. The wall temperature at the leading edge decreases from 1690 to 1675 K, the lowest wall temperature on the median line of leeward surface decreases from 762 to 601 K, the maximum wall temperature at the third compression surface decreases from 1028 to 964 K when $h _ {e q}$ increases from 0 to 20 ${\sf W} / ( \mathrm{m} ^ {2} \cdot \mathrm{K} )$

3. The TPS weight decreases with the increasing $T _ {b}$ and $h _ {e q} ,$ and increasing $h _ {e q}$ is more efficient when $T _ {b}$ is small and vice versa. However, although increasing $T _ {b}$ is a very efficient way, it is still limited by the material option and the internal environment requirement of the vehicle. The weight of TPS is reduced by 62.7%, 54.0%, 36.4%, 1.3%, 0.7%, 0%, 0% and 0% for $h _ {e q} = 0 , 0.1 , 1 , 2 , 5 ,$ , 10, 15 and 20 ${\sf W} / ( {\sf m} ^ {2} \cdot {\sf K} ) ,$ , respectively.

4. For RC network, the coolant m f decreases and the trend slows down with the increasing coolant temperature rise. The required $m _ {f}$ of $\mathrm{LH} _ {2}$ is much smaller than JET-A due to the larger specific heat capacity of $\mathrm{LH} _ {2}$ , and indicates that LH2 has stronger cooling ability. For $\mathrm{LH} _ {2}$ , the channel size has very little influence on the cooling performance, while for JET-A, the smaller channel size results into better cooling performance.

5. The RC network with JET-A of the second and third leeward surface parts, for cases of $h _ {e q} \ = \ 0.1 ,$ 1 and $2 \ \mathrm{W} / ( \mathrm{m} ^ {2} {\cdot} \mathrm{K} )$ the required $m _ {f}$ is mainly limited by $T _ {b}$ and it should be larger than 0.23 kg/s for $T _ {b} ~ = ~ 440$ K and 2.27 kg/s for $T _ {b} ~ = ~ 300$ K; while for cases of $h _ {e q} = 5$ and $10 \ \mathsf{W} / ( \mathsf{m} ^ {2} {\cdot} \mathsf{K} ) , m _ {f}$ is mainly limited by the heat capacity of the RC network and it should be larger than 2.5 and 20.1 kg/s, respectively; for $h _ {e q} = 15$ and 20 ${\sf W} / ( \mathrm{m} ^ {2} {\cdot} \mathrm{K} )$ , the RC coolant convection involved in this work is unable to meet the heat capacity requirment.

6. The RC network with LH2 of the second and third leeward surface parts, for cases of $h _ {e q} \ = \ 0.1 ,$ , 1 and $2 \ \mathrm{W} / ( \mathrm{m} ^ {2} {\cdot} \mathrm{K} )$ , the required $m _ {f}$ is mainly limited by $T _ {b}$ and it should be larger than 0.015 and 0.02 kg/s for $T _ {b} = 440$ and 300 K, respectively, while for cases of $h _ {e q} = 5 \ \mathrm{W/(m} ^ {2} {\cdot} \mathrm{K} )$ , m f is mainly limited by the heat capacity of the RC network and it should be larger than 0.04 kg/s; for $h _ {e q} = 10$ , 15 and 20 ${\sf W} / ( \mathrm{m} ^ {2} \cdot \mathrm{K} )$ , the coolant convection involved in this work is unable to meet the heat capacity requirments.

7. For the TE-AFRSI concept and the cruising trajectory in this work, the largest TE conversion efficiency is 0.90%, the largest electric power supply per unit volume density is $14.2 \times 10 ^ {- 6}$ $W {\cdot} \mathrm{m} ^ {3} / \mathrm{kg} ,$ the largest electric power supply per unit weight is 9.56 W/kg, the largest electric power supply per unit area is 178.4 W/m and the largest electric power supply per unit areal density is $0.94 \times 10 ^ {- 3}$ W·m2/kg.

8. The maximum heat amount proportion that can be reused by RC network and TE component is 31.5%; if such a TE-AFRSI concept is used in the second and third leeward surface part, the largest electric power supply can be about 6.16 kW, while if it is adopted for the whole vehicle surface, the largest electric power supply can be above 20 kW.
