## 1. Introduction

For hypersonic vehicles with the characteristics of slender configuration and high-speed, the long-time flight in dense atmosphere will lead to serious aerodynamic heat concentration and accumulation [1,2]. In traditional thermal protection system (TPS), the heat is mainly dissipated by the thermal radiation of the insulated structure, whose size and weight could be unaffordable under hypersonic conditions [2,3]. Besides, from the point view of energy reuse, the efficiency of traditional TPS is low. Nowadays, the TPS integrated with thermoelectric generators (TEGs) become a more efficient and promising technique for hypersonic vehicles [4–6]. A TEG always consists of four type of materials (N- and P-type TE legs, electrodes and substrates) and includes about eight key contacting interfaces (four contacting interfaces between substrates and electrodes, four contacting interfaces between electrodes and TE legs). Actually, substrates, electrodes and TE legs have micro-scale rough surfaces, and such type of roughness will lead to the very limited real contact region and a large proportion of contact clearance. When the heat flow through the hot-side substrates, the hot-side electrodes, the N- and P-type TE legs, the cold-side electrodes and the cold-side substrates, the contact clearance of each contacting interfaces leads to thermal contact resistance (TCR). Such accumulated interfacial TCRs have significant influence on the heat transfer process, and weaken the TE performance of TEGs.

The TCR prediction mainly contains three steps, i.e., the characterization of rough surface, the analysis of contact deformation and the calculation of heat transfer. The proper characterization of rough surface is essential in accurately estimating the TCR. The rough surface can be reconstructed based on GW statistical model [7], Weierstrass–Mandelbrot fractal ones [8], Euclidean geometric statistical ones [9], facial ones [10], and semi-deterministic-based approach [11]. While the assumptions involved in the above characterizations weaken their accuracy. The optical profiler makes it accurate to measure the rough surface topography [12]. Frekers et al. [13] proposed a micro-scale numerical method to predict the contact heat transfer based on the generation model of optical profiler. The authors [14] established a TCR prediction model based on the measured real topology and the model is validated by experiments. Dai et al. [15] and Ren et al. [16] developed the method from rectangle-like cross section contacting model to the circular ones. Recently, the authors [17] studied the TCRs between TE legs and electrodes by real-topology-based simulations. However, the above researches mainly concentrated in the effect of solid conduction on the heat transfer process, and few of them consider the far-field thermal radiation (FFTR) effect.

In fact, when the separation between two objects is comparable to or below the dominant emitted wavelength, the net radiative heat flux can be enhanced [18], and that is so-called the near-field thermal radiation (NFTR) effect. The near-field thermal radiative heat flux between two planes separated by a nanoscale vacuum clearance can exceed several orders of magnitude of the blackbody limit [19] and even reach a similar order of magnitude of heat conduction [20,21]. The characteristic wavelength of thermal radiation is about 10 μm at 300 K according to Wien’s displacement law [22], and the engineering surface roughness is comparable to or less than such a magnitude. For the recent research of radiative heat transfer in nanoparticles system, Luo et al. [23] derived a continuum-scale normal-diffusion type governing equation to calculate the radiative thermal conductivity, and Liu et al. [24] developed a method based on the radiation distribution factor. For the studies of TCRs with near-filed effect, Persson et al. [25] approximately estimated the influence of the NFTR on the TCR at the first time, and found that the contribution of the radiative heat transfer is not weaker than that of the solid conduction. Xian et al. [26] adopted the fluctuational electrodynamics to calculate the NFTR on the TCR, and found that the NFTR should not be neglected even at room temperature when the effective roughness is near to the submicron scale. Chen et al. [27] predicted the high temperature TCR between HTA and $\mathrm {C / Z r B _ {2}} - S \mathrm{iC}$ considering NFTR, and found that the TCR account for the solid conduction and the thermal radiation is reduced to 1/25 of that only considering the solid conduction at 1100 K. Li et al. [28] theoretically investigated the effect of rough surfaces on the performance of the near-field thermophotovoltaic systems. The materials that involved in above NFTR researches are common materials like Al, C/SiC composite, HTA, etc. The study of TE materials is very limited.

(b) Micro-scale TCR with radiation effect

For the TCR measurements, the steady-state method [21,29–34] is widely used for bulk materials [35]. Most of the current work involved in TEGs [34,36,37] are measured the TCR between the TEG and cold/- heat source, and only a few focused on the heterogeneous interfaces inside of the TEG. The authors [17] measured the TCRs between TE legs and electrodes by indirect method, but did not measure the TCRs between substrates and electrodes. For the influence of the TCR on the TEG, a lot of works in theoretical analysis [38] and numerical simulations [39–42] are carried, but lack of complete and reliable data on the TCRs of the heterogeneous interfaces inside of TEGs. The authors [17] quantitatively evaluated the influence of the TCRs between TE legs and electrodes on the performance of TEGs, but did not consider the TCRs between substrates and electrodes. For a TEG, the number of the contacting interfaces between the substrates and the electrodes is almost equal to that of the TE legs and the electrodes. Thus, the influence of the TCRs between substrates and electrodes on the TEG performance cannot be ignored.

In this work, a thermal contact model encompassing NFTR of TEGs with heterogeneous interfaces between substrates, electrodes and TE legs is established and experimentally validated in hypersonic conditions. The fluctuational electrodynamics (an ab initio calculation) is adopted to calculate the radiative heat transfer. The effects of average interfacial temperature, clearance distance and opposite heat transfer paths on the thermal radiation are analyzed. Furthermore, the effects of average interfacial temperature, clearance distance and opposite heat transfer paths on the thermal radiation are analyzed, and the influences of interfacial temperature, loading pressure and clearance mediums on the TCR are then clarified. Finally, the TCR is equivalently considered in a TEG performance evaluation model and the NFTR effects is quantitatively counted.

## 2. Thermal contact models

## 2.1. The multi-interface TEG and TCR

Fig. 1 is the schematic figure of the thermal contact model. Fig. 1(a) and 1 (b) show the TEG with multi-interface and thermal contact resistances (TCRs) with radiation effect. As shown in Fig. 1(a), the multiinterface TEG is applied to the leeward regions of the hypersonic vehicle with a working temperature range of about 600 to 800 K (more detailed descriptions of the vehicle can be found in the authors’ previous work [2]). The multi-interface TEG consists of four alumina ceramic substrates, N- and P-type TE legs, three copper electrodes and one poly-ether-ether-ketone (PEEK) shell. The envelope sizes of the TEG and the TE legs are about 17 × 5 × 23 mm and 5 × 3 × 12 mm, respectively. The thickness of the electrodes and the substrates are about 2.5 and 3 mm, respectively. A hole with a diameter of 1.5 mm is drilled in the thickness center of the electrodes and the substrates for temperature sensors. The materials of the substrates, the electrodes, the P- and N-type TE legs are alumina, copper, La0. $\mathrm {_ 1 C e _ {0.8} F e _ {3} C o S b _ {12}}$ and Yb0.29Co4Sb12, respectively. It should be noted that the configurations and geometric sizes of the TEG, the TE legs, the substrates and the electrodes shown in Fig. 1(a) are consistent with the samples used in the experimental measurements of this work. The multi-interface TEG has about eight crucial interfaces, and the micro-scale rough surfaces will lead to very limited real contacting region and thus the constriction of heat flow. Such constriction of heat flow leads to TCR and weakens the performance of TEGs. As shown in the right part of Fig. 1(a), when the clearance medium is vacuum or air, the heat flow is transferred through the solid conduction in the real contact region and thermal radiation in the clearance area. It should be noted that since the convective effect is very limited for the micro-scale clearance, only the radiation effect of the contact clearance is considered in the heat transfer simulation of the contacting pairs.

As shown in Fig. 1(b), the TCR is a result of three interaction among geometry, mechanics and thermal [43]. The heat transfer modes at the interfaces of the contacting pairs can be interpreted as the transport in the form of energy carriers (i.e., phonons, electrons and photons) from the perspective of micro-nano scale [44]. As shown in the right part of Fig. 1(b), the energy exchange of thermal radiation can be described by radiative transfer equation (RTE) or Planck’s blackbody radiation law when the distance between the two objects is far greater than the characteristic wavelength of thermal radiation. The energy transfer in the far field is considered to be incoherent, and the thermal radiation can be abstracted as light particles. However, when the distance between the two objects is less than the radiation characteristic wavelength, that is, the electromagnetic wave will present a total internal reflection effect at the interface when the electromagnetic wave propagating in the first medium meets the optical sparse medium (such as vacuum) and the incident angle is greater than the critical angle. Although the electromagnetic wave is completely reflected at the interface between the first medium and the optical sparse medium, there are still evanescent waves whose amplitude decays exponentially in the direction perpendicular to the interface. The energy of the evanescent wave can be transferred to the second object (also known as the photon tunneling effect), thus enhancing the energy exchange between the two objects. In the near field, the heat source will show a high degree of space-time coherence due to the existence of surface waves. The surface wave is also called surface polaritons, the mixed mode of free electrons and electromagnetic radiation is called surface plasmon polaritons (SPPs), and the mixed mode of lattice vibration and electromagnetic field is called surface phonon polaritons (SPhPs). Similar to the evanescent wave, the amplitude of surface wave decays exponentially in the direction perpendicular to the interface. Above all, the near-field thermal radiation indicates that the energy transfer is mainly dependent on dielectric function of material and separation distance between contact components, and it should be analyzed by fluctuational electrodynamics [45].

## 3. Numerical models

The numerical prediction of thermal contact resistances with thermal radiation effects in TEGs includes four processes, $\mathrm{i.e.,}$ radiative heat transfer calculation, contact deformation, heat transfer and thermoelectric conversion. The radiative heat transfer of the contacting pairs is firstly calculated based on wave electrodynamics. The contact deformation is obtained by mechanical analysis, and then the temperature field is obtained by the coupling simulation of the last two processes. It should be noted that considering the limited influence of the temperature on the contact state, it is reasonable to adopt step-by-step coupling method.

## 3.1. Governing equations

The simulation of the contact deformation involves elastic and plastic processes. For the elastic stage, the equilibrium, geometric and constitutive equations are Eqs. (1), (2) and (3), respectively. For the plastic stage, the equilibrium, geometric and constitutive equations are Eqs. (1), (2) and (4), respectively.

Equilibrium equation:

$$\frac {\partial {\sigma _ {x}}} {\partial x} + \frac {\partial {\tau _ {y x}}} {\partial y} + \frac {\partial {\tau _ {z x}}} {\partial z} + f _ {x} = 0$$

$$\frac {\partial \tau _ {x y}} {\partial x} + \frac {\partial \sigma _ {y}} {\partial y} + \frac {\partial \tau _ {z y}} {\partial z} + f _ {y} = 0\tag{1}$$

$$\frac {\partial \tau _ {x z}} {\partial x} + \frac {\partial \tau _ {y z}} {\partial y} + \frac {\partial \sigma _ {z}} {\partial z} + f _ {z} = 0$$

where $f _ {x} , f _ {y}$ and $f _ {z}$ are components of unit body force in $x , y$ and z directions; $\tau _ {i j}$ is the shear stress; $\sigma _ {i} ( i = x , y , z )$ is the normal stress. The strain and displacement satisfy the geometric equations as Eq. (2).

Geometric equation:

$$\varepsilon _ {x} = \frac {\partial u} {\partial x} ; \varepsilon _ {y} = \frac {\partial \nu} {\partial y} ; \varepsilon _ {z} = \frac {\partial w} {\partial z}$$

$$\gamma _ {y z} = \frac {\partial \nu} {\partial z} + \frac {\partial w} {\partial y} ; \gamma _ {x z} = \frac {\partial u} {\partial z} + \frac {\partial w} {\partial x}\tag{2}$$

$$\gamma _ {x y} = \frac {\partial u} {\partial y} + \frac {\partial \nu} {\partial x}$$

where $\scriptstyle \varepsilon _ {i} ( i = x , y , z )$ is the strain; $u ,$ v and w are displacements in $x , y$ and z directions; $\gamma _ {i j}$ is the shear strain. The elastic stress-strain relation follows the Hooke’s law as shown in Eq. (3).

Constitutive equations for elastic stage:

$$d \sigma _ {i j} = D _ {i j k l} ^ {e} d \varepsilon _ {k l} ^ {e}\tag{3}$$

where $\varepsilon _ {k l} ^ {e}$ is the elastic strain; $D _ {i j k l} ^ {e}$ is the elasticity matrix.

Constitutive equations for plastic stage:

$$d \sigma _ {i j} = D _ {i j k l} ^ {e p} \cdot d \varepsilon _ {k l} = \left( D _ {i j k l} ^ {e} - D _ {i j k l} ^ {p} \right) d \varepsilon _ {k l}\tag{4}$$

where $D _ {i j k l} ^ {e}$ is the elasticity matrix; $D _ {i j k l} ^ {p} \mathrm{i} s$ the plasticity matrix.

The source term for the energy equation (heat conduction) of TE materials is the Joule heating and Peltie effect (see Eq. (5)), while for the electric potential equation of TE materials it is the Seebeck effect (see Eqs. (6)), and the thermoelectric coupling analysis is realized by such terms.

$$\frac {\partial ( \rho T )} {\partial t} = d i \nu \bigg ( \frac {\lambda} {c _ {p}} \mathbf {g r a d} T \bigg ) + \left( j \psi + j ^ {2} / k \right)\tag{5}$$

$$\frac {\partial ( \rho U )} {\partial t} = d i \nu \Big ( \frac {k} {\varepsilon} \mathbf {g r a d} U \Big ) + k S \Delta T\tag{6}$$

where ρ is the desity; λ is the thermal conductivity; $c _ {p}$ is the specific heat capacity; j is the electrical current density; ψ is the Peltier coefficient and equals to Seebeck coefficient multiplying absolute temperature; U is the electrical potential; k is the electrical conductivity; S is the Seebeck coefficient; ΔTis the temperature difference.

## 3.2. Calculation method of TCR

The thermal contact resistance can be calculated by Eq. (7):

$$\mathrm{TCR} = \frac {\Delta T} {q _ {c} + q _ {r}} = \frac {\Delta T} {q _ {c} + q _ {n r} + q _ {f r}} = \sum _ {i = 1} ^ {n} \frac {1} {h _ {i}}\tag{7}$$

where ΔT is the average temperature difference of two contact interfaces, while $q _ {c}$ and q are the heat flux of solid conduction and thermal radiation, respectively. In which $q _ {n r}$ and $q _ {f r}$ are the heat flux of nearfield and far-field thermal radiation, respectively. h is the thermal contact conductance (TCC) of each contact element, and is considered as the function of gap distance and given as a tabular input in ABAQUS. The clearance distance range of the tabular is from ${{10} ^ {- 6}}$ to 10◦ mm, which is larger than the possible clearance distances $( 6.30 \times 10 ^ {- 5}$ to $3.43 \times 10 ^ {- 2}$ mm of “S-E”, $1.79 \times 10 ^ {- 4}$ to $1.05 \times 10 ^ {- 1}$ mm of $\mathrm {^ {66} E} {\cdot} N \bot \Gamma ^ {\prime} ,$ $1.33 \times 10 ^ {- 3}$ to 4.82 $\times 10 ^ {- 2}$ mm of “E-N\_u”, $\phantom {+} 1.19 \times 10 ^ {- 3}$ to $4.85 \times 10 ^ {- 2}$ mm of “E-P\_l”, $2.92 \times 10 ^ {- 3} \mathrm{to} 2.90 \times 10 ^ {- 2}$ mm of $\mathrm {^ {66} E} {\cdot} P _ - \mathrm{u} ^ {\prime \prime} )$ of this work. The TCC of each contact element is defined as:

$$h _ {i} = \left\{\begin{array} {c} {{h _ {c c} ( d _ {i} = 0 )}} \\ {{h _ {m c} + h _ {n r} + h _ {f r} ( d _ {i} \neq 0 )}} \end{array} \right.\tag{8}$$

(b) Comparison with Fig. 11 in literature [26] and Fig. 6 in literature [27]

where $d _ {i}$ is the distance between the corresponding contact elements; $h _ {c c}$ is the TCC for the real contact elements; $h _ {m c}$ is the TCC of clearance medium for the non-contact elements. $h _ {n r}$ and $h _ {f r}$ are the equivalent thermal conductance of near-field and far-field thermal radiation, respectively. In this study, the TCC for the real contact elements is assumed to be perfect and given as $1 \times 10 ^ {10} \mathrm{W/m} ^ {2} / \mathrm{K}$ The influence of such value on the TCR can be found in Appendix B in the authors previous work [17]. For the non-contact elements, $h _ {m c}$ is 0 when the clearance medium is vacuum, and is equal to the thermal conductivity divided by the clearance distance when the clearance medium is air. The equivalent thermal conductance of thermal radiation can be calculated as follows:

$$\left\{\begin{array} {c} {h _ {n r} ^ {T} = \displaystyle \frac {q _ {n r} ^ {T}} {( T + 1 / 2 ) - ( T - 1 / 2 )} = q _ {n r} ^ {T}} \\ {h _ {f r} ^ {T} = \displaystyle \frac {q _ {f r} ^ {T}} {( T + 1 / 2 ) - ( T - 1 / 2 )} = q _ {f r} ^ {T}} \end{array} \right.\tag{9}$$

where T represents temperature. That is, the value of the equivalent thermal conductance for the NFTR of A (source surface) to B (target surface) at T, is equal to the heat flux of the NFTR when set the temperatures of A and B to $T + 1 / 2$ and $T - 1 / 2$ , respectively. The equivalent thermal conductance of the FFTR is similar.

## 5.3. The evaluation of thermal radiation on TEG performance

Fig. 21 shows the temperature contour of the TEG in considering of the solid conduction, the far- and the near-filed thermal radiation under vacuum and air clearance. As shown in Fig. 21, the FFTR and the NFTR both obviously affect the temperature distribution of the TEG under vacuum clearance, and have slight influence under air clearance. Besides, the FFTR and the NFTR have a greater impact on the temperature distribution of the TEG when the loading pressure is small, and this is consistent with the quantitative description of the thermal radiation (see the 1st to 4th paragraphs in Section 5.2.1). In addition, when the grid number increases to 19,864 elements and 86,408 nodes, the distribution of the temperature and potential nephogram have little change, and the maximum output power with the consideration of the solid conduction has deviation of less than 1.7% (0.4 MPa, vacuum clearance); and when the grid number decreases to 12,468 elements and 58,892 nodes, the deviation is less than 2.1%. The relatively small deviations indicate mesh-independence of the mesh size.

Fig. 22 shows the influence of the thermal radiation effect on the performance of the TEG. One can find that the temperature differences of the N- and the P-type TE legs are obviously increased by considering the FFTR and the NFTR under vacuum clearance, and slightly increased under air clearance, thus the voltage and the output power generated by the TE legs are both increased. The quantitative description can be summarized as follows: Compared to only considering the solid conduction at heterogeneous interfaces of the TEG, with the consideration of the FFTR and the NFTR, the temperature difference of the N-type TE leg increased by about 5.47% and 4.71% (0.1 MPa, vacuum clearance), 3.21% and 2.34% (0.4 MPa, vacuum clearance), 0.939% and 0.879% (0.1 MPa, air clearance), 0.623% and 0.630% (0.4 MPa, air clearance), respectively; the temperature difference of the P-type TE leg increased by about 6.25% and 5.32% (0.1 MPa, vacuum clearance), 3.74% and 2.85% (0.4 MPa, vacuum clearance), 0.480% and 0.492% (0.1 MPa, air clearance), 0.308% and 0.262% (0.4 MPa, air clearance), respectively; the voltage of the TEG increased by about 4.69% and 4.13% (0.1 MPa, vacuum clearance), 2.90% and 2.16% (0.4 MPa, vacuum clearance), 0.664% and 0.635% (0.1 MPa, air clearance), 0.434% and 0.441% (0.4 MPa, air clearance), respectively; and the maximum output power of the TEG increased by about 9.58% and 8.02% (0.1 MPa, vacuum clearance), 4.51% and 5.88% (0.4 MPa, vacuum clearance), 1.33% and 1.29% (0.1 MPa, air clearance), 0.873% and 0.883% (0.4 MPa, air clearance), respectively. In conclusion, with the consideration of the FFTR, the voltage and the maximum output power of the TEG increased by about 0.434% to 4.69% and 0.873% to 9.58%, respectively; and that increased by about 0.441% to 4.13% and 0.883% to 8.02% with the consideration of the NFTR.

## 6. Conclusion

In this work, a thermal contact model encompassing near-field thermal radiation (NFTR) effects of thermoelectric generators (TEGs)

with heterogeneous interfaces between substrates, electrodes and thermoelectric (TE) legs is established and experimentally validated in hypersonic conditions. To begin with, a real-topology-based thermal contact resistance (TCR) prediction model is developed and the far- and near-field radiative heat transfer at heterogeneous interfaces is considered by the fluctuational electrodynamics, and the model is validated by direct and indirect experimental measurements. For another thing, the effects of average interfacial temperature, clearance distance and opposite heat transfer paths on the thermal radiation are analyzed, and the influences of average interfacial temperature, loading pressure and clearance mediums on the TCR are then clarified. And thirdly, the TCR is equivalently considered in a TEG performance evaluation model and the NFTR effects is quantitatively counted. The results show some conclusions:

1 In the numerical prediction of the TCR, the thermal analysis is completed by considering of the solid conduction, the far- and the near-field thermal radiation between the real-topology-based rough surfaces. The proposed approach can be used to predict the TCR with thermal radiation effect under different temperatures, loading pressures and clearance mediums.

2 The thermal radiation effect has an enormous contribution to the heat transfer between the rough surfaces of the TE legs and the electrodes under vacuum clearance. The contribution of the thermal radiation to the total heat flux of “E-N\_l”, “E-N\_u”, “E-P\_l” and “E-P\_u” reached about 48.9%, 46.6%, 71.4% and 81.6% at 725 K, respectively; in which the contribution of the NFTR is about 20.9%, 20.0%, 29.3% and 34.2%, respectively.

3 The NFTR effect is essential for precisely evaluate the TCRs at heterogeneous interfaces of TEGs. With the consideration of the NFTR effect, the TCRs between the TE legs and the electrodes, the substrates and the electrodes reduced by about 3.90% to 17.0% and 0.510% to 6.40% under vacuum clearance, 1.30% to 5.50% and 0.220% to 2.00% under air clearance, respectively.

4 It is necessary to consider the heat transfer path when the equivalent thermal contact conductance of the NFTR effect for two materials has large difference in magnitude. The TCR of the “S-E” reached a maximum difference of about 10.0% at 480 K in different heat transfer paths under vacuum clearance.

5 The TCR prediction model is validated by direct and indirect experimental measurements under different temperatures and loading pressures, and the experimental and numerical results have a relatively good agreement with the largest differences of about 6.07%, − 6.00%, − 5.76%, 6.56% and 6.13% for the “S-E”, “E-N\_l”, “E-N\_u”, “E-P\_l” and “E-P\_u”, respectively.

6 For a TEG containing about 8 heterogeneous interfaces, it is crucial to consider the NFTR effect for accurate evaluating its TE performance. The maximum output power of the TEG increases by about 0.883% to 8.02% with the consideration of the NFTR effect.
